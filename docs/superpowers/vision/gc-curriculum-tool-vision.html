The GC Curriculum Tool

Working backwards from career destinations and upwards from foundational courses — a periodically-refreshed view of where the two meet, where the scaffolding holds, and where the curriculum has drifted. The end state is genuinely living; the current build is a manually-refreshed snapshot grid, with self-updating machinery scheduled for Phase 2.

§What we're building

Today, the questions of "how well does this curriculum prepare students for the careers we claim?" and "do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what this course expects?" get answered through committee discussions, gut intuition, and the occasional seven-year program review. Faculty know parts of the answer for the courses they teach. Nobody has the whole picture, and nobody has a way to update it as courses evolve.

The tool makes evidence-backed draft judgments visible and disputable — for advising students, for accreditation conversations, for industry advisory-board reviews, for the next curriculum redesign, and for hiring decisions about what kind of faculty the program needs. Defensibility depends on source coverage, instructor confirmation, and review of flagged cells.

§The core questions

Q1 · Program → Careers
How well does the GC curriculum, as a whole, prepare students for the career destinations we claim to lead them toward?
Q2 · Prereqs → Course
For any individual course, do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what the course expects?
Q3 · Constraints → Best Choice
Within the fixed rules of our degree plan, which constrained-choice options most directly build toward each career destination — and where are our open slots a wasted opportunity?
Q4 · Student → Target
For a student declaring a career target, which path through required courses, constrained choices, and elective pools gives them the strongest preparation — and what do employers in that role actually look for?

Both questions get answered against the same competency framework — career destinations defined as sub-competencies at Know / Understand / Do levels. The two answers feed each other: program coverage rolls up from course-level judgments; course-level prerequisite gaps surface where the program isn't actually scaffolding what its own upper-division courses expect. Questions Three and Four introduce the constraint dimension: not every building block is freely chosen. The degree plan's fixed requirements, constrained-choice slots, and elective pools define the real design space — and within that space, some choices prepare students for their declared targets far better than others.

§What we're producing

Beneath the curriculum sits a clear picture of what every GC graduate should be: a tenacious, fearless problem-solver, equipped with both program-wide foundational habits and deep expertise in one of the five career destinations. Career targets are where a graduate is heading; the foundational competencies are what they are when they get there.

The foundational competencies — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication — should show up across every course in the program, regardless of act or specialty. They're program-wide threads, not something any single course delivers in isolation.

The five career targets — Account Management, Brand Strategy, Production & Ops, Creative Generalist, and AI Workflow — are the domain-specific destinations where deep expertise builds across multiple courses. These are what the coverage matrix and scaffolding analysis read at the course level. The pyramid and the target framework are the same thing: every coverage score, every scaffolding judgment, and every slot-optimizer rating maps directly to one of these five bands.

The tool maps both layers. The clusters get explicit coverage analysis; the foundational competencies show up implicitly inside every reasoned judgment. A course developing Quality Control at Do level may create strong conditions for Attention to Detail, but the disposition should be scored only when assignments or rubrics require accuracy, error-catching, revision, or similar observable behaviors. Over time the tool can surface which courses are doing the most for each foundational competency — and which aren't pulling their weight.

§The through-line: course → curriculum → career

The surfaces in this document — the audit, the coverage matrix, the wiki, the partner side — are one continuous chain of evidence at three zoom levels, not separate tools. The chain is what makes Q1 answerable.

Course. A single capture produces an immutable Course Outcome Profile: what this course is evidenced to develop, scored K/U/D at graduated depth, with every finding tagged by provenance (instructor claim / course materials / cited student work) and citations back to the source. This is the atom.

Curriculum. As confirmed snapshots accumulate, they aggregate with no further capture work into the cross-course picture: the program coverage matrix (snapshots × career-target sub-competencies), the scaffolding analysis (does a competency get introduced, then practiced, then integrated across the sequence — or land at depth with no setup?), the prerequisite graph (do courses actually hand off what their successors expect?), and the self-maintaining wiki (a cross-linked, queryable narrative and typed-graph of the whole program). This is where "what does the program build?" stops being oral tradition.

Career. The career targets name the destinations the program claims to prepare students for. Position Capture turns real job postings into structured demand — what each destination actually requires, per competency, at depth — and the partner survey + AI synthesis keep the target definitions honest against industry.

The join is Q1. "Does the curriculum build students toward the careers we claim?" is precisely the comparison of the program's measured coverage against the careers' captured demand — surfaced as a sufficiency read per target. Because every level is the same KUD+ evidence-scored data zoomed in or out, a course-level audit, a program-level gap, and a career-readiness claim are one chain of evidence — defensible end to end, never a marketing assertion. (The sufficiency computation is built and flag-gated, awaiting activation and the first employer captures; everything it draws on is live.)

§The 3-Act program (proposed)

The framework proposes a three-act organizing structure for the GC program: students learn the field, integrate the field, then apply a focus. The tool would read each course in light of where it sits in this proposed progression: what foundations it depends on, what role it plays in integration, what specialty it serves. Like every other commitment in this framework, the 3-Act structure is a proposal — a working organizing model offered for faculty deliberation, not an existing institutional policy.

The design intent. The structural target of Act 2 would be the empirical "aha" moment students consistently report from GC 4400 and GC 4060 — the mini-capstones where "it all came together" and they first recognized where they could add professional value. The proposal positions this integrative threshold at mid-Junior year so the back half of the program — 1.5+ years — would be available for career-path-specific Act 3 specialization. This would invert the cornerstone-capstone pattern common in engineering education (where integration is reserved for the senior capstone, leaving no time for post-integration specialization). The framework's bet is that students develop better career-path-specific capability when they reach integration earlier and have substantial time afterward to apply it in a focused way — and that the curriculum analysis is most useful when it diagnoses scaffolding against the act-boundary the program is actually trying to hit.

Grounding. The proposed 3-Act structure is consistent with established curriculum-design traditions (Bruner's spiral, the engineering cornerstone-capstone model, the modern medical-education three-phase structure, cognitive apprenticeship) but is not derived from any single one. The scaffolding analysis treats act boundaries as analytical waypoints; if the structure is refined or replaced through faculty deliberation, the scaffolding-diagnosis logic is revised accordingly. The full research synthesis — what the structure is consistent with, what it isn't grounded in, how it engages the strongest critiques (Lave & Wenger on situated learning, Schön on artificial boundaries, prerequisite-transfer findings), and an honest accounting of which design choices are research-supported vs. stipulated — is in the companion document The 3-Act Program Structure: A Research Synthesis.

§120 credits, one map

The GC major — 16 required GC courses plus electives — is roughly half the degree. The other half has always been analyzed separately, treated as university requirements the program works around. But those courses are inside the curriculum, not outside it. When you map the full 120 credits back to the five career targets, the curriculum's contribution to career preparation becomes analyzable in ways the current approach cannot support.

An illustrative example Illustrative

PSYC 2010 introduces consumer psychology before the marketing sequence — one of the most direct foundations for Brand Strategy in the entire degree, sitting in a slot labeled "Social Science requirement." ACCT 2010 and 2020 build the financial fluency that Account Management requires at Do level, listed as business prerequisites. MKT 3010 may be the single highest-scoring non-GC course for Brand Strategy, yet it appears in the plan as a supporting requirement rather than a curriculum asset. PKSC 1020 scaffolds the production literacy that GC 4480 expects — quietly, in a two-credit slot most students treat as a checkbox.

These are faculty hypotheses about what the analysis will surface once captures are confirmed — not findings from actual snapshots (zero confirmed snapshots exist at this writing). The tool makes that work visible once courses are captured — and when a non-GC course isn't contributing, that's visible too.

The three things this surfaces

1. Which non-GC required courses are career-prep assets — and which are just boxes. Some required non-GC courses score strongly against specific career targets; others score near zero for all five. Knowing which is which changes how advisors talk about them and gives the department evidence-based grounds to advocate for specific course choices when the university gives flexibility.

2. Which constrained-choice slots are sub-optimal for the program's goals. The Business Communication requirement illustrates this. Depending on catalog year, the slot may include several approved options whose framings differ substantially — courses oriented toward client correspondence, technical documentation, or presentation design develop different audience-and-purpose judgment. Which option best serves a Brand-Strategy track is not the same as which best serves a Production or Sales-Engineering track. The tool should import the official catalog-year option set rather than hard-code comparisons: which option best serves each career target will depend on actual course content captures, not assumed depth levels. The same logic applies to the Econ choice, the Statistics choice, and every other constrained slot in the plan. Illustrative

3. Where the Specialty Area's 15 credits can close gaps that nothing else can. The most flexible curriculum space in the degree lines up exactly with the coverage gaps the analysis surfaces. For a student aiming at Brand Strategy, the right Specialty Area choices close the consumer research gap that no required course addresses at Do level. For AI Workflow, they add the systems-thinking component that the GC major alone doesn't reach. The tool makes these recommendations specific — not "consider marketing electives" but a ranked comparison of captured options against each career target's sub-competency gaps, with the reasoning visible and disputable. Illustrative

Why this matters for curriculum design

Changing a required course is a multi-year committee process. Changing which option within a constrained-choice slot the department recommends can happen through advising. Redirecting the Specialty Area approved list can happen at the department level. The coverage analysis provides the case for both — with AI reasoning faculty can read, dispute, and refine. The coverage analysis identifies which of those decisions — constrained-choice selections, Specialty Area guidance — have the most measurable impact on career-target preparation, without requiring a full Curriculum Committee proposal.

This is where the industry partner data closes the loop. When an employer submits a Brand Strategy position and says "I need someone who can design and execute consumer research," that signal flows into the coverage analysis. The tool can then say: "among the options approved for the statistics slot in this catalog year, option X scores higher for Brand Strategy — and here's the employer language that explains why." Faculty aren't choosing between statistics courses in the abstract. They're choosing between options with real hiring signal attached. End-state concept

§Why Know / Understand / Do — with depth

The tool classifies every competency at three levels. Know is facts and discrete information — terms, conventions, the names of things. Understand is the big ideas, principles, and transferable concepts that hold the facts together and explain why they matter. Do is skill, process, and applied performance — what a student can actually execute.

This is the KUD framework, drawn from Carol Ann Tomlinson's work on differentiated instruction (Tomlinson, 1999/2014; Tomlinson & Imbeau, 2010) and compatible with Wiggins and McTighe's Understanding by Design (2005), which centers curriculum planning on durable understanding and transfer rather than coverage.

Each of K, U, and D is scored on a 0–5 depth scale. Binary coverage ("covered / not covered") collapses in practice — most courses touch most relevant competencies at some level, and the matrix loses its ability to discriminate between a brief mention and an assessed performance. The depth scales restore that resolution. The anchors at each level describe what the student does or grasps rather than what the syllabus claims. All nonzero scores require evidence of delivery or assessment. K=1 can be supported by delivery evidence such as slides, readings, modules, or instructor confirmation; K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of student engagement, reasoning, or performance.

KUD+ scores are course-opportunity scores — what the course is designed and evidenced to develop. They become student-learning claims only when linked to assessed student work; the graduate-outcome-validation work is what bridges that gap. The full scale definitions, the evidence rule, and the theoretical grounding behind the extension are documented in the Background document.

Why not Bloom's Taxonomy? Bloom's six-level cognitive taxonomy (Bloom, 1956; Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) is the right tool for analyzing the cognitive demand of an assessment task — it discriminates finely between types of thinking and is well-suited to writing and evaluating assessments. That is a different job than the one this tool has to do. The GC Curriculum Tool needs a small set of faculty-readable categories that can drive coverage analysis, scaffolding analysis, prerequisite checks, and advising decisions across an entire program. For that purpose, three categories with internal depth outperforms six categories without: the question "does this prior course develop the Understand-level concept the focal course expects, and at what depth?" is clean and answerable. The finer distinctions Bloom provides — Analyze vs. Evaluate, Apply vs. Create — compound across hundreds of (course × competency × dimension) cells into judgment calls that make faculty review harder, not easier. KUD with depth is better suited to curriculum mapping; Bloom remains the right lens for assessment design and continues to inform the audit-conversation probes used inside each cell.

The framework fits a professional field like Graphic Communications naturally. Knowing color theory, understanding why it drives brand perception, being able to color-grade a project to brand spec are three real, separable things — and exactly the kind of distinction the program needs to track across courses and career destinations. The depth dimension then captures the difference between a course that briefly mentions color theory in a lecture and a course that assesses students on color-managed production files against a rubric.

Stepping back, the shape this produces is a familiar one: a graph of competencies in which prerequisites gate what each course can expect, and every node carries a depth level. That is, in effect, an evidence-gated skill tree — the same prerequisite-and-depth structure that adaptive-learning systems formalize as a knowledge space (the object behind tools like ALEKS), except every edge here must be earned by evidence, not authored. The framing, and the three places the metaphor misleads, are in the Background document.

Further reading: the KUD+ Background document is the academic companion to this vision; ASCD's Understanding by Design white paper — files.ascd.org … UbD_WhitePaper0312.pdf — remains the standard reference for the backward-design tradition the framework builds on.

§The end state

When the full tool is in production, it is the department's shared, versioned working view of the GC program — a maintained evidence surface for curriculum discussion. These are the capabilities it provides:

A career-target framework
A faculty-defined set of named career destinations, each with sub-competencies described at Know / Understand / Do levels. Editable by track leaders; changes propagate through the rest of the system.
A complete course inventory
Every active GC course has a structured record: description, learning objectives, projects, assumed prior skills. Editable by the course owner and synced from each semester's syllabus.
A coverage matrix Live (faculty trial)
For every course × every career target, the system generates an AI-drafted, evidence-cited coverage judgment from confirmed snapshots; faculty can review, dispute, and override the judgment.
Scaffolding analysis Live (Stage 1)
The scaffolding analysis uses confirmed course snapshots to generate scaffold judgments for faculty review — introduced in earlier courses, developed in middle courses, applied in capstones — and flags cases that look fine cell-by-cell but fail program-wide. Deterministic Stage 1 is live at /program/scaffolding; the AI-narrative Stage 2 is next.
Prerequisite-gap analysis Live
For any course, the tool reports whether the prior coursework students actually take supports what this course expects. Not what the registrar says is required — what the competencies require. Shipped as skill-tagged prerequisite edges with a per-course gap view at /courses/[code].
A flag & dispute trail Live
Every AI judgment on the program matrix and every competency in a review profile can be flagged with a named faculty note. Flags persist across re-scores — if a flagged score changes, the change is shown alongside the flag, never applied silently — and stay open until someone explicitly resolves them with a recorded name, note, and date.
Advising views Specified, not built
For a student who wants to graduate ready for Brand Strategy roles (or any other target), a recommended sequence and the gaps to watch — backed by the same AI judgments faculty have reviewed.
Accreditation & advisory views
Industry partners see what the program actually delivers. Accreditors see defensible competency mappings. The program sees what industry says is missing, made persistent.
A program constraint model Planned
Every slot in the BS in GC degree plan — fixed required courses, constrained-choice sets, and elective pools — imported from the official catalog. The constraint layer that keeps all curriculum design work grounded in the actual rules of the degree.
A constraint-aware slot optimizer Planned
For every open or constrained slot in the degree plan, an AI-rated comparison of available options: which choice best serves each career target, which maximizes coverage across multiple targets, and where the current selection is leaving opportunity on the table.
A student career path advisor Planned
For any declared career target, a recommended path through the full curriculum — which constrained choices to make, which electives matter most, which minor complements the target. Grounded in actual industry partner signal: what employers in this space told us they look for.

§What it enables

  • Faculty conversations grounded in evidence. When two faculty disagree about whether a course teaches workflow design at Do level, they have an AI-drafted reading to argue against — and a way to make their disagreement persistent for the next reviewer.
  • Curriculum changes with feedback before they ship. A faculty member redesigning a course can see how their proposed syllabus would change coverage and scaffolding before the redesign goes through Curriculum Committee.
  • Honest career-readiness claims. "Graduates of this program are ready for X" becomes a verifiable statement, not a marketing assertion.
  • A starting point for accreditation and advisory-board conversations. Industry partners can see what the program actually delivers; the program can see what industry says is missing, made persistent.
  • Defense against drift. Curricula erode over decades. Each captured snapshot is a dated record of what a course developed at a moment in time, and the snapshot system is designed so that cross-snapshot comparison surfaces erosion at the pace of re-captures rather than at the pace of every seven-year program review. The cross-snapshot diff that operationalizes this comparison is Phase 2 work; what the system can do today is hold the snapshots that will, over time, accumulate into the comparison.
  • Student self-advising with honest tradeoffs. A student who says "I want to work in AI Workflow" gets a specific recommended path through constrained choices and electives — not a generic advising sheet, but a recommendation backed by the same AI analysis faculty have reviewed. When a new industry partner submits an AI Workflow position, the recommendation sharpens.
  • A living, navigable record of the curriculum. Confirmed snapshots don't just sit in a database — they are compiled into a cross-linked knowledge base: course, competency, career-target, and concept pages that faculty, the curriculum committee, or a newly-hired instructor can browse or simply ask plain-language questions of, with every claim citing back to the snapshot evidence behind it. This is the program's institutional memory made legible — the "living record" a department otherwise keeps only in scattered syllabi and the heads of the people who happen to still be around. It is the thing that makes the accumulation pay off: as more courses are captured, the record gets richer and more answerable, not just larger.

§Where the data comes from

Two data streams power every analysis the tool runs. They flow in at different layers — and the coverage analysis is the join between them.

Industry Partner Advisory Survey

Structured, periodic input from employer partners: competency importance ratings per career target, emerging skill flags, and observations about what they see — and don't see — in recent hires. The survey is the source of truth for whether the curriculum's coverage matters. Without it, career target definitions are standing hypotheses. With it, they are empirical — and updatable as the field moves.

The survey runs continuously, not as a one-time advisory board event. When a new employer partner submits, target definitions can update; coverage scores for the affected targets rescore automatically. The partner-facing interface is now live as Position Capture (see What's live now) — a structured, role-at-a-time flow that routes employer signal directly into the career-target framework, keyed to sub-competencies, rather than sitting in a spreadsheet.

Course content ingestion

Three sub-sources at each course, layered by reliability:

  • Canvas + linked materials (primary) — assignments, rubrics, pages, discussions, quizzes, file attachments, linked Google Docs/Slides/Sheets, Drive PDFs, YouTube captions. The full current ingestion surface — what is supported, what is suppressed, and what is not yet supported — is documented in the Background document.
  • Direct uploads + catalog values (supplementary) — uploaded PDF/DOCX/PPTX and other file formats; learning objectives, skills, and projects from the shared catalog sheet (these suppress Canvas Syllabus body when present).
  • Faculty annotation (tertiary) — structured input where faculty confirm, extend, or correct the AI-generated profile; disputes and corrections persist as part of the permanent record

All three sub-sources flow into the Course Profile — a single, timestamped, AI-drafted and faculty-reviewed record of what a course actually teaches at Know, Understand, and Do level.

The join

The industry survey tells the tool what matters. Course content ingestion tells it what learning opportunities and assessed demands the curriculum documents. The coverage analysis is the join between them — and the gap between the two streams is the design opportunity. Faculty aren't arguing about curriculum in the abstract; they're arguing about a specific delta between what employers say they need and what the courses demonstrably teach.

§What's live now

(The original M-trial prototype was retired on 2026-06-02; everything it did is now covered by CourseCapture, Explore, and Program.)

Uncaptured prerequisites show as no-data, never an assumed gap.

It fills in as captures accumulate. This is v1; the self-reconciling compile loop (concept promotion, broken-link/staleness lint, a scheduled reconcile) is Phase 2.
  • /partners/<token> — Position Capture (the demand side). Live The employer-facing counterpart to CourseCapture: a magic-linked, multi-page flow where an industry partner captures one role at a time — job-description ingest, an AI-led interview, and AI-rated "experiences worth having" — keyed to career-target sub-competencies. What the trial is for

The point is unchanged: confirm the analysis is good enough to be useful. If faculty find the readings credible, defensible, and worth disputing on the merits, the remaining Phase 1 view — the advising view — becomes the next implementation work (scaffolding and prerequisite-gap analysis have since shipped). If they don't, those views would be misleading at scale and the analysis layer needs more work first.

§Operational realities

A research tool that runs on real institutional data sits inside an institutional context, and the Vision should be honest about the parts of that context that determine whether the tool will actually be useful.

Career-target governance

The five named destinations — Account Management, Brand Strategy, Production & Operations, Creative Generalist / AI-Native, AI Workflow / Orchestrator — were established through faculty judgment informed by advisory-board conversations and a scan of typical entry-level job postings in the field. They are a working set, not the result of a formal labor-market study; the framework treats them as research input rather than ground truth. Authority to revise the set sits with the faculty leading each track plus the program coordinator; advisory-board feedback over time is expected to refine the descriptors and possibly add or retire targets. When a sub-competency descriptor changes, the existing coverage scores against that target become stale — the system marks them for re-scoring rather than silently invalidating them. Cross-target stability is not guaranteed; it is a working hypothesis that we'll know more about after the first year of use.

Faculty engagement model

The coverage matrix is only as complete as the set of confirmed snapshots. CourseCapture sessions are voluntary in the trial period, with department-level coordination by the program coordinator and the curriculum committee chair. The trial is being run with a cohort of faculty who have agreed to capture their courses; if participation across the full 28-course program remains incomplete, the matrix renders the missing courses as unscored rather than imputing values — partial participation produces a partial matrix, not a misleadingly complete one. The plan for low participation is incremental: the tool's usefulness has to be demonstrable on the courses that are captured before broader adoption is requested.

Relationship to the curriculum committee

The curriculum committee retains all authority over curriculum changes. This tool is designed to inform that body's deliberations — by surfacing patterns, gaps, and conflicts that gut-intuition curriculum review tends to miss — not to replace its judgment or sidestep its process. A proposed curriculum change still goes through the committee; what the tool adds is the ability to bring evidence-grounded readings into the conversation and to model the proposed change's coverage impact before the committee votes.

When the AI is wrong

Faculty will sometimes disagree with the AI's reading of their own course. The framework's commitment is that disagreements persist (as flags) rather than vanishing into a button click — and that flagged cells are not silently overwritten in future re-scores. The flag mechanism is live: ⚑ buttons on program-matrix cells and review-panel competencies file a named dispute note; flagged scores are shown alongside their current value if the score changes; flags stay open until someone resolves them with a recorded name, note, and date. A program-wide "⚑ N open" panel surfaces all open flags with drift display. How systematic patterns of open flags inform future prompt or rubric changes is Phase 2 work.

Snapshot governance

Each captured snapshot is owned by the faculty member who confirmed it, visible to the program coordinator and the curriculum committee, and retained as part of the program's longitudinal record. A faculty member who wants a snapshot retired can request retirement; retired snapshots are soft-deleted (removed from downstream views) but not purged, so the historical trail remains. Department leadership and individual faculty share ownership of the data; this is a working policy the framework expects to refine.

Cost

AI-model cost is a manageable variable as adoption grows. The current trial runs against the OpenAI API on credits; if usage scales beyond what credits absorb, the architecture supports a swap to Clemson RCD's campus-hosted models or a local runtime without changes to the application layer. The tool optimizes for prompt-cache reuse and exposes function-tier settings (/settings) so the program can tune cost-per-call against analytical depth on a per-function basis.

Failure modes

The most consequential failure mode is systematic over-scoring (the coverage matrix shows healthier coverage than the program actually delivers, and the program adopts the misleading reading for accreditation or advising purposes). The framework's mitigations are the evidence rule (all nonzero scores require evidence of delivery or assessment; K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of student engagement or performance), the dispute trail (faculty can flag and contest cells), and the deliberately conservative scoring discipline built into the AI prompts. Even with those, the tool is not infallible; a recurring audit of the most consequential cells against actual student work and graduate-outcome data is the long-term validation path described in the Validation proposal.

§What success looks like

The tool generates data. The data only matters if it connects to specific, verifiable claims about what the program delivers — and if those claims are falsifiable. Here is what success means at each level, and what the gate data either confirms or refutes.

For a Student

By graduation they demonstrate Do-level competency in their declared career target — assessed by a rubric-scored capstone with at least one industry reviewer, not a multiple-choice test. Their three-point growth curve across the three gates shows the program moved them, not just credentialed them.

For the Department

Each cohort shows measurable gate-to-gate growth. Curriculum gaps identified by gate data become Proposals within the same academic year — not at the next program review. Placement data confirms that declared tracks map to real careers; when they don't, advising changes before the next cohort graduates.

For the Field

Industry partners can see their input reflected in the actual course profiles and coverage scores, not only in periodic accreditation reporting. Graduate placement data corresponds to the career outcomes the program claims to prepare students for, and the advisory survey and placement record can be compared directly.

At each gate Planned

Gates are curriculum audits, not student evaluations. The question at each gate is not "which students passed?" but "did the curriculum deliver what it promised?"

Proposed pilot metric
The numbers below are proposed pilot starting thresholds for faculty review, not validated standards. They should be recalibrated against the first cohort's baseline distributions.

End of Act 1 · Gate 1 · Diagnostic
Did Act One build the foundation?
≥70% of the cohort reaches Know level in each common foundation sub-competency. No student is dismissed — gaps produce advising records, not academic consequences. Red flag: any sub-competency below 60% cohort-wide means a 1000-level course is reviewed before year-end, not at the next program review.
End of Act 2 · Gate 2 · Consequential
Did Act Two develop the execution layer?
≥15 percentile point growth from Gate 1 on the common foundation. ≥75% of students declaring each track reach Understand level in at least 3 of 5 track sub-competencies. Gate 2 is consequential — students who don't clear it enter their specialization track with a documented advising plan, not a free pass. Red flag: <10 point growth on any sub-competency means the Act 1–2 sequence failed to develop it, and a Proposal is triggered, not just a review.
End of Act 3 · Gate 3 · Summative
Did Act Three build Do-level competency?
≥65% of each track's cohort reaches Do level on the capstone assessment. ≥80% inter-rater agreement between faculty and industry reviewers on the same capstone. The full three-point growth curve (Gate 1 → Gate 2 → Gate 3) is documented for every graduating cohort — the kind of longitudinal learning-evidence metric accreditation reviewers often value, when it is available. Red flag: below 50% Do-level in any track means that track's curriculum is failing its primary mission; a Proposal is initiated immediately.
Alignment with accreditation requirements

SACSCOC Quality Enhancement Plans and ACEJMC outcomes assessment requirements ask for the same things: evidence of student learning at program exit, a growth curve showing the program produced that growth, and documented evidence that data changed the curriculum. The gate framework produces all three as a byproduct of running the program — internal accountability and external reporting draw from the same underlying evidence.

§Research & Validation

The KUD methodology generates structured, comparable course outcomes — but how do we know the methodology is capturing the right things? A dataset of 268 Clemson GC graduate employment records (2018–2026), spanning 43 occupational categories across 58 industries, provides an independent empirical baseline. A proposed validation module would compare AI-generated course KUDs against O*NET competency profiles for the occupations graduates actually enter — testing whether the methodology produces outcome descriptions that correspond to documented career destinations.

First-Job Alignment
For each SOC occupation in the graduate employment dataset, pull the O*NET competency profile (Knowledge → Know, Skills → Understand, Work Activities → Do). Compare those profiles against the baseline KUDs generated from current course materials. Score alignment per course × career destination. Surface gaps and coverage maps at the program level.
Career Trajectory Alignment
Using longitudinal LinkedIn career history, trace what graduates do in their first role versus where they end up 5–10 years later. Identify which curriculum KUDs appear in advanced roles but not entry-level requirements — the long-game competencies the program builds that don't show up until later in a career.
The graduate employment dataset

An initial dataset of 268 Clemson GC graduates (2018–2026) spans 43 occupational categories across 58 industries. The current 6-code CIP 10.03 SOC crosswalk accounts for 3.4% of those records. A proposed 21-code crosswalk grounded in the actual distribution accounts for 88.4% — and serves as the reference set for the validation analysis.

What validation tells us — and what it doesn't

Strong alignment between course KUDs and the competency requirements of the occupations graduates enter would provide evidence that the methodology produces outcome descriptions corresponding to real occupational competencies. It would not establish that the curriculum caused those career outcomes — correlation and causation are distinct claims — but it would establish that the curriculum's stated competencies correspond to the destinations graduates reach. That correspondence is relevant to accreditation documentation, program review, and curriculum planning discussions.

§What it is not

  • Not a replacement for faculty judgment. The tool drafts; faculty decide. Every AI reading is disputable, and disputes persist as part of the record.
  • Not a grading or evaluation tool for individual students or instructors. Coverage scores describe what the course delivers — not how well any one student learned it or how any one instructor taught it.
  • Not an automated curriculum redesigner. It surfaces what is, makes scaffolding visible, and supports faculty conversations. The redesigning is still humans-in-a-room work.
  • Not a substitute for the Curriculum Committee process. It gives the committee a richer working surface; the committee still decides.
  • Not an external marketing claim. The public-facing view in Phase 3 will report what the program actually delivers — backed by the same analysis faculty have already reviewed and flagged.
