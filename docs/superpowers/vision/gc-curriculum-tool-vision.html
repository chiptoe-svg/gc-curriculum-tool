The GC Curriculum Tool

Working backwards from career destinations and upwards from foundational courses — a living view of where the two meet, where the scaffolding holds, and where the curriculum has drifted.

§What we're building

Today, the questions of "how well does this curriculum prepare students for the careers we claim?" and "do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what this course expects?" get answered through committee discussions, gut intuition, and the occasional seven-year program review. Faculty know parts of the answer for the courses they teach. Nobody has the whole picture, and nobody has a way to update it as courses evolve.

The tool makes those answers visible and defensible — for advising students, for accreditation conversations, for industry advisory-board reviews, for the next curriculum redesign, and for hiring decisions about what kind of faculty the program needs.

§The core questions

Q1 · Program → Careers
How well does the GC curriculum, as a whole, prepare students for the career destinations we claim to lead them toward?
Q2 · Prereqs → Course
For any individual course, do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what the course expects?
Q3 · Constraints → Best Choice
Within the fixed rules of our degree plan, which constrained-choice options most directly build toward each career destination — and where are our open slots a wasted opportunity?
Q4 · Student → Target
For a student declaring a career target, which path through required courses, constrained choices, and elective pools gives them the strongest preparation — and what do employers in that role actually look for?

Both questions get answered against the same competency framework — career destinations defined as sub-competencies at Know / Understand / Do levels. The two answers feed each other: program coverage rolls up from course-level judgments; course-level prerequisite gaps surface where the program isn't actually scaffolding what its own upper-division courses expect. Questions Three and Four introduce the constraint dimension: not every building block is freely chosen. The degree plan's fixed requirements, constrained-choice slots, and elective pools define the real design space — and within that space, some choices prepare students for their declared targets far better than others.

§What we're producing

Career targets are where a graduate is heading; the foundational competencies are what they are when they get there.

The foundational competencies — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication — should show up across every course in the program, regardless of act or specialty. They're program-wide threads, not something any single course delivers in isolation.

The five career targets — Account Management, Brand Strategy, Production & Ops, Creative Generalist, and AI Workflow — are the domain-specific destinations where deep expertise builds across multiple courses. These are what the coverage matrix and scaffolding analysis read at the course level. The pyramid and the target framework are the same thing: every coverage score, every scaffolding judgment, and every slot-optimizer rating maps directly to one of these five bands.

The tool maps both layers. The clusters get explicit coverage analysis; the foundational competencies show up implicitly inside every reasoned judgment (a course developing "Quality Control" at Do level is also building Attention to Detail by definition). Over time the tool can surface which courses are doing the most for each foundational competency — and which aren't pulling their weight.

§The 3-Act program

Underneath the career-target framework sits the program's own mental model: GC unfolds as a three-act progression — students learn the field, integrate the field, then apply a focus. The tool reads each course in light of where it sits in this progression: what foundations it depends on, what role it plays in integration, what specialty it serves.

§120 credits, one map

The GC major — 16 required GC courses plus electives — is roughly half the degree. The other half has always been analyzed separately, treated as university requirements the program works around. But those courses are inside the curriculum, not outside it. When you map the full 120 credits back to the five career targets, things come into view that no one has seen before.

The insight

PSYC 2010 introduces consumer psychology before the marketing sequence — one of the most direct foundations for Brand Strategy in the entire degree, sitting in a slot labeled "Social Science requirement." ACCT 2010 and 2020 build the financial fluency that Account Management requires at Do level, listed as business prerequisites. MKT 3010 may be the single highest-scoring non-GC course for Brand Strategy, yet it appears in the plan as a supporting requirement rather than a curriculum asset. PKSC 1020 scaffolds the production literacy that GC 4480 expects — quietly, in a two-credit slot most students treat as a checkbox.

These courses are doing curriculum work. The tool makes that work visible — and when a non-GC course isn't contributing, that's visible too.

The three things this surfaces

1. Which non-GC required courses are career-prep assets — and which are just boxes. Some required non-GC courses score strongly against specific career targets; others score near zero for all five. Knowing which is which changes how advisors talk about them and gives the department evidence-based grounds to advocate for specific course choices when the university gives flexibility.

2. Which constrained-choice slots are sub-optimal for the program's goals. The STAT requirement can be satisfied by STAT 2220 (Elementary Statistics) or STAT 3090 (Statistical Methods). Only STAT 3090 develops the quantitative analytical capability that Brand Strategy employers say they look for — and that AI Workflow requires at the Understand level. The current choice may be making two career targets harder to serve without anyone having made a conscious decision about it. The same logic applies to the Econ choice, the Business Communication choice, and every other constrained slot in the plan.

3. Where the Specialty Area's 15 credits can close gaps that nothing else can. The most flexible curriculum space in the degree lines up exactly with the coverage gaps the analysis surfaces. For a student aiming at Brand Strategy, the right Specialty Area choices close the consumer research gap that no required course addresses at Do level. For AI Workflow, they add the systems-thinking component that the GC major alone doesn't reach. The tool makes these recommendations specific: not "consider marketing electives" but "MKT 4250 scores highest for Brand Strategy because it builds the research design sub-competency at Understand level — the gap the rest of the degree leaves open."

Why this matters for curriculum design

Changing a required course is a multi-year committee process. Changing which option within a constrained-choice slot the department recommends can happen through advising. Redirecting the Specialty Area approved list can happen at the department level. The coverage analysis provides the case for both — with AI reasoning faculty can read, dispute, and refine. The highest-leverage curriculum decisions in the degree are ones that don't require a full Curriculum Committee proposal. The tool finds them.

This is where the industry partner data closes the loop. When an employer submits a Brand Strategy position and says "I need someone who can design and execute consumer research," that signal flows into the coverage analysis. The tool can then say: "STAT 3090 serves this better than STAT 2220 — and here's the employer language that explains why." Faculty aren't choosing between statistics courses in the abstract. They're choosing between options with real hiring signal attached.

§Why Know / Understand / Do

The tool classifies every competency at three levels. Know is facts and discrete information — terms, conventions, the names of things. Understand is the big ideas, principles, and transferable concepts that hold the facts together and explain why they matter. Do is skill, process, and applied performance — what a student can actually execute.

This is the KUD framework, drawn from Carol Ann Tomlinson's work on differentiated instruction (Tomlinson, 1999/2014; Tomlinson & Imbeau, 2010) and compatible with Wiggins and McTighe's Understanding by Design (2005), which centers curriculum planning on durable understanding and transfer rather than coverage.

Why not Bloom's Taxonomy? Bloom's six-level cognitive taxonomy (Bloom, 1956; Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) is the right tool for analyzing cognitive demand — it discriminates finely between types of thinking and is well-suited to writing and evaluating assessment tasks. That is a different job than the one this tool has to do. The GC Curriculum Tool needs a small set of faculty-readable categories that can drive coverage analysis, scaffolding analysis, prerequisite checks, and advising decisions across an entire program. For that purpose, three levels outperforms six: the question "Does this prior course cover the Understand-level concept the focal course expects?" is clean and answerable. The finer distinctions Bloom provides — Analyze vs. Evaluate, Apply vs. Create — would introduce judgment calls that compound across hundreds of course × competency cells and make faculty review harder, not easier. KUD is better suited to curriculum mapping and program design; Bloom remains the right lens for assessment design.

The framework fits a professional field like Graphic Communications naturally. Knowing color theory, understanding why it drives brand perception, being able to color-grade a project to brand spec are three real, separable things — and exactly the kind of distinction the program needs to track across courses and career destinations.

§The end state

When the full tool is in production, it is the canonical living view of the GC program. These are the capabilities it provides:

A career-target framework
A faculty-defined set of named career destinations, each with sub-competencies described at Know / Understand / Do levels. Editable by track leaders; changes propagate through the rest of the system.
A complete course inventory
Every active GC course has a structured record: description, learning objectives, projects, assumed prior skills. Editable by the course owner and synced from each semester's syllabus.
A coverage matrix
For every course × every career target, an AI-maintained judgment of how well the course covers each sub-competency, with confidence and reasoning faculty can read, dispute, and flag.
Scaffolding analysis
Across the program, the AI judges how well each competency is scaffolded — introduced in earlier courses, developed in middle courses, applied in capstones. Flags cases that look fine cell-by-cell but fail program-wide.
Prerequisite-gap analysis
For any course, the tool reports whether the prior coursework students actually take supports what this course expects. Not what the registrar says is required — what the competencies require.
A flag & dispute trail
Every AI judgment can be flagged with a faculty note. Flags get reviewed; prompts and rubrics improve over time; the curriculum's understanding of itself sharpens.
Advising views
For a student who wants to graduate ready for Brand Strategy roles (or any other target), a recommended sequence and the gaps to watch — backed by the same AI judgments faculty have reviewed.
Accreditation & advisory views
Industry partners see what the program actually delivers. Accreditors see defensible competency mappings. The program sees what industry says is missing, made persistent.
A program constraint model
Every slot in the BS in GC degree plan — fixed required courses, constrained-choice sets, and elective pools — imported from the official catalog. The constraint layer that keeps all curriculum design work grounded in the actual rules of the degree.
A constraint-aware slot optimizer
For every open or constrained slot in the degree plan, an AI-rated comparison of available options: which choice best serves each career target, which maximizes coverage across multiple targets, and where the current selection is leaving opportunity on the table.
A student career path advisor
For any declared career target, a recommended path through the full curriculum — which constrained choices to make, which electives matter most, which minor complements the target. Grounded in actual industry partner signal: what employers in this space told us they look for.

The key insight

None of these constrained-choice decisions require a Curriculum Committee proposal to change. The department can recommend STAT 3090 instead of STAT 2220 through advising and degree-plan guidance. These aren't abstract curriculum questions — they're specific, actionable decisions the coverage analysis can rank and support with employer evidence from the Advisory Survey.

§What it enables

  • Faculty conversations grounded in evidence. When two faculty disagree about whether a course teaches workflow design at Do level, they have an AI-drafted reading to argue against — and a way to make their disagreement persistent for the next reviewer.
  • Curriculum changes with feedback before they ship. A faculty member redesigning a course can see how their proposed syllabus would change coverage and scaffolding before the redesign goes through Curriculum Committee.
  • Honest career-readiness claims. "Graduates of this program are ready for X" becomes a verifiable statement, not a marketing assertion.
  • A starting point for accreditation and advisory-board conversations. Industry partners can see what the program actually delivers; the program can see what industry says is missing, made persistent.
  • Defense against drift. Curricula erode over decades. The tool surfaces erosion as it happens, instead of every seven-year program review.
  • Student self-advising with honest tradeoffs. A student who says "I want to work in AI Workflow" gets a specific recommended path through constrained choices and electives — not a generic advising sheet, but a recommendation backed by the same AI analysis faculty have reviewed. When a new industry partner submits an AI Workflow position, the recommendation sharpens.

§Where the data comes from

Two data streams power every analysis the tool runs. They flow in at different layers — and the coverage analysis is the join between them.

Industry Partner Advisory Survey

Structured, periodic input from employer partners: competency importance ratings per career target, emerging skill flags, and observations about what they see — and don't see — in recent hires. The survey is the source of truth for whether the curriculum's coverage matters. Without it, career target definitions are standing hypotheses. With it, they are empirical — and updatable as the field moves.

The survey runs continuously, not as a one-time advisory board event. When a new employer partner submits, target definitions can update; coverage scores for the affected targets rescore automatically. The partner-facing interface is already live in a lightweight form — the Advisory Survey is the formalized structure that routes employer signal directly into the career target framework rather than sitting in a spreadsheet.

Course content ingestion

Three sub-sources at each course, layered by reliability:

  • Syllabi (primary) — uploaded PDF → AI extraction of objectives, assessment structure, and skills claimed at course level
  • Coursework (secondary) — assignment sheets, rubrics, project briefs that reveal actual practice-level demands, not just stated objectives
  • Faculty annotation (tertiary) — structured input where faculty confirm, extend, or correct the AI-generated profile; disputes and corrections persist as part of the permanent record

All three sub-sources flow into the Course Profile — a single, timestamped, AI-drafted and faculty-reviewed record of what a course actually teaches at Know, Understand, and Do level.

The join

The industry survey tells the tool what matters. Course content ingestion tells it what the curriculum delivers. The coverage analysis is the join between them — and the gap between the two streams is the design opportunity. Faculty aren't arguing about curriculum in the abstract; they're arguing about a specific delta between what employers say they need and what the courses demonstrably teach.

§The prototype & trial

The version available now is the M-trial: a faculty-facing slice that demonstrates the analysis quality on real GC courses against the career targets. It does not yet have the program-wide views above, the official course record store, rescore-on-edit, or accreditation reporting.

What it has:

  • Pick a course from the GC course list, add 1–8 prior courses, pick a career target.
  • The tool drafts Know / Understand / Do outcomes for each course, scores coverage against the target's sub-competencies, judges scaffolding quality, and identifies prerequisite gaps.
  • Every judgment is expandable — click Why? to read the reasoning. Flag any judgment that looks wrong with a note. Flags persist for tuning.
Why this slice first

The point of the prototype is to confirm the analysis is good enough to be useful. If faculty find the readings credible, defensible, and worth disputing on the merits, the rest of the tool is mostly plumbing. If they don't, the program-wide views in the visualizations above would be misleading at scale.

Trial period

Test drive the prototype over roughly the next two weeks, focusing on the courses you know best. Pay attention to whether the AI's reasoning matches your lived experience of those courses, and whether the gaps it surfaces match the gaps you already worry about. After the trial settles, the next phase (the program-wide views shown earlier) starts based on what the feedback surfaces.

Where to leave feedback

Two channels — both visible to other faculty so feedback compounds instead of sitting in one person's inbox:

In-tool
Flag specific judgments
Every AI score and gap analysis has a "Flag this" button with a note field. Best for specific feedback — "this score is wrong because…" or "the reasoning missed X." Flags persist for review.
On the shared sheet
Open feedback tab
Open the Feedback tab on the shared sheet and add your name, the topic, and your thought. Everyone in the department can see it and react. Best for cross-cutting questions ("should we add a career target for X?") and anything worth other faculty seeing.

§How we get there

Each phase ships as something faculty can use before the next phase arrives. Sequencing reflects what's load-bearing for the next phase, not what's "biggest."

Now · M-trial
Faculty-facing analysis prototype
Demonstrates analysis quality on real GC courses + career targets. Includes the standardized course-record sheet (28 courses) synced into the tool, the editable career-target framework, and per-analysis flagging. Goal: confirm AI readings are credible and worth disputing on the merits.
Phase 1 · Coverage Matrix
Every course × every target, in production
The full coverage matrix kept current: every course × every career target, with the dispute/flag pipeline live, the program-wide heat-map view, and the per-course coverage view. Admin tooling for career-target evolution. Faculty can flag and discuss without an engineer in the loop.
Phase 2 · Scaffolding & Advising
Program-wide scaffolding and advising views
Scaffolding-quality view across courses. Advising view: "to be ready for X, take this sequence and watch these gaps." Rescore-on-edit so curriculum changes propagate. The constraint-aware slot optimizer: for every open slot in the degree plan, AI-rated options showing coverage impact per target. The first views useful for accreditation and advisory-board conversations.
Phase 3 · External-facing
Public, employer, and alumni views
Public/employer-facing views of what the program delivers. A public student career path advisor — connecting industry partner signal to actionable advising recommendations, usable as a recruiting tool and an external signal of program rigor. Alumni and industry feedback integration. The analytics needed to drive program-level decisions (faculty hiring, curriculum redesign, new tracks).

§What success looks like

The tool generates data. The data only matters if it connects to specific, verifiable claims about what the program delivers — and if those claims are falsifiable. Here is what success means at each level, and what the gate data either confirms or refutes.

For a Student

By graduation they demonstrate Do-level competency in their declared career target — assessed by a rubric-scored capstone with at least one industry reviewer, not a multiple-choice test. Their three-point growth curve across the three gates shows the program moved them, not just credentialed them.

For the Department

Each cohort shows measurable gate-to-gate growth. Curriculum gaps identified by gate data become Proposals within the same academic year — not at the next program review. Placement data confirms that declared tracks map to real careers; when they don't, advising changes before the next cohort graduates.

For the Field

Industry partners who describe what they look for can see their input reflected in the curriculum — not in a glossy accreditation report, but in the actual course profiles and coverage scores. Graduates land in roles that match what the program claimed to prepare them for. The advisory survey and placement data tell the same story.

At each gate

Gates are curriculum audits, not student evaluations. The question at each gate is not "which students passed?" but "did the curriculum deliver what it promised?"

End of Act 1 · Gate 1 · Diagnostic
Did Act One build the foundation?
≥70% of the cohort reaches Know level in each common foundation sub-competency. No student is dismissed — gaps produce advising records, not academic consequences. Red flag: any sub-competency below 60% cohort-wide means a 1000-level course is reviewed before year-end, not at the next program review.
End of Act 2 · Gate 2 · Consequential
Did Act Two develop the execution layer?
≥15 percentile point growth from Gate 1 on the common foundation. ≥75% of students declaring each track reach Understand level in at least 3 of 5 track sub-competencies. Gate 2 is consequential — students who don't clear it enter their specialization track with a documented advising plan, not a free pass. Red flag: <10 point growth on any sub-competency means the Act 1–2 sequence failed to develop it, and a Proposal is triggered, not just a review.
End of Act 3 · Gate 3 · Summative
Did Act Three build Do-level competency?
≥65% of each track's cohort reaches Do level on the capstone assessment. ≥80% inter-rater agreement between faculty and industry reviewers on the same capstone. The full three-point growth curve (Gate 1 → Gate 2 → Gate 3) is documented for every graduating cohort — the strongest evidence of program effectiveness available, and the metric accreditation reviewers most want to see. Red flag: below 50% Do-level in any track means that track's curriculum is failing its primary mission; a Proposal is initiated immediately.
Why this framework satisfies accreditation automatically

SACSCOC Quality Enhancement Plans and ACEJMC outcomes assessment requirements ask for the same things: evidence of student learning at program exit, a growth curve showing the program caused the growth, and documented evidence that data changed the curriculum. The gate framework produces all three as a byproduct of running the program. Build it right for internal accountability and the external review writes itself.

§What it is not

  • Not a replacement for faculty judgment. The tool drafts; faculty decide. Every AI reading is disputable, and disputes persist as part of the record.
  • Not a grading or evaluation tool for individual students or instructors. Coverage scores describe what the course delivers — not how well any one student learned it or how any one instructor taught it.
  • Not an automated curriculum redesigner. It surfaces what is, makes scaffolding visible, and supports faculty conversations. The redesigning is still humans-in-a-room work.
  • Not a substitute for the Curriculum Committee process. It gives the committee a richer working surface; the committee still decides.
  • Not an external marketing claim. The public-facing view in Phase 3 will report what the program actually delivers — backed by the same analysis faculty have already reviewed and flagged.
