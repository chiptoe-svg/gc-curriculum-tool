Section 1 — Background

Classification data and documented graduate outcomes

The CIP–SOC crosswalk for graphic communications programs (CIP 10.03) — a crosswalk being a mapping table that connects academic programs (CIP: Classification of Instructional Programs) to the occupations their graduates are expected to enter (SOC: Standard Occupational Classification) — was established in the late 1990s and has not been substantively updated. Career advising databases, state workforce systems, and federal reporting tools draw from this crosswalk. For CIP 10.03, it points primarily to prepress technicians and printing press operators: occupations in projected decline.

The employment dataset documented in this proposal tells a different story. Clemson GC graduates between 2018 and 2026 entered 43 distinct occupational categories across 58 industries, including design, marketing, technology, sales, and management roles at a wide range of employers. The distribution differs substantially from what the current crosswalk would predict.

The gap between the crosswalk and the employment record suggests that the program has been producing outcomes that its official classification does not describe. The data presented here is not aspirational — it represents verified employment records. The question this proposal addresses is whether those outcomes can be connected systematically to what the curriculum teaches, course by course.

The 268 graduate records in this dataset precede the curriculum analysis tool described in this proposal. They provide an independent empirical baseline against which AI-generated course learning outcomes can be evaluated — a comparison that is possible precisely because the two datasets were developed separately.

Any curriculum analysis tool that draws from standard federal career data for CIP 10.03 would be working from a reference set that accounts for approximately 3.4% of actual Clemson GC graduate outcomes. The 21-code proposed crosswalk — grounded in 603 graduate records across 9 programs — provides a more representative reference set for curriculum alignment work.

The primary purpose of presenting this coverage gap is not to advocate for federal reclassification, though the data does support that case. The purpose is to establish that the current classification framework does not describe what this program produces, and that a more accurate reference set exists and is being used as the basis for this proposal.

Section 4 — Research Motivation

Connecting curriculum outcomes to documented graduate destinations

What the program currently lacks is a systematic framework for describing, at the course level, how specific learning outcomes connect to the career destinations graduates enter. The employment distribution is documented; the curriculum exists; the connection between them has not been formally analyzed.

The KUD framework — Know, Understand, Do — provides structured learning outcome statements derived directly from course materials. The proposed validation module would map those statements to O*NET competency profiles for each of the 21 SOC codes that account for 88.4% of documented graduate placements. The central analytical question is whether the curriculum's stated outcomes correspond to the competencies those occupations require.

This analysis is grounded in outcomes the program has already produced, not outcomes it is working toward. The graduate data provides the empirical reference point; the curriculum analysis provides the explanatory framework. The goal is to understand the relationship between the two — and to identify where that relationship is strong, where it is weak, and where outcomes are being produced without explicit curricular claim.

Section 5 — The Proposed Validation Functions

Two proposed analytical functions

The proposed Graduate Outcome Validation module would link AI-generated course learning outcomes (KUDs) to graduate career destinations via O*NET — the federal occupational competency database — as an intermediary. The result would be a comparison of curriculum-stated outcomes against the competency requirements of the occupations graduates actually enter.

Does what we teach match where graduates start?

For each of the 21 SOC codes, the module would construct a KUD-structured career profile — not invented, but derived directly from the federal occupational database. Those profiles become the benchmark. Each course's AI-generated KUDs are then compared against the competency requirements of the career destinations graduates actually enter.

Illustrative outputs

Course-level finding "GC 3460's KUDs align strongly with Graphic Designer (27-1024) and Marketing Specialist (13-1161) requirements. They have weak coverage of Project Management Specialist (13-1082) requirements — which 3.7% of graduates become."
Program-level finding "Aggregating KUDs across all courses, the curriculum has strong coverage of the Design, Media & Technology cluster and partial coverage of the Management & Business cluster. The 11 graduates who became Chief Executives are drawing on entrepreneurship and business fundamentals that don't appear explicitly in any course's KUDs."

The program-level finding is particularly notable: outcomes the curriculum produces without explicitly claiming to. Identifying where graduates succeed in roles that don't appear in any course's stated KUDs is one of the more analytically useful things this comparison would surface.

The role of individual records

O*NET describes what "Graphic Designers" generically require. The 268 individual records provide context O*NET cannot: Clemson GC graphic designers work specifically at sports franchises, packaging companies, advertising agencies, and financial services firms. Competency requirements shift across those industry contexts — a graphic designer at a packaging manufacturer operates differently than one at an advertising agency. The individual records would allow the analysis to cluster by industry and examine whether those differences are reflected in the curriculum's stated outcomes.

2
Career Trajectory Alignment
Does what we teach enable where graduates go over time?

Point-in-time employment data shows where graduates are when observed. Career trajectory data shows the arc — where they start, where they are five years later, and what that progression reveals about what the program actually enables over time.

A graduate who enters as a Graphic Designer and advances to Creative Director at a national agency, or who starts in print production and moves into brand management at a consumer goods company — those trajectories are evidence of curriculum impact that first-job data cannot capture.

What it would produce: Trajectory pattern map — which starting roles lead where across the graduate population. Curriculum-to-trajectory alignment: does the KUD profile explain career advancement patterns, not just entry placement?

Section 6 — Methodological Significance

Empirical validation of the KUD framework

Curriculum frameworks are typically validated theoretically — by alignment with established taxonomies or expert judgment about what constitutes good outcome language. This proposal would enable a less common form of validation: comparison against an empirical record of graduate outcomes that preceded the tool's development by a decade.

The historical graduate data is fixed and independent. It was not generated by this methodology, and it was not shaped by it. Those 268 records represent careers that materialized before this analysis framework existed. That independence is what makes it a genuine validation rather than a circular argument. The data is the benchmark. The tool is what we test against it.

If the KUDs generated from GC course materials align well with the O*NET competency profiles of jobs that 88.4% of Clemson GC graduates actually hold, that is evidence the methodology captures something real — not just plausible-sounding academic language. If they don't align, that is a calibration signal: the framework tells us where the methodology over- or under-weights certain competencies relative to what the careers actually require.

The validation chain

Course materials → AI-generated KUDs → O*NET competency alignment → Graduate career destinations

Each link is independently verifiable. The graduate data is fixed — it preceded the tool. That independence is what makes this a genuine validation rather than a circular argument.

The tuning opportunity

Where the KUDs and career data don't align, the methodology can be adjusted. If the AI consistently over-weights certain Bloom's levels relative to what the jobs actually require, that is a calibration signal.

The historical data is the benchmark. The tool is what we tune against it.

The analytical chain — course materials → AI-generated KUDs → O*NET competency alignment → 268 independent graduate career records — constitutes, to our knowledge, the first empirically grounded curriculum-to-career alignment analysis attempted in graphic communications education. Prior work in this area has relied on crosswalk-based mapping or expert judgment; individual graduate outcome data at this scale has not previously been available alongside a systematic course-level outcomes analysis.

If the analysis yields strong alignment, the results would be relevant beyond Clemson: they would provide evidence that AI-assisted curriculum materials analysis produces outcome descriptions that correspond to documented career placements. That finding would be generalizable to other GC programs and potentially to adjacent programs sharing similar occupational distributions.

Section 7 — Proposed Data Collection

Extending the dataset for trajectory analysis

The 268 Clemson records currently capture point-in-time employment. The trajectory analysis would require longitudinal data — multiple positions per graduate in chronological sequence — collected via LinkedIn.

What we would collect

For each graduate: full employment history from graduation to present — job title, company, dates, industry. Each position coded to SOC and NAICS.

Going back to 2010 (or program inception). Priority: 2018–2026 cohorts for full history; pre-2018 for current-job baseline extension.

What it enables

Graduates 8–16 years out have mature career trajectories — the most informative for understanding what the program enables over time, not just at graduation.

Combined with the first-job alignment analysis, this creates a complete picture: entry alignment and trajectory alignment.
