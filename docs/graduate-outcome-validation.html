Section 1 — The Disconnect

What the federal system says vs. what actually happens

For 25 years, the federal classification system has told prospective students, counselors, state governments, and college administrators that a graphic communications degree leads to declining trade jobs — prepress technicians, printing press operators, occupations with decades of projected losses. That is what the CIP–SOC crosswalk for graphic communications programs (CIP 10.03) says. That is what career databases used by millions of students and advisors draw from when a student searches "graphic communications degree."

Meanwhile, Clemson GC graduates have been landing at PwC, JP Morgan, BBDO, Wieden+Kennedy, Amazon, Bank of America, and the PGA Tour — across 58 industries, at companies that represent real and growing career trajectories. That is what the employment records show.

The program has been producing strong outcomes in spite of a data infrastructure that makes the degree look worthless. Students who found the program anyway. Faculty who taught toward real industry. Graduates who figured out how to translate their education into career language that didn't match their degree's official description.

"We have 268 records proving the outcomes already exist. The question is no longer whether the curriculum produces valuable career preparation — it does. The question is whether we can make that connection explicit, understand how it works, and reinforce it intentionally."

Section 3 — The Classification Gap

The scale of the mismatch

The federal CIP–SOC crosswalk for graphic communications programs (CIP 10.03) was written in the late 1990s and has not been substantively updated. Career databases used by millions of students, counselors, and administrators draw from this crosswalk — and return results that bear almost no relation to where GC graduates actually work.

This data is not primarily an argument for federal reclassification — though it is that too. It is evidence that there is something real to describe. The program has been producing documented outcomes in careers that don't appear anywhere in the degree's official profile. That's not a coincidence. It's a curriculum that works, operating without the infrastructure to say what it produces.

Section 4 — Making the Implicit Explicit

The language the program has been missing

What the program has not had is the language to describe what it's actually doing — at the course level, in terms that connect explicitly to where graduates go. Faculty have been teaching toward real industry outcomes. Graduates have been landing in real careers. But the infrastructure for saying this course produces these outcomes, which prepare students for these roles, which is why graduates end up here has not existed.

The KUD-based curriculum analysis — Know, Understand, Do outcomes derived directly from course materials — is the infrastructure for making the implicit explicit. Course by course, the tool generates structured outcome statements. The proposed validation module would then map those statements to the O*NET competency profiles of the 21 career destinations 88% of graduates reach. The question becomes: does the curriculum's language describe the competencies those careers actually require?

The key distinction: This is not about building curriculum for aspirational outcomes. It is about understanding and intentionally reinforcing a curriculum that has already been producing documented outcomes — and giving the program the language to say so explicitly, course by course.

Section 5 — The Proposed Validation Functions

Two functions, one closed loop

The proposed Graduate Outcome Validation module would link AI-generated course learning outcomes (KUDs) to real career destinations via O*NET — the federal occupational competency database — as an intermediary. The result would be an empirical test of whether the curriculum's stated outcomes match what the careers graduates enter actually require.

1
First-Job Alignment
Does what we teach match where graduates start?

For each SOC code in the graduate dataset, the module would pull O*NET competency profiles — knowledge areas, skills, and work activities — and map them to KUD structure. Each course's AI-generated KUDs would then be compared against the competency requirements of the career destinations graduates actually enter.

What it would produce: Course-level alignment scores against each of the 21 career destinations. Program-level coverage report: does the full curriculum collectively prepare students for the careers 88% of graduates reach? Gap report: which destinations have weak curriculum coverage?

Data available now 268 Clemson records O*NET API

2
Career Trajectory Alignment
Does what we teach enable where graduates go over time?

Point-in-time employment data shows where graduates are when observed. Career trajectory data shows the arc — where they start, where they are five years later, and what that progression reveals about what the program actually enables over time.

A graduate who enters as a Graphic Designer and advances to Creative Director at a national agency, or who starts in print production and moves into brand management at a consumer goods company — those trajectories are evidence of curriculum impact that first-job data cannot capture.

What it would produce: Trajectory pattern map — which starting roles lead where across the graduate population. Curriculum-to-trajectory alignment: does the KUD profile explain career advancement patterns, not just entry placement?

Section 6 — Methodological Significance

Ground truth validation of the KUD approach

Most curriculum frameworks are validated theoretically — "this aligns with Bloom's taxonomy, therefore it accurately describes learning outcomes." This initiative enables something rarer: empirical validation against real outcomes that preceded the tool by a decade.

The historical graduate data is fixed and independent. It was not generated by this methodology, and it was not shaped by it. Those 268 records represent careers that materialized before this analysis framework existed. That independence is what makes it a genuine validation rather than a circular argument. The data is the benchmark. The tool is what we test against it.

If the KUDs generated from GC course materials align well with the O*NET competency profiles of jobs that 88.4% of Clemson GC graduates actually hold, that is evidence the methodology captures something real — not just plausible-sounding academic language. If they don't align, that is a calibration signal: the framework tells us where the methodology over- or under-weights certain competencies relative to what the careers actually require.

The validation chain

Course materials → AI-generated KUDs → O*NET competency alignment → Graduate career destinations

Each link is independently verifiable. The graduate data is fixed — it preceded the tool. That independence is what makes this a genuine validation rather than a circular argument.

The tuning opportunity

Where the KUDs and career data don't align, the methodology can be adjusted. If the AI consistently over-weights certain Bloom's levels relative to what the jobs actually require, that is a calibration signal.

The historical data is the benchmark. The tool is what we tune against it.

The full closed loop — course materials → AI-generated KUDs → O*NET competency alignment → 268 independent graduate career records — is, to our knowledge, the first empirically grounded curriculum-to-career alignment analysis attempted in graphic communications education. No one has done it because no one has had both pieces simultaneously: individual graduate outcome data at this scale and a systematic way to analyze course-level outcomes. This program now has both.

If validated, this becomes a field-wide contribution: evidence that AI-assisted course materials analysis produces outcome descriptions that predict real career alignment. Publishable. Generalizable to other GC programs. Potentially the methodological foundation for making this a discipline-wide tool, not just a Clemson tool.

Section 7 — Proposed Data Collection

Extending the dataset for trajectory analysis

The 268 Clemson records currently capture point-in-time employment. The trajectory analysis would require longitudinal data — multiple positions per graduate in chronological sequence — collected via LinkedIn.

What we would collect

For each graduate: full employment history from graduation to present — job title, company, dates, industry. Each position coded to SOC and NAICS.

Going back to 2010 (or program inception). Priority: 2018–2026 cohorts for full history; pre-2018 for current-job baseline extension.

What it enables

Graduates 8–16 years out have mature career trajectories — the most informative for understanding what the program enables over time, not just at graduation.

Combined with the first-job alignment analysis, this creates a complete picture: entry alignment and trajectory alignment.
