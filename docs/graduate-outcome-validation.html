Section 1 — Background

Classification data and documented graduate outcomes

The CIP–SOC crosswalk for graphic communications programs (CIP 10.03) — a crosswalk being a mapping table that connects academic programs (CIP: Classification of Instructional Programs) to the occupations their graduates are expected to enter (SOC: Standard Occupational Classification) — was established in the late 1990s and has not been substantively updated. Career advising databases, state workforce systems, and federal reporting tools draw from this crosswalk. For CIP 10.03, it points primarily to prepress technicians and printing press operators: occupations in projected decline.

The employment dataset documented in the pilot tells a different story. Clemson GC graduates in the 2022–2025 pilot coded set entered 43 distinct occupational categories across 58 industries, including design, marketing, technology, sales, and management roles at a wide range of employers. The distribution differs substantially from what the current crosswalk would predict.

The gap between the crosswalk and the employment record indicates that Clemson GC graduates have entered outcomes not well described by the current crosswalk. The pilot data presented here is not aspirational — it documents observed graduate placements. The question this study addresses is whether the curriculum's stated learning outcomes can be compared systematically against the competency profiles of the occupations graduates actually enter, course by course.

The 268 graduate records coded to date precede the curriculum analysis tool described in this study. They provide an independent reference set against which AI-generated course learning outcomes can be compared — a comparison that is possible precisely because the two datasets were developed separately. Important scope note: strong alignment between AI-generated KUDs and the competency profiles of occupations graduates entered would provide evidence of external relevance. It would not, by itself, establish student attainment, instructional effectiveness, or causal impact of the curriculum on career outcomes. The study is therefore better described as an external-alignment / criterion-relevance check than as validation in the stronger sense.

Data Methods
  • Source of records. Clemson Graphic Communications department alumni tracking, supplemented by the ACCGC Graduate Employment Dataset (603 records across 9 GC programs, 2018–2026). The 268 Clemson records are the program-specific subset used for this study's curriculum-alignment work.
  • Collection window. The initial coded set covers 2022–2025 (268 records). Collection is now extending toward approximately 10 years of Clemson graduates; the total graduate population over that span is well over 268. The window is pre-registered before analysis begins — the same discipline as the study's pre-committed failure criteria — so collection runs against a fixed design, not ad-hoc extension.
  • Job mapping. Every job each graduate has held is captured, with the first career-relevant job specifically labeled. All positions are coded to SOC (job title) and NAICS (employer industry).
  • Analytic anchor rule. The criterion-relevance alignment is anchored on the first (or first 1–2) career-relevant destination. Later jobs are retained as career-trajectory context in a separate analytic lane, not folded into the "where graduates land" alignment test — because later roles reflect post-graduation learning and market moves the curriculum cannot be credited or debited for.
  • Definition of "first." First career-relevant full-time role; a summer or retail stopgap is not coded as a GC career destination.
  • Pre-registration. The collection window, the first-vs-all-jobs rule, and the job-title→SOC coding method are committed in advance before analysis runs. Any methodology change after that point requires a new pre-registered run reported alongside the original.
  • Definition of "verified." Currently used as shorthand for records confirmed against at least one external source (LinkedIn, employer directory, or alumni-office confirmation); to be tightened before analysis.
  • Privacy / FERPA / IRB status. To be confirmed before full collection completes.
Section 2 — Study Design

Two-phase design: Pilot complete, expansion underway

The study runs in two phases. The phases differ in scope, analytic depth, and the rigor of pre-registration — the pilot established that the method works and that the gap is real; Phase 2 is designed to produce a result that can stand independent scrutiny.

Phase 1 — Pilot (complete)

A first pass over 268 Clemson GC graduates (2022–2025), each coded job-title → SOC occupation and employer → industry. It established the core finding: the official CIP→SOC crosswalk — which still points GC toward declining prepress and press-operator roles — badly mismatches where graduates actually land.

The pilot found 43 occupations across 58 industries (design, marketing, technology, management), with the updated 21-code reference set capturing 88% of placements versus 3.4% for the federal crosswalk.

The pilot proved the method and surfaced the gap. It was deliberately time-bounded, not designed for full coverage.

Phase 2 — Expansion (underway)

Extends the dataset to a full ~10 years of Clemson graduates, capturing every job each graduate has held with the first career-relevant destination labeled. Two analytic layers the pilot did not include:

(a) Demand-side skills map. For the top destination occupations, seed each one's competency demand from its O*NET work-activity profile, then enrich and validate against real job postings.

(b) Criterion-relevance alignment. Compare that destination demand against the curriculum's captured KUD+ coverage, course by course.

The collection window, the first-vs-all-jobs rule, and the coding method are pre-registered before analysis. Phase 2 is run with a student researcher (see Section 3).

Pre-registration discipline

The collection window, inclusion/exclusion rules, the definition of "first career-relevant job," and the job-title→SOC coding scheme are committed in writing before analysis begins. Any methodology change after that point requires a new pre-registered run reported alongside the original. This is the same discipline as the study's pre-committed failure criteria (Section 9) — it prevents the most common form of invisible bias in retrospective career-outcome studies: adjusting the collection window or coding rules until the numbers look clean.

Section 3 — The Student-Researcher Role

A 2-credit research course: partner, not assistant

Phase 2 is run with a student researcher enrolled in a 2-credit-hour research course (~6 hours/week). The role is structured as a genuine research partnership — the student contributes intellectual work (authoring the coding scheme, making judgment calls on edge cases, designing the visualization) — not data entry. Deliverables are divided into Core (required for the course) and Extension (pursued if the timeline allows).

Core deliverables (required)

1. Data collection & coding. Compile the ~10-year graduate employment record from LinkedIn and alumni sources — every position per graduate, first career-relevant destination flagged — coding each job title to its SOC occupation and each employer to its industry, against a documented coding scheme she maintains and versions.

2. A documented, pre-registered method. Author and version the coding rules (the definition of "first career-relevant" job, inclusion/exclusion criteria, the verification standard) and pre-register the collection window and rules before running the analysis. The pre-registration is the methodological contribution; it is what separates this from a retrospective convenience study.

3. The destinations finding + a visualization. Produce the descriptive result — the occupation and industry distribution and its gap against the official CIP→SOC crosswalk — with at least one clear visualization (for example, a major→first-job→current-job flow, or an occupation and industry breakdown).

4. A presentation deliverable. A research poster or short talk (TAGA — the Technical Association of the Graphic Arts — or a Clemson undergraduate research symposium) and a contribution to the writeup, with co-authorship credit.

Extension deliverables (if the timeline allows)

5. Demand-side skills mapping. For the top ~10 destination occupations, build the competency-demand profile (O*NET seed + real-posting enrichment), surfacing where GC-specific skills — Typography, Color Science, Press Chemistry — have no clean O*NET match. The absence of a match is itself a finding, not a defect to paper over: it means the federal occupational database has not yet caught up with what GC-trained practitioners actually do in those roles.

6. Reliability check. Have a second coder independently re-code a sample of the job→SOC mappings and compute inter-rater agreement (Cohen's κ). This is the measurement-quality evidence the broader project needs before the criterion-relevance alignment result can be taken seriously.

Guardrails

Three principles govern the student researcher's analytic work. First, seed-then-refine: O*NET profiles are the starting point, not the truth — real job postings and judgment calls on GC-specific skills are used to refine them. Second, "no clean match" is a finding: if a GC competency has no natural O*NET descriptor, that gap is reported and documented, not silently mapped to the nearest bucket. Third, pre-register before looking at results: the coding rules and collection window are committed before the data is analyzed, so the conclusions cannot be shaped by knowing what they will show.

Section 4 — Pre-Registration & Validation Plan

Design fixed before the data is analyzed

Phase 2's credibility rests on a single discipline: the study design is committed before analysis begins. The collection window, the unit and job rules, the coding method, the indicators, the analysis plan, and the failure criteria are all pre-registered here. Any later deviation will be reported as a deviation — not folded in silently as though it were always the plan.

What the study tests — and what it does not

The study tests one hypothesis: that the competencies the curriculum is evidenced to emphasize — as captured in CourseCapture KUD+ coverage, scoped to the courses that build toward a given career target — correspond to the competency demands of the occupations graduates actually enter, above a chance baseline. The null hypothesis is that there is no better-than-chance correspondence.

Scope: criterion relevance only

This study does not test student attainment, instructional effectiveness, or any causal effect of the curriculum on career outcomes. A positive result says the curriculum emphasizes the right things relative to where graduates go — not that any individual graduate attained those competencies, and not that the curriculum caused the outcome. Throughout this document, "criterion relevance" is the correct term; "validates" and "proves" are not.

The two sides, kept independent

The alignment test has two sides, and their independence is methodologically essential.

  • Predictor (curriculum). KUD+ coverage per competency at depth, drawn from confirmed CourseCapture snapshots for the courses that build toward a given career target.
  • Criterion (destinations). The occupational competency demand of each graduate's first career-relevant destination — job title coded to SOC, the SOC profiled from O*NET work activities, then refined by faculty and employer input through Position Capture.
  • Independence guardrail. The criterion (where graduates actually landed, plus its occupational profile) is kept methodologically separate from the curriculum's career-target definitions. The test must not reduce to "O*NET vs. O*NET." O*NET data is used as a seed for the demand profile, then refined — it is never treated as the final truth, and it is never allowed to circularly define the curriculum's own targets.

Pre-registered specifications

The following are committed before any data is analyzed. Where a value is described as "fixed at registration," it means the value will be recorded in a timestamped pre-registration document before the collection window opens; it is not a number chosen here.

  • Sample & window. All Clemson GC graduates across approximately 10 years; the exact span is fixed at registration. The pilot's 268 records (2022–2025) are a subset of this population. The window is set before analysis begins — not extended after the results are known.
  • Job rule. Every position per graduate is captured. The first career-relevant full-time role is labeled the primary destination and is the unit of the alignment test. Later positions are retained as trajectory context in a separate analytic lane and are not folded into the alignment measure.
  • "First career-relevant" defined. The first full-time role plausibly drawing on GC preparation. This definition explicitly excludes summer employment, stopgap retail or service roles, and other clearly unrelated first paychecks. Edge-case decisions are recorded in the coding scheme before analysis.
  • Coding scheme. Job title → SOC and employer → NAICS, against a documented, versioned coding scheme. "Verified" means confirmed against at least one external source: LinkedIn, an employer directory, or an alumni-office record.
  • Inter-rater reliability. A second coder independently codes a random sample. Inter-rater agreement is computed as Cohen's κ and reported alongside the primary results. The pre-registered threshold follows the conventional standard for substantial agreement: κ ≥ 0.70. A result below threshold is reported as a finding, not adjusted away.
  • Inclusion / exclusion. Requires a verifiable employer and job title. Graduate-school enrollees, non-completers, and no-response or unfindable cases are tracked and reported in a separate accounting; they are not silently dropped from the denominator.

Analysis plan — committed before looking at results

  • Primary analysis. Per-target alignment between the curriculum's emphasized competencies and first-destination occupational demand, compared against a chance baseline. This is the headline criterion-relevance test.
  • Descriptive analysis. The full destination distribution compared against the official CIP→SOC crosswalk — scaling the pilot finding to the complete dataset.
  • "No-clean-match" findings, pre-specified as first-class outputs. Two asymmetric absence findings are defined in advance: (a) a GC competency that has no O*NET match signals a blind spot in the occupational framework — the federal database has not caught up with what GC practitioners actually do; (b) a high-demand destination work-activity with no corresponding curriculum coverage signals a candidate gap in the program. Both are findings, not defects to paper over.

Pre-committed failure criteria

The pre-committed failure criteria C1–C3 stated in Section 9 are the formal gates for this study. The following integrates with those criteria and adds the pre-registration condition that makes any result count:

  • If per-target coverage shows no better-than-baseline alignment with first-destination demand, the criterion-relevance claim fails for that target. A failed result is reported as such, not adjusted after the fact.
  • If graduates whose curricular history matches the framework's "well-scaffolded" pattern do not land in better-aligned destinations than those whose history does not, the scaffolding claim is unsupported.
  • A result counts only if the collection window and rules above were fixed and recorded before the analysis was run. Post-hoc adjustment of the window or coding rules requires a new pre-registered run, reported alongside the original.

Coverage gap

Any curriculum analysis tool that draws from standard federal career data for CIP 10.03 would be working from a reference set that accounts for approximately 3.4% of actual Clemson GC graduate outcomes. The 21-code proposed crosswalk — grounded in 603 graduate records across 9 programs — provides a more representative reference set for curriculum alignment work.

The primary purpose of presenting this coverage gap is not to advocate for federal reclassification, though the data does support that case. The purpose is to establish that the current classification framework does not describe what this program produces, and that a more accurate reference set exists and is being used as the basis for this study.

Section 7 — Research Motivation

Connecting curriculum outcomes to documented graduate destinations

What the program currently lacks is a systematic framework for describing, at the course level, how specific learning outcomes connect to the career destinations graduates enter. The employment distribution is documented; the curriculum exists; the connection between them has not been formally analyzed.

The KUD framework — Know, Understand, Do — provides structured learning outcome statements derived directly from course materials. The proposed analysis module would map those statements to O*NET competency profiles for each of the 21 SOC codes that account for 88.4% of documented graduate placements. The central analytical question is whether the curriculum's stated outcomes correspond to the competencies those occupations require.

Analytical grounding

This analysis is grounded in outcomes the program has already produced, not outcomes it is working toward. The graduate data provides the empirical reference point; the curriculum analysis provides the explanatory framework. The goal is to understand the relationship between the two — and to identify where that relationship is strong, where it is weak, and where outcomes are being produced without explicit curricular claim.

Section 8 — The Proposed Analysis Functions

Two proposed analytical functions

The Graduate Outcome Criterion-Relevance module would link AI-generated course learning outcomes (KUDs) to graduate career destinations via O*NET — the federal occupational competency database — as an intermediary. The result would be a comparison of curriculum-stated outcomes against the competency requirements of the occupations graduates actually enter.

1
First-Job Alignment
Does what we teach match where graduates start?

O*NET (Occupational Information Network), maintained by the Department of Labor, provides a public competency profile for each SOC code: knowledge areas, skills, abilities, and work activities. O*NET categories do not map one-to-one onto KUD — O*NET "Skills" often describe performed capabilities closer to Do than Understand; "Abilities" do not map cleanly to any KUD dimension; "Tasks" may be more directly Do-like than the generalized work activities; "Knowledge" may involve both Know and Understand. Illustrative outputs

Course-level finding
"GC 3460's KUDs align strongly with Graphic Designer (27-1024) and Marketing Specialist (13-1161) requirements. They have weak coverage of Project Management Specialist (13-1082) requirements — which 3.7% of graduates become."
Program-level finding
"Aggregating KUDs across all courses, the curriculum has strong coverage of the Design, Media & Technology cluster and partial coverage of the Management & Business cluster. The 11 graduates who became Chief Executives are drawing on entrepreneurship and business fundamentals that don't appear explicitly in any course's KUDs."

The program-level finding is particularly notable: outcomes the curriculum produces without explicitly claiming to. Identifying where graduates succeed in roles that don't appear in any course's stated KUDs is one of the more analytically useful things this comparison would surface.

The role of individual records

O*NET describes what "Graphic Designers" generically require. The 268 pilot records provide context O*NET cannot: Clemson GC graphic designers work specifically at sports franchises, packaging companies, advertising agencies, and financial services firms. Competency requirements shift across those industry contexts — a graphic designer at a packaging manufacturer operates differently than one at an advertising agency. The individual records would allow the analysis to cluster by industry and examine whether those differences are reflected in the curriculum's stated outcomes.

2
Career Trajectory Alignment
Does what we teach enable where graduates go over time?

Point-in-time employment data shows where graduates are when observed. Career trajectory data shows the arc — where they start, where they are five years later, and what that progression reveals about what the program actually enables over time.

A graduate who enters as a Graphic Designer and advances to Creative Director at a national agency, or who starts in print production and moves into brand management at a consumer goods company — those trajectories provide external context about where graduates move over time. Trajectories are influenced by labor markets, employer training, individual aptitude, networks, internships, geography, and post-graduation experience; on their own they can suggest whether curricular competencies remain relevant to later roles, but they do not establish curriculum impact without additional study design.

What it would produce: Trajectory pattern map — which starting roles lead where across the graduate population. Curriculum-to-trajectory alignment: does the KUD profile explain career advancement patterns, not just entry placement?

Section 9 — Methodological Significance

External-alignment check for the KUD framework

Curriculum frameworks are typically evaluated theoretically — by alignment with established taxonomies or expert judgment about what constitutes good outcome language. This study would enable a less common form of evidence: comparison against an empirical record of graduate placements that preceded the tool's development by a decade. The check is best characterized as external-alignment validation or criterion-relevance: it tests whether AI-generated course profiles correspond to the occupational competency profiles for the roles graduates actually enter. It does not, by itself, establish student attainment, instructional effectiveness, or causal impact on career outcomes.

The historical graduate data is fixed and independent. It was not generated by this methodology and was not shaped by it. The records document placements that materialized before this analysis framework existed. That independence is what makes the comparison non-circular — but non-circular is not the same as causal. The data is a useful external reference; the tool is what we test against it.

Strong alignment between AI-generated KUDs and the O*NET competency profiles of occupations 88.4% of Clemson GC graduates hold would provide evidence that the methodology produces outcome descriptions corresponding to documented placements — not a claim that students learned those competencies, nor that the curriculum caused the career outcomes. Weak alignment would be a calibration signal: the comparison would tell us where the methodology over- or under-weights certain competencies relative to occupational requirements (with the caveat that O*NET itself is a generic public profile, not a precise proxy for any specific first-job's requirements).

The alignment chain

Course materials → AI-generated KUDs → O*NET competency mapping → Graduate career destinations

Each link is independently inspectable. The graduate data is fixed — it preceded the tool — which makes this comparison non-circular. Non-circular is not the same as causal: this chain establishes external alignment, not curriculum impact.

The tuning opportunity

Where the KUDs and career data don't align, the methodology can be adjusted. If the AI consistently over-weights certain Bloom's levels relative to what the jobs actually require, that is a calibration signal.

The historical data is the benchmark. The tool is what we tune against it.

The analytical chain — course materials → AI-generated KUDs → O*NET competency mapping → independent graduate career records — would be an unusually concrete curriculum-to-career alignment analysis for this program, because it joins course-level evidence with documented graduate destinations rather than relying on crosswalk-based mapping or expert judgment alone. Whether comparable empirical work has been done in graphic-communications education elsewhere is a literature-review question that has not been completed for this study.

If the analysis yields strong alignment, the results would be evidence that AI-assisted curriculum materials analysis produces outcome descriptions corresponding to documented placements for this program. Replication in other programs would be needed before generalizing.

Pre-committed criteria — what would count as failure

The problem-solving research synthesis sets the standard this study must meet: a framework "must pre-commit to the effect sizes and controls that would count as a genuine failure rather than a call for adjustment." The criteria below are stated before any data collection. Thresholds are proposals pending confirmation by the curriculum committee; once data collection begins they are frozen — a failed criterion is reported as a finding, never adjusted away after the fact.

C1 — Mapping-reliability gate (precondition)

The KUD→O*NET mapping rubric must reach inter-rater agreement of Krippendorff's α ≥ 0.70 between two independent raters on a sample of at least 20 occupations before any alignment result counts. Below the gate, no downstream finding is reportable — the instrument failed, not the curriculum.

C2 — Discrimination criterion (the real test)

Mean alignment for the 21 destination SOC codes must exceed alignment for a matched control set of 21 non-destination SOC codes (similar education level, drawn from outside graphic communications) by at least Cohen's d ≥ 0.5. If the program's KUDs align with graduates' actual occupations no better than with arbitrary occupations, the analysis has no criterion validity — the KUDs describe work generically, not GC destinations specifically. This is the headline falsification test.

C3 — Coverage criterion

At least 60% of the program's central (high-emphasis) competencies must map at Direct or Partial match strength to at least one O*NET descriptor within the 21-code destination set. Below that, either the curriculum-to-destination claim fails for the mapped framework, or the career-target taxonomy itself is the wrong unit of analysis — both reportable findings.

What failure does NOT license

A failed criterion does not license re-running with adjusted prompts until the number clears, narrowing the destination set post hoc, or reclassifying control occupations. Any methodology change after a failure requires a new pre-registered run, reported alongside the failed one. The "tuning opportunity" described above applies to calibration of the tool, and only on the timeline these criteria make explicit.

Section 10 — Data Collection

Extending the dataset for trajectory analysis

The 268 Clemson records in the pilot capture point-in-time employment. The trajectory analysis would require longitudinal data — multiple positions per graduate in chronological sequence — collected via LinkedIn. Phase 2 is extending the collection window toward approximately 10 years of Clemson graduates; the pilot's 268-record set reflects the initial coding pass, not the full population over that window.

What we would collect

For each graduate: full employment history from graduation to present — job title, company, dates, industry. Each position coded to SOC and NAICS.

Going back to 2010 (or program inception). Priority: 2022–2025 cohorts for full history; earlier cohorts for current-job baseline extension.

What it enables

Graduates 8–16 years out have mature career trajectories — the most informative for understanding what the program enables over time, not just at graduation.

Combined with the first-job alignment analysis, this creates a complete picture: entry alignment and trajectory alignment.
