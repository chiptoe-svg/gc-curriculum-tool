Research synthesis & framework design

Problem-Solving as a Program-Level Emergent Property

A research-tool position on how problem-solving competence actually develops in undergraduate education, and how the KUD+ curriculum-mapping framework operationalizes that view into per-course audit probes and program-level diagnostics. Companion to the KUD+ Background; extends §8.

Date 2026-05-25 Status Research-tool framing — defensible commitments, not settled science Audience Faculty, curriculum committee, advisory partners

What this tool is — and is not (read this first)

Honest frame

This is, unapologetically, a curriculum-conversation engine — not yet a measurement instrument. It turns scattered course evidence into a structured, shared picture that a curriculum committee can argue over productively. Its inputs are faculty testimony plus document interpretation; its outputs are legible maps, not validated measurements. The measurement and evidence layers can be added as data accumulates (see "the evidence ladder" below and the graduate-outcome validation plan), but the tool earns its keep today by making the conversation better, not by scoring the program. We state this up front because the rest of this document builds a serious case, and a serious case deserves an honest frame: the framework is stronger as a conversation engine than as a measurement instrument, and it should be read that way until the evidence ladder, artifact checks, and longitudinal validation are in place.

One-page version (for the curriculum committee)

  • Claim. Problem-solving develops when students repeatedly apply domain knowledge to novel problems, experience informative failure, and reflect/revise with feedback. What eventually transfers is the habit of running that cycle — but the habit forms only after enough domain-specific repetitions, which is why it can't be front-loaded into a standalone course. It is a program-level emergent property, not a course you can add.
  • What this is not a claim about. Not that problem-solving fails to transfer. A capable problem-solver in one domain does carry cross-domain habits — decomposing an ill-defined task, tolerating being stuck, abstracting the principle that survives a change of context. The framework's position is that those habits are grown through domain cycles, not taught generically. We reject generic problem-solving courses, not transfer itself. The transfer to watch for is usually readiness to learn a new area faster ("preparation for future learning") rather than cold cross-domain performance — and it requires abstracting a principle across varied cases and bridging it outward, which a single rich domain can supply.
  • What the tool captures. Per-competency K/U/D depth, productive-failure conditions, structured reflection, and program sequence — surfaced as maps.
  • What it does NOT produce. A single "problem-solving score," and (today) artifact-verified measurement. Depth maps show opportunity, not confirmed formation.
  • What would change a curriculum decision. Missing upper-depth opportunities for a target; high-performance courses with absent productive-failure scaffolding ("unproductive success"); absent reflection in capstone/upper-division courses; a depth progression that expects mastery before it is built.

The evidence ladder (claimed vs. verified)

Every claim the tool records sits on a credibility ladder. The tool's job is to make the level visible, not to adjudicate the score:

Level Meaning
0 Instructor claim only
1 An assignment prompt supports the claim
2 A rubric explicitly assesses the condition
3 Student-work artifacts show the condition occurred
4 Pre/post, revision history, or external review confirms a learning effect

A program diagnostic should distinguish claimed scaffolding from artifact-verified scaffolding. Today most inputs sit at Level 0–2; the recently-added discipline that a credited structured post-mortem must cite a graded artifact pushes that one signal toward Level 2–3. Surfacing the level per claim — not gating the score — is the design direction (spec'd separately). This is the concrete answer to the sharpest critique of the framework: that a tool like this can produce beautifully structured maps from weak input evidence. The defense is not to pretend the evidence is strong; it is to make its strength visible.

Falsification conditions

The framework is a working hypothesis, and it should be able to be wrong. It would be materially weakened if programs with high upper-depth distribution, strong productive-failure scaffolding, and structured reflection show no advantage on externally-rated workplace problem-solving after controlling for incoming student profile, internship exposure, and GPA. "Refinement" is not an acceptable response to every disconfirmation; the validation plan must pre-commit to the effect sizes and controls that would count as a genuine failure rather than a call for adjustment.

What we claim, and how strongly

  • Claims we make strongly (well-supported): problem-solving is domain-bound; expertise is schema-mediated; far transfer needs explicit scaffolding; structured debriefs improve performance; productive failure improves conceptual understanding and near transfer under PS-I conditions.
  • Claims we treat as design hypotheses (plausible, not established): that curriculum snapshots + K/U/D depths + audit probes can diagnose program-level problem-solving formation; that cumulative exposure across four years produces better problem-solvers.
  • Claims we do NOT make: that the tool measures graduate problem-solving today; that a high map equals a strong program; that "covered" equals "developed"; that problem-solving does not transfer — it does, via the cumulative cross-domain habit the moderate-generalist position describes, which is precisely why generic stand-alone courses (that skip the domain cycles) are the wrong delivery vehicle.

§ 1Why this document exists

Employers consistently rate problem-solving among the most desirable graduate competencies. The National Association of Colleges and Employers' annual Job Outlook surveys have placed problem-solving at or near the top of the competencies list for years running. Yet faculty conversations about how to develop problem-solving regularly stall: the natural moves — add a "Problem-Solving" course, list it as a generic outcome, ask each instructor to teach it — are exactly the moves the cognitive-science literature below predicts will underperform. Employer surveys establish the demand for problem-solving; they do not themselves measure whether these curricular moves succeed, so the claim that generic instruction underperforms rests on the transfer and domain-specificity research that follows, not on a measured employer signal.

The mismatch is not a measurement artifact. The intuition that a faculty member developing this tool articulated — that problem-solving requires high levels of K/U/D in a specific domain, followed by forced experience with failure, post-mortem, troubleshooting, and corrective action — turns out to be substantially supported by four decades of cognitive-science research. The natural curricular moves miss because they treat problem-solving as a transferable generic skill. The literature does not support treating problem-solving as a free-floating skill that transfers reliably without domain knowledge and explicit transfer scaffolding. Far transfer is difficult; domain knowledge is usually required; generic problem-solving instruction without domain anchoring is weak. Moderate-generalist positions (metacognition, transfer scaffolding) acknowledge contested transfer rather than no transfer.

This document does two things. First, it reviews the research carefully — surfacing what is well-supported, what is genuinely contested, and where the literature can be cited too confidently. Second, it operationalizes the research into the curriculum-mapping tool's data shape: the per-course audit probes that surface productive-failure conditions, the depth scales that encode problem-solving facets within domains, and the program-level views that aggregate the cumulative pattern. The goal is to make a property that has resisted measurement — problem-solving competence in graduates — legible at the level where it can actually inform curriculum decisions.

A framing note

This tool does not need to be perfect or 100% grounded in proven science. It is a research instrument in its own right — a way to make defensible commitments visible, generate data that tests those commitments over time, and revise as evidence accumulates. The document below makes commitments rather than hedges them, while naming the genuine limits where they matter.

Part I — The research

§ 2The domain-specificity finding

The most consequential body of work for this question is the cognitive-science research on problem-solving and critical thinking that began in the late 1970s and has remained remarkably stable in its core claim since. The claim is that thinking is not a portable skill that can be cultivated independently of subject matter; it is constitutively entangled with what is being thought about.

Daniel Willingham's 2007 synthesis in American Educator is the most widely cited accessible articulation. Willingham argues that "the processes of thinking are intertwined with the content of thought" — that even highly trained scientists fail at critical thinking when reasoning about problems outside their domain of expertise. His implication for instruction is direct: it makes no sense to try to teach critical thinking devoid of factual content, because critical thinking is the operation of disciplinary knowledge under the conditions where it matters.

John McPeck made the philosophical case earlier and more sharply in his 1981 monograph Critical Thinking and Education. McPeck argued that "there are no general thinking skills, since thinking is always thinking about some subject-matter" — and that courses devoted to generalized skill instruction should give way to subject-specific instruction that develops the same capacities within domains where they actually operate. McPeck's strong-specificity position has been contested by Ennis, Paul, Siegel, and others who defend a more moderate generalist view, and the philosophical debate remains genuinely open. What has accumulated is empirical support for the related-but-distinct domain-dependence finding: expert performance, problem-solving capability, and transfer of learning all show strong domain-specific patterns in the cognitive-science literature. The framework rests on the empirical finding, not on philosophical settlement of the McPeck-Ennis debate.

The empirical bedrock is the expert-novice literature in cognitive science, beginning with Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser's 1981 study of physics problem-solving. Chi and colleagues gave the same set of physics problems to experts (physics professors) and novices (introductory students) and asked them to sort the problems into groups. The novices sorted by surface features — problems that mentioned a pulley went together; problems that mentioned an inclined plane went together. The experts sorted by underlying principle — problems governed by conservation of energy went together, regardless of their surface details. The finding was not that experts knew more. It was that experts had categorically different mental representations of the same problems, organized around principles rather than surface features.

Larkin, McDermott, Simon, and Simon's contemporaneous 1980 paper extended the analysis to problem-solving strategies. Experts predominantly use forward-chaining inference (starting from given quantities and working toward what is asked); novices predominantly use means-ends analysis (working backward from the goal, searching for operations that reduce the gap to the goal). The difference is not that novices lack a strategy — they have one, and it is laborious. The expert strategy becomes available because the schemas required to recognize which principles are triggered by a problem are in place, allowing immediate forward inference. The novice strategy is what's left when the schemas aren't there yet.

John Sweller's cognitive load theory, articulated in his 1988 paper, provides the mechanistic account. Working memory has limited capacity. Schema acquisition is what allows complex domain operations to fit within that capacity; in their absence, the operations themselves consume working memory and learning is impaired. The pedagogical implication is sharp: for genuine novices, open-ended problem-solving imposes high cognitive load that crowds out the schema acquisition needed to eventually solve such problems independently. Worked examples and well-structured direct instruction more efficiently build the schemas that, once acquired, make independent problem-solving possible.

The position that emerges across these literatures is consistent. Problem-solving in a domain is not a separable transferable skill that operates on top of domain knowledge. It is the operation of well-organized domain knowledge under conditions that require it — and the organization is principle-based, schema-mediated, and built through extended engagement with the domain. A "Problem-Solving" course that is decoupled from any specific domain is, on this view, asking students to operate a skill whose substrate they do not yet possess.

§ 3The productive-failure finding

If domain knowledge is the substrate, what produces the capacity to deploy it under novelty? The research converges on a specific developmental mechanism: repeated cycles of attempted solutions in conditions where failure is informative, followed by structured sense-making that converts the failure into transferable understanding.

Manu Kapur's program of research on productive failure has produced the most concentrated empirical investigation of this mechanism. Kapur's original 2008 paper documented a counterintuitive finding: students who struggled with ill-structured problems before receiving instruction outperformed students who received direct instruction first on measures of transfer and conceptual understanding. The struggling students' surface-level performance was worse during the intervention. Their learning was better.

The mechanism Kapur identifies is the activation of prior knowledge under the demand to generate solutions. When students are asked to solve a problem they don't yet have the canonical method for, they reach for what they do know, attempt to extend it to the new situation, and fail in characteristic ways. The failures expose conceptual gaps, generate diverse partial-solution attempts, and prime learners to attend to subsequent instruction in ways that students given direct instruction first do not. The instruction that follows the struggle is the consolidation phase; the failures encountered in the struggle become the scaffolding around which the consolidation organizes itself.

The 2×2 framework — productive/unproductive × failure/success

Kapur's 2016 conceptual paper introduces a 2×2 framework that is more useful than a simple productive/unproductive failure binary. The framework crosses two axes — failure versus success, productive versus unproductive — and produces four learning conditions:

  • Productive failure — students struggle, fail, and learn the underlying concepts deeply through the consolidation phase that follows
  • Productive success — students succeed, and the success itself produces learning (most common when direct instruction is well-matched to readiness)
  • Unproductive failure — students struggle without the consolidation that converts failure into understanding; they learn that the material is hard and they cannot do it
  • Unproductive success — students perform well on familiar problems through rote repetition without developing the underlying conceptual structure; they look competent but the learning is brittle

The two unproductive quadrants are the operationally important ones for curriculum design. Unproductive failure is the consequence of designing struggle without the supporting consolidation — Kapur's central warning. Unproductive success is the consequence the framework's depth-only lens cannot detect: a course that reaches D-5 through repetitive practice on familiar problems produces high performance and brittle understanding. A graduate of such a course can perform on known problems and freezes on novelty. This quadrant is exactly what the productive-failure audit area is designed to flag — a course with high depth but absent productive-failure conditions is the unproductive-success scenario, and the framework reads it as exactly that.

Effect sizes and the domain-general caveat

Sinha and Kapur's 2021 review in Review of Educational Research aggregates the conditions under which the productive-failure effect holds. The meta-analysis found an overall effect favoring problem-solving followed by instruction for conceptual knowledge and transfer (g ≈ 0.36 across 53 studies, 166 comparisons), with no significant advantage for procedural knowledge. The effect is strongest when the problem-solving and instruction phases are carefully designed around the same target concept. Applying this to domain-general dispositions or design judgment is a framework hypothesis, not a direct finding.

The same meta-analysis finds no significant advantage for procedural-knowledge outcomes, and the effect is moderated by design fidelity. Generalization beyond conceptual learning and near-transfer outcomes requires its own evidence.

The framework treats this as informative rather than disqualifying. The GC curriculum's core (press operations, ink, plates, prepress, color management, finishing) is substantially closer to the evidence base for Kapur's PS-I framework than open-ended design judgment — canonical solutions and concrete failure modes. Productive failure in the Kapur sense is a strong analog for these canonical technical modules — strongest where assignments target conceptual troubleshooting rather than procedural fluency, since the PS-I evidence is for conceptual knowledge and transfer, not procedural skill (Sinha & Kapur, 2021). A smaller portion of the program reaches design-judgment territory where multiple defensible solutions coexist (typography, layout, brand identity); for those modules productive struggle — critique, iteration, revision, reflective practice in the Schön tradition — is the more defensible framing.

Program-level cumulative claims (that exposure to productive-failure cycles over a four-year program builds a generalized problem-solving disposition) remain framework hypotheses. Validation path: graduate-outcome study (see graduate-outcome-validation.html). The audit area surfaces the conditions in either zone; the program-level reading interprets the scaffolding with the warrant strength appropriate to where the course sits.

Degrees, not thresholds

A 2023 paper in npj Science of Learning examined the prior-knowledge question directly: the productive-failure effect benefits lower-achieving students disproportionately if they have at least minimal relevant prior knowledge to activate. The bar for "enough prior knowledge" is not deep expertise; it is enough to generate meaningful, even if wrong, solution attempts.

The Bjork lab's "desirable difficulties" framework generalizes the finding to a broader principle. Robert Bjork's 1994 chapter laid out the underlying observation: the conditions of practice that maximize short-term performance are often different from the conditions that maximize long-term retention and transfer. Spaced practice (with intervals between sessions) suppresses in-the-moment performance but dramatically improves long-term retention. Interleaved practice (mixing problem types rather than blocked drilling on one type at a time) suppresses fluency on individual problems but improves the ability to discriminate among problem types and select the right approach. Retrieval practice (testing oneself rather than re-reading) feels less productive than passive review but produces stronger memory traces.

The unifying principle is that struggle, properly designed, is the mechanism of durable learning. Not all struggle qualifies. Productive struggle is calibrated to the learner's current capability and accompanied by the feedback or consolidation that lets the struggle resolve into understanding. Unproductive struggle — too far beyond current capability, or without the resolving phase — does not produce learning and may damage motivation.

Critically for the curriculum question: Kapur's framework does not establish a discrete threshold of prior knowledge below which productive failure becomes unproductive. The 2023 npj study showed the effect operating in degrees, with the quality of inventive production during the struggle phase being a stronger predictor of learning than starting depth. The implication the framework draws for curriculum mapping — that lower-depth productive-failure cycles are candidate contributions to problem-solving development, weighted by the domain schema available to make the failure informative — is an extrapolation beyond what Kapur, Saba, and Roll (2023) tested. That study showed inventive production predicting learning, and topic-specific prerequisite knowledge mattering; it did not test the lower end of the prior-achievement range. Higher-depth productive failure plausibly contributes more per cycle; whether lower-depth cycles accumulate into a transferable disposition across a four-year curriculum remains a framework hypothesis awaiting validation.

§ 4The reflection finding

Failure that resolves into understanding requires more than the struggle itself. Donald Schön's The Reflective Practitioner (1983) established the theoretical case that professional expertise develops through cycles of reflection-in-action (adjusting during practice) and reflection-on-action (retrospective analysis). David Kolb's Experiential Learning (1984) made the developmental cycle explicit and pedagogically operable: concrete experience leads to reflective observation, which leads to abstract conceptualization, which leads to active experimentation, which produces the next concrete experience. The cycle is the unit, not any single stage; experience without reflection does not produce abstraction, and abstraction without experimentation does not produce capability.

The most empirically grounded statement of how to operationalize reflection is the 2013 meta-analysis by Tannenbaum and Cerasoli on team and individual debriefs. Aggregating 46 studies across simulated and real settings, military and civilian populations, individual and team contexts, the authors found that structured debriefs produced approximately 25% performance improvement over control conditions, with effect sizes stable across moderator variables. The structure that worked was not elaborate. What happened, why did it happen, what would you do differently next time — applied specifically to the concrete events of the just-completed performance, applied close in time to the performance itself, applied in a way that yielded explicit takeaways. The features that distinguished effective from ineffective debriefs were not depth of psychological insight or therapeutic processing; they were specificity, timeliness, and outcome orientation.

Generic reflection prompts — "reflect on your learning," "what did you take away from this course" — do not have the same effect, and the literature is honest about why. The mechanism of effective reflection is the conversion of concrete experience into abstract principle, and that conversion requires the reflection to be anchored to the concrete details of what just happened. Reflection that floats free of the concrete cannot do the abstraction work.

For curriculum mapping, the implication is that reflection scaffolding is a separable design dimension from productive-failure conditions. A course can require productive struggle and skip the consolidation phase; a course can include reflection prompts that are too generic to convert experience into transfer. Both produce sub-optimal problem-solving development. The conditions are gradable independently and should be observed independently in the audit.

§ 5The transfer finding

The cumulative point of the literature above — domain-specificity plus productive failure plus reflection — is the development of capacity to transfer learning to novel situations. The transfer literature is the place where this becomes most directly visible.

Perkins and Salomon's 1992 chapter in the International Encyclopedia of Education distinguishes low-road transfer (near transfer, automatic, triggered by surface similarity) from high-road transfer (far transfer, deliberate, requiring conscious abstraction of principles). The deep general finding of the transfer literature is uncomfortable: transfer is hard and rarely happens without explicit scaffolding. Students who can solve a class of problems in the context where they learned them often cannot solve the same problems in a different context, especially if the new context disguises the underlying structure. Far transfer, in particular, requires students to abstract the principle that applies across contexts, and the abstraction is not automatic. It must be deliberately fostered.

This is the empirical reason why "problem-solving" as a generic course produces such poor results when measured by transfer to actual problem situations: the transfer that would make the course's content useful in graduates' work lives is exactly the kind of high-road far transfer that the research says rarely happens without explicit scaffolding around the principles that bridge the abstract content and the specific application. The scaffolding the literature recommends is exactly what the productive-failure-and-reflection cycle provides: encountering problems in domains where the student already has some schema base, failing in informative ways, and reflecting on those failures in terms that surface the transferable principles.

The Perkins and Salomon framework is also the reason for caution about the claim that domain expertise alone produces transferable problem-solving. It does not; it produces in-domain problem-solving capability. The transferable capability — what graduates draw on when they encounter genuinely new situations — comes from the meta-pattern of having done the depth + failure + reflection cycle enough times, across enough surface-varied instances of the same deep structure, that the habit of attempting before being told, failing informatively, and reflecting productively becomes itself something that transfers. This is the moderate-generalist position in the critical-thinking literature: there is a kernel of cross-domain habit, but its cultivation requires repeated domain-specific cycles to operate. (An earlier draft said "across enough domains." The evidence below sharpens this: the active ingredient is varied instances plus explicit abstraction, which a single structurally rich domain can supply — not a tour of many unrelated subjects.)

Three refinements from the transfer literature make the framework's position more precise and reconcile it with a common faculty intuition — that "developing deep problem-solving in one area, and the habit of applying it, lets students problem-solve elsewhere."

What actually transfers is readiness to learn, not cold skill. The most useful correction the literature offers is about what to look for. Bransford and Schwartz's "preparation for future learning" (1999; empirically anchored in Schwartz & Martin, 2004) argues that the standard transfer test — drop a person into a new domain and check whether prior skill fires unaided — systematically under-detects transfer, because it measures cold application. Measure instead how fast the person learns the new domain, and deep prior problem-solving shows up clearly: prepared learners extract more from each new learning opportunity than unprepared peers do. The honest mechanism behind the faculty intuition is usually not "I already knew how to do the new thing" but "I learned the new thing faster, because I knew what understanding-through-struggle feels like and what to look for." The transfer is real; it surfaces at the moment of encountering new instruction, not at the moment of blind application. (Belenky & Nokes-Malach, 2012, show the advantage is largest for learners with a mastery-approach orientation — the disposition and the readiness reinforce each other.)

The mechanism is abstraction across varied cases plus deliberate bridging — and one rich domain can supply it. What is carried to a new context is an abstract schema, and the cleanest evidence on how schemas form is Gick and Holyoak's analogical-transfer work (1980, 1983): spontaneous transfer from a single worked case is poor (≈10%, rising to only ≈30% with an explicit hint), but having learners compare two surface-different cases and articulate what they share induces a schema and roughly doubles transfer (≈50%). Gentner, Loewenstein, and Thompson (1999, 2003) replicated this in professional negotiation training. Perkins and Salomon (1989) name the design moves: low-road transfer rides on surface similarity and varied practice; high-road (far) transfer requires mindful abstraction of the principle and deliberate bridging to new contexts, and the assumption that good teaching produces it automatically they call "inordinately optimistic." The curriculum-relevant consequence: a single deep domain produces transferable problem-solving only when it is taught as a stream of surface-varied instances of the same deep structure, with that structure explicitly abstracted and bridged outward. GC's diagnostic troubleshooting — a hundred different press, ink, and color faults that share one underlying diagnostic logic — is exactly such a stream. Deep-but-narrow drilling on one instance type is the opposite: it produces Kapur's unproductive success — fluent in-context performance that does not travel.

Skill transfers poorly; disposition and readiness transfer better. Two claims that are easy to conflate should be kept apart. That cognitive skill itself generalizes across distant domains is the version the evidence rejects most firmly: a century from Thorndike and Woodworth (1901) through Barnett and Ceci's far-transfer taxonomy (2002) to Sala and Gobet's meta-analyses of chess, music, and working-memory training (2017; second-order meta-analysis, Sala et al., 2019, placing the far-transfer effect at ≈zero after correcting for bias) finds that trained skills stay near their training context. On this evidence depth increases specificity, not portability — chess masters pushed just outside their specialty reason like much weaker players (Bilalić et al., 2009). What the framework claims transfers is the narrower, better-supported thing: the disposition and the readiness to learn that a well-designed cycle of productive failure and reflection builds — not the domain skill. So the faculty intuition and the framework are not in conflict; the framework is the moderate-generalist position the intuition describes, with the conditions made explicit.

§ 6The expertise findings — and their qualifications

The deliberate-practice literature, beginning with Ericsson, Krampe, and Tesch-Römer's 1993 paper in Psychological Review, sits adjacent to the productive-failure literature and is sometimes conflated with it. Ericsson's framework identifies the conditions that distinguish ordinary practice from the kind of practice that produces expertise: focused effort at the edge of current capability, immediate feedback, repeated cycles of attempt-fail-adjust, and accumulated time on task in the tens of thousands of hours. The mechanism overlaps with productive failure — failure at the edge, feedback, adjustment — but the time horizons and the framing differ.

Two qualifications matter for the curriculum question. First, the original framework has been substantially tempered by subsequent research. Macnamara, Hambrick, and Oswald's 2014 meta-analysis found that deliberate practice explains 26% of performance variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports — but only 4% in education and less than 1% in professions. The mechanism is highly domain-dependent, and the "10,000 hours" popularization significantly overstated the original findings, particularly for the domains most relevant to undergraduate professional preparation. Second, the timescales involved in expert performance development (tens of thousands of hours, years to decades) are not achievable in undergraduate education in any case. What undergraduate curriculum can produce is not expertise but the habit of productive struggle — the disposition to attempt before being told, to fail informatively, to reflect productively, to seek feedback. That disposition is what the program can build, and it is what the deliberate-practice literature converges with the productive-failure literature on identifying as the developmental engine.

A third qualification sharpens which kind of expertise is worth building. Hatano and Inagaki's (1986) distinction between routine and adaptive expertise maps directly onto the transfer question. Routine experts are fast and accurate on practiced procedures but brittle when conditions change; adaptive experts understand why their procedures work, can mentally simulate unfamiliar cases, and invent new approaches — and they develop specifically under conditions of variation, novelty, and a culture that rewards understanding over speed, which are the same conditions productive failure and structured reflection create. The transferable kernel the program can realistically build is adaptive expertise plus its accompanying disposition; routine expertise is precisely the unproductive success the audit is designed to catch. One honesty note: the empirical demonstrations of adaptive expertise are mostly within-domain (handling novel problems in one's own field); the leap to cross-domain readiness is the preparation-for-future-learning claim above — better evidenced as accelerated learning of new domains than as cold cross-domain transfer.

§ 7Honest limits of the research case

Six honest limits on the research case as it applies to the undergraduate curriculum question.

First, the literature does not establish a clean threshold for "how much prior knowledge before productive failure becomes productive." Kapur's labs work with students who have completed prerequisite coursework or read pre-assigned material; the bar is "enough to attempt," not a specific depth value. The 2023 npj paper showed the effect operating in degrees, with depth as a contribution-weight rather than a gate. The framework treats productive failure as contributing in graded degrees rather than gated by a fixed threshold — but treats the stronger claim, that it contributes at every depth including the lowest, as an extrapolation beyond the cited evidence rather than as established.

Second, longitudinal effects across a four-year curriculum are essentially unstudied. Most productive-failure research measures outcomes weeks or months out. Whether cumulative exposure across a degree program produces graduates who are better problem-solvers four years later is an extrapolation. The framework takes the extrapolation as a working commitment for a specific reason: the underlying mechanism is well-established, the intermediate outcomes (depth distribution, productive-failure conditions, reflection scaffolding) are individually measurable, and the framework is itself designed to generate the longitudinal evidence that will eventually test the cumulative claim. Each captured snapshot is a data point about the program at a moment in time; repeated captures across semesters and graduate outcome data linked back to the snapshot record will, over years, produce exactly the longitudinal signal the literature currently lacks. The tool is the experiment.

Third, the metacognition question — whether some general habits of mind transfer across domains — is better described today as a qualified yes under conditions than as fully open. Metacognitive- and self-regulated-learning-strategy instruction shows reliable cross-domain effects in meta-analysis (Donker et al., 2014), and the preparation-for-future-learning and adaptive-expertise constructs give the cross-domain readiness claim a real evidence base. The load-bearing caveat is that none of this transfer is automatic: it appears when the habit is explicitly cultivated — varied cases, abstraction, deliberate bridging, reflective control (Schoenfeld, 1985, 1992; Perkins & Salomon, 1989; Veenman et al., 2006) — and largely fails to appear from depth or content mastery alone. So the framework's working commitment is now better supported than when first written, with the qualification sharpened: it structures data around domain-bounded K/U/D and treats cross-domain habit as emergent from the cumulative pattern given the abstraction-and-bridging conditions. It would still tell us if it were wrong — a program that satisfies the structural conditions and still produces graduates with weak problem-solving competence is evidence the account of the mechanism is incomplete.

Fourth, the failure-confidence connection is theoretically appealing but thin in direct evidence. The faculty intuition that productive-failure experiences build confidence and self-efficacy as a consequence is plausible and consistent with the productive-failure literature, but the mechanism is not well-studied independently. Albert Bandura's self-efficacy framework actually suggests that mastery experiences — succeeding after effort — build self-efficacy more reliably than failure does, and the productive-failure literature is best read as saying that productive failure works by producing understanding that emerges from struggle, which is then followed by mastery experiences, which then build confidence. The chain is more indirect than "failure builds confidence."

Fifth, generalizing across disciplinary cultures requires care. The productive-failure paradigm has been most extensively studied in mathematics and science contexts where canonical solutions exist and failure modes are relatively well-defined. For Graphic Communications, this caveat applies narrowly: the program's core (press, ink, plates, prepress, color management, finishing) is settled technical knowledge under exactly those conditions. The smaller portion of horizontal-knowledge work (typography, layout, brand judgment) does need the softer framing of productive struggle rather than productive failure, but it is adjunct, not the curriculum's character. The framework should be applied with humility about empirical reach; the reach covers the bulk of what GC students actually do.

Sixth, the framework's founding intuition is itself partly grounded in personal experience of transfer — a faculty member who became a deep problem-solver in one area and found the capability useful elsewhere — and personal experience is a trustworthy guide to the phenomenon but an unreliable guide to the mechanism. Three well-established effects push the same way. Introspective reports of one's own cognitive processes are reconstructed after the fact, not read off directly (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977), so "I used a general problem-solving skill" can be the feeling of deploying domain knowledge one cannot observe oneself using (the curse of knowledge; Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber, 1989). Selection confounds the inference: people who go deep in one domain and succeed in others are disproportionately high in general ability, conscientiousness, and need for cognition — traits that independently predict success in any new domain (deliberate practice explains only a minority of expertise variance; Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald, 2014). And survivorship hides the disconfirming cases — those who went deep and did not transfer do not write frameworks. None of this makes the intuition wrong; it means a curriculum cannot rest on the phenomenological claim alone. The design must specify the conditions that reproduce the effect in a median student — which is exactly why this document locates the transferable element in cultivated disposition + readiness + abstraction-and-bridging, rather than in depth as such.

Part II — The framework's response

The cumulative position the framework takes is that problem-solving is real, valued, and measurable — but not as a single transferable skill. It is a program-level emergent property that arises from the interaction of three things, each of which can be measured at the appropriate scale, and the cumulative pattern of which can be aggregated to a program-level opportunity/risk read.

This section operationalizes that position into the curriculum-mapping tool's data shape. Three operational claims structure the framework's response.

§ 8Claim 1 — The KUD+ depth scale encodes problem-solving facets within domains

The Do dimension's upper-range anchors are not abstract scale-tops. They are explicit definitions of what problem-solving competence looks like within a domain:

  • Do-4 — Adapts performance to new conditions or constraints
  • Do-5 — Performs creatively with critical judgment; can guide others

These descriptors map directly onto the transferable-capability targets of the cognitive-science literature. D-4 names transfer to novel conditions, which is precisely the capacity the Perkins-Salomon transfer framework identifies as the hard goal of education. D-5 names creative judgment and the ability to teach, which is the marker Chi's expert-novice work identifies as evidence of principle-level rather than surface-level understanding. (Teaching others requires articulating the principles, which surface-level knowledge cannot do.) The Understand dimension's upper-range anchors do similar work:

  • Understand-4 — Reasons through novel cases not previously seen
  • Understand-5 — Critiques the principle, identifies limits, extends to new domains

U-4 and U-5 describe the metacognitive capacity to operate principles under novelty and to know where they break down. This is the moderate-generalist position made operational: the cross-domain habit of mind that can transfer is the habit of reasoning through novelty and critiquing principles, and it is observable at the upper depths of the Understand dimension within each technical competency the program develops.

The operational consequence is that the framework does not need a separate "Problem-Solving" measurement. The depth scales already encode the problem-solving surface — at the right scale, which is depth-within-domain rather than free-floating-skill. The Program Coverage Matrix, which shows confirmed snapshots × career-target sub-competencies with K/U/D depth values, can be read as an upper-depth opportunity read — depth alone — when filtered to the upper-range depths. The matrix shows where the program reaches D-4 and D-5; it shows where it does not. The upper-depth opportunity map in the matrix view makes this surface visible by emphasizing the upper depths and de-emphasizing the lower depths in a graded rather than binary way.

This is also where a Do-bearing framework earns its keep over Bloom's taxonomy — the obvious alternative for anyone reaching for “higher-order thinking.” Bloom's revised hierarchy is purely cognitive: Remember through Create are operations of thought, and it carries no behavioral or performance axis. There is, in Bloom, no place to record what a student can do under novel conditions, accumulating across a sequence of courses — which is exactly the evidence problem-solving development consists of here. Worse, the common move of equating problem-solving with Bloom's upper levels (Analyze, Evaluate, Create) re-imports the free-floating-skill assumption the research in Part I dismantles: it treats problem-solving as a transferable cognitive level a single assessment can certify, rather than a domain-bound disposition built by repeated doing-under-difficulty. The KUD+ Do and Understand depth scales exist precisely to put that disposition on an axis a curriculum map can see.

§ 9Claim 2 — The CourseCapture audit surfaces productive-failure and reflection conditions per course

Depth alone is not sufficient. A course can reach D-5 through repetitive practice on familiar problems and never develop transferable problem-solving capacity in students; graduates of such a course can perform on known problems but freeze when novelty arrives. This is exactly Kapur's "unproductive success" quadrant from §3 — high performance, brittle learning — and the framework's depth-only view would not detect it. The literature on transfer is broadly consistent on this point: depth-without-pedagogy produces in-domain competence without the conditions that make it transferable.

The audit area added to CourseCapture today probes for the conditions that make the depth productive. The probes are four research-backed audit conditions plus one framework depth-weighting signal:

  1. Generate-then-consolidate structure — does any assignment require students to attempt a solution before being taught the canonical method? This is Kapur's two-phase unit.
  2. Open-ended ill-structured problems — does the course assign problems where the canonical answer is not pre-given and multiple defensible solutions exist? This is the substrate that makes generation-before-instruction possible.
  3. Revision cycles with consequential failure — does the course require students to respond to specific identified failures with substantive revision? This is the mechanism by which failure becomes informative.
  4. Structured post-mortem — does the course require students to articulate what happened, why, and what to do differently — tied to concrete failure modes rather than as generic reflection? This is the reflection scaffolding that converts experience to transfer.
  5. Domain depth that supports productive struggle — what is the highest K/U/D depth reached in the course's strongest technical competencies? This grades the contribution rather than gating it: productive failure at D-1 contributes less than productive failure at D-4, but each cycle is a candidate site for building dispositional habit; whether this accumulates into a generalized disposition across a four-year program is a framework hypothesis that the graduate-outcome validation is designed to test.

A sixth condition is implied by the transfer research but is not yet probed, and is flagged here as a candidate addition rather than a shipped capability: abstraction-and-bridging — does the course require students to abstract a principle across multiple surface-varied cases and apply it to a genuinely new context? This is the active ingredient the analogical-transfer literature identifies as what makes problem-solving transfer at all (Gick & Holyoak, 1983; Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson, 2003; Perkins & Salomon, 1989): comparing varied cases to induce a schema, then deliberately bridging it outward. The current five probes capture productive failure and reflection well but are largely silent on whether a course does this abstraction-and-bridging work — which is precisely what converts deep in-domain habit into something that travels. Adding it would be the highest-value research-driven extension of the audit. It is deferred pending its own design pass, which must distinguish it cleanly from probe 2 (open-ended ill-structured problems concerns the problem; abstraction-and-bridging concerns reasoning across problems and toward new contexts).

Each condition is observed as graded: present, partial, or absent for the qualitative conditions; a numeric depth for the supporting depth signal. The findings are surfaced into the snapshot's audit-notes structure alongside prerequisite gaps and objective misalignments. They do not modify the K/U/D depth values themselves. They are read by program-level analyses that aggregate the conditions across the curriculum to produce a program-level problem-solving opportunity/risk map — an indication of where the conditions for formation are present or absent, not a validated diagnostic that formation occurred.

§ 10Claim 3 — The conditions for problem-solving are read at the program level

No single course produces a problem-solver. The temptation to ask "does GC 3460 develop problem-solving?" is structural confusion: problem-solving is not a course-level property. It is a property of what the program collectively does across the four-year curriculum.

The program-level opportunity/risk read the framework produces (a read of conditions, not a validated measure of formation) is structured across three questions:

  • Does the curriculum reach the upper-range depths in the right domains? The Program Coverage Matrix's upper-depth opportunity map (live now) shows the U/D upper-depth distribution across the program. A career target whose sub-competencies require D-4 and D-5 development but the curriculum tops out at D-2 is a problem-solving gap, even if every cell is "covered" at some depth. The lens highlights this immediately.
  • Are productive-failure conditions placed where the depth progression makes them productive? The Phase 1B Scaffolding Analysis (Phase 1B Stage 1 shipped 2026-05-28; Stage 2 pending) reads the sequence of productive-failure conditions across courses ordered by program position. The diagnostic asks whether the conditions are introduced in courses where prior coursework has built enough depth for the failure to be productive — graded by depth weight rather than gated by threshold.
  • Is reflection scaffolding present in the courses where the cumulative pattern should consolidate? Upper-division courses, capstones, and integrating projects are where the developmental pattern should bring the cumulative productive-failure exposure into transferable habit. The Phase 1B analysis flags upper-division courses without structured-post-mortem conditions as a load-bearing absence.

A program that satisfies all three structural conditions should, by the framework's hypothesis, create stronger conditions for graduates to attempt novel domain problems, fail informatively, and converge on understanding — which is what employers mean when they say their graduates need to be better problem-solvers. Whether this pattern produces measurable graduate-level problem-solving gains is an empirical question for the validation plan. A program that satisfies one or two but not all three should, on the same reasoning, produce a weaker pattern that the employer signal would reflect; this too is hypothesis, not finding.

Part III — The implementation

§ 11The end-to-end chain

The operational chain that produces the program-level problem-solving opportunity/risk read — a diagnostic of conditions, not yet a validated diagnostic of formation — in implementation order:

  1. Per-course capture. An instructor opens CourseCapture for one of their courses. The audit chat covers eight systematic areas including Area 7 (Productive failure and reflection conditions). The instructor responds turn by turn, citing specific assignments and rubrics. The auditor surfaces what conditions are present, partial, or absent and at what depths the conditions occur.
  2. Snapshot generation. When the instructor clicks Generate Course Outcome Profile, the scoring layer reads the audit transcript plus the course materials and emits a structured snapshot. The snapshot includes the standard K/U/D depths per competency and now also includes a productive_failure_conditions block in audit_notes: the four qualitative conditions (each present/partial/absent), the max supporting depth, and short notes citing specific assignments. The snapshot is immutable once saved.
  3. Per-target scoring. The Phase 1A Program Coverage Matrix scorer runs (on demand) against each (snapshot × career target) pair, producing per-sub-competency K/U/D depth values. The conservative scoring discipline established in the Manning-encoded scorer ensures depths are not inflated by topic overlap or aspirational language.
  4. Program-level reading. The Program Coverage Matrix's upper-depth opportunity map reads the resulting coverage cells and produces the upper-depth distribution: across all snapshots that contribute to each sub-competency, where does the program reach D-4 or D-5? Per target, the rollup is visible at a glance. (The lens currently reads depth alone; the sequencing question is answered by Phase 1B.)
  5. Scaffolding analysis (Phase 1B, Stage 1 shipped 2026-05-28). The Scaffolding Analysis (Stage 1, live) reads each snapshot's productive_failure_conditions together with its K/U/D depth values, aggregates across the program-sequence order, and produces three views: the per-target scaffolding strip (which courses contribute what, in what order), the brittle-scaffold list (where the depth progression and pedagogical scaffolding fail to match), and the course-contribution summary (which courses are doing disproportionate problem-solving formation work).

Each step is independently shippable and useful. The CourseCapture audit-area addition is the first to produce data; the matrix lens is immediately readable from existing data. Phase 1B will become useful as the snapshot corpus accumulates the productive-failure findings.

Part IV — Limits and what to watch for

§ 12What the framework does not do

Several positions to make explicit, both to constrain over-claiming and to surface what should remain on faculty agendas rather than be delegated to the tool.

The framework does not produce a single "problem-solving score" per graduate or per course. The right diagnostic is the program-level pattern: depth distribution, productive-failure-condition distribution, reflection scaffolding presence. A summary score would falsely simplify a property whose value lies in the pattern.

The framework does not treat Problem-Solving as a foundational competency, and the reason is conceptual before it is operational. The five baseline foundationals (Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication) are behavioral dispositions: a student exhibits them or doesn't, in whatever domain the course operates, and the per-course question is whether the conditions to develop the disposition were present. Problem-solving is not that kind of construct. It cannot be observed in the absence of domain content — a student who "exhibits problem-solving" in a course where they have K=1, U=0 is exhibiting flailing, not problem-solving. The capability requires the domain substrate, the productive-failure conditions, and the reflection scaffolding cumulatively across the program. The construct lives at the program level, not at the course level, and it is constitutively domain-bound rather than domain-independent in the way the foundational dispositions are. Treating it as a foundational would force the framework to score it in every course on the same dimension that scores Resilience and Communication, which would mis-encode the construct. As a secondary consequence, the data signal would also be polluted by every-course D-1-or-2 inflations that do not in fact contribute to problem-solving development — but the construct-level reason is the primary one, and the operational hygiene follows from it.

Problem-solving competence vs. problem-framing behaviors. A fair objection: faculty do observe students getting better at approaching ambiguous tasks — decomposing an ill-defined problem, asking clarifying questions, testing assumptions, distinguishing symptoms from causes, seeking feedback, escalating appropriately — before they have deep domain knowledge, and that is not mere flailing. The resolution sharpens rather than weakens the position: distinguish problem-solving competence (domain-bound, program-level — the subject of this document) from problem-framing behaviors (genuinely cross-domain, course-observable, lighter-weight). The former is what employers ultimately need and what the framework maps the conditions for; the latter are real and are already partly captured inside the foundational dispositions — Agency ≈ ownership/initiative, Curiosity ≈ asking questions, Communication ≈ surfacing constraints to a manager — and worth naming so the distinction is explicit. The framework does not add a separate captured "problem-framing" dimension on present evidence (it would overlap the existing foundationals and re-open the categorization the framework deliberately closed); instead it treats professional problem-framing (clarifying goals, constraints, stakeholders, tradeoffs) as something that should be expressible as a first-class competency or sub-competency where a career target demands it, not as a purely technical residue. This also names the corresponding gap on the demand side: "problem-solving," as employers use the word, blends technical-diagnostic troubleshooting, adaptive transfer to unfamiliar constraints, and professional problem-framing. The framework is strongest on the first two; it keeps the third visible through the foundationals and the sub-competency vocabulary rather than letting it vanish into a single undifferentiated "problem-solving" label.

The framework does not claim that productive failure is the only mechanism of problem-solving development. The literature is honest about multiple contributing factors (intrinsic capability, coaching, time on task, deliberate practice, exposure to expertise) and about its own limits (limited research on longitudinal undergraduate effects, contested metacognition findings, varying applicability across disciplinary cultures). The framework operationalizes the mechanism best-evidenced for technical domains (the GC core) — and the productive-struggle tradition for the smaller portion of design-judgment work — into curriculum mapping; it does not claim to capture every contributor, and the program-level cumulative claim remains a framework hypothesis.

The framework does not replace faculty judgment about which courses should develop which competencies at which depths. It surfaces patterns; faculty deliberate; the curriculum changes through committee work informed by the patterns. The tool's role is to make legible what is otherwise tacit, not to make decisions that humans should make.

§ 13What to watch for as data accumulates

Two empirical questions that will become answerable as the snapshot corpus grows.

First, do the productive-failure conditions cluster predictably? Theory suggests that courses with strong generate-then-consolidate structure tend to also have strong revision cycles (both are signatures of an instructor who designs around productive struggle), while courses with structured post-mortem may or may not have those upstream conditions. If the empirical clustering surprises us, it will be informative about how the conditions actually develop in practice.

Second, do the upper-range depth distributions and the productive-failure scaffolding correlate at the program level? If they do, the integration is straightforward. If they diverge — programs reach D-5 in courses without productive-failure scaffolding, or have rich productive-failure conditions in courses that never reach D-4 — the divergence itself is the curriculum-design finding worth surfacing.

The framework's claim is that both must be present, sequenced correctly, and cumulating across the curriculum for graduates to develop transferable problem-solving capability. The data will tell us, over time, where each program actually stands.

The tool as research instrument

This framework's central commitment is one that current research cannot fully verify: that cumulative productive-failure exposure plus depth plus reflection produces transferable problem-solving capability four years out. That commitment is the framework's hypothesis, expressed as design. The tool is built to test it. As snapshot data accumulates, as graduates are tracked into the workforce, as employer signals are correlated back to program patterns, the framework either accumulates evidence for itself or surfaces the divergence that will require revision. In the meantime, the commitments are defensible — well-grounded in mechanism, consistent with the established research, and structured to fail informatively if they're wrong.
