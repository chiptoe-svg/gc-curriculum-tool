# Donker, de Boer, Kostons, Dignath-van Ewijk & van der Werf (2014) — Learning Strategy Instruction Meta-Analysis

**Full citation:** Donker, A. S., de Boer, H., Kostons, D., Dignath-van Ewijk, C. C., & van der Werf, M. P. C. (2014). Effectiveness of learning strategy instruction on academic performance: A meta-analysis. *Educational Research Review*, 11, 1–26. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.edurev.2013.11.002. Quantifies how much teaching cognitive, metacognitive, management, and motivational strategies improves student performance, and which strategies matter most.

**What the doc claims it says:** "Self-regulated/metacognitive strategy instruction improves performance across domains when taught with when/why/how (conditional) framing."

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The report is a meta-analysis of 95 strategy-instruction interventions in primary and secondary education across four subject domains (reading comprehension, writing, mathematics, science). The aim was to determine not just *whether* strategy instruction works but *which* strategies, and under which conditions, produce the largest gains. Strategies were coded into four families — cognitive (elaboration, rehearsal, organization), metacognitive (planning/prediction, monitoring/control, evaluation/reflection, plus general and personal metacognitive knowledge), management (effort, environment, peers/others), and motivational (self-efficacy, task value, goal orientation). Effects were estimated with Comprehensive Meta-Analysis and Hierarchical Linear Modeling, using the measurement instrument (self-developed vs. intervention-independent test) as a covariate throughout.

The headline result: across all 95 interventions, strategy instruction produced an average weighted effect of **Hedges' g = 0.66** (SE ≈ 0.05; 95% CI 0.56–0.76), a medium-to-high effect (§6.2). Heterogeneity was high (I² = 78.6%), so the report drilled into moderators. Trim-and-fill found no publication bias (Rosenthal's fail-safe N = 5196).

By subject domain (Table 4), effects were largest for **writing (g = 1.25)**, then **science (g = 0.73)** and **mathematics (g = 0.66)**, and smallest for **reading comprehension (g = 0.36)** and "other" subjects (g = 0.23). The differences were significant; writing was significantly higher than all others even after controlling for measurement instrument.

On individual strategies (Table 8, meta-regression with each strategy vs. its absence), four strategies produced significantly higher effects: **general metacognitive knowledge (B = 0.31, p < .01)**, **metacognitive planning and prediction (B = 0.20, p < .05)**, the cognitive strategy **rehearsal (B = 0.42, p < .01)**, and the motivational strategy **task value (B = 0.94, p < .01)** — task value had by far the largest positive impact. Notably, **goal orientation had a significant *negative* coefficient (B = −0.35, p < .05)**. "Personal" metacognitive knowledge, monitoring/control, evaluation/reflection, elaboration, organization, and the management strategies were not significant predictors on their own.

When the significant strategies were entered simultaneously (Table 9), general metacognitive knowledge (B = 0.25), planning and prediction (B = 0.17), and task value (B = 0.81) remained significant; rehearsal dropped out and goal orientation stayed negative. The report concludes that the most effective package combines **general metacognitive knowledge + planning/prediction + task value**, with an expected added value of **Hedges' g = 1.23** (sum of the three coefficients).

Mechanistically, the report frames metacognition as the controller of self-regulated learning, and (drawing on Flavell, Alexander et al.) treats metacognitive knowledge as the "knowing why/when/how" that lets learners deploy cognitive strategies appropriately — consistent with the conditional ("when/why/how") framing in the doc's claim. It also notes that longer interventions had slightly *smaller* effects, and that grade level did not moderate outcomes.

**Verdict:** Consistent — with one important sourcing caveat. The doc's claim that metacognitive/self-regulated strategy instruction improves performance across domains is well supported: the cross-domain average is g = 0.66 and gains appear in all four subjects (though with a wide spread, 0.36–1.25). The "when/why/how (conditional) framing" emphasis maps onto the report's finding that *general metacognitive knowledge* and *planning/prediction* are among the few strategies that independently boost effects, and onto its theoretical framing of metacognition as conditional procedural knowledge. The only material caveat is provenance: the held PDF is the open GION project-report version (*Effective Strategies for Self-regulated Learning*, 2012), not the *Educational Research Review* (2014) article named in the citation — the dataset and effect sizes match, but author order and title differ, and citations to specific pages should be made against the report. One nuance the doc should not overstate: task value (a motivational strategy), not a metacognitive strategy, carried the single largest coefficient, and goal orientation actually hurt — so "metacognition alone" undersells the role of motivation in the strongest-performing package.
