# Donker et al. (2014) — Learning Strategy Instruction Meta-Analysis

**Full citation:** Donker, A. S., de Boer, H., Kostons, D., Dignath van Ewijk, C. C., & van der Werf, M. P. C. (2014). Effectiveness of Learning Strategy Instruction on Academic Performance: A Meta-Analysis. *Educational Research Review*, 11, 1–26. A meta-analysis of 58 studies and 95 interventions in primary and secondary education examining which cognitive, metacognitive, management, and motivational strategy instruction approaches most effectively improve academic performance.

**What the background doc claims it says:** "Self-regulated/metacognitive strategy instruction improves performance across domains when taught with when/why/how (conditional) framing." The citation supports the argument in §4 that effective strategy instruction requires conditional knowledge — knowing not just how to execute a strategy but when and why to invoke it — as the mechanism through which metacognitive engagement lifts performance.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The meta-analysis synthesises 58 studies (95 interventions, 180 effect sizes) from primary and secondary education, covering four broad strategy categories: cognitive strategies (elaboration, organisation, rehearsal), metacognitive strategies (planning, monitoring, evaluation), management strategies (time, environment, effort regulation), and motivational components (task value, self-efficacy). Effect sizes are reported as Hedges' *g*. Domain-level mean effects range from Hedges' *g* = 0.36 (reading) to *g* = 0.66 (mathematics), *g* = 0.73 (science), and *g* = 1.25 (writing), with an overall mean in the *g* = 0.40–0.71 range depending on the domain. The highest single-strategy effects are associated with planning and task-value instruction.

The conditional-knowledge finding is the paper's central moderator result. Interventions that embedded explicit *metacognitive knowledge* — teaching students not merely which strategy to apply and the mechanical steps (declarative + procedural knowledge) but also *when* and *why* to use a strategy (conditional knowledge) — produced significantly larger effects than strategy training alone. This "informed strategy instruction" framing increases metacognitive engagement and is effective across all subject domains examined. The paper further identifies that effects are inflated when outcome tests are designed by the intervention researchers themselves relative to independent tests, a methodological caveat worth noting.

The strategy categories, coverage, and the centrality of the conditional-knowledge moderator are consistent across sources: the first-search synthesis, the Groningen repository abstract, and the Semantic Scholar page all converge on the same finding. There is no indication the paper is disputed or retracted.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the background.html gloss accurately captures the paper's scope (meta-analysis of learning-strategy instruction across domains) and its key moderator (conditional "when/why/how" framing lifts effectiveness). Effect sizes ranging from *g* = 0.36 to *g* = 1.25 by domain confirm "improves performance across domains." The one nuance the gloss elides is that highest effects come from a combination of conditional knowledge *plus* planning instruction *plus* task-value components, not from conditional framing alone — but the gloss is a one-sentence citation note, not a full summary, and what it asserts is accurate.
