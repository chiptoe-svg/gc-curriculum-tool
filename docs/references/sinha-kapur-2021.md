# Sinha & Kapur (2021) — When Problem Solving Followed by Instruction Works

**Full citation:** Sinha, T., & Kapur, M. (2021). When Problem Solving Followed by Instruction Works: Evidence for Productive Failure. *Review of Educational Research*, 91(5), 761–798. Review of conditions under which the productive-failure effect holds.

**What the background doc claims it says:** The 2021 meta-analytic review found an overall effect favoring problem-solving followed by instruction for conceptual knowledge and transfer (g ≈ 0.36 across 53 studies, 166 comparisons), with no significant advantage for procedural knowledge. The evidence is strongest when the problem-solving and instruction phases are carefully designed around the same target concept.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** This systematic review and meta-analysis examined 53 studies and 166 comparisons that compared problem-solving followed by instruction (PS-I) designs with instruction followed by problem-solving (I-PS) designs across STEM and related disciplines. The central research question: does the sequence matter — specifically, does solving problems before receiving instruction produce better learning than the conventional order (instruction first, then problems)?

Key quantitative findings: The overall meta-analytic effect favored PS-I over I-PS with Hedges' g = 0.36 (95% CI: 0.20–0.51), a significant moderate effect. When restricted to studies implementing PS-I with high fidelity to the principles of Productive Failure specifically (not just any problem-first sequence), effect sizes were stronger (Hedges' g ranging from 0.37 to 0.58). This distinction matters: not every PS-I design produces equivalent effects; the design must embody the specific PF principles (generating multiple solutions, activation of prior knowledge, consolidation phase using student failures).

Boundary conditions identified: The PS-I advantage was stronger for older students (middle school and above; younger students in grades 2–5 showed the reverse pattern favoring I-PS), for conceptual knowledge and transfer outcomes (but NOT for procedural knowledge — no significant advantage), and when studies used well-designed problems and genuine consolidation phases. Domain-general skills showed no advantage for PS-I over I-PS.

The "no significant advantage for procedural knowledge" finding is important and is correctly cited in the background doc. The productive failure effect is specifically a conceptual and transfer effect, not a route to faster procedural acquisition.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's specific claims are accurate: g ≈ 0.36 across 53 studies, 166 comparisons is confirmed (the exact figure is 0.36 with 95% CI 0.20–0.51). The "no significant advantage for procedural knowledge" finding is correctly noted. The doc's qualifier — "the evidence is strongest when the problem-solving and instruction phases are carefully designed around the same target concept" — is consistent with the meta-analysis's boundary conditions. This is one of the load-bearing references in §4 and the numbers check out.
