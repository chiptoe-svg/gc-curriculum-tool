**What it actually says (synthesis):** The Tuning Educational Structures in Europe project (Phases 1 and 2: 2001–2004, with subsequent phases through 2008) was an EU-funded initiative to create shared reference points for degree programs across European higher education, enabling transparency and comparability while preserving institutional autonomy. The editors González (University of Deusto, Spain) and Wagenaar (University of Groningen, Netherlands) coordinated contributions from faculty across hundreds of European institutions.

The project's central conceptual distinction is between *competences* (the holistic capacities that students develop through their education — combining knowledge, skills, and attitudes in an integrated ability) and *learning outcomes* (the statements of what a learner is expected to know, understand, and be able to demonstrate after completion of a learning experience). Learning outcomes describe the level of competence achieved; competence describes the capacity itself. This distinction is significant because it forces curriculum designers to specify the depth and quality of learning expected, not merely its presence or absence.

Tuning classifies competencies into two types: subject-specific competencies (the skills and knowledge proper to a particular field) and generic competencies (common to graduates of all disciplines, such as analytical ability, communication, teamwork, and ethical judgment). The project conducted extensive employer and graduate surveys to identify which generic and subject-specific competencies are most valued, and produced reference-point profiles for degree programs in numerous disciplines.

The competency-profile approach organizes a degree program around a compact set of competency descriptors at different levels of attainment, rather than a comprehensive topic list. This allows cross-institutional comparison: two institutions' programs can be evaluated against the same profile even if their courses differ in content delivery.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization of the Tuning project is accurate: the project does distinguish competence from learning outcome and does provide a compact-and-leveled competency-profile approach. The doc's description of Tuning as "bureaucratically heavy" for single-department scale is a practical judgment, not a claim about what the project says — and it is reasonable. The claim that Tuning "influences the framework's treatment of program-level competency descriptors" is a consistency relationship, correctly labeled as such.
