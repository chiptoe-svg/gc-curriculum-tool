**What it actually says (synthesis):** *Teaching for Quality Learning at University* is a widely-used higher education textbook that expands the constructive alignment framework from the 1996 article into a comprehensive guide for university instructors. The 4th edition (2011) draws on Biggs and Tang's consulting experience implementing constructive alignment across Australia, Hong Kong, Ireland, and Malaysia, incorporating a wider range of disciplines and teaching contexts.

The book applies the SOLO (Structure of the Observed Learning Outcome) taxonomy as the primary tool for grading the sophistication of learning outcomes. SOLO describes five levels: Prestructural (task not addressed), Unistructural (one aspect addressed), Multistructural (many aspects but unconnected), Relational (aspects integrated into a coherent whole), and Extended Abstract (generalized to new domains). This taxonomy plays the same role for Biggs that Bloom's taxonomy plays for others — it provides a hierarchical vocabulary for specifying outcome depth — but SOLO is specifically designed to describe the structure of a student's response, not the type of cognitive process demanded by a task.

The book's practical guidance covers how to write intended learning outcomes (ILOs) at appropriate SOLO levels, how to align teaching and learning activities to elicit those outcomes, and how to design assessments that directly test the stated ILOs. Part 3 provides discipline-specific examples across STEM, social sciences, and professional fields.

Biggs and Tang also address the institutional dimension of constructive alignment: how to implement it at program and departmental levels, how to handle the interaction between individual course ILOs and program-level graduate attributes, and how to build faculty capacity to write and assess aligned outcomes.

**Verdict:** Consistent with its position in the reference list. The book is a well-established extension of the 1996 constructive alignment framework. The background doc cites both works together as the theoretical grounding for the audit's mismatch-detection function; this is appropriate given that the book provides the practical curriculum-design vocabulary (SOLO levels, ILO writing, assessment design) that operationalizes the 1996 principle.
