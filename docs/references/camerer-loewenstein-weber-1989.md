# Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber (1989) — The Curse of Knowledge

**Full citation:** Camerer, C., Loewenstein, G., & Weber, M. (1989). **What it actually says (synthesis):**

The paper studies a specific judgmental bias in settings of asymmetric information. Standard economic models of asymmetric information assume better-informed agents can accurately reproduce, or "anticipate," the judgments of less-informed agents — a seemingly innocuous assumption the authors set out to test. They show it is systematically violated: people who possess private information are *unable to ignore it* when asked to predict what someone *without* that information would judge. They call this the "curse of knowledge." Formally, an agent with the richer information set I₁ who must forecast the forecast of an agent with the poorer information set I₀ — i.e., estimate E[E(X|I₀)|I₁] — over-weights their own information and overestimates how much the less-informed agent knows. The bias has two consequences: better-informed agents can actually suffer losses (more information can hurt), and, paradoxically, the bias can *mitigate* market failures from asymmetric information (e.g., a seller who can't fully suppress knowledge of a defect prices the "lemon" lower), so individual irrationality can enhance collective rationality.

The novel contribution is moving the curse out of the psychology lab (where prior evidence, e.g., Fischhoff's hindsight work, came from incentive-free individual judgment) and into incentivized economic *market* experiments to see whether financial incentives, feedback, and market discipline make it disappear. In the main design, a first group of 51 subjects predicted the actual 1980 earnings of eight real companies given financial histories; a second, *informed* group was told the true earnings and then had to predict the first group's (uninformed) predictions, with cash incentives for accuracy (e.g., $1 per estimate within 10% of the target). The informed predictors could not set aside what they knew: their forecasts of the naïve forecasts were pulled toward the true earnings they had been shown. The key quantitative result is that the curse persisted under incentives and feedback, and that **market forces reduced its magnitude by approximately 50 percent but did not eliminate it** — feedback alone had little effect.

The mechanism is an inability to *un-know*: once information is held, it contaminates the attempt to simulate a mind that lacks it, and the contamination operates below the level of deliberate control, which is why incentives to ignore it don't work. This is the bridge to the document's §7 argument. The curse of knowledge is direct experimental evidence that people cannot accurately introspect on, or factor out, the influence of knowledge they already possess. Applied by analogy to expert self-report, an expert solving a problem is steeped in domain knowledge they can no longer "see" themselves using; when they explain their solution they cannot subtract that knowledge to recover what they are actually doing, so the deployment of dense, automatic domain knowledge gets *misattributed* to a generic, content-free faculty — "I just used logic / general problem-solving." The feeling of exercising a general skill is, on this reading, partly the phenomenological signature of fluent domain knowledge that is invisible to its own owner.

**Verdict:** Partially inconsistent — but only in the narrow sense that the document is applying the construct *by analogy*, which it openly flags. The paper's literal claim is about predicting others' less-informed judgments in market settings, not about introspecting on one's own problem-solving. The bias it documents (inability to ignore/factor out one's own knowledge, robust to incentives, only halved by markets) is genuine and accurately reported. The extension — that this same un-know-ability explains why experts misdescribe domain-knowledge deployment as a "general skill" — is a reasonable and well-motivated analogy, and it pairs naturally with Nisbett & Wilson (1977) on the introspection side, but it is an inference the 1989 paper does not itself make. The document handles this honestly by labeling it an analogy; cited that way, the use is sound. It would be an overstatement only if the curse-of-knowledge paper were presented as *direct* evidence about expert self-report rather than as a transferable mechanism.
