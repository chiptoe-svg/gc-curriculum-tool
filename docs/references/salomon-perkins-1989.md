# Salomon & Perkins (1989) — Rocky Roads to Transfer

**Full citation:** Salomon, G., & Perkins, D. N. (1989). Rocky Roads to Transfer: Rethinking Mechanisms of a Neglected Phenomenon. *Educational Psychologist*, 24(2), 113–142. The foundational statement of the low-road / high-road distinction: transfer is not unitary but proceeds by two distinct mechanisms, and far transfer requires mindful abstraction plus deliberate bridging rather than occurring automatically.

**What the doc claims it says:** Low-road (surface-triggered) vs. high-road (mindful abstraction + deliberate bridging) transfer; far transfer is not an automatic byproduct of good teaching. The deep-dive §5 adds that Perkins & Salomon "name the design moves: low-road transfer rides on surface similarity and varied practice; high-road (far) transfer requires mindful abstraction of the principle and deliberate bridging to new contexts," and they call the assumption that good teaching produces far transfer automatically "inordinately optimistic."

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The paper's stated aim is to confront a body of contradictory transfer findings (the LOGO programming debates, learning-strategy training, literacy studies) and to explain the paradoxes by separating the *how* of transfer — the internal mechanisms — from the *what* — the kind of knowledge or skill that gets transferred. Salomon and Perkins argue that "transfer is not at all a unitary phenomenon"; rather it "can occur by different routes dependent on different mechanisms," and they organize these into two classes, the low road and the high road. The abstract states the thesis directly: "Low-road transfer depends on extensive, varied practice and occurs by the automatic triggering of well-learned behavior in a new context. High-road transfer occurs by intentional mindful abstraction of something from one context and application in a new context."

**Low-road transfer.** This route "primarily reflects extended practice; distance of transfer depends on amount of practice and the variability of contexts in which the practice has occurred." The mechanism is automatic: a well-learned routine fires when the new situation is perceptually similar enough to the practice contexts to trigger it. Their illustrative case is driving — moving from car to truck "is quite painless and automatic; your old skills only require some fine tuning." Crucially, low-road transfer does not require conscious recognition of an analogy; it is stimulus-driven, and its reach is limited by how varied the original practice was. This is the route that "rides on surface similarity and varied practice," exactly as the deep-dive paraphrases.

**High-road transfer.** The high road "depends on the mindful abstracting of knowledge from a context." Salomon and Perkins name its hallmark "mindful abstraction," defined as "the deliberate, usually metacognitively guided and effortful, decontextualization of a principle, main idea, strategy, or procedure, which then becomes a candidate for transfer." They split it into two sub-types: *forward-reaching* transfer (one abstracts a principle in anticipation of later, not-yet-known applications) and *backward-reaching* transfer (one meets a novel situation and deliberately searches memory for relevant prior knowledge). The mechanism is metacognitive and intentional, not reflexive.

The mechanism they emphasize for why high-road transfer is hard is worth quoting: "The reason for abstraction is plain: The abstract formulation provides the bridge from one context to the other. But why mindfulness? The reason is that: (a) the abstraction must be understood, and (b) the understanding requires mindfulness; automatic processes just do not yield novel abstractions that are well understood." A rote, unparsed abstraction "will not suffice"; the learner "must grasp the relationship between the decontextualized representation and the 'raw' instances of which it is an abstraction." This is precisely the deep-dive's "mindful abstraction of the principle and deliberate bridging." Salomon and Perkins also note that learners who *generate* abstractions themselves tend to transfer farther than those handed "ready-made" abstractions, and they discuss Gick and Holyoak's work as bearing on this point — the abstraction has to be genuinely owned to function as a bridge.

The overarching claim is diagnostic: "Findings of transfer or nontransfer reflect whether the conditions for either low-road or high-road transfer were met." Apparent transfer failures (LOGO not improving planning, strategy training not generalizing) are reinterpreted as cases where neither mechanism's preconditions — varied practice for the low road, mindful abstraction plus bridging for the high road — were actually established. The implication for instruction is the deep-dive's point: far transfer is not a free byproduct of competent teaching; it has to be engineered through the high-road moves.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's characterization of the low-road/high-road distinction and the claim that far transfer requires mindful abstraction plus deliberate bridging (rather than arising automatically from good teaching) faithfully represents the paper's central argument and its own language ("mindful abstraction," abstraction as "the bridge from one context to the other"). One precision note: the exact phrase "inordinately optimistic" does not appear in this 1989 article — it is associated with the authors' later transfer writing (the 1992 *International Encyclopedia of Education* entry) and reads here as the deep-dive's gloss on the same stance. The sentiment is fully supported by the 1989 paper's thesis that transfer findings depend on whether the mechanisms' conditions were met, but if the doc attributes the literal quotation to the 1989 source it slightly overstates; the substantive claim it carries is accurate.
