# Salomon & Perkins (1989) — Rocky Roads to Transfer

**Full citation:** Salomon, G., & Perkins, D. N. (1989). Rocky roads to transfer: Rethinking mechanisms of a neglected phenomenon. *Educational Psychologist, 24*(2), 113–142. The foundational journal article proposing the two-mechanism model of transfer (low-road and high-road) and arguing that transfer is not a unitary phenomenon but depends on distinct psychological routes.

**What the background doc claims it says:** The gloss in §4 reads: "Low-road (surface-triggered) vs. high-road (mindful abstraction + deliberate bridging) transfer; far transfer is not an automatic byproduct of good teaching." The 1989 paper is the foundational journal article establishing this two-mechanism framework; the 1992 Perkins & Salomon encyclopedia entry (`perkins-salomon-1992.md`) is the companion synthesis piece that consolidates the same framework and adds the hugging/bridging instructional strategies. The two pieces share the conceptual core but differ in form: the 1989 article is a 30-page empirical-theoretical argument with literature review and qualitative predictions; the 1992 entry is a compact encyclopedic distillation. Background.html cites both; the 1989 paper is the primary source of record for the mechanism distinction.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The paper opens by separating the *how* of transfer (the mechanisms that produce it) from the *what* (the kind of knowledge or skill transferred). The authors argue that transfer is not a unitary phenomenon and that conflating mechanisms has produced contradictory findings in the literature. Their core claim: transfer occurs by two qualitatively different routes, each with distinct psychological demands and distinct conditions for success.

**Low-road transfer** depends on extensive, varied practice leading to automatization. A cognitive element is practiced across a sufficiently wide range of contexts that it becomes "somewhat flexible" and context-independent; when a new situation presents stimulus characteristics resembling those earlier contexts, the element is triggered automatically — the learner need not recognize the analogy consciously. The car-to-truck driving example illustrates this: years of car driving automatize skills that fire spontaneously when the driver enters a truck cab. The key conditions are *varied practice* (training across multiple related contexts, not just repeated drill in one setting) and *automaticity* (the Shiffrin & Schneider 1977 sense: fast, effortless, stimulus-controlled processing). Critically, automatization also inhibits high-road transfer, because stimulus-controlled responding preempts the analytic awareness high-road transfer requires. Low-road transfer is well-suited to near, amount-of-transfer situations (more performance gain in adjacent domains) but unlikely to reach distant contexts where surface similarity is absent.

**High-road transfer** depends on *mindful abstraction* — the deliberate, metacognitively guided decontextualization of a principle, strategy, or main idea from its learning context so that it becomes a candidate for application elsewhere. The hallmark is that the abstraction must be genuinely understood, not merely rote-encoded: "the person must grasp the relationship between the decontextualized representation and the 'raw' instances of which it is an abstraction" (p. 126). The authors define *mindfulness* (drawing on Salomon & Globerson 1987 and Langer) as volitional, nonautomatic, metacognitively guided engagement — the learner inhibits an initial surface response, elaborates situational cues, and actively constructs connections. High-road transfer subdivides into two variants: *forward-reaching* (the learner abstracts general principles during the original learning, setting them up for spontaneous application later) and *backward-reaching* (the learner faces a new problem, deliberately abstracts from it, and reaches back into prior experience to find relevant connections). Both variants require that the conditions of the learning context or the application context provoke mindfulness and abstraction; without such provocation, the abstract representation either is not formed or is not retrieved. Far transfer — application across genuinely different contexts — is the characteristic outcome of high-road transfer, but it is not automatic: it fails whenever neither the training nor the application context triggers the deliberate search for connections.

The paper's concluding argument makes the implication for teaching explicit: "unless the conditions for low- or high-road transfer are met, transfer will not occur no matter how relevant the previous learning" (p. 135). Relevance of prior learning is necessary but not sufficient. Good teaching that builds genuine mastery does not by itself produce far transfer; the conditions for mindful abstraction must be specifically engineered. The authors are optimistic that this *can* be done — "if only we teach it, we are most likely to get it" (p. 137) — but the default in instruction (passive reception of information, content-specific practice only) satisfies neither low-road nor high-road conditions.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the background.html gloss ("Low-road [surface-triggered] vs. high-road [mindful abstraction + deliberate bridging] transfer; far transfer is not an automatic byproduct of good teaching") accurately captures the paper's central claims. The terminology is slightly compressed: the paper uses "mindful abstraction" as the high-road hallmark; "deliberate bridging" maps onto what the paper calls forward- and backward-reaching high-road transfer. The claim that far transfer is not automatic is stated plainly and repeatedly in the original. No material mischaracterization is present.
