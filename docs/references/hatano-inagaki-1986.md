# Hatano & Inagaki (1986) — Two Courses of Expertise

**Full citation:** Hatano, G., & Inagaki, K. (1986). Two Courses of Expertise. In H. Stevenson, H. Azuma, & K. Hakuta (Eds.), *Child Development and Education in Japan* (pp. 262–272). New York: Freeman. **What the doc claims it says:** Routine vs. adaptive expertise; the adaptive (more transferable) form develops under variation, novelty, and understanding-why. Adaptive experts understand WHY their procedures work, can mentally simulate unfamiliar cases, and invent new approaches. The deep-dive §6 adds the honesty note that the empirical demonstrations of adaptive expertise are mostly within-domain.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** Hatano and Inagaki frame cognitive development as "spontaneous expertise" — children acquiring domain-specific knowledge through accumulated problem solving — and then raise three issues. The organizing claim is that expertise can follow *two courses*, and that they differ in transferability.

**Routine expertise.** Some people, "in solving a large number of problems, merely learn to perform a skill faster and more accurately, without constructing/enriching their conceptual knowledge." Their canonical example is the amateur who repeatedly grows Saint Paulia flowers in a climate-controlled greenhouse "without understanding the nature of the flowers, the conditions under which they grow best, or the contents of the fertilizer mix." Such skill "is useful only as long as the object and its constraints are constant." Routine experts are "outstanding in terms of speed, accuracy, and automaticity of performance, but lack flexibility and adaptability to new problems." Hatano and Inagaki devote a section to the *generalized consequences* of routine expertise (e.g., abacus experts interiorizing a "mental abacus" that lets them rapidly hold and manipulate 15-digit strings) — but they are careful that these consequences arise "not through understanding but through well-established patterns and/or modes of processing," and remain tied to the practiced format.

**Adaptive expertise.** The contrasting course rests on *conceptual* knowledge. A skill is performed with understanding "when the performer can explain why it works, i.e., verbalize the principle involved," can judge variations as appropriate or inappropriate, and can modify the skill as conditions change. Crucially, conceptual knowledge takes the form of mental models "with which people can run mental simulation, and thus make predictions" — and it "may even enable him/her to invent new procedures and/or make new predictions." This maps directly onto the doc's three claims: adaptive experts (1) understand *why* their procedures work, (2) can *mentally simulate* unfamiliar cases, and (3) can *invent* new approaches. Their illustrative adaptive expert is the traditional farmer (after Hatano, 1982a) who, facing variation in plants and weather "beyond human control," must modify the skill from feedback and so acquires "the how and why of each step."

**How the adaptive course is fostered (variation, novelty, understanding-why).** The paper's third issue identifies, following Piaget, that understanding requires "systematically examin[ing] the effects of variations in action upon outcome," and proposes three factors that push someone onto the adaptive rather than routine course:
1. **Built-in randomness/variation in the object.** When a skill deals with a "natural" object whose parameters vary (traditional agriculture, home cooking with whatever ingredients are on hand), the original procedure keeps failing, forcing modification and giving "many opportunities for observing the effects of modification." Highly standardized systems (greenhouse growing, cooking with an electronic oven and a precisely quantified recipe) remove the need to modify and thus "by no means facilitate the acquisition of conceptual knowledge." (This is the "variation"/"novelty" condition.)
2. **Context of use.** When outcomes are low-stakes, people "playfully" produce minor variations and examine their effects — active experimentation that builds conceptual knowledge. Under reward pressure or evaluation, people adopt a "safety strategy" and stick to the conventional version; the authors cite Inagaki & Hatano (1984), where students expecting external evaluation translated more cautiously yet produced shallower, less inferential work. They warn that "overemphasis on giving right answers and making no errors, which we often see in some school settings, may prevent students from constructing conceptual knowledge."
3. **Whether understanding is valued by the reference group.** In a culture/community where understanding the system is the goal, people are encouraged to try new versions "even at the cost of efficiency" and are "often requested to explain the appropriateness of the skill," which drives them to integrate and elaborate conceptual knowledge through mental experimentation.

**The within-domain caveat (deep-dive §6).** The empirical material in this paper is overwhelmingly *within-domain*: abacus/mental-arithmetic experts, Kanji readers, agriculture, cooking. The transferable flexibility Hatano and Inagaki describe is flexibility to *novel problems within the domain of practice* (a farmer adapting to a new pest, an abacus expert handling a longer digit string), not demonstrated leaps to distant domains. The paper itself is candid that the evidence on what differentiates the two courses is "scanty" and that the differentiating factors are offered as "speculations" and "tentative" predictions to be tested cross-culturally. So the deep-dive's honesty note — that the empirical demonstrations of adaptive expertise are mostly within-domain — is well grounded in the source.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's characterization is faithful: the routine/adaptive distinction, the three hallmarks of adaptive expertise (understanding *why*, mental simulation of unfamiliar cases, inventing new procedures), and the developmental conditions (variation, novelty, understanding-why orientation) are all stated in the paper's own terms. The deep-dive §6 caveat is also accurate and, if anything, commendably restrained: the paper's demonstrations are within-domain and its differentiating mechanisms are explicitly tentative.
