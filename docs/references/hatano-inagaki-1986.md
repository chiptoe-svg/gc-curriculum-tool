**What it actually says (synthesis):**

Hatano and Inagaki coined the terms *routine expertise* and *adaptive expertise* to distinguish two trajectories that domain experience can produce. Routine experts achieve exceptional speed, accuracy, and automaticity of performance through repeated problem-solving, but their knowledge remains procedure-bound: they can execute skills effectively in familiar situations yet struggle when contexts shift. Adaptive experts, by contrast, develop conceptual knowledge alongside procedural fluency — they understand *why* their procedures work, can predict outcomes in unfamiliar situations, modify procedures flexibly when needed, and invent new ones when none of their existing procedures applies. Their sushi-chef illustration contrasts a chef who perfects the same items with one who regularly creates new menus; the latter's understanding-why is what makes novelty tractable rather than threatening.

Three conditions are consistently attributed to the 1986 chapter across independent secondary sources as fostering adaptive over routine development: (1) **environmental variability / built-in randomness** — situations that require observing co-variations and modifying skills rather than applying fixed routines; (2) **low-incentive / low-urgency settings** — fewer external rewards for efficiency reduce the pull toward a success-orientation and leave room for understanding-oriented exploration; (3) **cultural or reference-group values** — contexts that prize understanding over speed normalize the effortful conceptual work that adaptive expertise requires. A fourth condition — **dialogical interaction** (discussion, controversy, reciprocal teaching) — is reliably attributed to Hatano and Inagaki but appears most explicitly in their 1992 paper ("Sharing Cognition through Collective Comprehension Activity" and related work) rather than the 1986 chapter. The mechanism underlying all four conditions is *cognitive incongruity*: the felt inadequacy of current understanding that drives deeper inquiry.

The adaptive–routine distinction maps onto the Piagetian framework the authors invoke: adaptive expertise arises through genuine assimilation-and-accommodation cycles rather than simple accretion of procedure. The chapter critiques efficiency-oriented school assessment culture implicitly, arguing that optimizing for accuracy and speed systematically steers learners toward the routine trajectory.

**Note on "dialogical interaction" as a condition:** The background.html gloss lists "variation, novelty, and understanding-why" — not dialogical interaction. That three-element gloss accurately reflects what the 1986 chapter emphasizes. The fourth condition (dialogical interaction) is better attributed to Hatano & Inagaki 1992, though it is organically continuous with the 1986 framework. Attributing all four conditions to the 1986 chapter is a minor anachronism common in secondary literature; attributing the core routine/adaptive distinction and the variation-novelty-understanding-why cluster to 1986 is well-supported.

**Verdict:** Consistent — The core distinction (routine vs. adaptive expertise) and the three conditions foregrounded in the background.html gloss (variation, novelty, understanding-why) are reliably and repeatedly confirmed across multiple independent secondary sources and are not in dispute. The gloss accurately characterizes what the 1986 chapter is credited with establishing. "Dialogical interaction" — mentioned in the task brief but not in the background.html gloss itself — belongs more precisely to the 1992 elaboration; its omission from the gloss is accurate rather than a gap. The claim that the adaptive form is "more transferable" is also consistent with the chapter's argument: adaptive expertise's conceptual grounding is precisely what enables application to novel situations.
