# Schwartz & Martin (2004) — Inventing to Prepare for Future Learning

**Full citation:** Schwartz, D. L., & Martin, T. (2004). Inventing to Prepare for Future Learning: The Hidden Efficiency of Encouraging Original Student Production in Statistics Instruction. *Cognition and Instruction*, 22(2), 129–184. The experimental anchor for preparation-for-future-learning (PFL): inventing (suboptimal) methods before instruction builds readiness to learn from later instruction — a benefit invisible to standard cold-application transfer tests but revealed by a "double transfer" test that embeds a learning resource.

**What the doc claims it says:** Experimental demonstration that struggling to invent methods before instruction builds readiness to learn from later instruction — an effect invisible to standard cold-application transfer tests. Empirically anchors PFL.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The paper reports two design experiments teaching descriptive statistics (graphing, variability/mean deviation, standardized scores) to ninth graders. The instructional model is "Inventing to Prepare for Learning" (IPL): students are asked to invent original methods for novel problems *before* receiving canonical instruction, even though they typically produce suboptimal solutions and so look bad on conventional assessments. The authors' thesis is that this apparent inefficiency is hidden efficiency — invention "may be particularly useful for preparing students to learn," which pays off later. The work explicitly operationalizes Bransford and Schwartz's (1999) PFL argument that "even the best instruction in problem-solving procedures is unlikely to prepare students for many situations they may encounter."

**The core experiment — the "double transfer" paradigm.** Embedded in Study 1 is a controlled assessment experiment that is the paper's empirical heart and the part the doc relies on. It is a 2 × 2 (× pre/post) design crossing **instructional treatment** (invention vs. "tell-and-practice") with **presence/absence of a learning resource embedded in the posttest**. In the invention condition, students tried to invent a way to compare whether a long-jumper or a pole-vaulter had broken a world record by a greater *relative* amount (i.e., they were groping toward standardized scores) — with no class presentations, no sharing, and no feedback; the authors note flatly that "no students invented a correct solution in the invention condition." In the tell-and-practice condition, students were shown and drilled a demonstrated visual procedure on an analogous grading problem. Importantly, neither group was taught the canonical standardized-score (z-score) procedure during instruction.

The transfer test then either did or did not embed a *learning resource* — a worked example showing how "Cheryl" computed standardized scores. Students who got the resource had to read it, learn from it, and apply it to a later target transfer problem (the "double transfer" design — first transfer the early knowledge to learn from the resource, then transfer that to the target). Students who did not get the resource faced the standard cold "sequestered" transfer test.

**Key finding (the crossover).** As the abstract states: "A 'tell-and-practice' instructional condition led to the same transfer results as an invention condition when there was no learning resource, but the invention condition did better than the tell-and-practice condition when there was a learning resource." This is the crucial interaction. On the *standard* cold test (no resource), invention and tell-and-practice were indistinguishable — exactly the situation in which the value of invention is invisible. The advantage of invention appeared only on the *double transfer* test, where invention students were better prepared to learn from the embedded worked example and then apply it. The prediction the authors set up and confirm: tell-and-practice students "would not be prepared to learn from the worked example, and they would interpret it as a 'plug and chug,'" whereas invention students had developed early knowledge (about variability and the need to normalize) that let them interpret the worked example's significance. Study 1 also found IPL produced strong gains in procedural skill, "symbolic insight" into formulas (e.g., why the mean-deviation formula divides by n), and the ability to evaluate data in an argument. A counterbalancing control (sports vs. grading cover stories, swapped across conditions) ruled out the explanation that surface similarity between instruction and test was driving the effect.

**Study 2 — replication by classroom teachers.** The second design experiment had regular classroom teachers (not the researchers) deliver the IPL curriculum and replicated the pattern, addressing the practicality worry that such student-centered activities can't be implemented in ordinary schools within a short time frame.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's claim is accurately supported: this is an experimental demonstration that pre-instruction invention/struggle (students who, by design, did *not* reach the correct method) builds readiness to learn from later instruction, and that this benefit is invisible to a standard cold-application test — it surfaces only when the assessment itself includes an opportunity to learn (the double-transfer / embedded-resource design). The crossover interaction (parity with no resource; invention advantage with a resource) is exactly the evidence that anchors the PFL construct, so calling this the empirical anchor for PFL is faithful, not overstated.
