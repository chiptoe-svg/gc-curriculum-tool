# Schwartz & Martin (2004) — Inventing to Prepare for Future Learning

**Full citation:** Schwartz, D. L., & Martin, T. (2004). Inventing to prepare for future learning: The hidden efficiency of encouraging original student production in statistics instruction. *Cognition and Instruction*, 22(2), 129–184. Experimental demonstration that struggling to invent statistical measures before direct instruction builds readiness to benefit from that instruction — an advantage that is invisible to standard cold-application transfer tests and only appears when the transfer assessment includes a learning resource.

**What the background doc claims it says:** background.html cites the paper in §4 ("What transfers is readiness to learn, not cold skill") as one of two supports for the "preparation for future learning" (PFL) reframe: rather than testing whether prior learning fires unaided on a novel problem, measure how *fast* learners acquire new material given that prior preparation. The inline gloss in the reference list reads: "Experimental demonstration that struggling to invent methods before instruction builds readiness to learn from later instruction — an effect invisible to standard cold-application transfer tests." The §4 claim co-cites this paper with Bransford & Schwartz (1999), which introduced the PFL concept theoretically; Schwartz & Martin (2004) is positioned as the first robust empirical test of that concept.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The paper reports two studies teaching 9th-grade students descriptive statistics. The core pedagogical manipulation is an *invention* condition: instead of receiving a worked explanation first ("tell-and-practice"), students were given structured contrasting-cases tasks — including the now-canonical "pitching machine" task — and asked to invent their own measures before any canonical instruction. The pitching machine task presents data from two machines with different distributions and asks students to devise a single index for comparing reliability across incommensurable sample sizes; this forces engagement with the logic of deviation and standardization before the mean-deviation formula is introduced. A second contrasting-cases set involving density comparisons (pitchers of different sizes) similarly targets ratio reasoning. Neither task expects students to arrive at canonical formulas; the point is the exploratory engagement with the structural features that make the problem hard.

The critical methodological innovation is the embedded PFL assessment. Within Study 1, students were split into four cells crossing instructional condition (invention vs. tell-and-practice) × transfer test type (with or without a learning resource). Half of each condition's posttest included a brief worked example or resource relevant to the target transfer problem; half did not. On the *no-resource* (cold-application) posttest, the two instructional conditions performed equivalently — invention looked no better than tell-and-practice by the standard criterion. On the *with-resource* posttest, the invention condition substantially outperformed tell-and-practice: students who had invented were better able to extract understanding from the new resource and apply it to the transfer problem. This is the "hidden efficiency" of the title — not hidden efficiency of time-on-task, but hidden efficiency of learning readiness that is concealed by cold tests and only revealed when a learning opportunity is present. Schwartz and Martin call this structure the preparation-for-future-learning (PFL) assessment, directly operationalizing Bransford & Schwartz (1999)'s theoretical proposal.

Study 1 was a controlled laboratory study. Study 2 replicated the instructional design with classroom teachers delivering the invention curriculum at scale, with comparable results: the invention-first sequence, followed by direct instruction, produced stronger procedural skills, better insight into the rationale behind formulas, and improved ability to critically evaluate statistical arguments. Together, the two studies establish that the benefit of invention before instruction is real, replicable in classroom conditions, and specifically tied to preparedness to *learn from* subsequent instruction rather than to unaided performance.

The relationship to Kapur's productive failure is direct and acknowledged in the productive-failure literature: Schwartz & Martin (2004) is treated as the paradigm case of the PS-I (problem-solving before instruction) design, and productive failure is framed as a subset of PFL designs that emphasize the generative value of failure specifically. The key shared mechanism is that pre-instruction struggle — even unsuccessful struggle — sensitizes students to the features of the domain that direct instruction will later address, making subsequent instruction more efficient. The difference from Kapur's framing is one of emphasis: Schwartz & Martin focus on the assessment design needed to *detect* the benefit (PFL tests, not cold SPS tests); Kapur foregrounds the conditions under which failure is productive versus unproductive and the boundary conditions on prior knowledge.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the background doc's claims are accurate in every material respect. The paper is an experimental demonstration of the PFL paradigm with the specific design the gloss describes: invention activities before instruction, benefit detectable only on PFL tests (not cold-application), two studies including a classroom replication. The background.html gloss ("struggling to invent methods before instruction builds readiness to learn from later instruction — an effect invisible to standard cold-application transfer tests") is a precise one-sentence compression of the paper's central finding. The pairing with Bransford & Schwartz (1999) is also correct: the 1999 paper introduced the PFL concept theoretically; the 2004 paper is its first major controlled empirical test. No overclaim; no distortion.
