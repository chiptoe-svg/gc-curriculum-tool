**What it actually says (synthesis):** *Leading and Managing a Differentiated Classroom* extends the differentiated instruction framework from *The Differentiated Classroom* (1999) to address the practical management challenges of implementing differentiation in real classrooms. Tomlinson and Imbeau argue that leading and managing are interdependent — a teacher who understands the rationale for differentiation (leadership) must also master the mechanics of running a classroom where students work simultaneously on different tasks, in different groupings, with different materials (management).

The book is organized in two parts: Part I (Leading) focuses on the conceptual and motivational dimensions — helping teachers develop a clear purpose and philosophy for differentiation, communicate that purpose to students and parents, and sustain it over time. Part II (Managing) addresses practical logistics: how to use time, space, and materials efficiently; how to structure flexible grouping; how to set up routines that allow multiple activities to run simultaneously; and how to monitor and give feedback in a differentiated environment.

The book references the KUD vocabulary from the 1999 work (Know, Understand, Do) as the foundational planning structure that differentiation serves. Teachers differentiate content, process, and product based on student readiness, interest, and learning profile — all organized around the same KUD outcomes for the unit.
