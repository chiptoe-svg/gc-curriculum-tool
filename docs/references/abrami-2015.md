# Abrami, Bernard, Borokhovski, Waddington, Wade & Persson (2015) — Strategies for Teaching Students to Think Critically: A Meta-Analysis

**Full citation:** Abrami, P. C., Bernard, R. M., Borokhovski, E., Waddington, D. I., Wade, C. A., & Persson, T. (2015). Strategies for teaching students to think critically: A meta-analysis. *Review of Educational Research*, 85(2), 275–314. https://doi.org/10.3102/0034654314551063. A comprehensive meta-analysis (341 effect sizes) of how — and how well — instruction develops critical-thinking (CT) skills and dispositions, including which instructional approaches and which CT-to-content relationships work best.

**What the doc claims it says:** This is a **recommended addition**, not a claim already in the docs. Per `Writeup_Review_and_Recommendations.md` §3.4, it was flagged as the strongest counter-evidence that actually *corroborates* the in-domain thesis: CT instruction works best under immersion/mixed approaches embedded in subject content, and is weakest under general/standalone instruction.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The meta-analysis adopts the American Philosophical Association (APA/Delphi) definition of critical thinking — "purposeful, self-regulatory judgment which results in interpretation, analysis, evaluation, and inference" — and synthesizes 341 effect sizes drawn from quasi- and true-experimental studies that used standardized CT measures. The aim was to establish whether CT can be taught at all, and which instructional strategies and conditions maximize it. The headline result: the weighted random-effects mean effect size for generic CT outcomes was **g+ = 0.30 (p < .001)**, a "modest but robust" effect (Cohen-small). The collection was significantly heterogeneous (p < .001), motivating extensive moderator analysis. (Note: g+ here is a Hedges'-g family standardized mean difference; the abstract's value of 0.30 is for the 341 generic CT outcomes.)

On the **CT-vs-content relationship**, the most striking finding is that **content-specific CT outcomes (CT assessed with measures tied to the subject being taught) produced a substantially *larger* effect, g+ = 0.57 (k = 97, random effects)**, than generic CT outcomes (g+ = 0.30). Achievement (course-content) outcomes were g+ = 0.33, and CT dispositions g+ = 0.23 (Table 9). So critical thinking measured *within* a content domain improved roughly twice as much as free-floating generic CT — consistent with a domain-embedded view of CT.

On **instructional approach**, the authors coded studies against Ennis's (1989) four-type typology — *generic* (CT taught separately, no specific content), *infusion* (deep content instruction with CT principles made explicit), *immersion* (thought-provoking content but general CT principles left implicit), and *mixed* (a content course plus a separate CT track). Table 4 reports the descriptive ordering: **Direct/generic = 0.26, Infusion = 0.29, Immersion = 0.23, Mixed = 0.38.** Mixed was descriptively highest and immersion descriptively lowest. **Crucially, however, the omnibus test of difference between the four approaches was NOT significant: Q-Between = 4.10, df = 3, p = .25.** The authors state plainly that "all four of the methods... produced significantly positive average effect sizes, but the categories did not differ from one another." So the four approaches each *work*, but this meta-analysis does not establish a statistically reliable ranking among them.

What the meta-analysis *does* establish about effective strategy comes from its three-category scheme (dialogue, authentic/anchored instruction, mentoring). **Dialogue** (especially teacher-posed questions and teacher-led whole-class and small-group discussion, g+ ≈ 0.38–0.42) and **authentic/situated instruction** (applied problem solving g+ = 0.35; role-playing g+ = 0.61) both significantly favored the experimental group. The strongest combination was **authentic instruction + dialogue + mentoring (A+D+M), g+ = 0.57**, significantly higher than authentic + dialogue alone (z = 1.98, p = .024); mentoring alone was weak but "catalytic" in combination (Table 6). Moderators of educational level, subject matter (STEM 0.31, non-STEM 0.29, health 0.20), and treatment duration all showed CT effects to be robustly positive and *not* significantly different across categories, supporting generalizability of the instructional effect (Table 3).

Mechanistically, the authors conclude that CT develops when students engage in dialogue, are confronted with authentic/situated problems and examples, and are mentored — i.e., when CT is exercised in the course of grappling with real content, not drilled as an abstract skill. The much larger content-specific effect (0.57 vs 0.30) reinforces that CT gains are most pronounced when measured against, and taught through, subject content.

**Verdict — assessment of how it would function if cited (it is not currently cited):** Abrami et al. (2015) would **support the framework's in-domain position**, and is a strong, recent, high-N addition. It demonstrates that CT instruction reliably works (g+ = 0.30 generic; 0.57 content-specific) and that the largest gains come from CT exercised through authentic content, dialogue, and mentoring — i.e., embedded rather than abstract. The doubling of effect for content-specific over generic CT outcomes is the single most useful corroborating statistic for a domain-embedded thesis.

**One important accuracy caveat the framework must respect when citing it:** the specific claim attributed to it in Writeup_Review §3.4 — that CT works "best under immersion/mixed... weakest under general standalone" — overstates what the Ennis-typology results actually license. Descriptively, *mixed* was highest (0.38) and *immersion* was lowest (0.23), with generic/direct at 0.26 and infusion at 0.29; but the between-approach difference was **non-significant (p = .25)**, so the meta-analysis does **not** support a reliable claim that any one Ennis approach beats another, and in particular it does not support "immersion is strongest" (immersion was descriptively the *weakest* of the four). The defensible, evidence-backed version of the claim is: (a) content-specific CT outcomes improve about twice as much as generic ones (0.57 vs 0.30), and (b) the effective ingredients are dialogue, authentic/situated problems, and mentoring used together — both of which point toward teaching CT *through* domain content rather than as a standalone generic course. If §3.4's recommendation is reworded to those two claims, the citation is fully supported; the "immersion/mixed best, general weakest" phrasing should be dropped or softened to avoid misrepresenting a non-significant moderator result.
