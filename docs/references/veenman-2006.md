# Veenman, Van Hout-Wolters & Afflerbach (2006) — Metacognition and Learning: Conceptual and Methodological Considerations

**Full citation:** Veenman, M. V. J., Van Hout-Wolters, B. H. A. M., & Afflerbach, P. (2006). Metacognition and learning: Conceptual and methodological considerations. *Metacognition and Learning*, 1(1), 3–14. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11409-006-6893-0. The inaugural ("theoretical article") position paper of the journal, laying out ten unresolved issues in metacognition research, including the general-vs-domain-specificity question and the conditions for effective metacognitive instruction.

**What the doc claims it says (§7):** Cited on metacognitive-strategy transfer and "reflective control" — that metacognition supports cross-domain learning when explicitly cultivated.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** This is a **conceptual and methodological position paper, not an empirical study.** It is the introductory article for the new journal *Metacognition and Learning*, and its stated purpose is to lay out ten themes "in need of further investigation" rather than to report data. The authors note up front that metacognition "matters" — citing Wang, Haertel and Walberg's (1990) review finding it among the most powerful predictors of learning — but that the field lacks a unified definition and a coherent domain.

Several strands support the doc's §7 citation. First, on **components**, the paper separates metacognitive *knowledge* (declarative knowledge about person/task/strategy interactions) from metacognitive *skills* (procedural knowledge for regulating problem-solving and learning). Crucially, metacognitive skills "have a feedback mechanism built-in": monitoring detects when performance goes astray and control processes are activated to resolve the problem. This monitoring-and-control loop, drawn from Nelson's (1996) meta-level/object-level model, is the substance behind the doc's phrase "reflective control."

Second, the paper explicitly takes up **General vs. Domain-Specificity of Metacognition** (its own section heading). It frames this as "an issue of particular importance to educators": general metacognition could be "instructed concurrently in different learning situations and may be expected to transfer to new ones, whereas specific metacognition has to be taught for each task or domain separately." The authors review *conflicting* evidence — Schraw et al. found monitoring to be general; Kelemen, Frost and Weaver found against a general skill; Veenman's own studies (Veenman, Elshout & Meijer, 1997; Veenman & Verheij, 2003; Veenman et al., 2004) "obtained strong support for the generality of metacognitive skills." They conclude the results are equivocal partly because of the "grain of analysis": divergent surface activities across tasks "may spring from similar metacognitive grounds." They also report (citing Veenman & Spaans, 2005) that metacognitive skills "initially develop in separate domains, and later on become generalized across domains," and that the processes responsible for this transfer "include, amongst others, high road transfer (Salomon & Perkins, 1989), and linking metacognition through instruction and feedback provided by teachers." This is the directly relevant mechanism: cross-domain generalization is not automatic; it depends on deliberate cultivation.

Third — and most load-bearing for the doc's "when explicitly cultivated" claim — the section **Conditions for the Acquisition and Instruction of Metacognition** states that while most students pick up metacognition incidentally, a substantial group does not, and "metacognitive instruction appears to enhance metacognition and learning in a broad range of students." The authors name **three fundamental principles for successful metacognitive instruction**: (a) **embedding metacognitive instruction in the content matter to ensure connectivity**; (b) **informing learners about the usefulness** of metacognitive activities so they exert the initial extra effort; and (c) **prolonged training** to guarantee smooth, maintained application. Veenman (1998) labeled these the **WWH rule — teaching What to do, When, Why, and How** (the conditional framing). They add that metacognition "draws on cognition" and that adequate metacognitive knowledge of a domain requires "substantial (cognitive) domain-specific knowledge" — i.e., metacognition is not a free-floating skill.

The paper also reports a quantitative figure relevant to metacognition's importance (in the "Conscious vs. Automatic" section): metacognitive skills "uniquely account for 17 percent of variance in learning" beyond intelligence (which uniquely accounts for ~10%), with another ~20% shared — implying "an adequate level of metacognition may compensate for students' cognitive limitations." It also raises the question (relevant to "reflective control") of whether checking that has "become a firmly established or 'good' habit" run in the background as automatic processes still count as metacognitive — flagging this as unresolved.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The §7 use of Veenman et al. (2006) is accurate and well-targeted: the paper explicitly treats cross-domain transfer of metacognitive skills as something that emerges with development and is supported by "high road transfer" plus deliberate instruction and feedback — i.e., "when explicitly cultivated." The "reflective control" language maps onto the paper's monitoring-and-control feedback loop. The three instructional principles (embed in content, inform usefulness, prolonged training) and the WWH/conditional rule directly underwrite the framework's argument that metacognitive habit transfers only when taught with conditional, embedded framing. One caveat to honor in citing it: this is a *position/agenda-setting paper*, so it should be cited for its conceptual claims and its synthesis of the (acknowledged equivocal) literature, not as primary empirical evidence that transfer occurs — the authors themselves stress the evidence is mixed and the grain-of-analysis problem unresolved. The "17% unique variance" figure is the paper's most concrete quantitative claim and is itself a citation to Veenman's prior empirical work, not new data in this article.
