# Gick & Holyoak (1980, 1983) — Analogical Problem Solving & Schema Induction

**Full citation:** Gick, M. L., & Holyoak, K. J. (1980). Analogical Problem Solving. *Cognitive Psychology*, 12, 306–355; and Gick, M. L., & Holyoak, K. J. (1983). Schema Induction and Analogical Transfer. *Cognitive Psychology*, 15, 1–38. The classic Duncker-radiation-problem studies: transfer from a single analog is poor and depends heavily on a hint; comparing two analogs to induce a shared schema roughly doubles spontaneous transfer.

**What the doc claims it says:** Transfer from a single case is poor (≈10%, rising to ≈30% with an explicit hint); comparing two surface-different cases to induce a shared schema roughly doubles it (≈50%). (The doc instructs verifying these percentages against the papers — the Duncker radiation problem studies.)

**What it actually says (synthesis):** Both papers use Duncker's "radiation problem": how can a doctor destroy an inoperable tumor with rays that, at the intensity needed, would also destroy the healthy tissue they pass through? The canonical "convergence" (a.k.a. dispersion) solution is to send many weak rays from different directions that converge on the tumor. The studies probe whether reading an analogous story (most famously the "Attack-Dispersion" military story, where a general splits his army to converge on a fortress) helps people solve the radiation problem.

**Base rate.** Across replications, the spontaneous solution rate *without any analog* is low: the papers repeatedly cite "less than 10%" and adopt 10% as the assumed base rate for producing the convergence solution unaided.

**Single analog + hint (1980).** When subjects read one appropriate analog *and were given a (nonspecific) hint* to use the prior story, transfer is dramatic. In the 1980 Experiment IV, 92% (11/12) of the Hint group produced the complete dispersion solution, versus "under 10% in both Experiments I and II" for subjects who received no story. The 1983 paper summarizes the typical figure as "about 75% of those subjects who received the appropriate military analog produced the convergence solution... as compared to less than 10% of control subjects." So a single analog *with a hint* is highly effective.

**Single analog WITHOUT a hint — the noticing failure (1980/1983).** The central, sobering finding is that transfer collapses when subjects must *spontaneously notice* the analogy. In 1980 Experiment IV's No-Hint condition, only 20% (3/15) produced the dispersion solution, and the authors note that 2 of those 3 gave only partial solutions and said they did not consider the stories — so "no more than 3 subjects, and perhaps only 1," actually noticed and applied the analogy unaided. The 1983 paper restates the general pattern: with the analog memorized but no hint, "only about 30% of our subjects produced the convergence solution, as opposed to about 75% who produced it when a hint was given." Netting out the ~10% base rate, this means only a third or so of the people who *could* have used the stored analog actually retrieved it spontaneously. The gap between hint and no-hint is the paper's signature result: people often *have* the relevant knowledge but fail to access it across surface-different contexts.

**Two analogs and schema induction (1983).** The 1983 paper's Part II shows that having subjects compare *two* prior analogs — and describe the similarities between them — leads many to induce a shared "convergence schema" as an incidental product of the comparison, and that this schema strongly predicts transfer. In the key comparison, "45% of subjects in the two-analog conditions generated the convergence solution prior to the hint, as opposed to only 21% of subjects in the one-analog condition" (G²(1)=6.35, p<.01); the same advantage held for total solution frequency (80% vs 53%, p<.003). The authors conclude flatly, "two analogs were better than one." Schema quality mediated the effect: good induced schemas yielded transfer rates above 90%, poor schemas far less. Notably, abstraction aids that *failed* with one analog (a verbal statement of the principle, a diagram) "proved highly beneficial when paired with two" — the comparison process is what makes the abstraction usable.

**Mapping to the doc's numbers.** The doc's framing is faithful to the studies' shape, with minor rounding: single-case transfer is poor (≈10% true base rate; the spontaneous, no-hint single-analog rate runs ~20–30%); a single analog *with an explicit hint* rises sharply (the doc's "≈30% with a hint" understates the with-hint figure, which is typically ~75–92% — see note below); and two-analog comparison roughly doubles *spontaneous* transfer to ≈45–50% (and ~80% total).

**Verdict:** Consistent on the load-bearing claim, with one numerical caveat. The qualitative story — poor single-case transfer driven by a spontaneous-noticing failure, sharply improved by a hint, and roughly doubled (in spontaneous transfer) by comparing two surface-different analogs to induce a shared schema — is exactly what both papers demonstrate, and the ≈10% base rate and ≈50% two-analog figures are accurate. The doc's "≈30% with an explicit hint" appears to conflate two different conditions: ~30% is the *no-hint, single-analog* spontaneous rate (1983), whereas the *with-hint, single-analog* rate is much higher (~75–92%, 1980/1983). If the doc means "single case rising to ≈30%," that maps to the no-hint condition and is fine; if it means "≈30% is the with-hint ceiling," that understates the hint effect. Either way the comparison-doubles-transfer conclusion the doc relies on is solidly supported.
