# Bransford & Schwartz (1999) — Rethinking Transfer (Preparation for Future Learning)

**Full citation:** Bransford, J. D., & Schwartz, D. L. (1999). Rethinking Transfer: A Simple Proposal with Multiple Implications. *Review of Research in Education*, 24, 61–100. Introduces the "preparation for future learning" (PFL) construct and argues that the standard "sequestered problem solving" transfer test, paired with a "direct application" theory, systematically under-detects transfer.

**What the doc claims it says:** Introduces "preparation for future learning" (PFL): transfer is better detected as accelerated new learning than as cold direct application. The standard transfer test under-detects transfer because it measures cold application; measure instead how fast the person learns the new domain.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The chapter is a review-and-proposal. Its target is the methodological assumption baked into a century of transfer research. The authors observe that essentially all the classic studies "use a final transfer task that involves what we call 'sequestered problem solving' (SPS)." The metaphor is deliberate: "Just as juries are often sequestered in order to protect them from possible exposure to 'contaminating' information, subjects in experiments are sequestered during tests of transfer. There are no opportunities for them to demonstrate their abilities to learn to solve new problems by seeking help from other resources such as texts or colleagues or by trying things out, receiving feedback, and getting opportunities to revise." Accompanying SPS is what they name the "direct application (DA) theory of transfer" — the view that transfer means directly applying prior learning to a new problem cold.

Their central thesis: "the SPS methodology and the accompanying DA theory of transfer are responsible for much of the pessimism about evidence of transfer." In other words, the recurring finding that learning fails to transfer is in part an artifact of how transfer is measured. The proposed alternative does not reject DA but "broadens the conception of transfer by including an emphasis on people's 'preparation for future learning' (PFL). Here the focus shifts to assessments of people's abilities to learn in knowledge-rich environments." The operational definition is exactly the doc's: "The better prepared they are for future learning, the greater the transfer (in terms of speed and/or quality of new learning)." Transfer becomes visible as *accelerated, higher-quality new learning* when the learner is allowed access to resources — not as cold one-shot application.

They motivate this with workplace logic: organizations hiring new employees "do not expect them to have learned everything they need... They want people who can learn." And with the example of new teachers: by the DA definition, the test is whether a beginning teacher can apply learned methods unaided; the larger story is "whether the novice teachers have been prepared to learn from their new experiences." Pre-service education "can only place them on a trajectory toward expertise."

The empirical leverage of the PFL lens is demonstrated through reinterpretation. Citing Singley and Anderson's text-editor studies, they note that "the benefits of previous experiences with a text editor did not reveal themselves immediately... much greater evidence of transfer on the second day than on the first. One-shot SPS tests of transfer are often too weak to detect effects such as these." The PFL claim is therefore both conceptual and methodological: measure learning *trajectories* (the questions learners ask, the speed with which they pick up the new domain), not just finished cold products. The chapter connects this to Broudy's notion of "knowing with" — knowledge that shapes how one perceives and interprets a new situation even when it cannot be recalled on demand — as the kind of knowing that DA tests miss entirely. The authors are explicit that the idea is not theirs alone (citing Bereiter, Greeno, Singley & Anderson, and others) but that the SPS-vs-PFL contrast had not been drawn sharply before, and that the two views "have different implications for educational practice," including a shift toward dynamic, learning-during-assessment evaluation.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's summary is an accurate and economical rendering of the paper's two linked moves: (1) the standard test is "sequestered problem solving," which under DA theory measures cold application and therefore under-detects transfer; and (2) the proposed PFL alternative measures transfer as the speed/quality of subsequent new learning in a resource-rich setting. The paper's own language ("sequestered," "direct application," "preparation for future learning," transfer as "speed and/or quality of new learning") matches the doc's claim closely; nothing is overstated.
