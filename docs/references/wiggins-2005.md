# Wiggins & McTighe (2005) — Understanding by Design

**Full citation:** Wiggins, G., & McTighe, J. (2005). *Understanding by Design* (Expanded 2nd ed.). ASCD. Foundational reference for backward design and the structural commitment to evidence-as-stage-2.

**What the background doc claims it says:** *Understanding by Design* defines a three-stage planning sequence: identify desired results, determine acceptable evidence, plan learning experiences. The KUD categories give outcomes a typed structure that fits Stage 1's "desired results"; the framework's evidence rule shares Stage 2's structural commitment that outcome claims must be backed by demonstrable evidence. The student-side anchoring of the depth scales is consistent with that commitment. Wiggins and McTighe do not themselves prescribe a 0–5 numerical depth scale; that operationalization is the framework's own.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** *Understanding by Design* (UbD) introduced "backward design" as the dominant alternative to "coverage-first" curriculum planning. The backward design process proceeds in three stages that deliberately invert the typical planning sequence:

**Stage 1 — Identify Desired Results:** Clarify what students should understand, know, and be able to do by the end of the unit or course. Wiggins and McTighe distinguish *transfer goals* (the long-term abilities students should develop), *understandings* (the specific insights that endure, stated as full sentences — "Students will understand that..."), *essential questions* (open questions that drive inquiry toward those understandings), *knowledge* (specific facts, concepts, and vocabulary), and *skills* (procedures and processes). The Know/Understand/Do distinction is embedded in Stage 1, though not labeled with those exact terms; UbD frames it as knowledge, understandings, and skills/transfer.

**Stage 2 — Determine Acceptable Evidence:** Before designing instruction, identify the evidence that would demonstrate whether students have achieved Stage 1's results. This is the stage the background doc emphasizes: Stage 2 requires specifying assessments — performance tasks, other evidence — that will demonstrate understanding, not just recall. The key principle is that you cannot claim a learning outcome is achieved unless you specify what evidence would count as demonstration of it. This is the structural source of the KUD+ framework's evidence rule.

**Stage 3 — Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction:** Only after Stages 1 and 2 are clear should instructional activities be designed. This prevents planning activities that are engaging but not aligned to the desired outcomes.

The UbD framework specifically distinguishes *six facets of understanding* (Explain, Interpret, Apply, Perspective, Empathy, Self-Knowledge) that describe what deep understanding looks like across contexts — these go beyond recall and procedural execution.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's description of UbD's three-stage structure is accurate. The doc's specific claim — that the evidence rule (depth values above zero require excerpts of student-produced work) "shares Stage 2's structural commitment that outcome claims must be backed by demonstrable evidence" — is a fair characterization of what Stage 2 requires. The doc correctly notes that Wiggins and McTighe do not prescribe a 0–5 numerical depth scale; the depth extension is the framework's own contribution.
