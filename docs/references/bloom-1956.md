# Bloom (1956) — Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: Cognitive Domain

**Full citation:** Bloom, B. S. (Ed.). (1956). *Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: The Classification of Educational Goals. Handbook I: Cognitive Domain*. David McKay.

**Reference ID in background.html:** `ref-bloom-1956`; cited in §5.

**Where it lives:**
- Original publisher: David McKay Company, New York. Now republished by various presses.
- Widely available in academic libraries; used textbook copies on Amazon.
- Open-access status: Book (commercial). No legal free digital version; widely excerpted and summarized in open sources including Wikipedia.

**Accessibility:** Partially accessible — Wikipedia article, extensive secondary descriptions, and the 2001 revision (Anderson & Krathwohl) all document the original taxonomy fully.

**What the background doc claims it says:** Bloom's 1956 taxonomy provides six cognitive levels — and, together with the 2001 Anderson & Krathwohl revision — remains the right vocabulary for analyzing the cognitive demand of an assessment task. The framework uses Bloom's verbs inside the audit conversation to probe each cell, with the discriminator between Bloom and KUD being scope: Bloom at assessment-level granularity, KUD at program-mapping granularity.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The 1956 *Taxonomy of Educational Objectives, Handbook I: Cognitive Domain* was produced by a committee of educational psychologists led by Benjamin Bloom following a series of conferences from 1949 to 1953. The goal was to create a classification system for educational goals that would enable examiners and curriculum designers to communicate precisely about the level of cognitive demand their courses required.

The original six-level hierarchy, from least to most complex: (1) Knowledge — recall of specifics, methods, patterns, structures; (2) Comprehension — the lowest level of understanding, translating and interpreting; (3) Application — using abstractions in specific and concrete situations; (4) Analysis — breaking down communication into its constituents; (5) Synthesis — putting together elements to form a whole; and (6) Evaluation — making judgments about the value of material or methods. The hierarchy was designed as cumulative: higher levels presuppose the lower ones.

Bloom's committee intended the taxonomy as a communication tool for curriculum development, not as a prescription for instruction. The 1956 handbook is famously concrete in its verb lists and test-item examples for each level, which is why it became the dominant tool for writing learning objectives and assessment items in subsequent decades.

The taxonomy was revised in 2001 by Anderson and Krathwohl (see that entry), who changed the nouns to verbs, reordered the top two levels (putting Create above Evaluate), and added the Knowledge Dimension.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc cites both Bloom 1956 and Anderson & Krathwohl 2001 together as the taxonomy used for assessment-design vocabulary inside the audit. This is accurate — the 1956 taxonomy is the source of the hierarchical cognitive levels that became standard in educational practice, and the 2001 revision is the current standard form. The doc's claim that these are appropriate at "assessment-design level" but not for "program-wide mapping" is a reasonable methodological judgment, not a claim about what the taxonomy itself says.
