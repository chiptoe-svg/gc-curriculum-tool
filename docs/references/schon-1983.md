# Schön (1983) — The Reflective Practitioner

**What the background doc claims it says:** Schön established the theoretical case that professional expertise develops through cycles of reflection-in-action (adjusting during practice) and reflection-on-action (retrospective analysis).

**What it actually says (synthesis):** *The Reflective Practitioner* emerged from Donald Schön's critique of what he called "technical rationality" — the dominant epistemology of professional practice at the time, which assumed that professionals apply scientific theories and technical knowledge to solve well-defined problems. Schön observed that professional practice rarely fits this model: the most important professional problems (in architecture, medicine, management, engineering, social work) are ill-defined, values-laden, and resist technical solutions. He argued that the knowledge that actually guides professional performance is largely tacit and emerges through practice, not from pre-existing theories.

Schön identified two types of reflection that characterize expert professional practice:

**Reflection-in-action** ("thinking on one's feet"): While performing a task, a skilled practitioner encounters a surprise — something that doesn't fit existing routines. Rather than stopping the activity, the practitioner thinks about it in the moment, reframes the problem, improvises a response, and adjusts the ongoing action. This is not deliberate application of theory; it is on-the-spot problem-reframing and response, executed within the action. Schön's examples include an architect who "talks back" with her drawing — the marks she makes suggest new configurations she hadn't planned, and she responds by adjusting her design in real time.

**Reflection-on-action** (retrospective analysis): After completing an activity, practitioners reflect on what happened — why did the surprise occur, what did they do, what would they do differently. This is the deliberate, after-the-fact examination that converts experience into learning. Schön argues this is distinct from reflection-in-action (which happens during the activity) and is the primary mechanism by which professional knowledge accumulates over time.

Schön contrasts this epistemology with the laboratory-science model of professional education and argues that professional schools should cultivate the "reflective practicum" — a learning environment where students practice under expert supervision, receive coaching in reflection-in-action, and gradually develop the capacity to reframe problems as professionals do.

The book draws on case studies from architecture, psychotherapy, engineering, management consulting, and music teaching, showing common patterns in how experts think across very different professional contexts.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization — "the theoretical case that professional expertise develops through cycles of reflection-in-action (adjusting during practice) and reflection-on-action (retrospective analysis)" — is an accurate one-sentence summary of Schön's thesis. The doc uses this as the theoretical foundation for the reflection component of the three-part developmental sequence (depth → productive failure → reflection), which is a reasonable application of Schön's framework to curricular design. Schön does not make explicit curricular recommendations for undergraduate programs, so the application to structured debrief protocols is an inference, not a Schön claim.
