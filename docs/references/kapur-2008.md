# Kapur (2008) — Productive Failure

**Full citation:** Kapur, M. (2008). Productive Failure. *Cognition and Instruction*, 26(3), 379–424. The original productive-failure paradigm: students who struggle with ill-structured problems before instruction outperform direct-instruction controls on transfer.

**What the background doc claims it says:** The original productive-failure paradigm. Students who struggle with ill-structured problems before instruction outperform direct-instruction controls on transfer.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The 2008 paper in *Cognition and Instruction* provides the original "existence proof" for productive failure as a learning design paradigm. The study involved eleventh-grade science students (in Singapore) in a computer-supported collaborative learning environment working on Newtonian kinematics problems.

Two conditions: In the productive failure condition (PF), students first worked collaboratively in small groups on ill-structured, complex problems — problems without a clearly specified solution method, requiring students to use their existing knowledge in new ways. They then solved well-structured problems individually. In the comparison condition (direct instruction first, DI), students first solved well-structured problems in small groups, then moved to individual well-structured problems. Finally, all students solved ill-structured problems individually on a transfer assessment.

Key findings: Groups in the PF condition struggled — they produced poor-quality solutions to the ill-structured problems. Yet despite this failure, these students significantly outperformed the DI-first students on both near and far transfer measures taken individually afterward. The students who struggled productively in their initial group phase outperformed students who had been scaffolded toward successful solutions.

Kapur's explanation involves what he calls "activation and awareness of prior knowledge" — students in the PF condition, by attempting the problems with their existing schemas, activated and made explicit the limits and gaps in their knowledge. This activation created an "affordance for learning" in the subsequent instruction phase, which students in the DI-first condition lacked (they were scaffolded past the struggle without activating these affordances). The failure modes themselves became informative scaffolding for the canonical approach.

The paper identifies the two-phase generate-then-consolidate structure as the design unit: students first generate multiple (often wrong) solutions, then an expert consolidation phase uses those attempts as scaffolding. This is not "discovery learning" (which lacks the consolidation phase); it is a specific two-phase design.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's summary is accurate. The claim that "students who struggle with ill-structured problems before instruction outperform direct-instruction controls on transfer" is supported by the paper's findings. The doc also correctly characterizes the two-phase generate-then-consolidate structure as the design unit and later correctly notes (citing Kapur 2016) that failure *without* expert consolidation is unproductive — the mechanism is failure followed by sense-making, not failure per se.
