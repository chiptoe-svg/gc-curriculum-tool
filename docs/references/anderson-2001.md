# Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) — A Taxonomy for Learning, Teaching, and Assessing

**Full citation:** Anderson, L. W., & Krathwohl, D. R. (Eds.). (2001). *A Taxonomy for Learning, Teaching, and Assessing: A Revision of Bloom's Taxonomy of Educational Objectives*. Longman.

**Reference ID in background.html:** `ref-anderson-2001`; cited in §2, §5.

**Where it lives:**
- Publisher: Longman (Pearson). ISBN 0-8013-1903-X (abridged).
- Amazon: https://www.amazon.com/Taxonomy-Learning-Teaching-Assessing-Educational/dp/080131903X
- Open-access status: Book (commercial). Abridged edition widely available.

**Accessibility:** Partially accessible — publisher descriptions, a freely accessible Krathwohl 2002 overview article, and extensive secondary descriptions available.

**What the background doc claims it says:** The revised Bloom's taxonomy remains the right vocabulary for analyzing the cognitive demand of an assignment. The framework does not replace Bloom's verbs; it employs them inside the audit conversation. The discriminator between Bloom and KUD is scope: Bloom is granular enough for designing a single assessment; KUD is compact enough for mapping an entire program. The revised taxonomy also includes a Knowledge Dimension (Factual / Conceptual / Procedural / Metacognitive) that partially overlaps with K and U.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** Anderson and Krathwohl assembled a committee to revise Bloom's original 1956 taxonomy, resulting in a two-dimensional framework: a Cognitive Process Dimension and a Knowledge Dimension. The Cognitive Process Dimension reorganizes the original six categories into Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create — with the key change being that Synthesis (now Create) was promoted to the top and Evaluation moved down, and that noun labels became verb labels to emphasize active cognition rather than stored content.

The Knowledge Dimension is new to the 2001 revision and introduces four types of knowledge that apply across all cognitive process levels: Factual (basic disciplinary facts, terminology, elements), Conceptual (classifications, principles, generalizations, theories, models), Procedural (how to do something — specific skills, algorithms, techniques, methods of inquiry), and Metacognitive (knowledge of cognition in general as well as awareness of one's own cognition). This two-dimensional taxonomy creates a 24-cell table (4 knowledge types × 6 cognitive processes) that curriculum designers can use to classify learning objectives.

The revision explicitly frames the taxonomy as a tool for curriculum alignment — ensuring that objectives, instruction, and assessment target the same cells of the table. It provides detailed rubric language for verbs appropriate to each cognitive process level, which makes it practical for writing learning objectives.

The framework's claim that Bloom is "the right vocabulary for analyzing the cognitive demand of an assignment" is consistent with how Anderson and Krathwohl frame their work: as a tool primarily for assessment design and objective writing at the course level, not for program-wide mapping.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization of the revised taxonomy — as a six-level cognitive hierarchy with an added Knowledge Dimension, appropriate for assessment-level analysis but too granular for program-wide mapping — accurately reflects the Anderson and Krathwohl 2001 work. The claim that the Knowledge Dimension "partially overlaps with K and U" is defensible: Factual and Conceptual knowledge map onto Know and Understand respectively, while Procedural maps partially onto Do.
