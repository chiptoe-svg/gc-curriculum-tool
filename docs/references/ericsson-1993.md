# Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer (1993) — The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The 1993 paper in *Psychological Review* introduced the deliberate practice framework and is frequently cited as the most influential paper on expertise in the second half of the twentieth century. Ericsson, Krampe, and Tesch-Römer conducted a study of student violinists at the Music Academy of Berlin, comparing three groups ranked by their teacher assessments: "best violinists" (those expected to become international soloists), "good violinists" (expected to have professional orchestral careers), and "music teachers" (expected to teach rather than perform professionally). A fourth group of professional pianists provided additional data.

Central finding: Differences in retrospective estimates of accumulated deliberate practice (practice alone, working on specific technique, highly focused) corresponded closely to each skill level. Best violinists had accumulated approximately 10,000 hours of deliberate practice by age 20; good violinists approximately 7,500 hours; music teachers approximately 5,000 hours. The authors concluded that individual differences in performance could be largely explained by accumulated deliberate practice, and that talent (innate ability) played a much smaller role than popular belief held.

The paper introduced the specific definition of "deliberate practice" that distinguishes it from mere repetition: it must be (1) designed to improve specific aspects of performance, (2) highly effortful (at the limits of current ability), (3) informed by immediate feedback, and (4) accompanied by full attention. Playing pieces you already know fluently does not qualify; working through difficult passages with immediate correction does.

The paper also proposed the "10,000-hour rule" (popularly misconstrued) — not that 10,000 hours guarantees expertise, but that experts in their study had accumulated approximately 10,000 hours of deliberate practice by early adulthood.

The paper's claims were significantly challenged by subsequent meta-analyses, most notably Macnamara et al. (2014), which found that deliberate practice explained substantially less of the variance in performance than Ericsson et al. implied.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc cites this reference appropriately — as a theoretical anchor for the deliberate-practice component of the framework's extrapolation, explicitly labeled as an extrapolation rather than an empirically settled finding. The doc also immediately follows this reference with the Macnamara et al. (2014) tempering caveat, which is methodologically honest. The framing is appropriate.
