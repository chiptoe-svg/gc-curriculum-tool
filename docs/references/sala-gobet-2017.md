# Sala & Gobet (2017) — Does Far Transfer Exist?

**Full citation:** Sala, G., & Gobet, F. (2017). Does far transfer exist? Negative evidence from chess, music, and working memory training. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 26(6), 515–520. https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721417712760. A short integrative review pulling together five of the authors' meta-analyses to argue that trained cognitive skills do not generalize far.

**What the doc claims it says:** "Trained cognitive skill does not generalize far; on this evidence depth increases specificity, not portability."

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

The article is a narrative integration of five meta-analyses by Sala, Gobet, and colleagues. Two are correlational/comparison reviews of the *cognitive correlates* of expertise; three are reviews of *training experiments* in chess, music, and working-memory (WM) training. The argument is framed against a century-old backdrop: Thorndike and Woodworth's (1901) common-elements theory, which predicts near transfer is common but far transfer is rare because transfer scales with shared features between domains. The authors couple this with chunking theory (Chase & Simon, 1973) and template theory (Gobet & Simon, 1996), under which expert performance rests on tens of thousands of domain-specific perceptual chunks that are inherently non-portable.

On the correlational side, the authors acknowledge that chess players and musicians are, on average, more cognitively able than the general population: chess players' overall cognitive ability exceeds non-players by roughly half a standard deviation (a moderate effect), and chess skill correlates with fluid intelligence, processing speed, short-term/working memory, and comprehension knowledge (Burgoyne et al., 2016). But correlation does not establish that training *causes* general cognitive gains — the more parsimonious reading is selection (smarter people gravitate to and excel in these activities).

The decisive evidence is the experimental meta-analyses. Headline effect sizes look "cautiously promising" (chess overall 0.34, music 0.16, WM 0.12 SD), but the authors' central finding is that **effect size is inversely related to design quality**: when experimental groups are compared against *active* control groups (controlling for placebo/novelty), the far-transfer effects collapse to near zero. For music, the effect drops from 0.25 SD (passive controls; 95% CI [0.17, 0.34], k = 64) to 0.03 SD (active controls; 95% CI [−0.07, 0.12], k = 54). For WM training, it drops from 0.18 SD (passive) to 0.05 SD (active controls; 95% CI [−0.05, 0.15], k = 40). The single chess study with an active control showed a minimal 0.10 SD effect. The one robust survivor is a *near*-transfer effect: WM training improves performance on other memory tasks (0.44–0.46 SD) — i.e., the skill transfers to highly similar tasks but not to reasoning, intelligence, or academic outcomes.

The mechanism, on the authors' reading, is that putative far-transfer gains "probably stemmed from confounds such as placebo effects," not genuine generalization. They marshal converging null results from brain-training (Simons et al., 2016), spatial training (Xu & LeFevre, 2016), and action video games (Oei & Patterson, 2015), all of which show improvements limited to the trained or closely similar tasks — consistent with common-elements theory. They draw an explicit educational implication: curricula "should focus on discipline-related material rather than general principles" and should not expect domain-specific training to generalize (e.g., "learning Latin to improve logical thinking in mathematics"). They go further than most, recommending that researchers and policymakers consider *stopping* funding for far-transfer cognitive-training research.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The paper directly supports the document's claim that trained cognitive skill does not generalize far. The PDF's own conclusion — "far transfer of learning rarely occurs" / "far transfer remains a chimera" — and its account of chess expertise resting on non-portable domain-specific chunks map cleanly onto the doc's "depth increases specificity, not portability" gloss. One calibration note: this is a *Current Directions* review summarizing the authors' own meta-analyses rather than primary data, and its rhetorical register is strong (advocating defunding the field); a careful citation should treat it as a position-defining review, with the underlying effect sizes coming from the cited primary meta-analyses. No overstatement in how the document uses it.
