# Sala & Gobet (2017) — Does Far Transfer Exist? (Negative Evidence)

**Full citation:** Sala, G., & Gobet, F. (2017). Does Far Transfer Exist? Negative Evidence From Chess, Music, and Working Memory Training. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 26(6), 515–520. https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721417712760. A companion second-order meta-analysis — Sala, G., Aksayli, N. D., Tatlidil, K. S., Tatsumi, T., Gondo, Y., & Gobet, F. (2019). **What the background doc claims it says:** The one-line gloss in §4 reads: "Trained cognitive skill does not generalize far." In context, §4 uses this paper as the empirical pessimism anchor: the evidence that chess, music, and working-memory training — activities that produce genuine local expertise — fail to produce corresponding gains in general cognitive ability or academic skills. It is the counterweight to "pull-up / broad transfer" claims and supports the conclusion that problem-solving cannot be imported as a generic skill but must be built inside domain-specific knowledge.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** Sala & Gobet (2017) is a review paper in the brief-review format of *Current Directions in Psychological Science* that packages three meta-analyses into roughly six pages. The three meta-analyses address chess instruction, music instruction, and working-memory training, each pooling effect sizes from studies measuring outcomes on cognitive and academic skills in children and adolescents. The headline finding across all three domains is the same: apparent benefits exist when trained groups are compared only to passive controls, but effect sizes shrink toward negligible when active control groups are used — that is, when the comparison condition is an equally engaging alternative activity rather than no treatment at all. The authors describe this as an inverse relationship between effect size magnitude and experimental-design quality. They interpret the correlation of chess and music expertise with higher general cognitive ability as reflecting selection (smarter or more organized children are more likely to pursue and persist in these activities) rather than causal training benefit. The conclusion is that "far transfer of learning rarely occurs" and that the optimism driving chess-in-schools and music-in-schools programs, as well as the commercial brain-training industry, is empirically unsupported.

The 2019 second-order meta-analysis (Sala et al.) extends this argument to the broadest possible evidence base: 233 effect sizes drawn from prior meta-analyses spanning working-memory training, video games, music, chess, and exergames. A second-order meta-analysis aggregates across meta-analyses rather than primary studies, giving it power to detect systematic bias across entire literatures. The key findings are: (1) working-memory training does produce reliable near transfer to other memory tasks, with effect sizes moderated by population type; (2) far-transfer effects across all training types are small or null; and (3) when placebo effects (comparison to active rather than passive controls) and publication bias are simultaneously corrected, the overall far-transfer effect size and its true between-study variance both equal zero. The authors conclude that "the lack of generalization of skills acquired by training is thus an invariant of human cognition" — a deliberately strong formulation intended to underscore that the near-zero far-transfer finding replicates across paradigm, age group, training type, and analytic method.

A natural counterpoint in the literature is that "near" transfer — improving tasks closely related to what was trained — does occur, and the 2019 paper confirms this specifically for working-memory training on memory-adjacent tasks. The pessimism in the 2017 and 2019 papers is specifically about *far* transfer: gains on tasks that differ substantially in content, context, or modality from what was practiced. This is the distinction that matters for curriculum analysis: whether domain-specific training (printing production workflow, color management, estimating) produces general problem-solving competence. The Sala & Gobet body of work is the primary empirical case that it does not do so automatically or reliably.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the background doc's gloss ("Trained cognitive skill does not generalize far") accurately captures the central empirical finding across both papers. The 2017 meta-analytic review and the 2019 second-order meta-analysis both converge on near-zero far-transfer effects after methodological corrections, precisely as the gloss implies. The role the paper plays in §4 — as the empirical counterweight to pull-up transfer claims — is also accurate: Sala & Gobet do not argue that domain expertise fails to form, but that it fails to spill over to dissimilar domains, which is the specific pessimism §4 invokes. The gloss slightly compresses nuance (near transfer is real; far transfer is the null case) but the compression does not misrepresent how the citation functions in the argument.
