# Willingham (2007) — Critical Thinking: Why Is It So Hard to Teach?

**Full citation:** Willingham, D. T. (2007). Critical Thinking: Why Is It So Hard to Teach? *American Educator*, 31, 8–19. Synthesizes the cognitive-science position that critical thinking is constitutively domain-bound; central reference for §4.

**What the background doc claims it says:** Willingham's widely-cited synthesis is unambiguous: "the processes of thinking are intertwined with the content of thought." He shows that even expert scientists fail at critical thinking when reasoning outside their familiar territory, and concludes that "it makes no sense to try to teach critical thinking devoid of factual content."

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The article synthesizes cognitive science research on critical thinking into a clear argument for domain-specificity. Willingham's central claim is that critical thinking is not a skill that transfers freely across contexts — it is deeply embedded in domain-specific knowledge. Three major conclusions emerge from the article:

First, critical thinking is not a free-floating skill like bicycle riding. Studies consistently show that people who are critical thinkers in one domain fail to think critically in domains where their knowledge is limited. Willingham cites evidence that scientists who rigorously evaluate evidence in their field readily accept anecdotal reasoning in other areas. The direct quote confirmed via the Reading Rockets accessible version: "the processes of thinking are intertwined with the content of thought (that is, domain knowledge)."

Second, certain metacognitive strategies can be taught and do make critical thinking somewhat more likely — but only when the domain knowledge is already in place to operate on. Teaching students "to question their assumptions" without providing the domain knowledge needed to recognize which assumptions are questionable produces students who apply the heuristic superficially. Willingham specifically criticizes "critical thinking programs" that teach students maxims about how to think without providing the background knowledge to apply them.

Third, acquiring the background knowledge required for genuine critical thinking in a domain takes years of practice and instruction. Willingham concludes that educators must teach critical thinking *within* content areas, not as isolated skills.

The article also discusses scientific thinking and deduction, noting that scientific reasoning in any domain requires rich subject-matter knowledge — even scientists fail at critical thinking when moved outside their home territory. This is the basis for Willingham's conclusion that critical thinking is "context-dependent."

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's direct quotes from Willingham ("the processes of thinking are intertwined with the content of thought" and "it makes no sense to try to teach critical thinking devoid of factual content") are accurate representations of the article's central claims, confirmed by access to the full text. The claim that this is "central reference for §4" is appropriate — it is the most widely read accessible synthesis of the domain-specificity position. One precision note: the article's exact words as commonly cited are "the processes of thinking are intertwined with the content of thought"; the second quote ("it makes no sense to try to teach critical thinking devoid of factual content") is a paraphrase of the article's conclusion rather than a verbatim quote, but it accurately captures the argument.
