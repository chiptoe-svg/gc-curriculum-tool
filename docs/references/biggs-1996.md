# Biggs (1996) — Enhancing Teaching through Constructive Alignment

**Full citation:** Biggs, J. (1996). Enhancing teaching through constructive alignment. *Higher Education*, 32(3), 347–364.

**Reference ID in background.html:** `ref-biggs-1996`; cited in §2.

**Where it lives:**
- DOI/URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00138871
- Open-access status: Paywall (Springer). A freely available copy exists at https://www.cs.auckland.ac.nz/courses/compsci747s2c/lectures/teaching-through-constructive-alignment.pdf

**Accessibility:** Partially accessible — abstract from Springer paywall; full text accessible via the Auckland University course mirror.

**What the background doc claims it says:** Biggs's principle of constructive alignment requires that intended outcomes, teaching and learning activities, and assessment all support one another. The framework's per-course audit conversation is shaped by this principle: mismatches between stated objectives and material evidence become first-class findings rather than silent inconsistencies. The audit conversation is one specific way to apply Biggs's principle, not the only one.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** This 1996 paper in *Higher Education* introduced the term "constructive alignment" and is the original scholarly statement of the concept, later expanded in Biggs and Tang (2011). The paper synthesizes two streams of educational theory: constructivism (knowledge is constructed by the learner's activities, not transmitted wholesale from teacher to student) and instructional design's emphasis on alignment between objectives, teaching, and assessment.

Constructive alignment as Biggs defines it requires three components to reinforce one another: (1) intended learning outcomes stated in terms of performances that represent a suitably high cognitive level, (2) teaching and learning activities judged likely to elicit those performances from students, and (3) assessment tasks that require students to demonstrate the intended performances (enabling summative judgment of whether the outcome was achieved). The critical design commitment is that the assessment must directly test the outcome, not act as a proxy — and that all three components must be consistent with each other.

The paper argues that misalignment is endemic in higher education: institutions state high-level outcomes (e.g., "critical thinking") but then deliver didactic teaching that models only low-level recall, and assess via examinations that test reproduction of facts. Students rationally respond by surface-learning rather than deep-learning. Constructive alignment breaks this cycle by making the assessment demand — not the stated objective — the actual driver of what students study and how they are taught.

Biggs distinguishes "content coverage" from "learning outcomes" and argues the latter must always be specified in terms of verifiable student performances. The paper uses the SOLO (Structure of the Observed Learning Outcome) taxonomy to grade the sophistication of those performances, though this aspect is expanded more fully in Biggs and Tang (2011).

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's description of constructive alignment — that outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment must all support one another, and that mismatches between stated objectives and material evidence are the central problem the audit surfaces — is an accurate paraphrase of Biggs's core argument. The doc's specific claim that the audit conversation surfaces mismatches as "first-class findings" is a direct application of the constructive alignment principle.
