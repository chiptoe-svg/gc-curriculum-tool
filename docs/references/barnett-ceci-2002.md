# Barnett & Ceci (2002) — A Taxonomy for Far Transfer

**Full citation:** Barnett, S. M., & Ceci, S. J. (2002). When and Where Do We Apply What We Learn? A Taxonomy for Far Transfer. *Psychological Bulletin*, 128(4), 612–637. Argues that a century of inconclusive far-transfer research stems from comparing "apples and oranges," and proposes a multi-dimensional taxonomy (content × context) for specifying exactly *how far* a given transfer claim reaches.

**What the doc claims it says:** Maps the content/context dimensions far transfer must bridge and why distant transfer is rarely demonstrated.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The article opens by juxtaposing the unresolved debate — Judd's "every experience has in it the possibilities of generalization" against Thorndike & Woodworth's denial of a "subtle transfer of practice effect," and modern echoes (Halpern: critical thinking "can be learned in ways that promote transfer"; Detterman: reviewers are "in almost total agreement that little transfer occurs"). Barnett and Ceci argue this century-long stalemate persists because researchers have failed to specify *which* kind of transfer, across *what* distance, they are claiming or denying — so studies that are not comparable get treated as if they bear on the same question. Their remedy is a taxonomy that situates each study at the intersection of multiple dimensions, making clear that "estimation of a single effect size for far transfer is misguided."

**The taxonomy — two top-level groupings.** The framework organizes transfer along (a) **content** — what is transferred — and (b) **context** — how far the transfer reaches across situations.

*Content dimensions* specify the nature of what is carried over and how it is measured:
- **Learned skill** — the nature of what is transferred, ranging from a specific procedure or fact to a general principle, heuristic, or attitude.
- **Performance change** — what is actually measured (speed, accuracy, or a change in the *approach* taken).
- **Memory demands** — whether the transfer task requires the learner to retrieve the relevant knowledge unaided (a "sequestered" cold test) or supports recognition/use of it. Barnett and Ceci stress that the memory demands of the test are routinely confounded with the question of whether transfer occurred, and that what looks like a failure of transfer is often a failure of spontaneous *retrieval*.

*Context dimensions* specify the distance between the training and transfer situations along six axes:
- **Knowledge domain** (e.g., mouse vs. rat → biology vs. economics, increasingly distant)
- **Physical context** (same room → different building → different institution)
- **Temporal context** (same session → next day → months later)
- **Functional context** (academic → academic vs. academic → play vs. academic → workplace)
- **Social context** (individual → individual vs. individual → small group → broad social)
- **Modality** (e.g., same format → book learning to a hands-on test; written to oral)

A given transfer claim is then plotted as a point in this space: "near" transfer keeps most dimensions constant, while genuinely "far" transfer moves on many of them at once (a different domain, much later, in a different physical and functional context, in a different modality). The authors illustrate with the canonical cases — Judd's underwater-dart-throwing refraction study (transfer across a depth change with a theoretical principle), the radar-troubleshooting recruit, the baker using school fractions — to show how each occupies a different cell.

**Why distant transfer is rarely demonstrated.** The taxonomy reframes the pessimism. Barnett and Ceci's reading of 100 years of research is that "evidence for transfer under some conditions is substantial, but critical conditions for many key questions are untested." Transfer is well documented when the training and transfer contexts are close on most dimensions; the cells corresponding to *simultaneously* large jumps across many dimensions — true far transfer — are sparsely populated and weakly supported, partly because such studies are rarely run and partly because, when run, they typically impose high memory demands (unaided retrieval) that depress measured transfer. So the apparent rarity of far transfer is jointly a fact about the phenomenon (the farther the contextual leap, the harder it is) and an artifact of how it is measured and which cells researchers have actually probed. The contribution is not a new experiment but a coordinate system that lets the field stop comparing apples and oranges and identify precisely where evidence is strong, weak, or absent.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's one-line claim — that the paper "maps the content/context dimensions far transfer must bridge and why distant transfer is rarely demonstrated" — is an accurate compression of the article. Barnett and Ceci do exactly this: they decompose transfer into content dimensions (skill type, performance change, memory demands) and six context dimensions, and they argue that genuine far transfer is rarely demonstrated both because large multi-dimensional leaps are intrinsically hard and because the relevant cells are under-tested and saddled with high memory demands. Nothing in the doc's gloss overstates the source.
