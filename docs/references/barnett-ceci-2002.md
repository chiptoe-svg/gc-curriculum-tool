# Barnett & Ceci (2002) — A Taxonomy for Far Transfer

**Full citation:** Barnett, S. M., & Ceci, S. J. (2002). When and Where Do We Apply What We Learn? A Taxonomy for Far Transfer. *Psychological Bulletin*, 128(4), 612–637. Proposes nine dimensions spanning what is transferred (content) and when/where transfer is applied (context), arguing that a century of inconclusive debate over far transfer stems from comparing studies that differ along these dimensions without specifying how.

**What the background doc claims it says:** The one-line gloss reads: "Maps the content/context dimensions far transfer must bridge and why distant transfer is rarely demonstrated." In §4's argument, this is used to establish that far transfer — applying learning across domain, physical, temporal, functional, social, or modality distance — is the hard case, and that the difficulty of far transfer implies problem-solving cannot be taught as a generic transferable skill but must be built within domain-specific knowledge.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The paper opens from an acknowledged impasse: despite more than a century of research on transfer, the field had made little progress on whether "far transfer" — the application of prior learning to significantly different contexts — actually occurs. Barnett and Ceci argue the root cause of this impasse is a definitional one: researchers have used "near" and "far" transfer as if they named a single binary distinction, when in reality any transfer event can be situated along nine distinct dimensions. Comparing studies that differ along multiple unspecified dimensions produces apples-to-oranges confusion rather than cumulative knowledge.

Their taxonomy organizes these nine dimensions into two categories. **Content dimensions** — what is transferred — comprise three: (1) *Learned skill*, ranging from a specific procedure to a more general principle; (2) *Performance change*, covering whether the measure is speed, accuracy, or approach; and (3) *Memory demands*, distinguishing whether transfer occurs spontaneously or only with hints and prompts. **Context dimensions** — when and where transfer is applied — comprise six: (4) *Knowledge domain* (same subject vs. different subject area); (5) *Physical context* (same location vs. different setting); (6) *Temporal context* (immediate vs. delayed application); (7) *Functional context* (academic vs. real-world setting); (8) *Social context* (same group composition vs. different social arrangement); and (9) *Modality* (same sensory/task format vs. different). "Far" transfer means crossing large distance on multiple dimensions simultaneously; "near" transfer means remaining close on most.

The authors then apply this taxonomy to reanalyze fourteen well-known transfer studies from the existing literature. Their empirical conclusion is cautious rather than simply pessimistic: they find that the evidence for transfer varies dramatically depending on which dimensions are crossed. Crucially, they write that as of 2002, the answer to how generalizable cognitive training is from one domain to another is "not very much" — distant transfer across multiple context dimensions remains the least-evidenced case. Many of the most important dimensional combinations for understanding cross-domain transfer had never been studied at all: "critical conditions for many key questions are untested." The paper does not claim far transfer never occurs; it claims the field lacks the dimensional precision to know when and where it occurs, and that the empirically supported cases cluster toward near rather than far ends of the dimensions.

The secondary literature treats this paper as one of the anchor references for the proposition that far transfer is rarely and inconsistently demonstrated in practice, which is consistent with the paper's own tone and the direct quotation from pp. 613–614 that circulates in the literature.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the background doc's gloss ("maps the content/context dimensions far transfer must bridge and why distant transfer is rarely demonstrated") accurately captures both contributions: the taxonomy is precisely that map of content and context dimensions, and the empirical review does support the conclusion that distant transfer is rarely and inconsistently demonstrated. The gloss slightly compresses the paper's nuance (Barnett and Ceci are more "the evidence is thin and the question underspecified" than "transfer never happens"), but this compression does not misrepresent how the paper functions in §4's argument about the domain-specificity of problem-solving.
