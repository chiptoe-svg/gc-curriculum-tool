# Kapur (2016) — Examining Productive Failure, Productive Success, Unproductive Failure, and Unproductive Success in Learning

**Full citation:** Kapur, M. (2016). Examining Productive Failure, Productive Success, Unproductive Failure, and Unproductive Success in Learning. *Educational Psychologist*, 51(2), 289–299. The conceptual paper that introduces the productive/unproductive distinction and identifies the two-phase generate-then-consolidate structure as the design unit.

**What the background doc claims it says:** This conceptual paper introduces the productive/unproductive distinction and identifies the two-phase generate-then-consolidate structure as the design unit. Kapur (2016) explicitly distinguishes productive from *unproductive* failure — failure without expert consolidation, without structured debrief, produces no learning advantage and can damage motivation and self-efficacy. The mechanism is not failure as such; it is failure followed by sense-making.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The 2016 paper in *Educational Psychologist* is a theoretical and conceptual contribution to the productive failure literature. Rather than reporting an experiment, it provides a 2×2 framework for analyzing instructional conditions along two dimensions: (1) whether initial activity performance is high or low, and (2) whether learning outcomes (retention, transfer) are high or low. This creates four quadrants:

**Productive Failure:** Initial performance is poor (students fail at the task), but long-term learning outcomes are strong. This is the signature condition Kapur's research has identified — struggle before consolidation.

**Productive Success:** Initial performance is high and learning outcomes are also high. Possible in principle, but the paper argues that highly scaffolded instructional designs that produce immediate success often do so by giving students the answers (removing the effortful processing that produces durable learning), making them unproductive success instead.

**Unproductive Failure:** Initial performance is poor and learning outcomes are also poor. Failure that is not followed by expert consolidation produces no learning advantage. This is the crucial distinction the background doc references — unscaffolded discovery learning, poorly designed problems, or failure without structured debriefing all fall here.

**Unproductive Success:** Initial performance is high but learning outcomes are weak. The most common condition in traditional direct instruction, Kapur argues — students succeed at correctly executing procedures during instruction (or at imitating the worked examples) but cannot transfer or retain the knowledge. The paper argues that traditional direct instruction, despite appearing as a "success" in the learning session, is often unproductive success.

The paper's key design principle: the goal of instruction should be to maximize productive learning outcomes, even at the expense of immediate performance during instruction. A learning environment that looks like it is going well (students succeeding on practice tasks) may be producing unproductive success; one that looks difficult (students failing) may be producing productive failure.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's description is accurate: the paper does introduce the productive/unproductive distinction, and it does identify the generate-then-consolidate two-phase structure as the design unit. The doc's claim that "Kapur (2016) explicitly distinguishes productive from unproductive failure — failure without expert consolidation produces no learning advantage and can damage motivation and self-efficacy" is substantiated — the paper's argument hinges on the consolidation phase as the necessary second step that converts struggle into productive learning. The background doc correctly summarizes the mechanism as "failure followed by sense-making, not failure as such."
