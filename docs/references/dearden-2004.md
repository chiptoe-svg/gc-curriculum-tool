# Dearden, McGranahan & Sianesi (2004) — Returns to NVQs at Level 2 (CEE DP46)

**Full citation:** Dearden, L., McGranahan, L., & Sianesi, B. (2004). *An In-Depth Analysis of the Returns to National Vocational Qualifications Obtained at Level 2* (CEE Discussion Paper 46 / CEEDP0046). London: Centre for the Economics of Education, London School of Economics. An econometric study of the wage and employment returns to NVQ Level 2 using the 1970 British Cohort Study (BCS70) and the Labour Force Survey (LFS).

**Reference ID in background.html:** `ref-dearden-2004`; cited in §11 (occupational frameworks).

**Where it lives:**
- Publisher: Centre for the Economics of Education (CEE), an independent research centre based at the LSE and funded by the (then) Department for Education and Skills. Discussion Paper series; ISBN 07530 1740 46. No journal DOI (grey-literature working paper).
- Open-access status: Open access. Free PDF via CEE/LSE (cee.lse.ac.uk) and mirrored by IFS, with which the authors are affiliated.

**Accessibility:** Clean born-digital working-paper PDF; the Executive Summary, Introduction, and Sections 2–3 (read here) carry the headline findings; full regression tables run to p. 53 with appendices.

**What the doc claims it says:** Per §11, "The clearest empirical datum is the UK's National Vocational Qualifications (1986–2015): an explicit competency-checklist system that **measurably hurt graduates' labour-market prospects (IFS)** and was ultimately retired."

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

The paper documents poor-to-negative *wage returns* to NVQ Level 2, but it is markedly more cautious and qualified than the doc's "measurably hurt prospects" framing. The Executive Summary opens by noting that prior LFS work found NVQ holders at levels 1 and 2 have "statistically significantly *lower* wage levels than otherwise similar individuals who lack NVQs, the estimates for NVQs at level 1 and 2 falling between –5 and –20 percent lower wages." In the authors' own BCS70 estimates, "controlling for all other qualifications held, men with NVQ2s earn 10% less per hour and women 9% less" (p. 3); after adding ability and family-background controls these move to roughly "–9% ... men ... –7% ... women" (p. 4). So "poor-to-negative returns" is well supported.

But the paper's distinctive contribution is to *interrogate the causal interpretation*, and it repeatedly resists the "NVQ2 harms you" reading. On ability bias: "individuals who hold NVQs are more likely to be of low ability... and less likely to be of high ability" (p. 4); controlling for ability and family background moves estimates "in the direction predicted" (toward zero), though "the changes in the magnitude of the estimates are quite small." When the comparison group is restricted to the policy-relevant population (those with no other, or only low-level, qualifications), returns "rise somewhat" and become statistically insignificant — "men with NVQ2s earn about the same or slightly more than men with no qualifications and that women with NVQ2s earn about the same or slightly less" (p. 5). The authors find "the negative returns to NVQ2s are concentrated among those of high ability who gain the qualification" (p. 7) and that returns "appear to be higher for individuals who are worse off."

The paper also documents substantial *heterogeneity* and positive sub-group returns that cut against any blanket "hurt prospects" claim: "substantial positive returns for some sub-groups and sectors," including "women in public administration, education and health and for male plant and machine operatives," and positive returns "for those who procure their NVQ2 at their place of employment." The large negatives are concentrated elsewhere: "This is in sharp contrast to NVQ2 holders who received government training who experience large negative returns" (Exec. Summary). And NVQ2 functions as a *stepping-stone*: "individuals receiving NVQ2s being more likely than similar individuals without NVQ2s to move on and obtain level 3 qualifications."

Most tellingly, the authors explicitly reject the causal-harm interpretation: "It is hard however to believe that earning an NVQ at level 1 or 2 should actively reduce earnings" (Exec. Summary). They favour selection-on-unobservables explanations — "individuals who need to go on a course to learn a job skill may... be less naturally gifted at their job than those who can do their job without training," and the comparison-group problem that employed non-NVQ workers may already be operating at NVQ3-equivalent competence. The headline takeaway is that NVQ2 offers "no benefit to most recipients," in "stark contrast to the large benefits... from most other Level 2 vocational qualifications" (City & Guilds Craft, BTEC, RSA First Diplomas, apprenticeships) — i.e., the critique is specific to NVQ2, not vocational qualifications generally.

Two further accuracy flags for §11. (1) The "1986–2015 ... ultimately retired" framing is outside this paper's scope: the paper (2004) describes the first NVQ as awarded in 1988 and ~four million awarded by March 2003, but says nothing about the system's later retirement. (2) The bare "(IFS)" attribution is loose. This is a *Centre for the Economics of Education / LSE* Discussion Paper (DP46); the authors are based at the Institute for Fiscal Studies, and IFS hosts/mirrors it, but the correct attribution is Dearden, McGranahan & Sianesi (2004), CEE/LSE Discussion Paper 46.

**Verdict:** Partially inconsistent. The paper supports "poor-to-negative wage returns to NVQ Level 2" (roughly –5% to –20% raw; ~–7% to –9% after controls), but it does **not** support the causal claim that NVQs "measurably hurt graduates' labour-market prospects." The authors explicitly state it is "hard... to believe that earning an NVQ... should actively reduce earnings," attribute much of the negative to ability/selection bias (returns moving toward zero — and insignificant — once ability and an appropriate low-qualification comparison group are used), find positive returns for several sub-groups and for workplace-obtained NVQ2s, concentrate the large negatives in *government-training* routes, and document a stepping-stone effect to Level 3. The doc also (a) imports an out-of-scope "1986–2015 ... retired" timeline this paper does not make, and (b) mis-attributes the source as "(IFS)" when it is CEE/LSE DP46. Suggested rewrite for §11: "The UK's NVQ Level 2 — an employer-led, outcome/competency-based qualification — has been associated with poor-to-negative wage returns: Dearden, McGranahan & Sianesi (2004, CEE/LSE Discussion Paper 46) estimate returns of roughly –7% to –9% after controls, though they attribute much of this to ability/selection bias rather than to the qualification causing harm, and find returns close to zero for the policy-relevant low-qualification population, with the large negatives concentrated in government-training routes and an offsetting stepping-stone effect toward Level 3."
