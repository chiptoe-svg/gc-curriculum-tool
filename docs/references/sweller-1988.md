# Sweller (1988) — Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning

**Full citation:** Sweller, J. (1988). Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning. *Cognitive Science*, 12(2), 257–285. The mechanistic account: open-ended problem-solving imposes working-memory load that crowds out schema acquisition; sequence worked examples before ill-structured problems.

**What the background doc claims it says:** Conventional open-ended problem-solving imposes high working-memory load, leaving little capacity for the schema acquisition that would eventually enable independent expertise. The remediation is not to remove the difficulty but to sequence it — worked examples and direct instruction early, when there is no schema base to activate; ill-structured problems later, once enough domain knowledge exists for the struggle to produce learning.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** This paper is the founding text of Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) and is one of the most influential papers in educational psychology. Sweller begins from the observation that domain expertise is largely a matter of possessing schemas — cognitive structures that allow experts to recognize problem states as belonging to categories for which they know solution strategies. Novices, lacking schemas, must use means-ends analysis (backward-chaining from goal to current state), which imposes high cognitive load on working memory.

The central paradox Sweller identifies: conventional open-ended problem-solving is the standard tool used to develop expertise, but it is not an efficient means of acquiring the schemas that expertise requires. The reason is structural — means-ends analysis occupies working memory with goal management (maintaining subgoals, tracking progress toward the target state) rather than with schema formation (recognizing patterns in the problem that correspond to principles). Students working hard problems without schemas learn how to manage the problem-solving process, not the domain principles they need.

Sweller's proposed remedy is the *worked example effect*: novices learn more efficiently from studying worked examples (step-by-step solutions that expose the solution structure) than from attempting to solve equivalent problems themselves. Worked examples allow the learner to focus attention on understanding the principle, not managing the solution process. A computational model and experimental evidence with mathematics problems support this claim.

The paper also introduces the concept of *intrinsic*, *extraneous*, and *germane* cognitive load — though the full three-way distinction is developed in later papers with Chandler and Paas. The 1988 paper focuses on the distinction between load that serves schema acquisition (good) and load that crowds it out (bad).

The framework's implication is that instructional sequencing matters deeply: worked examples and direct instruction should precede open-ended problem-solving in any domain where schemas are being built from scratch. This is the mechanistic complement to Kapur's productive failure research: Sweller explains why unsupported open-ended problem-solving is inefficient *before* schemas exist; Kapur's work identifies conditions under which problem-solving *before* instruction improves transfer *when* minimal prior knowledge is present.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization — "open-ended problem-solving imposes working-memory load that crowds out schema acquisition; sequence worked examples before ill-structured problems" — is accurate. The doc's specific framing — "the remediation is not to remove the difficulty but to sequence it" — is a reasonable interpretation of CLT's implications and is consistent with how Sweller's work is applied in instructional design. The doc also correctly positions Sweller's work in relation to Kapur (Kapur's productive failure requires a schema base to be effective), which reflects the standard reconciliation of CLT with the productive failure literature.
