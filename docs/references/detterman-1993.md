**What it actually says (synthesis — from knowledge, not a held copy):**

Detterman's chapter opens the *Transfer on Trial* volume by deliberately taking the most skeptical possible stance — the "case for the prosecution" — against the idea that transfer of learning is common or robust. His central claims, as widely cited, are these. First, that despite transfer being treated as the central goal of education and a cornerstone assumption of cognitive theory, the empirical record for *spontaneous*, *significant* transfer to genuinely novel situations is remarkably thin: in study after study, subjects who have learned a principle or solved an analogous problem fail to apply it to a new problem unless they are explicitly cued or the surface similarity is high. Second — and this is the source of the "epiphenomenon" label — that what looks like transfer in many demonstrations is an artifact of experimental design: experimenters arrange conditions, hints, and near-identical surface features such that the "transfer" is really just direct application of something the subject was effectively told to apply, or it reflects general intelligence rather than the trained content per se.

His most-quoted positions are deliberately provocative. He argues that significant transfer is rare, that most demonstrations of transfer are better explained by similarity of the two situations (echoing Thorndike's identical-elements view) or by general ability, and — in the line for which the chapter is famous — roughly that the proper conclusion from the literature is that people generally *do not* transfer, and that when they do, it is because the situations are so similar that little is being "transferred" in any interesting sense. He treats the burden of proof as resting on those who claim transfer, and finds that burden largely unmet. The constructive corollary, developed across the volume, is that transfer, where it occurs, must be deliberately taught and supported (a position other contributors such as Brown, and later Bransford & Schwartz, push back on by reframing what counts as transfer).

The chapter functions in the literature as the rhetorical anchor of the "transfer pessimism" camp. It is regularly paired with Thorndike & Woodworth (1901) as the historical-and-modern bookends of the skeptical tradition, and with later meta-analytic work (e.g., Sala & Gobet, 2017; Sala et al., 2019) as the empirical confirmation of the position Detterman stated polemically. The mechanism Detterman implies is the same identity-of-elements logic that runs through the whole skeptical lineage: people respond to specific situations with specifically learned responses, and the appearance of broad, content-free transfer is mostly an illusion produced by shared elements, cueing, or g.
