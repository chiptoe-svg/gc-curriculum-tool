# Tomlinson (1999/2014) — The Differentiated Classroom

**Full citation:** Tomlinson, C. A. (1999). *The Differentiated Classroom: Responding to the Needs of All Learners*. ASCD. (2nd ed., 2014.) Establishes the K/U/D three-category planning vocabulary that this framework adopts as a categorical structure. Tomlinson uses K/U/D as a pre-unit planning aid for differentiated instruction in K–12 contexts; the depth-scale extension and the program-mapping application are this framework's contributions, not Tomlinson's.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** Carol Ann Tomlinson's *The Differentiated Classroom* is a widely-adopted K–12 teaching guide that establishes a practical framework for differentiated instruction — the practice of tailoring teaching to students' varying readiness levels, interests, and learning profiles within a single classroom. The book's central contribution is the operational KUD planning vocabulary: before designing a unit, teachers explicitly articulate what they want students to Know (key facts, vocabulary, dates, definitions — content that can be recalled), Understand (transferable principles, big ideas, the "why" behind content — insights that endure), and Do (skills and processes — what students can do with their knowledge).

Tomlinson's KUD framework is a pre-unit planning tool, not a measurement instrument. Teachers use it to clarify their instructional priorities before designing lessons, ensuring that factual coverage (Know), conceptual development (Understand), and skill development (Do) are each intentionally addressed in unit design. The three categories are explicitly not hierarchical — the framework does not claim that Do requires Know and Understand, or that Understand is more important than Know. They are simply different types of outcomes that a well-designed unit should address.

Tomlinson does not attach numerical depth scales to any of the three categories, and the framework was not designed for program-wide mapping across a 28-course curriculum. The KUD vocabulary was designed to help a single teacher at the K–12 level think more clearly about what a specific unit is intended to develop before designing its lessons.

Secondary sources confirm that Tomlinson's KUD framework is widely taught in teacher-preparation programs, particularly in the context of differentiation, and is one of the most commonly cited pre-unit planning structures in American K–12 practice.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc is notably transparent about the limits of this citation: it explicitly states that "the depth-scale extension and the program-mapping application are this framework's contributions, not Tomlinson's." This is accurate. Tomlinson does provide the three-category KUD vocabulary, and the background doc correctly identifies this as the borrowed element. The doc does not over-claim Tomlinson's work as validating program-level mapping or numerical depth scales.
