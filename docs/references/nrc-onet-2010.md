# National Research Council (2010) — Review of O*NET (A Database for a Changing Economy)

**Full citation:** National Research Council. (2010). *A Database for a Changing Economy: Review of the Occupational Information Network (O*NET)* (N. T. Tippins & M. L. Hilton, Eds.). Washington, DC: The National Academies Press. https://doi.org/10.17226/12814. An expert-panel evaluation, commissioned by the U.S. Department of Labor, of the content, classification, data-collection, and uses of the O*NET occupational database.

**What the doc claims it says:** Per §11, the report holds that the Skills domain "lacks the extensive research base" supporting O*NET's Abilities taxonomy and smears "problem solving" redundantly across Skills, Abilities, Work Styles, and Work Activities, so raters cannot reliably distinguish adjacent descriptors.

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

The quoted phrase is faithful to the source. The Executive Summary states, as a formal Conclusion: "The construct validity of the taxonomies of descriptors varies across the different domains included in the content model. In the abilities domain, the descriptors reflect a long history of psychological research on the nature and measurement of human abilities, but many of the descriptors in the skills domain lack such an extensive research base" (p. 2). The same sentence is repeated nearly verbatim in the Chapter 2 Conclusions and Recommendations (p. 42). So the contrast the document draws — Abilities resting on a deep psychometric literature (Fleishman-tradition human-abilities research) while Skills do not — is exactly the panel's stated finding, not an embellishment.

The redundancy and rater-reliability points are also well grounded, with one terminology nuance worth flagging. The Chapter 2 Conclusions continue: "the different domains lack detailed and concrete specifications and often include descriptors with the same name, making it difficult to distinguish among them. 'Problem solving' appears in the Abilities, Skills, Work Styles, and GWA domains. Although there may legitimately be both a problem-solving Ability and a problem-solving Skill, the content model does not clearly distinguish the different meanings of problem solving in these two different domains" (p. 42). The doc's phrase "Work Activities" maps to O*NET's Generalized Work Activities (GWA) domain — the same thing the report abbreviates GWA — so the four-domain list (Skills, Abilities, Work Styles, Work Activities/GWA) is accurate. The panel's worry is precisely that the same descriptor label recurs across domains without concrete operational distinctions.

The rater-reliability link is the panel's own causal claim, not an inference the doc imposed. The recommendation that DOL "commission research to improve the content model, beginning with the skills and knowledge taxonomies" sets goals to "reduce the redundancy of descriptors within and across taxonomies, distinguish more clearly among the taxonomies" (p. 43). The panel then explains the mechanism directly: research is needed to determine whether better taxonomies "would yield clearer and better differentiated factor structures. However, if the descriptors in a domain lack clear, distinct, and meaningful specifications, raters will have trouble distinguishing among them, and fewer distinguishable factors would result" (pp. 43–44). It further suggests analyzing "which descriptors in each domain yield high standard errors across raters" as a diagnostic. So the doc's chain — weak research base → redundant/overlapping descriptors → raters can't reliably distinguish them — tracks the report's reasoning.

Two caveats keep the representation honest. First, the report's overarching verdict on O*NET is positive ("O*NET is used and useful"; the panel praises DOL for building a publicly funded, nationally representative database). The Skills/redundancy critique is a targeted content-model weakness inside an otherwise favorable review, so §11 should not let the critique imply the panel rejected O*NET. Second, the panel is careful that the problem is *under-specification and lack of recent factor-analytic evidence*, not a demonstrated finding of low inter-rater reliability for the Skills domain; the rater-distinguishability claim is framed as a predicted consequence and a research need, not a measured result.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc faithfully represents the source: the "lacks such an extensive research base" phrase is verbatim, the four-domain redundancy of "problem solving" is exactly the panel's example (with "Work Activities" being the correct gloss of O*NET's GWA domain), and the rater-distinguishability point reflects the panel's own stated mechanism and recommendation. The only thing to guard against in §11 is tone: this is a targeted content-model critique embedded in an overall favorable review, and the rater-reliability claim is a predicted/research-need consequence rather than a measured low-reliability finding — phrasing should not overstate it as an empirical demonstration.
