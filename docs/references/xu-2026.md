**What it actually says (synthesis):**

The design figures are accurate. The study builds "a benchmark dataset of 7,600 pairs of curriculum-competency alignment scores" (Study Context / Abstract) from "200 curriculum documents annotated across the three frameworks (38 competencies in total)" (Human Annotation). The arithmetic checks out: 200 courses × 38 competencies = 7,600 pairs. The 200 documents are five curriculum data types of 40 samples each (concise course description, detailed course description, learning objective, instructional schedule, learning activity content), drawn from Open Syllabus, public general catalogs, and a university Canvas LMS. The three competency frameworks are listed in Table 2: O*NET Workforce Competencies (US, 2013, 21 competencies), EU Key Competences (2018, 8), and ESDC Success Model (Canada, 2024, 9) — summing to 38. So "200 courses × 38 competencies across three frameworks incl. O*NET" is exactly right, and O*NET is one of the three.

The inter-rater-reliability (IRR) finding is genuine and notably framework-dependent. Two graduate annotators scored a calibration set; the reported Cohen's κ was "0.841 in the framework of EU (Key Competences), a κ of 0.288 in the framework of O*NET (Workforce Competencies), and a κ of 0.168 in the framework of ESDC (Success Model)" (Annotation and inter-rater reliability). After refining coding guidelines (notably how to use the 0 = "clearly unrelated" vs. NA = "insufficient information" codes, and whether course context should inform inference), final IRR rose to "κ = 0.94 in EU (Key Competences), κ = 0.92 in O*NET (Workforce Competencies), and κ = 0.92 in ESDC (Success Model)." The substantive point — that finer-grained frameworks (O*NET's distinctions such as "written expression" vs. "written comprehension") are harder to rate reliably and more prone to "insufficient information" (NA) than broad ones — is made directly in RQ1.

The "keyword-inference-inflation" finding maps to the paper's overestimation / over-interpretation results. The regression analysis (Table 6) reports that "after controlling for task context, LLMs generally overestimate the extent to which a course covers a given competency," with "positive and statistically significant intercepts across all classification granularities," and that "word count is positively associated with score overestimation, suggesting that longer or more content-rich curriculum documents lead LLMs to infer higher levels of competency coverage." The Error analysis names "Over-interpretation" as the first recurring error pattern: "LLMs tend to overinterpret available content, while human raters are more rigorous in seeking explicit pedagogical evidence" — e.g., "when a course document mentions an exam... LLMs tend to default to the assumption that exams are writing-based, leading them to confidently infer writing competencies." The Curricular CoT prompting strategy is reported to yield "modest improvements by reducing bias in instructional keyword inference and improving the detection of nuanced pedagogical evidence in long text" (Abstract). This is precisely "keyword-inference-inflation": surface keyword cues inflating inferred competency coverage beyond what explicit evidence supports.

The connection to an "evidence-above-zero rule" is interpretive but well motivated by the source. The human rubric (Table 3) is an ordinal 0–3 scale (3 = explicitly stated; 2 = reasonably inferred; 1 = vaguely implied; 0 = clearly unrelated; NA = insufficient information). The paper's core caution — that LLMs (and, by extension, naive keyword mapping) systematically over-credit competency coverage from thin or keyword-laden text, whereas human raters demand explicit pedagogical evidence — is a direct empirical basis for a rule that competency claims should require evidence above the zero/vague-implication floor rather than mere keyword presence.

**Verdict:** Consistent (after the 2026-06-20 background.html correction; see Audit history). The structural claims are exactly correct — 200 courses × 38 competencies = 7,600 pairs, across three frameworks (O*NET, EU Key Competences, ESDC) with O*NET included. The IRR findings are real and framework-dependent (initial κ as low as 0.288 for O*NET / 0.168 for ESDC vs. 0.841 for EU, rising to ~0.92–0.94 after guideline refinement), and the "keyword-inference-inflation" characterization faithfully reflects the paper's documented over-interpretation/overestimation results. The link to an "evidence-above-zero rule" is a fair, source-grounded inference.
