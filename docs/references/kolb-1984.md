# Kolb (1984) — Experiential Learning

**Full citation:** Kolb, D. A. (1984). *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development*. Prentice Hall. The canonical experiential learning cycle (concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation).

**Reference ID in background.html:** `ref-kolb-1984`; cited in §4.

**What the background doc claims it says:** Kolb's experiential learning cycle made the operational version of reflection-in-practice explicit: concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** Kolb's *Experiential Learning* theory draws on the work of John Dewey (learning through experience), Kurt Lewin (action research and feedback loops), Jean Piaget (cognitive development through interaction with the environment), and Carl Rogers (experiential learning as personally meaningful). Kolb defines learning as "the process whereby knowledge is created through the transformation of experience."

The four-stage experiential learning cycle:

1. **Concrete Experience (CE):** The learner encounters a new experience or situation, or reinterprets an existing one. This is the starting point — actual engagement with something.
2. **Reflective Observation (RO):** The learner reflects on the experience from multiple perspectives, looking for meaning and contradictions, thinking about what happened and why.
3. **Abstract Conceptualization (AC):** The learner forms abstract concepts or generalizations from the reflections — creating theories, rules, or principles that can be applied to new situations.
4. **Active Experimentation (AE):** The learner applies the new concepts and theories to new situations, testing whether the abstractions hold — which generates new concrete experiences and restarts the cycle.

A critical feature of the model: the learner can enter the cycle at any stage, not just at concrete experience. A student who reads a textbook about a concept (abstract conceptualization) and then applies it in a lab (active experimentation) and observes what happens (concrete experience) can then reflect on why it worked (reflective observation) — they entered at AC rather than CE. The cycle is a dynamic process, not a fixed sequence.

Kolb also proposed four learning styles — Divergers (CE/RO), Assimilators (AC/RO), Convergers (AC/AE), and Accommodators (CE/AE) — based on which stages of the cycle individuals prefer. The learning styles component has been extensively criticized (low test-retest reliability; educators using it to sort students rather than to diversify teaching), but the four-stage cycle itself remains widely influential and less contested.

The background doc uses Kolb to establish the "operational version" of the depth → failure → reflection developmental sequence: concrete experience (the problem attempt) → reflective observation (what happened) → abstract conceptualization (what does this imply about the domain) → active experimentation (revised approach). This is a plausible mapping of the productive failure cycle onto Kolb's stages.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The four-stage cycle is accurately described. The background doc's use of Kolb as the "operational version" of reflection in the developmental sequence is appropriate — the cycle provides a clear structure for how reflection converts experience into learning, which is exactly the claim being made. A minor nuance: the doc describes Kolb's cycle as "making the operational version explicit," which is accurate in the sense that Kolb provides a more prescriptive, actionable framework than Schön's phenomenological descriptions of reflection-in-action.
