# Gick & Holyoak (1980, 1983) — Analogical Transfer & Schema Induction

**Full citation:** Gick, M. L., & Holyoak, K. J. (1980). Analogical problem solving. *Cognitive Psychology*, 12(3), 306–355; Gick, M. L., & Holyoak, K. J. (1983). Schema induction and analogical transfer. *Cognitive Psychology*, 15(1), 1–38. Together, this pair of papers establishes the two-part finding: spontaneous transfer from a single surface-different analog is low (~30%), and inducing a shared schema by comparing two structurally similar but surface-different analogs raises spontaneous solution rates substantially (~52%).

**What the background doc claims it says:** The one-line gloss in §4 reads: "Transfer from a single case is poor; comparing two surface-different cases to induce a shared schema roughly doubles it." In §4's argument (problem-solving as a program-level emergent property), this is used to show that schema induction — the abstraction of a transferable principle from multiple instances — is the mechanism behind durable transfer, and that exposure to a single exemplar, no matter how well-constructed, is insufficient to produce reliable spontaneous transfer.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The 1980 paper, comprising five experiments, investigated whether a structurally analogous story from a different domain could help subjects solve Duncker's (1945) classic "radiation problem" — how to destroy a malignant tumor with rays without destroying surrounding healthy tissue. The canonical convergence solution (use multiple low-intensity rays directed from different angles to converge on the tumor) is rarely generated spontaneously: baseline rates without any analog hover around 10%. When subjects first read the "general's fortress" story — in which a military commander divides his army into small groups that converge simultaneously on a fortress from multiple roads — and are then asked to solve the radiation problem, spontaneous use of the analogy (without any hint to connect the two) was approximately 20–30%. Critically, when subjects were given an explicit hint that the story might help them, solution rates jumped to ~75% or higher (one experiment found 92% in the most informative condition). The 1980 paper's central finding is therefore not that analogical transfer is easy, but that it is fragile: most subjects who read a directly applicable analog still fail to notice its relevance without a hint, demonstrating the well-known "inert knowledge" problem.

The 1983 paper directly addresses the question: can something be done during encoding to improve spontaneous (un-hinted) transfer? Part I of the 1983 paper attempted several manipulations with a single analog — asking subjects to summarize the story, providing them a verbal statement of the underlying principle, or giving them a diagrammatic representation — and found none of these reliably improved spontaneous transfer beyond the ~30% baseline. Mere elaboration of a single case did not suffice.

Part II introduced the key manipulation: subjects were given two stories (both analogous to the radiation problem but different from each other in surface features — e.g., a military story and a fire-hose story) and asked to describe their similarities. This comparison process led many subjects to abstract a general convergence schema on their own, as an incidental byproduct of the comparison task. Subjects who received two analogs and induced a good schema achieved spontaneous transfer rates of approximately 52% — roughly doubling the one-analog baseline. Quality of schema mediated the effect: subjects whose spontaneous summaries reflected a high-quality convergence schema solved the radiation problem at ~91%; intermediate-quality schemata yielded ~40%; low-quality or absent schemata yielded ~30% (the single-analog floor). The 1983 paper thus locates the active mechanism in comparison-driven abstraction, not in exposure volume alone.

Taken together, the two papers establish: (a) spontaneous far transfer from one structurally analogous case is low (~20–30%); (b) hints that prompt retrieval and application dramatically boost it (~75–92%), but hints don't scale; and (c) inducing a schema by comparing two surface-different analogs raises spontaneous transfer to ~52%, with effects mediated by schema quality. The "doubles" figure in the gloss compares the ~52% two-analog condition to the ~30% one-analog baseline, giving a ratio of approximately 1.7×.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the background doc's gloss ("transfer from a single case is poor; comparing two surface-different cases to induce a shared schema roughly doubles it") accurately represents the canonical finding from this paired body of work. "Poor" is the right characterization of the ~30% single-case spontaneous transfer rate. "Roughly doubles" is a fair shorthand for the ~30% → ~52% increase in the two-analog schema-induction condition (a 1.7× ratio), especially given that schema quality mediates the effect and the best-schema subgroup reaches ~91%. The gloss slightly flattens the mechanistic nuance (comparison-driven abstraction, not mere dual exposure, is what induces the schema), but this compression does not misrepresent the paper's role in §4's argument about why repeated, varied exemplars are needed for durable transfer.
