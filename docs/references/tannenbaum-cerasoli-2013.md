# Tannenbaum & Cerasoli (2013) — Do Team and Individual Debriefs Enhance Performance? A Meta-Analysis

**Full citation:** Tannenbaum, S. I., & Cerasoli, C. P. (2013). Do Team and Individual Debriefs Enhance Performance? A Meta-Analysis. *Human Factors*, 55(1), 231–245. The quantitative anchor for the framework's reflection-scaffolding claim: structured debriefs improve subsequent performance by approximately 25%.

**What the background doc claims it says:** The 2013 meta-analysis of after-action reviews in professional contexts quantifies the effect: structured debriefs improve subsequent performance by approximately 25%, with effect sizes consistent across teams and individuals, simulated and real settings. The structure that works is not elaborate — what happened, why, what to do differently — but it must be specific to the concrete failure, timely, and outcome-oriented.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The meta-analysis examined 46 studies of debrief effects on performance in team and individual settings. Debriefs (also called after-action reviews) are structured retrospective discussions of recent performance, widely used in military, medical, aviation, and other high-stakes professional contexts.

**Overall effect:** On average, teams and individuals that conducted debriefs outperformed their non-debrief counterparts by approximately 25% (after excluding three extreme outliers, the average was still 21%). The effect was consistent: even "misaligned" debriefs (those not meeting all the best-practice criteria) produced positive effects, suggesting debrief efficacy is robust rather than fragile.

**Team vs. individual:** Both team and individual debriefs produced significant positive effects. Team debriefs specifically improved group performance by up to 38% in the most effective conditions.

**Simulated vs. real settings:** Effect sizes were similar across simulated (training environment) and real operational settings, suggesting that debrief benefits are not artifacts of controlled training contexts.

**Structural factors:** Structured debriefs outperformed unstructured ones. The use of multimedia aids showed no meaningful improvement. Average debrief duration in the studies was approximately 18 minutes — suggesting that brief, focused debriefs are effective, not just lengthy elaborate reviews. The strongest effects occurred when debriefs were aligned in purpose, participant, and focus.

**What makes debriefs work:** The analysis highlighted specific outcome-relevant questioning (what happened, why did it happen, what would improve performance next time) as the core productive structure. Generic reflection prompts ("how did you feel about the exercise?") were less effective than outcome-focused ones.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's specific claim — "structured debriefs improve subsequent performance by approximately 25%" — is supported by the meta-analysis (average ~25%, 21% after excluding outliers). The claim that "effect sizes are consistent across teams and individuals, simulated and real settings" is also accurate. The doc's characterization of what makes debriefs work ("what happened, why, what to do differently — specific to the concrete failure, timely, and outcome-oriented") is consistent with the paper's findings about structured vs. unstructured debriefs. Note: the ~25% figure applies across a range of professional training settings (military, medical, aviation), not specifically to undergraduate classroom settings — the application to curriculum design is an inference from professional training research, which the background doc does not misrepresent.
