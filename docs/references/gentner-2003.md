**What the background doc claims it says:** "Case comparison (analogical encoding) produces cross-context transfer in professional training, e.g. negotiation." Cited in §4 as supporting the claim that analogical encoding via comparing two cases improves schema abstraction and transfer across contexts.

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

The paper tests a specific adaptation of case-based learning called *analogical encoding* — defined as learning by drawing a structural comparison across two examples rather than studying them individually. The core theoretical claim, rooted in Gentner's structure-mapping theory, is that comparison forces learners to align cases, highlight common relational structure, and suppress surface differences, which produces a more abstract and portable schema than single-case study does.

The domain is negotiation strategy training with novices. Three experiments examine whether comparing two training cases produces better schema abstraction and transfer to novel negotiation scenarios. Experiment 1 established a baseline: analogical learning (comparing cases) outperformed no case study at all — participants who compared examples were more likely to apply the target strategy to a transfer task than an untrained control group. Experiment 2 was the pivotal test: comparing two cases produced a marked advantage over studying the same two cases *separately* (sequential single-case study). This is the critical finding — it is not simply exposure to two cases that helps, but the act of structural comparison between them. Studying cases one at a time performed no better than receiving no training. Experiment 3 extended the paradigm to face-to-face dynamic negotiation exercises and showed a dose-response: increasing the degree of comparison support during training increased the rate of transfer in live dyadic negotiation.

The target negotiation strategies in these experiments are value-creating tactics — trades on compatible issues and contingent contracts on incompatible ones. Transfer was measured by whether trained participants spontaneously used these strategies in a novel, structurally analogous negotiation scenario they had not seen during training. The key pedagogical implication the authors draw is that curriculum designers should structure case-based learning so students compare cases simultaneously (or with explicit mapping prompts), rather than encountering them in sequence without comparison. The 2003 paper is framed as demonstrating a "general role" for analogical encoding, extending beyond negotiation to suggest the mechanism operates broadly wherever learners need to abstract transferable schemas from examples.

**Verdict:** Consistent — The background.html gloss ("case comparison / analogical encoding produces cross-context transfer in professional training, e.g. negotiation") accurately reflects the paper's central finding and experimental domain. The claim is if anything conservative: the paper's Experiment 2 result — that separate case study produces no better transfer than no training, while comparison produces substantial transfer — is a stronger and more specific finding than the gloss conveys. No distortion or over-claim is present.
