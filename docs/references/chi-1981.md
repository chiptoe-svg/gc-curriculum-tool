**What the background doc claims it says:** Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser's landmark 1981 study: experts and novices were given the same set of physics problems and asked to sort them. Novices sorted by surface features (the problem mentions a pulley, an inclined plane); experts sorted by underlying principles (conservation of energy, Newton's second law). The implication is structural: problem-solving skill in physics is not separable from physics knowledge — it is restructured domain knowledge organized around principles rather than surface features.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** This is one of the most cited papers in cognitive science and educational psychology. The study involved four experiments with expert physicists (advanced graduate students and postdocs) and novice physics students at Carnegie Mellon University. The core task: participants were given 24 introductory physics problems on cards and asked to sort them into categories and explain their sorting.

Results were stark. Novices sorted by surface features — the physical objects or configurations mentioned in the problem (inclined plane problems together, spring problems together, pulley problems together). Experts sorted by the deep physics principle underlying the solution (conservation of energy problems together, Newton's second law problems together, regardless of whether pulleys, inclines, or other surface features were present). A novice and an expert given the same inclined-plane problem would place it in entirely different categories.

The paper also conducted protocol analyses showing that experts, having categorized a problem correctly by its deep principle, immediately activated associated knowledge about solution approaches — while novices, having categorized by surface features, activated only knowledge about the objects involved, not about the relevant physics. This explains why experts can solve physics problems faster despite the problems appearing equally complex: the principle-level categorization directly accesses the relevant solution strategies.

The authors concluded that the development of physics expertise involves not just learning more facts but a structural reorganization of knowledge — from surface-feature-indexed representations to principle-indexed representations. This restructuring does not happen automatically from exposure; it requires extensive practice solving problems with feedback, during which the learner gradually develops the principle-level schemas that experts use.

This paper, together with Larkin et al. (1980), established the empirical foundation for the claim that domain expertise is constitutively organized knowledge, not a general skill.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization is accurate: experts do sort by underlying principles while novices sort by surface features. The doc's inference — "problem-solving skill in physics is not separable from physics knowledge" — is a fair conclusion from the study's findings and is the standard interpretation in the expert-novice literature. The doc also uses the study's finding later in §4 (D=5 names "creative judgment and the ability to teach — the same marker Chi's expert-novice work identifies as evidence of principle-level rather than surface-level understanding") — this connection to the depth scale's top level is an inference not explicitly stated in the 1981 paper but is consistent with the paper's logic about principle-level organization.
