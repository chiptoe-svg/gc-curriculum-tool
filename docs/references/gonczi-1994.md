# Gonczi (1994) — Competency Based Assessment in the Professions in Australia

**Full citation:** Gonczi, A. (1994). Competency Based Assessment in the Professions in Australia. *Assessment in Education: Principles, Policy & Practice*, 1(1), 27–44. https://doi.org/10.1080/0969594940010103. A conceptual and policy article arguing that an "integrated" (holistic) conception of competence — not an atomized behaviourist task-list — is the only defensible basis for competency-based assessment of professionals.

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

This is the central organising move of the article, and Gonczi makes it explicitly. He lays out three "conceptions of the nature of competence." The first "and probably most widely held" is "task-based or behaviourist," in which "competence is conceived of in terms of the discrete behaviours associated with the completion of atomised tasks" (p. 28). He characterises its aim as "transparent specification of competencies such that there can be no disagreement about what constitutes satisfactory performance," with the consequence that "in effect the task becomes the competency." Crucially, he notes this approach "is not concerned with the connections between the tasks and ignores the possibility that the coming together of tasks could lead to their transformation. (The whole is not greater than the sum of the parts.)" He then enumerates its weaknesses: it is "positivist, reductionist, ignores underlying attributes, ignores group processes... is conservative, atheoretical, ignores the complexity of performance in the real world and ignores the role of professional judgment in intelligent performance" (pp. 28–29). His judgment is blunt: "this approach is inappropriate for conceptualising professional work and there are very serious doubts about its relevance to work at any level."

The second conception — "general attributes" of the practitioner (e.g., generic critical thinking) abstracted from context — Gonczi also rejects, on the evidence that expertise is domain-specific and does not transfer (citing novice/expert and critical-thinking research). The third conception is the one he endorses: the "integrated" or "holistic" approach (Gonczi et al., 1990), which "seeks to marry the general attributes approach to the context in which these attributes will be employed." Here "competence is conceived of as complex structuring of attributes needed for intelligent performance in specific situations" — "the notion of competence is relational. It brings together disparate things — abilities of individuals (deriving from combinations of attributes) and the tasks that need to be performed in particular situations" (p. 29). This integrated conception "overcomes all the objections to the competency movement" and lets one "incorporate ethics and values... the need for reflective practice, the importance of context and the fact that there may be more than one way of practising competently" (pp. 29–30).

Gonczi reinforces the distinction with his US teacher-education example: occupational-analysis techniques broke teaching "down into the variety of tasks that teachers were expected to perform" — hundreds of discrete tasks — yet "success in performing tasks identified did not seem to have any relationship to good teaching as most experienced professionals understood it" (p. 30). The integrated view instead conceives competent teaching "as the capacity of the teacher to employ a complex interaction of attributes in a number of contexts."

The assessment implications follow directly and are the practical payoff of the distinction. Because competence "cannot be observed directly... it can only be inferred from performance" (p. 33), Gonczi argues that assessment methods "should be used that are most capable of assessing competence in an integrated manner," combining "knowledge, understanding, problem solving, technical skills, attitudes and ethics in assessment" within single assessment events (p. 33), and that a *mix* of methods (triangulation) is needed because "the narrower the base of evidence... the less generalisable it will be" (p. 34). He explicitly contrasts the Australian professions' "described" prose standards (≈30–40 elements suggesting "the holism of the nature of competence") with the English NVQ model's "long checklists" of many elements with performance criteria (p. 31).

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's gloss — "Gonczi's distinction between behaviourist skill-decomposition and integrated competence" — is an accurate, even understated, summary of the article's thesis. Gonczi explicitly names and rejects the "task-based or behaviourist" decomposition of competence into "atomised tasks" and argues for the "integrated"/holistic, relational, context-dependent conception as the defensible alternative. No overstatement: if anything, the citation could draw more on Gonczi's specific point that the whole is not the sum of the parts and that task-performance does not predict professional competence (the teacher-education example), both of which directly support a critique of checklist competency frameworks.
