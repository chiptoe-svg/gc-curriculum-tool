**What the background doc claims it says:** Bjork's framework — "conditions that suppress short-term performance often improve long-term retention and transfer." Spaced practice, interleaving, retrieval testing, reduced feedback — all degrade in-the-moment performance and all dramatically improve learning that lasts. This is the empirical basis for the framework's commitment to evidence-of-attainment over evidence-of-coverage.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** This book chapter is the founding document of the "desirable difficulties" framework in educational psychology. Bjork's central observation is that there is a systematic dissociation between learning conditions that feel effective (and produce good immediate performance) and conditions that actually produce durable learning (strong long-term retention and transfer).

The counterintuitive finding: instructional manipulations that impede short-term performance often dramatically improve long-term learning. Bjork calls these "desirable difficulties" — they are desirable because they produce superior long-term outcomes, even though they feel harder and produce worse results on immediate tests. Examples Bjork discusses in the chapter include:

- **Spacing** (distributing practice across time): Massed practice (cramming) produces rapid improvement but poor long-term retention. Spaced practice (distributing practice sessions) produces slower initial learning but far superior retention weeks or months later.
- **Interleaving** (mixing different problem types): Blocked practice (all problems of one type, then all of another) produces faster within-session performance but worse long-term discrimination. Interleaved practice (mixing types randomly) is harder in-session but produces better long-term transfer.
- **Testing / retrieval practice**: Rereading is easier and feels productive; attempting to recall from memory without the text (testing) is harder but produces dramatically superior retention — the "testing effect."
- **Reduced feedback**: Frequent feedback feels helpful but reduces the learner's need to process deeply; reducing feedback frequency (while maintaining eventual feedback) improves long-term learning.

The metamemory component: students and trainers systematically misjudge which conditions are producing learning. Conditions that feel smooth and easy are typically not producing durable learning; conditions that feel difficult are. This metacognitive illusion is why performance during training is a poor indicator of learning. Training programs that optimize for smooth in-session performance are optimizing for the wrong metric.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization is accurate: "conditions that suppress short-term performance often improve long-term retention and transfer" is Bjork's core claim. The specific examples (spaced practice, interleaving, retrieval testing, reduced feedback) are the standard desirable difficulties. The doc's application of this to the "evidence-of-attainment over evidence-of-coverage" commitment is a reasonable inference — if smooth in-session performance does not indicate learning, then a coverage record does not indicate depth either.
