# Thorndike & Woodworth (1901) — The Influence of Improvement in One Mental Function Upon Others

**Full citation:** Thorndike, E. L., & Woodworth, R. S. (1901). The influence of improvement in one mental function upon the efficiency of other functions (I). *Psychological Review*, 8(3), 247–261. https://doi.org/10.1037/h0074898. The founding empirical study of transfer of training, originating the "common elements" theory and the modern skepticism toward broad, domain-general skill transfer.

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

This is the paper that founded the experimental study of transfer and dismantled the doctrine of "formal discipline" — the then-dominant educational theory that training a faculty (memory, attention, reasoning, observation) on *any* material strengthens that faculty *in general*, so that studying Latin or geometry would discipline the mind for all purposes. Thorndike and Woodworth define their terms carefully and unusually broadly: a "function" is the mental basis of any performance (spelling, multiplication, estimating size, marking letters on a page, chess, reasoning), and "improvement" is any change in how a function works. Their method is the now-canonical pretest–train–posttest design: measure a function, train a *different but related* function until substantial improvement is reached, then re-test the first function. Any gain in the untrained function measures transfer from the trained one. They also note the correlational logic — absence of correlation between two functions implies no transfer between them — citing Wissler's and Norsworthy's findings of little correlation across school tasks.

The empirical core reported here is the estimation-of-magnitudes experiments. Subjects were trained to estimate the area of paper rectangles of one shape and size range (with feedback after each guess), then tested on estimating areas of *different* shapes and of *different* sizes. The result was that improvement was sharply specific: training on one set of areas barely improved estimation of areas of the same size but *different shape*, and improvement fell off as the test material diverged from the trained material. Parallel results held for training to mark words with certain letters (which did not generalize to observing words in general) and for training in memorizing (which did not produce general memory improvement, confirming James's earlier finding). Across cases, transfer appeared only to the extent that the trained and tested functions shared concrete features.

From these data the authors draw conclusions that read as the charter document of transfer skepticism. They argue it is "misleading to speak of sense discrimination, attention, memory, observation, accuracy, quickness, etc., as [single] functions" — these are not unified faculties but vast collections of particular, partly independent tendencies tied to particular data. The mind, on their famous formulation, "is, on its dynamic side, a machine for making particular reactions to particular situations. It works in great detail, adapting itself to the special data of which it has had experience." Improvement in one function "rarely brings about equal improvement in any other function, no matter how similar," because each function is conditioned by the nature of its specific data. They infer that as test data become less like the trained data, the benefit of training shrinks toward zero, and that there is "always a point where the loss is complete" — and, strikingly, that this point "is nearer than has been supposed." Their proposed mechanism is identity of elements: "spread of practice occurs only where identical elements are concerned in the influencing and influenced function" — the seed of what later became the common-elements theory of transfer.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The document's §5 use is historically and substantively accurate. This is indeed the origin point of the century-long skepticism about broad transfer, and the paper does empirically refute formal discipline: trained skill stays near its training context, generalizing only via shared concrete elements. The doc's framing tracks the authors' own conclusions almost verbatim. Two fair calibration notes: (1) Thorndike and Woodworth do *not* claim transfer is impossible — they claim it is narrow and element-dependent, which is precisely the "near, not far" position the doc relies on, so cite it for the *limits* of transfer rather than its total absence; (2) the original studies are small-N by modern standards and the strong "the point is nearer than supposed" claim is an inference, but the qualitative finding has been confirmed repeatedly across the subsequent century (and is the foundation Sala & Gobet 2017 explicitly build on), so the citation is on solid ground.
