# Schoenfeld (1992) — Learning to Think Mathematically: Problem Solving, Metacognition, and Sense-Making

**Full citation:** Schoenfeld, A. H. (1992). Learning to think mathematically: Problem solving, metacognition, and sense making in mathematics. In D. Grouws (Ed.), *Handbook of Research on Mathematics Teaching and Learning* (pp. 334–370). New York: MacMillan. A landmark review chapter synthesizing the cognitive-science literature on mathematical problem solving, organized around a five-category framework in which metacognitive self-regulation ("monitoring and control") is a load-bearing component.

**What the doc claims it says (§7):** Cited (with Veenman; Perkins & Salomon 1989) for the point that cross-domain habit "appears when the habit is explicitly cultivated — varied cases, abstraction, deliberate bridging, reflective control." Schoenfeld's specific contribution is identified as metacognitive control / self-regulation in mathematical problem-solving.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The chapter's stated goals (p. 2) are to (a) outline and substantiate a broad conceptualization of what it means to *think mathematically*, (b) summarize the literature on mathematical thinking and problem solving, and (c) point to new research, development, and assessment directions. Schoenfeld is explicit that this is "selective and illustrative" rather than an encyclopedic review, and that the two terms in his title — "problem solving" and "metacognition" — are "perhaps the two most overworked and least understood buzz words of the 1980's," with metacognition carrying "multiple and almost disjoint meanings (e.g. knowledge about one's thought processes, self-regulation during problem solving)."

His core thesis is that knowing mathematics means more than mastering facts and procedures: "learning to think mathematically means (a) developing a mathematical point of view — valuing the processes of mathematization and abstraction and having the predilection to apply them, and (b) developing competence with the tools of the trade, and using those tools in the service of the goal of understanding structure — mathematical sense-making." He rejects the "body of facts and procedures" view as impoverished, analogous to reducing an English curriculum to grammar.

The substance the §7 citation rests on is the chapter's **framework for understanding mathematical cognition** (Part II), which has five fundamental, interacting categories (p. 5): (1) **core knowledge** (cognitive resources — the constructive nature of cognition, memory, and access to it); (2) **problem-solving strategies (heuristics)**; (3) **effective use of one's resources**, described under the heading **"Monitoring and control"** — "the aspect of metacognition known as self-regulation"; (4) **beliefs and affects** (an individual's relationship to mathematical situations); and (5) **practices** (enculturation into mathematical communities of practice). Schoenfeld argues all five are fundamental aspects of thinking mathematically. Category (3) is the explicit home of Schoenfeld's most-cited contribution: his observational work (developed at length in *Mathematical Problem Solving*, 1985) documented that students often fail not for lack of knowledge or heuristics but because they fail to *monitor and regulate* their own problem-solving — e.g., pursuing an unproductive approach for the entire session without ever stepping back to ask whether it is working. Effective problem solvers, by contrast, deploy executive/self-regulatory control: planning, monitoring progress, and reallocating effort. This monitoring-and-control loop is precisely the "reflective control" the §7 passage invokes.

The chapter also foregrounds **enculturation and "cognitive apprenticeship" / "cultures of learning"** (Part I and the "Practices" section), arguing that mathematical dispositions develop through participation in microcosms of genuine mathematical practice — instructional environments that model and coach mathematical thinking. This supplies the "explicitly cultivated" mechanism the doc attributes to it: a mathematical point of view and self-regulatory habits are not absorbed automatically from exposure to facts; they must be deliberately developed through practice in which they are valued, modeled, and coached.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The §7 attribution is accurate. Schoenfeld (1992) does treat metacognitive self-regulation — his "monitoring and control" category — as a central, separable component of competent mathematical problem solving, exactly the contribution the doc credits him with. The chapter's emphasis on enculturation, cognitive apprenticeship, and developing a "mathematical point of view" through deliberate practice in authentic mathematical activity supports the doc's framing that the habit "appears when the habit is explicitly cultivated." Pairing it with Veenman (metacognitive transfer) and Perkins & Salomon (high-road/low-road transfer, deliberate bridging) is appropriate, since all three converge on the claim that self-regulatory/metacognitive habits are cultivated, not automatic. One precision note: this chapter is a *review/synthesis* (and a conceptual framework), so the empirical demonstrations of metacognitive failure in problem solving largely live in Schoenfeld's 1985 *book* (which the doc separately cites but which is **not held** in this folder); the 1992 chapter restates and frames that work rather than reporting the raw protocols. Citing Schoenfeld 1992 for the *framework and the role of monitoring/control* is sound; citing it for the original empirical protocol data is better served by the (unheld) 1985 source.
