# Belenky & Nokes-Malach (2012) — Motivation and Transfer: Mastery-Approach Goals in Preparation for Future Learning

**Full citation:** Belenky, D. M., & Nokes-Malach, T. J. (2012). Motivation and transfer: The role of mastery-approach goals in preparation for future learning. *Journal of the Learning Sciences*, 21(3), 399–432. https://doi.org/10.1080/10508406.2011.651232. A laboratory experiment linking achievement-goal motivation to the "preparation for future learning" (PFL) view of transfer, testing how mastery-approach goals and invention vs. tell-and-practice instruction jointly predict transfer.

**What the doc claims it says (§5):** "the advantage [from preparation-for-future-learning] is largest for learners with a mastery-approach orientation — the disposition and the readiness reinforce each other."

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The study's aim was to bring a motivational construct — achievement goals — into research on knowledge transfer, which has historically been studied as a purely cognitive phenomenon. The authors used Schwartz and Bransford's "preparation for future learning" (PFL) double-transfer paradigm with N = 104 (later restricted to the 52 who received a worked example) undergraduates learning introductory statistics concepts (variability, standardization). Participants were randomly assigned to **invention** activities (asked to invent a procedure with no instruction given) or **tell-and-practice** activities (shown a worked procedure, then asked to apply it). Mastery-approach orientation (and the other three goals in Elliot & McGregor's 2×2 framework) was measured at the start via the Achievement Goal Questionnaire. Half of each instructional group then received an embedded learning resource (a worked example) before a transfer test; the transfer item was scored dichotomously (correct/incorrect) and analyzed via logistic regression.

Three hypotheses were tested. **H1 (existing mastery-approach predicts transfer):** Among students who received the worked example, mastery-approach orientation significantly predicted transfer (B = 0.37, Exp(B) = 1.45, p < .05) — each unit increase raised the odds of transfer by ~45%. Critically, **none of the other three goal orientations** (mastery-avoidance, performance-approach, performance-avoidance) predicted transfer. **H2 (invention promotes mastery goal adoption and deeper engagement):** On the standardization learning activity, invention students produced better attempts (M = 1.15) than tell-and-practice students (M = 0.69), t = 3.07, d = 0.61, suggesting deeper understanding of variability; on the activity questionnaire, invention students endorsed more mastery-related concern for the quality (d = 0.56) and understanding (d = 0.54) of their procedure. (On the *variability* activity itself, tell-and-practice scored higher, M = 1.85 vs 0.94, d = 2.18 — unsurprising since they were shown the procedure.) So invention raised in-task mastery-goal adoption.

**H3 — the moderation, and the part most relevant to the doc's claim:** The authors predicted that invention would *moderate* (reduce) the effect of pre-existing mastery-approach orientation, because invention itself induces mastery engagement and would therefore "level the playing field," whereas tell-and-practice would leave students dependent on their incoming orientation. This was supported. The interaction term was significant (B = −1.50, Exp(B) = 4.88, Wald χ² = 4.45, p < .05). In the **tell-and-practice** condition, mastery-approach orientation mattered enormously: B = 1.59, Exp(B) = 4.88, so each unit increase multiplied the odds of transfer by **~4.8**. In the **invention** condition, mastery-approach orientation barely mattered: each unit increase changed the odds by a factor of only **1.09 (≈ +9%)**. In other words, **invention/PFL produced transfer fairly uniformly regardless of incoming mastery orientation, while it was the tell-and-practice (non-PFL-style) group in which the mastery advantage was concentrated.** The overall worked-example effect was also large: those given a worked example were ~10× more likely to transfer (50% vs 11% correct).

The mechanism the authors propose: invention activities prompt learners to grapple with the deep conceptual structure (creating "well-differentiated knowledge") and induce mastery-related goal adoption *during the task*, so students do not need to bring a strong mastery disposition with them. Tell-and-practice, by contrast, supplies a procedure but does not induce that engagement, so transfer becomes dependent on the learner's prior mastery orientation.

**Verdict:** **Partially inconsistent — the §5 citation inverts the study's central interaction.** The doc states that the PFL/invention advantage "is largest for learners with a mastery-approach orientation," with disposition and readiness "reinforcing each other." The study found the opposite pattern for the interaction: under invention/PFL, the benefit of having a mastery-approach orientation was *smallest* (odds ×1.09, ~+9% per unit); it was under **tell-and-practice** that mastery orientation paid off most (odds ×4.8 per unit). The authors' own framing is that invention *compensates* for a lack of mastery orientation by inducing it in-task — a substitution relationship, not a synergistic "reinforce each other" relationship. The doc is correct that (a) mastery-approach orientation predicts transfer and uniquely among the four goals (H1), and (b) invention/PFL fosters mastery engagement and deeper learning (H2) — so the broad linkage of motivation, PFL, and transfer is faithful. But the specific causal sentence in §5 should be corrected: it is the learners with *low* mastery orientation who gain most from invention/PFL relative to tell-and-practice; high-mastery learners transfer well either way. If §5 means to argue that mastery-oriented learners do well and PFL helps everyone, that is supportable; the phrase "the advantage from PFL is largest for mastery-approach learners — disposition and readiness reinforce each other" is not what Belenky & Nokes-Malach (2012) found and should be reworded.
