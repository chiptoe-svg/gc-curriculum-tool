# Brockmann, Clarke & Winch (2008) — Knowledge, Skills, Competence: European Divergences in VET

**Full citation:** Brockmann, M., Clarke, L., & Winch, C. (2008). Knowledge, skills, competence: European divergences in vocational education and training (VET) — the English, German and Dutch cases. *Oxford Review of Education*, 34(5), 547–567. https://doi.org/10.1080/03054980701782098. A comparative analysis arguing that "skills," "competence," and "qualification" carry deeply divergent, socially constructed meanings across England, Germany, and the Netherlands, and that the English "skills-based" model strips out the integration and knowledge base that constitute occupational competence elsewhere.

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

The English-vs-Continental contrast is the paper's central thesis and is stated repeatedly and unambiguously. The abstract and Introduction conclude that "a major distinction needs to be made between two VET models: a knowledge-based model operating in Germany and The Netherlands, and a skills-based model in England" (p. 549). The authors trace the English notion of "skills" to "the old craft-based system of apprenticeship, where an apprentice would be expected to learn certain task-specific skills (usually of a physical nature)," so that "the concept of skills is conceived as an individual attribute, i.e., the mastery of a narrow range of tasks" (p. 551). This is set against Germany, where the nearest equivalent — *Fertigkeiten* — "are an integral part of a broad occupational field, importantly underpinned by theoretical knowledge."

The German *Beruf* and *Handlungskompetenz* concepts carry the holistic alternative. *Beruf* ("vocation") denotes "a broad spectrum of activities" and entry to "a life-long career, linked to social status and recognition" (p. 557); the qualification serves "as a guarantee of what is referred to as occupational capability (*berufliche Handlungsfähigkeit*), i.e., the ability of the holder of the qualification to act competently within the scope of the occupational field." *Handlungskompetenz* is "a holistic notion, which comprises particular knowledge, skills and competencies within each different dimension" — occupational (*Fachkompetenz*), social (*Sozialkompetenz*) and individual (*Humankompetenz*) — "linked to a curriculum" (p. 557). Competence here is "integrative rather than cumulative." The Dutch system is positioned as knowledge-based but moving toward employability, with competence understood (Mulder) as "integrated performance-oriented capabilities... clusters of knowledge structures" (p. 560).

The specific Winch claim the doc paraphrases — that achieving an occupational profile's individual skills does not guarantee occupational competence — is squarely supported, even if the exact wording is a paraphrase rather than a verbatim quotation found in this article. The paper's whole argument about England is that the NVQ "skills" model produces "highly fragmented skill sets" and "narrow skill sets that tend to trap individuals in low-skill sectors of the economy" (pp. 555–556). It diagnoses the English competence approach as containing "very little input of any formal knowledge" and relying "solely on tacit knowledge acquired in the workplace," with "no notion of an integration of different forms of knowledge" (p. 555). It quotes Biemans et al.'s description of the English model as "a rigid backward mapping approach, in which the state of the art on the shop floor is the untouchable starting point for the definition of occupational competencies, leading to routinised job descriptions in which the proactive and reflective worker is left out" (p. 555). The conclusion is that "in England the skills-based approach of the NVQ system has not only failed to produce the competencies and knowledge base needed... it has actively promoted the production of low-skilled labour" (p. 556). The point is exactly that accrediting the constituent narrow skills (the occupational profile's elements) leaves out the integration, underpinning knowledge, and broad occupational context that, on the Continental view, *constitute* competence — so it does not deliver occupational competence. Winch is a co-author and the source of this theoretical line (the paper repeatedly cites Clarke & Winch and Winch & Hyland), so attributing the argument to "Winch" is reasonable.

One precision note: the memorable phrase "does not guarantee occupational competence" should be treated as the doc's own paraphrase of the paper's argument, not as a direct quotation from this 2008 article. The article's own near-equivalents are the "does not include the notion of competence development based on the integration of theoretical and practical knowledge" formulation (p. 554) and the *berufliche Handlungsfähigkeit* "guarantee" language applied to the German case (p. 557). The substantive claim is fully supported; only the quotation-marking should be handled carefully.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc faithfully represents the source. The English skills-based vs. German/Dutch knowledge-based/holistic-*Beruf* contrast is the paper's headline thesis, stated explicitly. The Winch-attributed argument — that accrediting narrow individual skills strips out the integration and context that constitute occupational competence — is exactly what the paper argues about the NVQ model, and Winch is a co-author. Caveat: "does not guarantee occupational competence" is a paraphrase, not a verbatim quote from this article, so §11 should not present it inside quotation marks as if lifted directly from the 2008 text.
