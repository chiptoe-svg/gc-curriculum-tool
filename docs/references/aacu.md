**What the background doc claims it says:** The AAC&U Integrative Learning VALUE Rubric describes a progression from connecting prior knowledge across contexts to synthesizing learning in complex, real-world performances. The KUD+ Do scale's upper-range anchors (D=4 adapts to new conditions; D=5 performs creatively with critical judgment and guides others) are consistent with the Capstone-level descriptors of the AAC&U progression. The framework borrows the integrative-learning orientation rather than the rubric's structure; the VALUE Rubric is designed for assessing student work via portfolios across multiple dimensions, while the Do scale describes one dimension of what a course develops.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The VALUE (Valid Assessment of Learning in Undergraduate Education) Rubrics are 16 faculty-developed rubrics for cross-cutting learning outcomes, developed by teams of faculty across the country and published by the Association of American Colleges & Universities. They are explicitly designed for institutional-level assessment of student learning across programs, typically applied to ePortfolios or capstone collections, not for grading individual assignments.

The Integrative Learning VALUE Rubric has four performance levels — Benchmark (1), Milestones (2 and 3), and Capstone (4) — applied across dimensions including: Connections to Experience, Connections to Discipline, Transfer, and Integrated Communication. The overarching definition of integrative learning, as stated in the rubric, is: "an understanding and a disposition that a student builds across the curriculum and cocurriculum, from making simple connections among ideas and experiences to synthesizing and transferring learning to new, complex situations within and beyond the campus."

The Capstone level (4) across dimensions describes: synthesizing connections among experiences outside formal classroom contexts; adapting and applying skills, abilities, theories, or methodologies from prior coursework to complex problems; and solving them with awareness of limits of one's own approach. These descriptors are oriented toward the culminating performance of an undergraduate program — they describe the most sophisticated transfer and integration a graduate should demonstrate, not what a single course develops.

The rubric explicitly states that it is designed for collections of student work, not for single-course scoring, and that it works best for assessing holistic portfolios across multiple assignments and courses.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's claim that the Do scale's upper-range anchors are "consistent with" the Capstone-level descriptors is accurate; the Capstone descriptions of synthesizing across contexts, adapting methods to new problems, and demonstrating critical judgment are substantively similar to D=4 and D=5 on the Do scale. The doc correctly notes that the VALUE Rubric is designed for assessing student work (multi-artifact portfolios) while the Do scale describes what a course develops — this is an accurate characterization of the rubric's intended use.
