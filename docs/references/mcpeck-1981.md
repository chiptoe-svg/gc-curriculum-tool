**What the background doc claims it says:** McPeck makes the strong-specificity case earlier and more sharply than Willingham: "there are no general thinking skills, since thinking is always thinking about some subject-matter."

**What it actually says (synthesis):** McPeck's *Critical Thinking and Education* is a work of analytic philosophy of education, not an empirical study. McPeck argues from first principles that the concept of "critical thinking" as a domain-general skill is philosophically incoherent. His central argument proceeds as follows: any instance of thinking is always *thinking about something* — a specific proposition, problem, or subject matter. Therefore there is no content-free "thinking" that exists independently of subject matter. Programs that claim to teach "critical thinking" as a separate discipline are therefore teaching either: (a) nothing at all, because stripped of subject matter there is no actual thinking to do; or (b) very domain-specific thinking that they are mislabeling as general.

McPeck's view is the "strong specificity" position in the debate: not merely that critical thinking transfers better when domain knowledge is present (which would be a moderate position), but that domain-general critical thinking skills do not exist as a meaningful category at all. The specific formulation cited in the background doc — "there are no general thinking skills, since thinking is always thinking about some subject-matter" — reflects McPeck's Ryle-influenced argument about the nature of thinking as an intentional activity (always directed at something).

McPeck also critiques specific critical thinking programs (e.g., de Bono's lateral thinking, Ennis's critical thinking taxonomy) and standardized critical thinking tests for claiming to assess a domain-general competence that does not exist as a unified psychological capacity. He argues that assessing "critical thinking" meaningfully requires testing thinking within a specific subject-matter domain.

The book prompted significant philosophical debate; Ennis, Siegel, and others responded with "moderate generalist" positions arguing that while domain knowledge is necessary for critical thinking, some general strategies (e.g., checking for consistency, asking for evidence) do transfer. McPeck's strong-specificity position remains a minority view among philosophers of education, though it has found strong empirical support in cognitive science (Chi, Willingham, and others).

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc accurately represents McPeck as making the strong-specificity case, and the quoted claim is a fair characterization of his argument. The doc correctly positions McPeck's work as earlier and philosophically sharper than Willingham's empirical synthesis. The doc does not claim McPeck's position is settled — §4's caveat section explicitly acknowledges that "strong-specificity scholars (Willingham, McPeck) and moderate generalists (Ennis, Flavell) both hold defensible positions." This is an accurate framing of a genuine debate.
