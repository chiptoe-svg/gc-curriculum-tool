# Sala, Aksayli, Tatlidil, Tatsumi, Gondo & Gobet (2019) — Near and Far Transfer: A Second-Order Meta-Analysis

**What it actually says (synthesis):**

This is a *second-order* meta-analysis — a meta-analysis whose units of analysis are themselves first-order meta-analyses — designed to integrate the fragmented cognitive-training literature and separate genuine effects from artifacts. The authors build three models. **Model 1** (k = 99) examines WM training's effect on *near*-transfer (memory) measures. **Model 2** (k = 119) examines WM training's effect on *far*-transfer measures (reasoning, processing speed, language). **Model 3** (k = 233) extends Model 2 by adding six further first-order meta-analyses covering video-game, music, chess, and exergame training, to test whether the pattern generalizes across program types and across populations (children, adults, older adults).

The aim is not merely to estimate average effect sizes but to decompose the *variance* among first-order means into true heterogeneity (σ²) versus second-order sampling error, and crucially to estimate how much of the apparent effect survives correction for publication bias and placebo effects. The authors apply bias-correction techniques (e.g., PET/PEESE and trim-and-fill) to each first-order meta-analysis and then re-aggregate.

The results split sharply by transfer distance. **Model 1 (near transfer) shows a real effect**: WM training does improve memory performance, and the size of this near-transfer effect is moderated by population (it differs across children, adults, and older adults). Uncorrected near-transfer means were around g = 0.25 with most of the between-meta-analysis variance attributable to true heterogeneity. **Models 2 and 3 (far transfer) tell the opposite story.** Naïve (uncorrected) far-transfer effects are small — roughly g = 0.32 and g = 0.19 in the WM models, similarly modest in Model 3 — but the variance is largely an artifact. When publication bias and placebo effects are controlled (the authors emphasize estimates using *active* control groups), the corrected overall far-transfer effect collapses: in Model 1's far-transfer analogue the corrected effect was g = 0.01 (SE = 0.07), essentially zero, and across Models 2 and 3 the corrected overall effect size *and the true variance* both equaled zero. The PET estimate of the far-transfer effect was so imprecise (SE ≈ 0.15) and the correction so complete that the detected effect was attributable to publication bias rather than any genuine signal.

The headline mechanism is therefore methodological: the literature's positive far-transfer findings are a composite of small-sample false positives, publication bias toward significant results, and placebo/expectancy effects from passive-control designs. Once those are stripped out, "no impact on far-transfer measures was observed regardless of the type of population and cognitive-training program." The authors conclude, in the abstract's own words, that "the lack of generalization of skills acquired by training is thus an invariant of human cognition" — a deliberately strong, near-law-like framing.

**Verdict:** Consistent — the document's characterization is accurate and, if anything, conservative. The PDF explicitly states that after correcting for placebo effects and publication bias, "the overall effect size and true variance equaled zero." The doc's "≈zero after correcting for bias" is exactly the paper's claim. Two precision notes for honest citation: (1) the near-zero result is specifically about *far* transfer — the paper *affirms* a real, population-moderated *near*-transfer effect, so the reference should not be cited as evidence that training does nothing; (2) the paper's "invariant of human cognition" language is a strong inferential leap from a meta-analytic null and is the most contestable sentence in the article — the data support "no detectable far transfer in this corrected corpus," while the universal-invariant claim is interpretation. The document's narrower framing avoids that overreach.
