# Perkins & Salomon (1992) — Transfer of Learning

**Full citation:** Perkins, D. N., & Salomon, G. (1992). Transfer of Learning. In *International Encyclopedia of Education* (2nd ed.). Pergamon Press. **What the background doc claims it says:** Perkins & Salomon distinguish low-road (near, automatic) from high-road (far, deliberate-abstraction) transfer; establishes that far transfer requires explicit scaffolding. D-4 on the Do scale names transfer to novel conditions — exactly the capacity this transfer literature identifies as the hard problem of education.

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The encyclopedia entry synthesizes decades of transfer research into a framework that distinguishes types of transfer by mechanism rather than just by distance. Two complementary mechanisms account for different kinds of transfer:

**Low-road (reflexive) transfer:** Occurs when well-practiced routines are triggered automatically by stimulus conditions similar to the original learning context. This is near transfer — the situation is sufficiently similar to the training context that the learned response fires automatically. Low-road transfer requires extensive varied practice to build up the automatized response; it does not require conscious recognition that the new situation is analogous to the training situation.

**High-road (mindful) transfer:** Occurs when the learner deliberately abstracts from a learning experience, formulates general principles, and consciously applies those principles to a new context. This is far transfer — the new situation may look quite different from the training context, but the learner bridges between them via explicit abstraction and a search for connections. The mechanism is metacognitive: "What principle does this situation involve? Have I seen a structurally similar situation before?"

The key insight: high-road transfer does not happen automatically. It requires explicit scaffolding — teachers must prompt learners to formulate general principles during learning and to look for connections to new situations. Without this scaffolding, students may master a skill in the training context and fail to recognize when it applies elsewhere (a common finding in the transfer literature).

Perkins and Salomon propose two instructional strategies to promote transfer:
- **Hugging:** Design instruction to closely approximate the target performance contexts — train close to the context where transfer will be needed. Promotes low-road transfer.
- **Bridging:** Explicitly prompt learners to abstract general principles during instruction, to search for analogies, to formulate "what's the deep structure here?" Promotes high-road transfer.

The paper establishes that far transfer — applying learning to significantly different contexts — is genuinely difficult and does not happen reliably without deliberate instructional design that includes explicit abstraction prompts.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The background doc's characterization — "distinguishes low-road from high-road transfer; establishes that far transfer requires explicit scaffolding" — is accurate. The doc's claim that D=4 on the Do scale ("adapts performance to new conditions or constraints") names the capacity that "the transfer literature identifies as the hard problem of education" is a fair and defensible reading of the Perkins and Salomon framework. Far transfer (adapting to new conditions) is indeed the hard case in the transfer literature, and Perkins and Salomon's contribution is precisely to identify why it is hard and what scaffolding it requires.
