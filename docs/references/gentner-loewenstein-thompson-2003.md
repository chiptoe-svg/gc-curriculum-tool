# Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson (2003) — Analogical Encoding

**Full citation:** Gentner, D., Loewenstein, J., & Thompson, L. (2003). Learning and Transfer: A General Role for Analogical Encoding. *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 95(2), 393–408. Demonstrates that comparing two cases during learning ("analogical encoding") abstracts a transferable schema and produces markedly better cross-context transfer than studying the same cases separately — shown with novices learning negotiation strategies, including a live face-to-face negotiation.

**What the doc claims it says:** Case comparison (analogical encoding) produces cross-context transfer in professional training, e.g. negotiation. (The doc also cites a 1999 companion, Loewenstein, Thompson & Gentner — that paper is not held here; the held PDF is the 2003.)

**What it actually says (synthesis):** The paper's premise is that case-based teaching (the mainstay of business, law, and medicine) varies widely in effectiveness because learners tend to encode cases in surface-bound terms and then fail to retrieve them when a structurally similar but superficially different problem appears (the authors note that "over 80% of people's remindings were based on surface similarity"). Their proposed fix is *analogical encoding*: having learners explicitly *compare two examples* and articulate what they share. The act of comparison foregrounds the common relational structure, yielding a more abstract, portable problem schema. Three experiments test this with novices (MBA-type participants) learning negotiation principles — specifically *contingent contracts* (deals indexed to uncertain future outcomes) and *trade-offs* (logrolling across issues of differing importance).

**Experiment 1 — comparison vs. no case study.** Participants who drew a guided analogy across cases were far more likely to produce the target negotiation solution than baseline participants who had no case study: roughly 13% of analogy-trained participants applied the principle versus about 6% of baseline, with the comparison process also reducing reliance on naive compromise solutions. This establishes that case comparison helps relative to nothing, but the headline contrast is Experiment 2.

**Experiment 2 — comparison vs. studying the same two cases separately.** This is the key manipulation, because it holds the case content constant and varies only whether learners compared the two cases or studied them one at a time. "Participants in the comparison condition were over twice as likely (48% of participants) to transfer the principle to the test case as participants in the separate-cases condition (19%)," χ²(1, N=128)=11.85, p<.01. The advantage held for both principle types: for contingent contracts, 38% (comparison) vs 16% (separate); for trade-offs, 59% vs 22%. Comparison also reduced naive "split-the-difference" compromise solutions (61% comparison vs 83% separate). Schema quality mediated transfer: participants who, when describing the two study cases, articulated the underlying principle in full were the ones who transferred. So the same two cases yield roughly *double* the transfer when compared rather than read separately — exactly the analogical-encoding claim.

**Experiment 3 — live, face-to-face transfer.** The most ecologically demanding test. Participants trained by various methods then conducted an actual face-to-face negotiation in which a contingent contract was the efficient solution. The proportion of negotiating *pairs* who actually formed a contingent contract rose monotonically with the amount of comparison support: baseline .37 (7/19), separate-cases .55 (11/20), comparison .70 (14/20), and guided-analogy (the most comparison support) .90 (18/20). The two comparison-drawing groups formed reliably more contingency contracts than the non-comparison groups (χ²(1, N=79)=9.74, p<.01), with a reliable linear trend across levels of comparison support. Participants who had compared cases were also more likely to regard a contingency as the *ideal* settlement (≈68–73% vs ≈47–48%), indicating the schema shaped their judgment, not just their behavior under a cooperative partner. This experiment is what licenses the doc's phrase "cross-context transfer in professional training": the schema abstracted from written cases transferred to a live, dynamic, interactive negotiation — a genuinely different context.

**Verdict:** Consistent. The doc's claim — that case comparison (analogical encoding) produces cross-context transfer in professional training such as negotiation — is precisely the paper's demonstrated result, with the strongest evidence being Experiment 2 (comparison roughly doubled transfer over studying the same cases separately, 48% vs 19%) and Experiment 3 (the comparison-trained advantage carried into a live face-to-face negotiation, .70/.90 vs .55/.37). The doc's parenthetical about the 1999 Loewenstein, Thompson & Gentner companion is fair to mention; that earlier paper is not in the load-bearing set, but this 2003 article is its more complete successor and fully supports the cited point.
