Background & Justification

The KUD+ Framework

A theoretical and practical account of how the GC Curriculum Tool measures what a course is evidenced to require, assess, and support — scored on Know, Understand, and Do depth scales — and how those measurements feed program-level analysis and career-path alignment.

Companion to: Vision document Audience: Faculty, curriculum committee, advisory board Status: Working v1

§ 1The problem this addresses

Curriculum review at most programs operates on long cycles — seven years for accreditation, longer for systemic redesign — and on coarse evidence: catalog descriptions, faculty self-report, and the occasional advisory-board conversation. Between reviews, a program's understanding of itself drifts. Courses evolve, faculty change, employer expectations shift, and the gap between what the curriculum claims to deliver and what its assignments actually produce widens without anyone tracking it.

The GC Curriculum Tool proposes a continuous, evidence-backed alternative: each course described in commensurable terms, derived from its actual materials and confirmed by its instructor, available year-round to inform advising, accreditation, and revision. The unit of analysis is not what the syllabus claims a course will teach, but what the assignments and rubrics actually demand of students.

For that analysis to be useful, it must produce results that:

  • distinguish meaningfully between courses that touch a topic and courses that develop it,
  • survive review by other faculty without collapsing into subjective interpretation,
  • aggregate cleanly into program-level views (coverage matrices, scaffolding analysis, prerequisite checks), and
  • remain comprehensible to instructors who do not have time to learn a new technical vocabulary.

The framework described here, designated KUD+, is the working answer to those constraints. It begins with a well-established three-category outcome model (Know, Understand, Do) and extends each category with a 0–5 depth scale anchored to observable student attainment. The remainder of this document explains the theoretical basis for those choices, their practical implementation, and the limitations the approach acknowledges.

§ 2Theoretical grounding

The framework is consistent with several established traditions in curriculum design. None of them prescribe the specific depth scales used here; the language below describes how the framework is consistent with each tradition, not derived from any of them. The specific design choices — 0–5 grain, student-side anchors, the evidence-typed scoring rule, the null-vs-zero treatment of foundationals — are framework commitments that go beyond what the cited traditions individually establish, and are presented as such throughout this document.

Backward Design (Wiggins & McTighe)

Understanding by Design (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005) defines a three-stage planning sequence: identify desired results, determine acceptable evidence, plan learning experiences. The KUD categories give outcomes a typed structure that fits Stage 1's "desired results"; the framework's evidence rule — depth values above zero require excerpts of student-produced work — shares Stage 2's structural commitment that outcome claims must be backed by demonstrable evidence. The student-side anchoring of the depth scales is consistent with that commitment. Wiggins and McTighe do not themselves prescribe a 0–5 numerical depth scale on each of K, U, D; that operationalization is this framework's, taken in a direction the original Stage 2 is hospitable to but does not require.

Constructive Alignment (Biggs)

Biggs's principle of constructive alignment (Biggs, 1996) requires that intended outcomes, teaching and learning activities, and assessment all support one another. The framework's per-course audit conversation is shaped by this principle: mismatches between stated objectives and material evidence become first-class findings rather than silent inconsistencies. The audit conversation is one specific way to apply Biggs's principle, not the only one.

Bloom's Taxonomy (in support, not in place)

The revised Bloom's taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) remains the right vocabulary for analyzing the cognitive demand of an assignment. The framework does not replace Bloom's verbs; it employs them inside the audit conversation, where the auditor asks an instructor which Bloom level a high-stakes assignment most closely matches (remembering vs. applying vs. creating). This use is consistent with how Bloom is typically deployed at the assessment-design level. The discriminator between Bloom and KUD is one of scope: Bloom is granular enough for designing a single assessment; KUD is compact enough for mapping an entire program. The revised Bloom's also includes a Knowledge Dimension (Factual / Conceptual / Procedural / Metacognitive) that partially overlaps with K and U; the framework chooses its three-category structure over the Knowledge Dimension because the K/U/D distinction is more legible to non-specialist faculty audiences and because the Do dimension has no counterpart in A&K's Knowledge Dimension — and that gap is not incidental: the Do axis is where both career performance (work activities and tasks) and the development of habitual problem-solving are evidenced, so it is precisely the dimension a curriculum-to-career tool cannot do without.

Competency-Based Education

Competency-based frameworks define learning as the demonstrated mastery of specified competencies and often tie those competencies to external (employer) expectations. The accreditation-policy formulation is summarized in (NECHE, 2015); for a fuller theoretical account of the CBE tradition see Spady (1988, 1994). KUD+ shares the mastery orientation in its depth scales — the top step on Do (creative judgment, ability to guide others) describes a near-professional standard — and shares the external-alignment orientation through the planned career-path layer described in §11. The framework departs from strict CBE in two ways: it does not propose self-paced credit (it maps the existing time-based curriculum), and it treats program-level mapping as the primary deliverable rather than individual transcripts of demonstrated competencies. Less-strict CBE models that operate inside conventional credit-hour structures share more design choices with this framework than strict CBE does.

Integrative Learning

AAC&U's Integrative Learning VALUE Rubric (AAC&U) describes a progression from connecting prior knowledge across contexts to synthesizing learning in complex, real-world performances. The KUD+ Do scale's upper-range anchors (D=4 adapts to new constraints; D=5 performs creatively with critical judgment and guides others) are consistent with the Capstone-level descriptors of the AAC&U progression. The framework borrows the integrative-learning orientation rather than the rubric's structure: the VALUE Rubric is designed for assessing student work via portfolios across multiple dimensions, while the Do scale describes one dimension of what a course is designed to develop.

Knowledge spaces: the formal cousin of the “skill tree”

A recurring reaction from people seeing the tool for the first time is that it looks like a video-game skill tree. The reaction is apt, and worth reframing. The structure the tool maintains — a directed graph of skill-tagged prerequisite edges (§12) in which each competency carries a graduated 0–5 depth — is the kind of object Knowledge Space Theory formalizes (Doignon & Falmagne, 1985; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011): a domain of items linked by a surmise (prerequisite-like) relation, where a learner's “knowledge state” is the set of items they have mastered and the outer fringe of that state is the set of items they are now ready to learn. This is not academic exotica — it is what the ALEKS adaptive-learning system deploys to millions of students (Falmagne, Cosyn, Doignon & Thiéry, 2006), and the same prerequisite-graph structure recurs in K–12 learning-map systems such as the Dynamic Learning Maps project (Kingston & Broaddus, 2017), in the learning-progressions literature, in the mastery-tracking models of intelligent tutoring systems, and in the game-design “skill tree” itself. The framework was not built from any of it; it arrived at a knowledge-space-shaped structure from the two driving questions and the evidence rule alone — mild corroboration that the structure is a natural one. Two refinements the literature lends: the depth scale is what separates this from a binary unlock map (a competency is developed to a degree, not simply owned), and KST's outer fringe — the set of competencies a learner is now ready for — is precisely the computation a future advising view requires.

The metaphor is a good servant and a bad master, and three differences are load-bearing. First, a game skill tree is deterministic, closed, and fully authored — the designer decrees that node B unlocks when node A is bought. Curricular competence is none of these: it is probabilistic, contested, and known only through evidence. The tool's whole discipline — the evidence-above-zero rule (§7), the basis stamp that marks a prerequisite edge as measured, intended, or no-data (§12), the refusal to score an unaudited node zero — exists precisely to refuse to draw a crisp edge where there is no evidence. It builds the fuzzy, evidential knowledge space, not the crisp game one. Second, unlocking a node is not the same as having the skill: passing a prerequisite course does not deposit the competence in the student, which is exactly why attainment is scored from assessed evidence rather than from enrollment — the completion-is-not-acquisition distinction the knowledge-tracing literature exists to measure. Third, and most important, problem-solving is not a node: the framework treats it as a program-level emergent property of depth × productive failure × reflection (§4), the opposite of a discrete unlockable. Anyone taking the skill-tree metaphor literally would add a “problem-solving” node and gate it — the precise error §4 is written to prevent. The situated-learning critique that the 3-Act discussion (§9) already engages cuts the same way: competence is bound to its context of use, not a portable token that travels up the tree intact.

§ 4Problem-solving as a program-level emergent property

Problem-solving is among the attributes employers most consistently value when reviewing graduates' resumes — repeatedly ranked at the top of NACE's annual Job Outlook resume-attribute surveys, alongside teamwork and written communication (NACE, 2023). The natural curricular response is to add a "Problem-Solving" course, or to list problem-solving as a generic transferable skill the program develops. Both responses tend to underperform, in characteristic ways the cognitive-science research below explains, and the framework is deliberately structured to surface why and to support the alternative.

Why generic problem-solving instruction doesn't transfer

Four decades of cognitive science converge on the position that problem-solving competence is not a free-floating general skill that can be taught independently of subject matter. Willingham's widely-cited synthesis is unambiguous: "the processes of thinking are intertwined with the content of thought" (Willingham, 2007). He shows that even expert scientists fail at critical thinking when reasoning outside their familiar territory, and concludes that "it makes no sense to try to teach critical thinking devoid of factual content." McPeck made the strong-specificity case earlier and more sharply: "there are no general thinking skills, since thinking is always thinking about some subject-matter" (McPeck, 1981).

The empirical bedrock is the expert–novice literature, beginning with Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser's landmark 1981 study of physics problem-solving (Chi, Feltovich, & Glaser, 1981). Experts and novices were given the same set of physics problems and asked to sort them. Novices sorted by surface features (the problem mentions a pulley, an inclined plane); experts sorted by underlying principles (conservation of energy, Newton's second law). Larkin and colleagues converged on parallel findings the same year, showing experts use forward-chaining strategies that novices cannot access because they lack the schemas to recognize which principles are triggered (Larkin, McDermott, Simon, & Simon, 1980). The implication is structural: problem-solving skill in physics is not separable from physics knowledge — it is restructured domain knowledge organized around principles rather than surface features.

Sweller's cognitive load theory provides the mechanistic account (Sweller, 1988). Conventional open-ended problem-solving imposes high working-memory load, leaving little capacity for the schema acquisition that would eventually enable independent expertise. The remediation is not to remove the difficulty but to sequence it — worked examples and direct instruction early, when there is no schema base to activate; ill-structured problems later, once enough domain knowledge exists for the struggle to produce learning. Treating "Problem-Solving" as a generic course unit, decoupled from a specific domain in which schemas have been built, gives students the cognitive load without the schema acquisition.

The actual mechanism: depth → productive failure → reflection

The research converges on a specific developmental sequence. Problem-solving competence develops through repeated cycles of three things in a specific domain: building enough domain depth to make solution attempts meaningful, attempting solutions that fail in informative ways, and structured reflection that converts the failure into transferable understanding.

Productive failure — Manu Kapur's program of research — has identified a well-evidenced design pattern for this (Kapur, 2008) (Kapur, 2016). The unit of instruction is two-phase: students work on an ill-structured problem with their current knowledge, fail in various characteristic ways, and the consolidation phase uses those failure modes as scaffolding for the canonical approach. The 2021 meta-analytic review found an overall effect favoring problem-solving followed by instruction for conceptual knowledge and transfer (g ≈ 0.36 across 53 studies, 166 comparisons), with no significant advantage for procedural knowledge (Sinha & Kapur, 2021). The evidence is strongest when the problem-solving and instruction phases are carefully designed around the same target concept. Applying this design pattern to domain-general dispositions or design judgment is a framework hypothesis, not a direct finding from the productive-failure literature. Critically, Kapur (2016) explicitly distinguishes productive from unproductive failure — failure without expert consolidation, without structured debrief, produces no learning advantage and can damage motivation and self-efficacy. The mechanism is not failure as such; it is failure followed by sense-making.

Desirable difficulties — Robert Bjork's framework — generalizes the finding to a broader principle: conditions that suppress short-term performance often improve long-term retention and transfer (Bjork, 1994). Spaced practice, interleaving, retrieval testing, reduced feedback — all degrade in-the-moment performance and all dramatically improve learning that lasts. This is the empirical basis for the framework's commitment to evidence-of-attainment over evidence-of-coverage: a course that produces smooth performance during instruction but cannot demonstrate retention or transfer is producing the wrong thing.

Reflection — Donald Schön's reflective-practitioner work — established the theoretical case that professional expertise develops through cycles of reflection-in-action (adjusting during practice) and reflection-on-action (retrospective analysis) (Schön, 1983). Kolb's experiential learning cycle made the operational version explicit: concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation (Kolb, 1984). The 2013 meta-analysis of after-action reviews in professional contexts quantifies the effect: structured debriefs improve subsequent performance by approximately 25%, with effect sizes consistent across teams and individuals, simulated and real settings (Tannenbaum & Cerasoli, 2013). The structure that works is not elaborate — what happened, why, what to do differently — but it must be specific to the concrete failure, timely, and outcome-oriented. Generic "reflect on your learning" prompts do not.

How the KUD+ framework encodes this

The framework's depth scales were designed in part to make problem-solving development legible at the curriculum-mapping level. Look at the upper-range anchors:

  • Do — 4: Adapts performance to new conditions or constraints.
  • Do — 5: Performs creatively with critical judgment; can guide others.
  • Understand — 4: Reasons through novel cases not previously seen.
  • Understand — 5: Critiques the principle, identifies limits, extends to new domains.

These are not abstract scale-tops; they are explicit definitions of problem-solving facets within a domain. D-4 names transfer to novel conditions — exactly the capacity Perkins & Salomon's transfer literature identifies as the hard problem of education (Perkins & Salomon, 1992). D-5 names creative judgment and the ability to teach — the same marker Chi's expert–novice work identifies as evidence of principle-level rather than surface-level understanding. U-4 and U-5 name reasoning under novelty and the ability to critique principles, which together describe the metacognitive habits that the moderate-generalist position in the critical-thinking literature treats as the most cross-domain-portable component of problem-solving.

Treated this way, the framework already encodes problem-solving competence — but at the right scale (depth within a domain) rather than the wrong one (a generic transferable skill). When a curriculum analysis asks "does this program produce problem-solvers in graphic communications?", the right empirical question becomes: does the program develop technical competencies to D=4 and D=5 in the domains the career destinations require, with productive-failure and reflection scaffolding in the courses that get students there? The framework's data shape is built to answer exactly that.

What actually transfers, and how

A common and reasonable faculty intuition is that developing deep problem-solving in one area — and the habit of applying it — lets students problem-solve elsewhere. This is not in tension with the framework; it is the framework's position, with the conditions made explicit. The transfer literature sharpens it three ways.

What transfers is readiness to learn, not cold skill. Bransford and Schwartz's "preparation for future learning" reframes the usual transfer test: rather than dropping a person into a new domain to see whether prior skill fires unaided, measure how fast they learn the new domain (Bransford & Schwartz, 1999) (Schwartz & Martin, 2004). Deep prior problem-solving shows up clearly on that test: prepared learners extract more from each new learning opportunity. The honest mechanism behind the faculty intuition is usually not "I already knew the new thing" but "I learned it faster, because I knew what understanding-through-struggle feels like and what to look for."

The carrying mechanism is abstraction across varied cases plus deliberate bridging — and one rich domain can supply it. What travels is an abstract schema, and schemas form when learners compare multiple surface-different cases and articulate the shared structure: transfer from a single worked case is poor (~10%, ~30% with a hint) but roughly doubles when learners compare two cases and extract the principle (Gick & Holyoak, 1980, 1983), a result replicated in professional training via case comparison (Gentner, Loewenstein, & Thompson, 2003). Perkins and Salomon distinguish low-road transfer (surface similarity, lots of varied practice) from high-road transfer (mindful abstraction of the principle, deliberate bridging to new contexts), warning that to assume good teaching produces it automatically is "inordinately optimistic" (Salomon & Perkins, 1989). The curriculum consequence: a single deep domain produces transferable problem-solving only when it is taught as a stream of surface-varied instances of one deep structure, with that structure explicitly abstracted and bridged outward — GC's diagnostic troubleshooting (many faults sharing one diagnostic logic) is exactly such a stream; narrow drilling on one instance type is not.

Skill transfers poorly; disposition and readiness transfer better. The claim the evidence rejects most firmly is that cognitive skill itself generalizes across distant domains — meta-analyses of chess, music, and working-memory training find far transfer near zero after bias correction (Sala & Gobet, 2017), and a far-transfer taxonomy explains why distant transfer is rare (Barnett & Ceci, 2002). What the framework claims transfers is the narrower, better-supported thing: the disposition and readiness a well-designed cycle of productive failure and reflection builds, plus the adaptive (rather than routine) form of expertise that understanding-why and varied practice produce (Hatano & Inagaki, 1986). One implication for the audit: the active transfer ingredient — abstracting across varied cases and bridging to a new context — is not yet a CourseCapture probe; adding it would be the highest-value research-driven extension (a candidate sixth Area-7 condition, deferred pending its own design).

Why this is not a foundational competency

The framework treats five dispositions as foundational program-wide threads — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication — and the natural question is whether Problem-Solving should join the list. The literature reviewed above is the reason it does not. Foundational competencies are scored on Do only because they are dispositions demonstrated through behavior; a student exhibits Resilience or doesn't, and the question per course is whether the conditions to develop the disposition were present. Problem-solving is structurally different: it is not a disposition that lives in every domain in the same form, and it cannot be evidenced by behavior in the absence of domain content. A student who "exhibits problem-solving" in a course where they have K=1, U=0 is exhibiting flailing — not problem-solving. The disposition framework would mis-encode the construct, and the program-level signal would be polluted by every-course D=1-or-2 inflations that do not in fact contribute to problem-solving development.

The alternative — and the position this framework takes — is that problem-solving is a program-level emergent property. No single course produces a problem-solver. The program produces problem-solvers when its sequence of courses develops technical competencies to upper-range depths in the right domains and creates productive-failure conditions in the courses that get students there and builds reflection habits across the sequence. The right scale to evaluate problem-solving is the program, not the course.

What the framework does about it

Two operational decisions follow.

First, the CourseCapture audit explicitly probes for productive-failure and reflection conditions in every course. Live Audit Area 7 of the capture chat surfaces, per course: whether any assignment requires students to attempt solutions before being taught the canonical method (Kapur's generate-then-consolidate structure); whether the course assigns open-ended ill-structured problems; whether revision cycles respond to specific identified failures or are merely point-recovery iterations; whether structured post-mortems tie reflection to concrete failure modes; and the maximum domain depth the course reaches in its strongest technical competencies. These are graded findings — present, partial, or absent for the qualitative conditions, and a numeric depth for the supporting depth — surfaced into the snapshot's audit notes alongside prerequisite gaps and objective misalignments. They feed program-level analyses without distorting the K/U/D scoring of competencies.

Second, the program-level views read these findings together with the depth distribution to produce a problem-solving opportunity/risk read — the conditions for formation, not a validated measure that it occurred. The Program Coverage Matrix Live (faculty trial) surfaces where the curriculum reaches D-4 and D-5 against each career target's sub-competencies — the depth surface where problem-solving lives. The Scaffolding Analysis Live (Stage 1) reads the sequence of productive-failure conditions across courses, asking whether the curriculum's pedagogical scaffolding matches the depth scaffolding: are productive-failure experiences placed in courses where prior coursework has built enough K/U/D base for the failure to be productive, and is reflection scaffolding present in the upper-division courses where the cumulative pattern should consolidate?

Both reads are program-level by construction. A single course can be diagnosed for what it contributes — domain depth, productive-failure conditions, reflection scaffolding — but only the program-level view can answer whether the contributions cumulate into graduates who solve problems in the field.

Deeper treatment

This section summarizes the framework's position. The full research synthesis — including the Kapur 2×2 productive/unproductive framework, the Sinha & Kapur 2021 finding that the productive-failure advantage is outcome-dependent, with no significant advantage for procedural knowledge, and the framework's caution about applying the paradigm to domain-general dispositions or design judgment, the operational chain from cognitive science to audit probe to program-level opportunity read, and the framework's commitment to function as its own research instrument — is in the companion document Problem-Solving as a Program-Level Emergent Property: A Research Deep Dive.

Caveats and where the research is honestly thin

Five honest acknowledgments. First, the literature does not provide a clean threshold for "how much domain depth before productive failure becomes productive rather than unproductive." Kapur's labs work with students who have completed prerequisite coursework or read pre-assigned material; the bar is "enough prior knowledge to generate meaningful attempts," not a specific depth value. Empirical evidence suggests productive failure benefits do not require high prior math achievement provided topic-specific prerequisite knowledge is in place — the variety of student-generated solutions (inventive production) is a stronger predictor of learning than prior math achievement (Kapur, Saba, & Roll, 2023). From this evidence, the framework treats the question as degrees-not-thresholds: productive failure at lower depths contributes less per cycle than productive failure at higher depths; whether lower-depth cycles accumulate into a transferable disposition across a four-year curriculum remains a framework hypothesis (the paper's authors flag that they did not test the lower end of the prior-achievement spectrum). The scaffolding analysis weights contributions by depth rather than gating them at a threshold.

Second, longitudinal effects across a four-year curriculum are essentially unstudied. Most productive-failure research measures outcomes weeks or months out, not years. The framework's claim that cumulative productive-failure exposure across the degree produces better problem-solvers four years later is theoretically coherent and consistent with the deliberate-practice literature (Ericsson, Krampe, & Tesch-Römer, 1993) — but it is an extrapolation, not an empirically settled finding. Macnamara and colleagues' critiques further note that practice time alone explains only a fraction of expertise variance (Macnamara, Hambrick, & Oswald, 2014), which is why the framework requires the productive-failure and reflection conditions, not raw cycle counts.

Third, the productive-failure evidence base is strongest for technical domains with canonical constraints and identifiable failure modes — mathematics, physics, conceptual STEM. The GC curriculum's core sits in that zone: press operation, ink, plates, prepress and file diagnostics, color management, finishing — operations with canonical correct solutions and concrete, measurable failure modes. Productive failure is a strong analog for these canonical technical modules — strongest where assignments target conceptual troubleshooting rather than procedural fluency, since the PS-I evidence is for conceptual knowledge and transfer, not procedural skill (Sinha & Kapur, 2021). A smaller portion of the program reaches design-judgment territory where multiple defensible solutions coexist (typography, layout, brand identity); for those modules productive struggle — critique, iteration, revision, reflective practice in the Schön tradition — is the more defensible framing. Program-level cumulative claims about how repeated cycles build dispositional habits across the curriculum remain framework hypotheses awaiting longitudinal validation (see graduate-outcome-validation.html).

Fourth, the metacognition / dispositional question is better described today as a qualified yes under conditions than as fully contested. Metacognitive- and self-regulated-learning-strategy instruction shows reliable cross-domain effects in meta-analysis (Donker et al., 2014), and the preparation-for-future-learning and adaptive-expertise constructs give the cross-domain readiness claim a real evidence base. The load-bearing caveat is that none of this transfer is automatic — it appears only when the habit is explicitly cultivated (varied cases, abstraction, deliberate bridging, reflective control) and largely fails to appear from depth or content mastery alone. The framework is best read as the moderate-generalist position the faculty intuition describes: it structures the data shape around domain-bounded K/U/D and treats the cross-domain habit as emergent from the cumulative pattern given those abstraction-and-bridging conditions — and it would tell us if it were wrong, since a program that satisfies the structural conditions and still produces weak problem-solvers is evidence the account is incomplete. (Earlier drafts of this paragraph called this the "strong-specificity position"; that wording understated the cross-domain-readiness evidence the moderate-generalist reading now rests on.)

Fifth, the framework's founding intuition is itself partly grounded in personal experience of transfer, and personal experience is a trustworthy guide to the phenomenon but an unreliable guide to the mechanism. Introspective reports of one's own reasoning are reconstructed after the fact, not read off directly (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977), so "I used a general problem-solving skill" can be the feeling of deploying domain knowledge one cannot observe oneself using; selection confounds the inference (people who master one domain and succeed elsewhere are disproportionately high in general ability and conscientiousness, which independently predict both); and survivorship hides those who went deep and did not transfer. None of this makes the intuition wrong — it means the design must specify the conditions that reproduce the effect in a median student, which is why the framework locates the transferable element in cultivated disposition, readiness, and abstraction-and-bridging rather than in depth as such.

The position the framework takes, stated plainly: problem-solving competence is real, valued by employers, and consistently named among the attributes employers most want. The literature does not support treating it as a free-floating skill that transfers reliably without domain knowledge and explicit transfer scaffolding: far transfer is difficult, domain knowledge is required, and generic problem-solving instruction is weak. Problem-solving is the cumulative product of depth + productive struggle or failure + reflection, and the curriculum-mapping tool that wants to make it visible must measure each of those components at the right scale — the depths at the cell level, the conditions at the course level, the cumulative pattern at the program level. The framework is built to do that.

§ 5The KUD model

The framework adopts the three-category Know / Understand / Do structure as a planning vocabulary; Tomlinson's work on differentiated instruction (Tomlinson, 1999/2014) uses this same structure as a categorical pre-unit planning aid, and Wiggins & McTighe's Understanding by Design (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005) uses a structurally similar separation of desired understandings, knowledge, and skills in Stage 1 of backward design. Neither Tomlinson nor Wiggins/McTighe attach numerical depth scales to the categories; the depth extension introduced in §6 is this framework's contribution. The three categories are intended to separate what is often conflated in outcome statements:

  • Know. Facts, terms, conventions, vocabulary, procedural steps — content the student can recall, recognize, or reproduce. The cognitive operation is retrieval.
  • Understand. Principles, relationships, consequences, transferable ideas — the why behind the content. The cognitive operation is reasoning.
  • Do. Skills, procedures, performances, produced artifacts — what the student can execute or demonstrate. The cognitive operation is behavioral output.

The framework is widely deployed in outcome-writing guidance at the K–12 and undergraduate levels, where its three categories are valued for being faculty-readable without sacrificing the discrimination needed to distinguish a course that teaches definitions from one that teaches reasoning from one that teaches performance.

Why three categories rather than Bloom's six

Bloom's revised taxonomy (Bloom, 1956; Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) provides six cognitive levels — Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create — and is the established tool for analyzing the cognitive demand of an assessment task. For that purpose its granularity is appropriate.

But parsimony is not the main reason. The deeper reason is fit to purpose: the three categories are the native vocabulary of career competence — the destination this framework maps toward. Occupational models describe a role at the domain tier as required knowledge, work activities, and tasks — the tier this framework already treats as legitimate demand-side validation (see the occupational-frameworks discussion), as against the context-free “generic-skill” tier §4 rejects. KUD is near-isomorphic to that domain tier: Know ↔ required knowledge, Do ↔ work activities and tasks, Understand ↔ the transferable reasoning that carries either into an unfamiliar case. Mapping curriculum coverage onto a career destination is therefore a same-axis comparison rather than a translation. Bloom's six cognitive-process levels align with neither side of that mapping: built to classify assessment items inside a course, they force “Analyze vs. Evaluate vs. Create” onto “is this graduate prepared for the role,” a lossy translation at exactly the supply-meets-demand seam the framework exists to measure.

The Do dimension is load-bearing for a second reason Bloom cannot accommodate. This framework treats problem-solving not as a generic skill but as a program-level emergent property, built through repeated cycles of depth × productive failure × reflection (§4), and it encodes the facets of that development in the upper depth anchors — D-4's transfer to novel conditions, D-5's creative judgment and ability to teach. Problem-solving is thus read off accumulated performance under novelty across a program, not certified in any single cell. Bloom offers no surface to host this: its hierarchy is purely cognitive, with no behavioral axis on which a habit of deployment-under-difficulty could be observed accumulating — and its “higher-order thinking” framing invites the very category error §4 is written to prevent, treating problem-solving as a cognitive level a single task can certify rather than a disposition built by doing, over time, across courses.

The job of curriculum mapping at the program level is different. The tool must score hundreds of (course × competency × dimension) cells, each of which faculty may review and dispute. The finer the rubric, the more disputes resolve on the interpretation of category boundaries (Apply vs. Analyze, Evaluate vs. Create) rather than on the substantive question of whether the course covers the competency at all. The EU Tuning project's degree profiles use a comparably compact set of competency descriptors for the same reason, distinguishing competence (the holistic ability) from learning outcome (the level of competence achieved). This compact-and-leveled approach motivates the framework's choice of a small category set with depth extension over a fine-grained taxonomy without depth.

The three KUD categories give faculty enough resolution to express the dissociations that matter for curriculum work (a course can teach vocabulary without conceptual grasp, conceptual grasp without performance, or performance without articulation) while keeping inter-rater agreement tractable across a 28-course program. Bloom remains the right lens for assessment design at the course level; the verbs from Bloom's taxonomy continue to inform the prompts the auditor uses to probe each cell.

§ 6The depth extension (KUD+)

KUD as written treats coverage as essentially binary. A course either addresses a competency at the Know level or it does not; either at the Understand level or not; either at Do or not. Early piloting on the GC curriculum confirmed the limitation predicted by this design: when nearly every relevant course "covers" a competency, the binary cells collapse and the matrix loses its ability to discriminate. The prerequisite analyzer cannot distinguish a course that briefly mentions a skill from one that requires students to perform it independently; career-path alignment becomes a recitation of intersections rather than a depth-aware judgment.

KUD+ extends each of Know, Understand, and Do with a 0–5 depth scale anchored to observable student attainment. The scale's structure is intentionally uniform across dimensions; the anchors at each level differ because the underlying cognitive operations differ.

KNOW — recall and identification of content 0 Not present in this course 1 Exposure — student encountered it (heard about, read about), not tested 2 Recognize — student can identify the term/fact when shown options 3 Recall — student can produce the term/fact on cue, without prompt 4 Use correct terminology when discussing the domain 5 Fluent across the full vocabulary, including conventions and edge cases UNDERSTAND — reasoning about the why 0 Not present 1 Restates the explanation as given 2 Explains the rationale in own words 3 Predicts consequences (if X then Y, because…) 4 Reasons through novel cases not previously seen 5 Critiques the principle, identifies limits, extends to new domains DO — behavioral output 0 Not present 1 Performs with per-step direction or supervision 2 Performs using a reference or checklist 3 Performs independently in familiar conditions 4 Adapts performance to new conditions or constraints 5 Performs creatively with critical judgment; can guide others

Three properties of this scale are deliberate and worth naming explicitly.

Zero means absence, not weakness

A score of 0 indicates the dimension is not present in the course at all — a structurally different claim from a low non-zero score. Rendered in the coverage matrix, 0 produces an empty cell; 1 produces a faintly filled cell. This distinction matters for prerequisite analysis: a downstream course that needs students to use a term cannot rely on a prior course that scored only K1 (exposure).

Exposure (K1) is exclusively a Know event

Hearing a principle stated, watching a demonstration, reading about a technique — these are encounters with content the student has not yet engaged. They score K=1 with U=0 and D=0, because awareness is the definition of a Know event and the other dimensions require engagement. The scale enforces this asymmetry: the Understand and Do scales begin at active engagement (restating, performing with direction); they do not include a passive-exposure step. This prevents the inflation common in coverage rubrics where lecture-only content acquires a misleading depth score.

The scale is uniform in shape but distinct in anchor

Each dimension's level descriptors are specific to that dimension. "Mastery" of a fact (K5: fluent vocabulary command) is not the same achievement as mastery of a principle (U5: critique and extension) or mastery of a skill (D5: creative independence). Using the same numerical range across the three permits a clean coverage-matrix rendering; anchoring the levels differently keeps the model honest about what each dimension is measuring.

§ 7Student-side anchors and the evidence rule

Each level on each scale describes what the student does or grasps, not what the syllabus claims the course will teach. This is the most important practical commitment in the framework: aspirational syllabus language alone is not sufficient evidence for any score above the lowest meaningful level on its dimension. A syllabus statement that "students will understand X" does not by itself justify a score above U=1; the evidence above U=1 must come from student-produced reasoning (a graded written rationale, an oral defense, a journal entry, a memo that requires explaining the principle).

The evidence requirement is formalized in the scoring rule. All nonzero scores require evidence, but the kind of evidence differs by level:

  • k_depth = 1 requires evidence of delivery — a lecture topic listed, a textbook chapter assigned, a Canvas page covering the content.
  • k_depth > 1 requires an evidence excerpt naming where the student is tested or measured on the content.
  • u_depth > 0 requires an evidence excerpt showing student-produced reasoning.
  • d_depth > 0 requires an evidence excerpt showing student-produced behavioral output.

This anchoring is consistent with the second stage of Wiggins and McTighe's backward design (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005): outcomes are paired with the evidence that would demonstrate them. It is also the practical mechanism by which the binary collapse described in §6 is avoided. When the AI is required to cite evidence before assigning a level, lecture-only content cannot acquire above-K1 scores by accident, and the depth distribution across the program becomes informative.

What KUD+ scores measure — and what they don't

KUD+ operates on three distinct levels of evidence, and keeping them distinct matters for how the scores are interpreted:

  • Course materials evidence intended and assessed opportunities — what the course is designed to develop, as expressed in assignments, rubrics, prompts, and modules. This is the primary source for the audit.
  • Instructor confirmation during the audit conversation improves construct validity — it upgrades "the rubric suggests students wrote rationale" to "the instructor confirms this was graded reasoning, not template compliance." This closes the gap between what was assessed and what was actually developed, per the instructor's account.
  • Student attainment requires student work, rubric-scored performance, or external outcome evidence — what students demonstrably learned, not what the course aimed to develop.

A KUD+ score derived from course materials and instructor confirmation is a course-opportunity score: it characterizes the depth of learning opportunities the course creates and assesses. It becomes a student-learning claim only when linked to assessed student work or longitudinal outcomes — which is precisely what the Graduate Outcome Criterion-Relevance Study proposal addresses. This distinction should be carried forward wherever KUD+ scores are cited in program-level analysis: the coverage matrix reports the depth the curriculum was designed to develop; it does not yet directly report the depth graduates achieved.

What K=1 specifically requires

The evidence rule formally requires excerpts above K=1, which leaves K=1 itself uncovered by the rule and creates the appearance that K=1 can be assigned from syllabus mention alone. The framework's position is more constrained than this. The scoring rule in full:

  • K=1 can be supported by delivery evidence — lecture slides, module item titles, reading lists, or instructor confirmation that the topic was covered in class. A topic listed only in the aspirational syllabus, with no module, lecture, or assigned reading that actually delivered it, does not qualify for K=1 and scores K=0.
  • K>1 requires an evidence excerpt showing where the student is tested or measured on the content.
  • U>0 requires an evidence excerpt showing student-produced reasoning.
  • D>0 requires an evidence excerpt showing student-produced behavioral output.

What K=1 does not require is evidence of student engagement with the material — the whole point of K=1 is to mark presence-without-engagement. The auditor's job at this level is distinguishing actual delivery from aspirational mention.

An acknowledged measurement bias

The student-side evidence rule introduces a predictable measurement bias the framework should name explicitly: courses that assess through valid but text-poor methods — studio critique, oral defense, observed performance, portfolio pattern across multiple artifacts — produce less quotable rubric language than courses with written assignments and explicit rubric criteria. Both kinds of assessment can validly evidence high U and D depths; only the latter produces easily-cited text. Naïve application of the evidence rule would systematically under-score the former kind of course. The framework's mitigation is the instructor-confirmation step in the audit conversation: when the auditor cannot find a text-quotable excerpt but the instructor describes a graded oral defense or critique that demonstrates the depth, the instructor's transcript statement becomes the evidence excerpt. This works in practice but depends on the auditor probing for it. The audit conversation explicitly asks about non-text assessment modes for this reason.

External evidence supports this discipline. Xu et al. (2026), evaluating LLMs and expert annotators on mapping 200 postsecondary courses to 38 competencies across three frameworks (including O*NET), report two findings that bear directly on the evidence rule. First, the abstract competency frameworks were unreliable to score — inter-rater agreement among trained annotators initially collapsed to a Cohen's κ of 0.17–0.29 — until the coding guidelines explicitly separated “clearly outside the course” from “potentially relevant but lacking textual evidence,” after which agreement recovered to κ ≈ 0.92–0.94. That recovering distinction is the same one this framework draws between a foundational scored zero and one recorded null, and between evidence and aspiration: the disagreement was not noise but the absence of the evidence/aspiration line, and drawing it nearly resolved the disagreement. Second, no model reached human-level precision on fine-grained pedagogical judgment, the dominant error being inflation from “instructional keyword inference” — crediting a competency because its vocabulary appears. That is precisely the failure the evidence-above-zero rule exists to prevent. Their task operates one layer up from this one — judging whether a course document expresses a competency, the course-opportunity side — so the finding reinforces, rather than substitutes for, this framework's further step to assessed student evidence.

§ 8Foundational competencies

The KUD model fits cognitive and procedural outcomes cleanly. It fits affective and dispositional outcomes — agency, attention to detail, resilience, curiosity, communication — less cleanly, because there is no meaningful "Know resilience" or "Understand attention to detail" event in undergraduate practice. Students do not recall facts about resilience or reason their way through it in the moment; they exhibit it.

The framework treats five baseline foundational competencies as program-wide threads and scores each one on Do only:

  • Agency — initiative, ownership of decisions, drive.
  • Attention to Detail — care, accuracy, error-catching, follow-through.
  • Resilience — persistence through failure, adaptive recovery.
  • Curiosity — substantive questioning, inquiry beyond requirements.
  • Communication — written, oral, and visual articulation.

Their Know and Understand cells are recorded as null rather than zero; zero would imply the course tried to develop them at the K or U level and failed, which is not the right reading. A foundational with d_depth = 0 is a valid and informative finding: it means the course does not create the conditions (open-ended challenges, revision cycles, productive failure, peer-facing communication) that develop the disposition.

This shape is also why problem-solving — which employers name as often as any disposition — is deliberately not a sixth foundational. A foundational is scored on Do alone because its Know and Understand cells are vacuous: one exhibits resilience; one does not “know” it. Problem-solving is the inverse — it is irreducibly K×U×D, the deployment of principle-organized knowledge, reasoning through novelty, and adaptive performance together, with no existence apart from the domain content it operates on (§4). Modeling it as a Do-only attribute would amputate the organized knowledge that constitutes it, and scoring it as one cross-cutting line would re-commit the free-floating-skill error §4 dismantles. The framework instead captures both halves of what “problem-solving” colloquially fuses, in two places it already has: the disposition to engage hard problems lives in foundationals already in this list — Resilience (persistence through productive failure), Curiosity (inquiry into the unfamiliar), and Agency (self-directed deployment) — while the competence lives in the upper depths of the domain competencies (D-4/D-5, U-4/U-5). A sixth “problem-solving” foundational would double-count the first and re-abstract the second; the property is therefore read at the program level as an emergent pattern (§4), not stored in a per-course cell.

The framework adopts a hybrid approach: the five baseline foundationals are fixed and scored in every course, and the auditor may add additional foundational competencies (e.g., Collaboration, Professionalism, Ethical Judgment) when the course materials evidence them. This permits institutional stability in the baseline while allowing the data to surface dispositions the original list did not anticipate.

The selection of the five baselines

The choice of these five — rather than four, seven, or a different set — is a framework commitment rather than a derivation from a single canonical source. The selection draws on three influences. First, the NACE Career Readiness Competencies framework, which identifies eight competencies employers most consistently name (Critical Thinking, Communication, Teamwork, Technology, Leadership, Professionalism, Career & Self-Development, Equity & Inclusion). Communication appears in the framework's baseline directly; Critical Thinking is handled separately in §4 as a program-level emergent property rather than a per-course foundational, on the reasoning the same section articulates. Second, the broader dispositional-learning literature — including the AAC&U Essential Learning Outcomes, the CASEL social-emotional learning framework (Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness, Relationship Skills, Responsible Decision-Making), and the executive-function research in developmental psychology — converges on a small set of dispositional capacities that include initiative, persistence, attention regulation, and curiosity. Third, faculty surveys and advisory-board input specific to the Graphic Communications program identified Attention to Detail as a domain-relevant disposition consistently named in employer feedback for the program; this is the most GC-specific entry in the list.

The choice of five rather than a larger number reflects two practical commitments: every baseline foundational is scored in every captured snapshot, so the marginal cost of each additional foundational compounds across the program, and inter-rater agreement on dispositional Do scoring degrades when the list is long enough that auditors reach for the closest fit rather than recognizing absence. Five is a working hypothesis the framework treats as research input: as the snapshot corpus accumulates and advisory-board feedback surfaces gaps, the baseline can be revised. Collaboration is the most consistently mentioned candidate for inclusion; the framework's current treatment is to allow the auditor to add it per-course when materials evidence it, with the explicit acknowledgment that this creates uneven coverage across the program. If repeated captures show Collaboration is genuinely program-wide, it is a strong candidate for promotion to the baseline in a future revision.

The framework explicitly does not claim the five baselines are the right set. It claims they are a defensible working set, derived from the named influences, that the framework can revise as the data accumulates. This is the research-instrument framing applied to the baseline competency choice itself.

§ 9Program-level structure: the 3-Act model

Program-level analyses — scaffolding, prerequisite-gap, problem-solving capacity — require an organizing structure for the program against which the analyses can be run. A flat list of 28 courses is too undifferentiated for the scaffolding analysis to surface useful findings; a faculty-readable structure that says where in the program each course sits is what makes "this course expects mastery of something never introduced earlier" a tractable diagnostic. The framework requires a program-organization model. It does not prescribe a specific one.

The structure this framework proposes for the GC program is a three-act model: Foundations & Agency (learn the field), Integration & Mastery (integrate the field), Specialty & Application (apply a focus). Like every other commitment in this framework, the 3-Act model is a proposal — a working organizing structure offered for faculty deliberation, not an existing institutional policy or a settled curriculum design. The proposed design intent is to accelerate students through foundations into integration as fast as the field allows — anchored on the empirical observation that GC 4400 and GC 4060 are the courses where graduating students consistently report that "it all came together" and they first saw where they could add professional value. Completing Act 2 by mid-Junior year is proposed so that students would cross this integrative threshold while they still have 1.5+ years remaining in the program to develop career-path-specific skills in Act 3. This would invert the cornerstone-capstone bookend prevalent in engineering education (where integration is reserved for the senior capstone, leaving no time for post-integration specialization): the 3-Act structure deliberately positions integration earlier so specialization can follow.

The 3-Act proposal is consistent with established curriculum-design traditions but is not derived from any single one. The strongest theoretical parallels are Bruner's spiral curriculum (where each act revisits the disciplinary content at higher complexity), the engineering cornerstone-capstone model (with the 3-Act structure adding the named middle act that cornerstone-capstone leaves implicit), the modern medical-education three-phase structure (preclinical → clinical → specialty, though with externally-validated gates the 3-Act structure does not yet have), and cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown & Newman, 1989, as a response to the Lave & Wenger critique of decontextualized foundational instruction). The framework explicitly acknowledges these as consistency relationships, not derivations — the design choices (three acts specifically; the named pairing of Foundations with Agency; the mid-Junior year timing) are proposed commitments anchored in program-coordinator judgment and observed student experience, not literature prescriptions.

The scaffolding analysis treats act boundaries as analytical waypoints. A senior-level (Act 3) course that expects mastery of a competency no Act 1 or Act 2 course actually introduces is flagged as a brittle scaffold — the diagnostic concept that operationalizes prerequisite-network analysis (an established methodology in CS education research since at least 2014) at the level of named program phases. The framework's contribution at this level is pairing the diagnostic logic with depth-aware K/U/D scoring in a design curriculum; the diagnostic logic itself has antecedents in the broader curriculum-mapping literature.

The full research synthesis — including the corrections an adversarial review surfaced, the substantive response to Lave & Wenger's situated-learning critique via cognitive apprenticeship, the distinction between SDT (a motivational-environment theory) and self-regulated-learning (an agency-as-developmental-outcome theory), and an honest accounting of which 3-Act design choices are research-supported vs. stipulated — is in the companion document The 3-Act Program Structure: A Research Synthesis. The framework's program-level views (the coverage matrix shipped in Phase 1A, the scaffolding analysis specified for Phase 1B) read against the 3-Act structure; if the structure is revised, the diagnostics revise with it.

Inputs consumed

No re-ingestion is required at capture time. Where prerequisite courses have themselves been captured previously, their confirmed profiles are included as authoritative evidence of what students arrive with.

The audit conversation

An AI auditor opens the session with a brief summary of what the materials show and the single most consequential gap or contradiction it noticed, then asks one targeted question at a time. The audit covers six areas, worked through across the conversation:

  1. Prerequisite sufficiency. The auditor walks the catalog's required incoming skills one at a time, asking whether each is genuinely arrived with or re-taught in this course. Where a prerequisite course has a captured profile, the auditor reads it directly and cites its depths rather than asking the instructor to recall.
  2. Stated objectives vs. evidenced outcomes. The auditor performs the comparison in both directions: objectives without material evidence (aspirational), and material outcomes not captured in the stated objectives (taught but unstated).
  3. Cross-source overlaps and contradictions. Differences in point values, assignment lists, or outcomes language between the syllabus, Canvas, and uploaded materials are surfaced as findings, with one source designated authoritative by the instructor.
  4. Bloom's-level probe per major assignment. For the highest-stakes assignments, the auditor asks which Bloom verb most accurately describes what students are doing, and what evidence supports that classification.
  5. Threshold concept. The conceptual shift that separates students who genuinely understand the course from students who memorized its content.
  6. Foundational competency conditions. For each of the five baseline dispositions, the auditor probes whether the course creates the behavioral conditions that develop the disposition, rather than asking whether the course "teaches" it.

The instructor sees a readiness signal on every turn: a 0–100 score reflecting the auditor's honest self-assessment of how defensibly the profile could be generated right now, with short labels for what is covered and what is still being probed. The instructor may stop the session at any point; the score is advisory, not gating.

Inaccessible content

The source table above identifies the pipeline's current reach. Sources listed as "Not supported" — principally video platforms other than YouTube (Panopto, Canvas Studio, Vimeo, Kaltura) and external LTI tool content — produce no retrievable text. Where materials reference such sources, the auditor is required to detect them, surface them with their specifics (title, URL, location in the materials, estimated content, scoring implication), and keep scoring conservative pending instructor confirmation. Above-zero scores that depend on inaccessible content are flagged in the rationale fields accordingly.

Output: the Course Outcome Profile

On instructor command, the system produces a structured profile:

  • 5–15 technical competencies discovered from the materials, each scored on K/U/D depth with evidence excerpts and a short rationale per competency.
  • All five baseline foundational competencies scored on Do only (with d_depth = 0 when the course does not develop the disposition), plus any additional foundationals the materials evidenced.
  • Audit notes covering prerequisite gaps, objective misalignments, cross-source conflicts, and suggested objective revisions.
  • If requested, a draft revision of the learning objectives grounded in the materials evidence; this is informational only and does not modify the catalog automatically.

The profile is reviewed by the instructor in a structured editor — sliders per dimension, evidence visible per cell, audit notes in a sidebar — and confirmed before downstream views consume it.

Methodological note: build-time pedagogical encoding

The auditor's reasoning is not free-form. Each AI integration point in the system is governed by a version-controlled system prompt that encodes one or more named pedagogical skills from an external library — Manning's Education Agent Skills Library (Manning, 2025). The prompts encode procedural skill definitions from this open GitHub-based library. It is used as an implementation scaffold, not as independent validation of KUD+. The scholarly grounding for the analytical commitments the prompts encode — backward design (Wiggins & McTighe), assessment validity, evidence-first planning — is the pedagogical research those prompts operationalize; Manning's library is the implementation artifact through which that grounding is applied to AI agent behavior.

The encoding is build-time, not runtime: the skill definitions (Backwards Design, KUD Chart Authoring, Coverage Audit, Developmental Band Translator, Assessment Validity Checker, Disciplinary AI Literacy, KUD Knowledge Type Mapper) are read at design time and their reasoning frameworks are embodied directly in the prompt bodies. Each prompt names the skills it encodes in its frontmatter as the contract that the encoding happened. The intent is to make the AI's analytical disposition inspectable and citable rather than opaque. Where Manning frameworks describe the analytical scaffold (how to read evidence, how to type knowledge correctly, how to weigh validity threats), the depth scales described in §6 remain the operational rubric — Manning frames the question; KUD+ supplies the answer space.

Occupational frameworks (O*NET, ESCO): where they fit, and where they don't

A natural question is whether the career-destination side should be anchored in a standardized occupational framework — the US Department of Labor's O*NET or the EU's ESCO — rather than in bespoke, employer-derived Role Outcome Profiles, and whether the sub-competency vocabulary the program maps against should be sourced from one. The answer the evidence supports is a split verdict: these frameworks are useful at one tier and on one side, and actively counter-indicated at another.

Rejected — the generic-skill tier as a structural backbone. O*NET's 35 cross-occupational "Skills" (Critical Thinking, Complex Problem Solving, Active Listening) are precisely the context-free, transferable-skill construct §4 of this document spends its length rejecting — and they are rejected by the same authorities §4 already cites (Willingham; Perkins & Salomon; Gick & Holyoak). The independent National Academies review of O*NET (2010) reached the same conclusion from the measurement side: the Skills domain "lacks the extensive research base" supporting O*NET's Abilities taxonomy and smears "problem solving" redundantly across Skills, Abilities, Work Styles, and Work Activities, so raters cannot reliably distinguish adjacent descriptors. Deeper still, the competency-based-education critique — Gonczi's distinction between behaviourist skill-decomposition and integrated competence, and Winch's argument that achieving an occupational profile's individual skills "does not guarantee occupational competence" because it strips the integration and context that constitute it — warns against anchoring any curriculum to an external skill checklist as its backbone. The clearest empirical datum is the UK's National Vocational Qualifications (1986–2015): an explicit competency-checklist system that measurably hurt graduates' labour-market prospects (IFS) and was ultimately retired. Atomizing competence into an external list optimizes for adequacy — the central tendency of current practice — rather than the depth and integration that produce expertise and prepare students to learn what the framework has not yet named.

Aligned — the domain tier, and the demand side. O*NET's domain-anchored layers — Knowledge areas, Generalized and Detailed Work Activities, and occupation-specific Tasks — describe the within-field content where the transfer literature says near transfer is reliable, and where occupational frameworks have the most construct validity. Used at that tier, an occupational framework is a defensible external-validation vocabulary: a sourced checklist against which a hand-curated, GC-specific sub-competency set can be cross-checked for omissions. And O*NET's Importance and Level ratings are a genuine career-demand signal — they belong on the demand side, where they can seed a Role Outcome Profile so that employer input refines a baseline rather than originating one from scratch. Two limits keep this bounded: O*NET is not a ready-made GC vocabulary (its 33 Knowledge areas are far too coarse — there is no Typography, Color Science, or Press Chemistry; the GC-specific content must be hand-extracted from the Tasks and Detailed Work Activities of GC-relevant SOC occupations), and at least two GC destinations (an emergent "AI Workflow Orchestrator"; a cross-occupational "Creative Generalist") have no clean SOC home at all.

The reframe that matters. Every occupational framework — and every commercial skills-intelligence system built on one — shares a documented blind spot: it records the presence of a skill but not its depth. O*NET's Level scale is a job-demand rating, not a learner-attainment measure; ESCO encodes no within-skill proficiency at all. The KUD+ depth scale — attainment scored at graduated depth against student-side evidence — is that missing layer. The framework is therefore not behind the skills-intelligence field for lacking a fourteen-thousand-node taxonomy; it is ahead on the dimension that field is documented to be missing, and the design risk runs the other way: atomizing the evidence-anchored depth core into an external checklist would trade the framework's actual differentiator for the field's actual weakness. The operational consequence — keep the hand-curated, evidence-anchored sub-competencies as the backbone; use occupational frameworks only at the domain tier as a sourced checklist and on the demand side as a benchmark; and never push them into the evidence-first capture interview — is developed, with the full research base and a utilisation plan, in the companion occupational-frameworks research memo.

§ 12Prerequisite-gap method

The prerequisite-gap analysis answers Q2 at the course level: do the skills students bring in from declared prerequisite courses actually support what this course's assignments require? The method is implemented as a set of persisted, faculty-confirmed prerequisite edges from which a deterministic gap engine reads. This section explains the methodological commitments that distinguish this approach from simpler alternatives.

Direct edges, not authored transitivity

Each prerequisite edge is a direct, skill-tagged relationship: it connects one focal course to one prerequisite course and names the specific sub-competency the focal course's assignments expect, along with the K/U/D depths it presupposes. These edges are the only authored artifact. Transitivity — the fact that a course two levels back in the prerequisite chain may also contribute to the expected depth — is never authored explicitly; it is derived at analysis time by traversing the graph of direct edges. This design choice prevents the combinatorial maintenance burden that arises when transitive dependencies are hand-curated and prevents double-counting: each course's contribution is counted once, at its position in the chain, not once per downstream consumer.

Ordinal-MAX aggregation: why depths are not summed

When multiple prerequisite courses all develop the same sub-competency, the gap engine computes the gap by taking the maximum attained depth across all direct prerequisites, then comparing that maximum against the focal course's expected depth. It does not sum the depths across prerequisites.

The reason is structural: KUD+ depth values are ordinal levels, not quantities. A student who has encountered a skill in two separate courses at depth 3 each has not thereby attained depth 6 — they have attained depth 3, perhaps reinforced. What matters for prerequisite sufficiency is whether any course in the student's background has developed the skill to the required level; additional courses developing the same skill at the same or lower level add practice value but do not change the attained depth for the purpose of the gap calculation. Summing ordinal levels would produce a number that has no interpretable meaning in the depth-scale framework and would cause the analysis to erroneously judge students well-prepared when no single course reaches the required depth.

The intended-vs-attained distinction and the basis stamp

Every gap computation carries a basis field that distinguishes three conditions:

  • measured — the prerequisite course's attained depth comes from a confirmed, evidence-backed Course Outcome Profile (a captured snapshot). This is the highest-quality basis: it reflects what students who completed the course actually demonstrated, per instructor confirmation during an audit conversation.
  • intended — the prerequisite course's depth is estimated from the catalog description, syllabus, or the focal course's incoming_expectations field, without a confirmed snapshot. This is intended coverage — what the course claims or is understood to provide — not attained depth. Per the evidence-above-zero rule (§7), a syllabus statement is not by itself evidence of student attainment; the intended basis exposes this caveat transparently rather than presenting the estimate as equivalent to measured depth. In the gap view, intended-basis cells are rendered as claimed — matching the evidence ladder's terminology — and are flagged as unverified.
  • none — the prerequisite course has not been captured and no intended estimate is available. The gap engine returns no data for this edge, not a gap. This is the honesty commitment: an uncaptured prerequisite cannot be assumed to provide zero coverage (which would make every unprobed edge look like a critical gap) or assumed to provide full coverage (which would make the absence of data invisible). no_data cells are displayed explicitly with a "not yet captured" label; the burden of resolving them is to capture the prerequisite course, not to infer from absence.

The intended-vs-measured distinction maps directly onto the evidence-above-zero rule described in §7: measured depth has the same evidentiary status as a confirmed Course Outcome Profile cell; intended depth has the status of a syllabus claim (K=1 in the framework's evidence hierarchy). Program-level analyses that aggregate across courses should weight these differently and surface the distinction, not collapse them into a single gap count.

What the gap engine computes

For each (focal course, sub-competency) pair where the focal course specifies an expected incoming depth, the engine:

  1. Collects all direct prerequisite edges on that sub-competency.
  2. For each edge, looks up the prerequisite course's attained or intended depth for that sub-competency, with its basis.
  3. Selects the edge with the maximum depth (if multiple edges share the same sub-competency) — ordinal-MAX, no double-count.
  4. Computes the gap as expected_depth − max_attained_depth per dimension (K, U, D). A positive result is a gap; zero or negative means the requirement is met or exceeded at that depth.
  5. Stamps the result with the basis of the max-depth edge, since the reliability of the gap judgment is that of its best-evidenced source.

The result is a per-(course × sub-competency × dimension) gap record, stamped with its evidence basis, that feeds both the per-course gap view and any future program-wide rollup.

Design status

The prerequisite-edge method described here is implemented in the tool as of 2026-06-05. The per-course gap view is live at /courses/[code] for confirmed snapshots. The program-wide graph visualization is deferred.

The intended-basis rough pass is also live as of 2026-06-05. A one-LLM-call-per-course extractor (intended-skills-extract) reads catalog fields (description, learning objectives, major projects, required skills) against the sub-competency catalog and stores syllabus-asserted K/U/D depths in a separate course_intended_coverage table, banded claimed on the evidence ladder. This is the mechanism that produces intended coverage for uncaptured courses, feeding the gap engine's basis:'intended' seam so cold-start gap views are non-empty before any instructor has been audited. The binding rule applies without exception: intended coverage is reference-only in CourseCapture. The credibility band leaves claimed only through real evidence gathered in the audit conversation — never by the instructor confirming what the syllabus already says. This rule prevents acquiescence-bias laundering, where an instructor's natural inclination to agree with their own syllabus would otherwise launder aspiration into attainment.

§ 13Validation: how we'd know if this works

The framework makes substantive commitments that established research does not directly validate. The depth scales are working-hypothesis instruments. The 3-Act program structure is a proposed organizing model. The claim that cumulative depth + productive-failure scaffolding + reflection produces transferable problem-solving competence four years out is theoretically coherent but empirically untested at the program scale this tool operates at. The framework's stance is not that these commitments are settled — it's that the tool is itself the research instrument that generates the evidence to test them.

Three layers of validation are designed into the framework's data shape.

Internal validation: the data the tool generates about itself

Every captured snapshot is a dated, evidence-backed record of what one course developed at one moment in time. Snapshots accumulate. Each program-level analysis (the coverage matrix, the per-course prerequisite-gap view, and the scaffolding strip today; the scaffolding AI-narrative layer and the advising view still to come) reads against the snapshot corpus and produces diagnostic outputs that are themselves measurable: does the matrix surface gaps that faculty review confirms as real gaps, or does it surface patterns that faculty review dismisses as noise? Does the scaffolding analysis flag brittle scaffolds that hold up under interrogation, or does it produce false positives at a rate that erodes trust? The flag-and-dispute pipeline is the framework's internal validation surface — every AI judgment can be disputed, disputes persist, and patterns of disagreement update the AI's prompts over time. The framework's bet is that this internal validation loop, run continuously across the program for years, produces a more durably calibrated tool than a one-time external study would.

External validation: graduate-outcome data against career-target competencies

The empirical question the framework most needs answered is whether graduates who pass through a 3-Act-structured curriculum, with the coverage and scaffolding patterns the matrix surfaces, actually achieve the career-target competencies the framework predicts. The companion Graduate Outcome Criterion-Relevance Study proposal Planned describes the planned external-data check: a dataset of 268 Clemson GC graduates spanning 2018–2026 across 43 occupational categories, used to test whether the framework's career-target classifications and competency predictions match actual graduate employment trajectories. This is the closest the framework can come to a randomized comparison — the alternative-program comparison-group data the literature would ideally call for does not exist for graphic communications at the scale needed. The graduate-outcome dataset is the framework's path to evidence-against-an-external-standard.

Process validation: faculty and advisory-board review

Curriculum-design decisions are not the kind of empirical claim that can be settled purely by data. Career-target descriptors, sub-competency definitions, act boundaries, foundational-competency lists — these are choices made by faculty leadership and refined by advisory-board input. The framework's validation surface at this layer is whether the categories the tool uses match the categories faculty and advisory partners actually think with. When the matrix surfaces a finding that contradicts faculty intuition, the resolution may be that the matrix is wrong; it may be that the intuition is wrong; it may be that both are pointing at a real ambiguity in how the program defines what it's trying to develop. The framework treats all three resolutions as productive — each one teaches something — provided the disagreement is named and acted on rather than dismissed.

What "wrong" would look like

The framework should be able to fail informatively. Concrete signals that would warrant revising or replacing it:

  • The coverage matrix consistently surfaces gaps that faculty review uniformly dismisses as artifacts — across multiple snapshots, multiple targets, multiple semesters. (Matrix scoring would need recalibration or the underlying KUD+ depth scales would need revision.)
  • Graduates whose curricular history matches the framework's "well-scaffolded" pattern do not, on the graduate-outcome dataset, actually perform better in their target career destinations than graduates whose history doesn't. (The framework's central scaffolding claim would be falsified.)
  • The brittle-scaffold diagnostic flags Act 3 courses as expecting unsupported mastery, faculty redesign those courses to address the diagnostic, and student performance in those courses does not improve. (The diagnostic isn't tracking what it claims to track.)
  • Inter-rater reliability on the AI's K/U/D scoring proves unacceptably low — two captures of the same course by the same instructor with the same materials produce systematically divergent profiles. (The instrument isn't measuring stably.)

None of these would invalidate the underlying ambition of evidence-backed program-level curriculum mapping. They would tell the framework which of its specific design choices need to change. That is what it means for a tool to be a research instrument in good faith: the framework commits to revising its own design when the data tells it to.

§ 14What this framework is not

Several limitations and scope decisions are worth naming explicitly.

  • Not an evaluation of faculty or students. Depth scores describe what the course's materials and assignments require, not how well any instructor delivers the course or how well any student performs in it.
  • Not an automated curriculum designer. The framework surfaces what is and supports faculty deliberation; redesign decisions remain committee work informed by the evidence, not generated by it.
  • Not a replacement for faculty judgment. The auditor drafts, the instructor confirms. Every depth value is editable in the review step; reviewer status is recorded on each profile.
  • Not empirically validated yet. The framework's specific scales, the 3-Act structure, and the cumulative-scaffolding claim are working hypotheses. The framework's response to the absence of validation is structural rather than evasive: the tool generates the longitudinal evidence its own commitments would need from external research. The full three-layer validation approach — internal flag-and-dispute pipeline Live, external graduate-outcome comparison Planned, faculty/advisory-board review — is described in §13 above, with the graduate-outcome dataset path detailed separately in the Graduate Outcome Criterion-Relevance Study proposal.
  • Not extensible to fully automated assessment scoring. Bloom's taxonomy and AAC&U VALUE rubrics remain the appropriate lenses for evaluating student work; this framework is designed for describing what a course develops, not for grading what a student produced.
  • Not insensitive to material limitations. The pipeline's source coverage — what it can retrieve, what it cannot, and the scoring implication for each — is documented in the canonical ingestion table in §10. In brief: Canvas materials (assignments, rubrics, pages, discussions, quizzes, modules, file attachments), linked Google Docs/Sheets/Slides and Drive PDFs, YouTube captions, direct uploads, and catalog values are all within reach. Video platforms other than YouTube (Panopto, Canvas Studio, Vimeo, Kaltura) and external LTI content are not. The auditor is required to detect references to unsupported sources, flag them with their specifics, and keep scoring conservative pending instructor confirmation; profiles produced from incomplete materials carry that uncertainty in their rationale fields.
