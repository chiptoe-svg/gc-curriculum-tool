The 3-Act Program Structure

An honest accounting of what curriculum-design literature the GC program's 3-Act structure (Foundations & Agency → Integration & Mastery → Specialty & Application) is consistent with, what it isn't grounded in, and how it engages the critiques that staged curricula face.

Date 2026-05-25 Status Research-tool framing — consistency claims, not derivation claims Audience Faculty, curriculum committee, advisory partners

§ 1Why this document exists

The Vision document proposes organizing the GC program in three acts: Learn the field (Foundations & Agency), Integrate the field (Integration & Mastery), Apply a focus (Specialty & Application). The framework treats act boundaries as analytical waypoints — the planned Phase 1B Scaffolding Analysis uses them to surface "brittle scaffolds," where an Act 3 course expects mastery of a competency no Act 1 or Act 2 course actually introduces.

The 3-Act structure is a proposal, not an existing institutional policy. It is load-bearing for the scaffolding diagnostic, and it makes substantive claims about how undergraduate professional education should be organized. Both deserve careful treatment.

This document does two things. First, it situates the 3-Act structure in the curriculum-design literature — what it is consistent with, what it isn't grounded in, what it shares with peer fields. Second, it engages the strongest critiques honestly, including ones the framework's first-draft response did not address adequately. The intent throughout is honesty about which parts are research-supported, which are framework commitments, and which are working hypotheses the tool's data will eventually test.

A framing note

The framework is a research instrument. It does not need every design choice to be derived from settled literature. What it does need is honesty about which choices are derived and which are stipulated, so the diagnostic outputs can be interpreted with appropriate confidence — and so the structure can be revised as evidence accumulates.

Part I — What the structure is consistent with

The 3-Act structure is consistent with several established curriculum-design traditions. It is not derived from any of them. The distinction matters: a structure that is consistent with multiple frameworks is defensible; a structure that claims to be derived from those frameworks claims more than the consistency relationship supports.

§ 2Bruner's spiral curriculum

Jerome Bruner's The Process of Education (1960, Harvard University Press) introduced the spiral curriculum concept. Bruner's full description is worth reading completely rather than condensed:

"Successful efforts to teach highly structured bodies of knowledge … often took the form of a metamorphic spiral in which at some simple level a set of ideas or operations were introduced in a rather intuitive way and, once mastered in that spirit, were then revisited and reconstrued in a more formal or operational way, then being connected with other knowledge, the mastery at this stage then being carried one step higher to a new level of formal or operational rigour and to a broader level of abstraction and comprehensiveness."

Two features of Bruner's spiral are essential and don't collapse into a bounded three-act structure: the iterative escalation (each turn raises rigor while broadening scope) and the connection to other knowledge (mastery at one level requires connecting back across the disciplinary network). The 3-Act structure preserves the iterative-revisitation logic — Act 2 courses revisit Act 1 competencies at higher integration — but bounds it into three named phases rather than a continuous spiral. This is a legitimate variation; Gibbs (writing in Phi Delta Kappan, 2014, "Reconfiguring Bruner: Compressing the Spiral Curriculum") argues the spiral can be "compressed" or "punctuated" into identifiable stages without losing its theoretical integrity. But the bounded version is a variant, not a direct implementation. The framework should be honest that Bruner's spiral is a theoretical cousin, not a parent.

§ 3Constructive alignment at program scale

John Biggs's Teaching for Quality Learning at University (1999/2011, Open University Press) defines constructive alignment as coherence between intended learning outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment. The principle was originally specified at the course level but has been systematically extended to program scale.

The 3-Act structure is consistent with program-level constructive alignment provided that (a) each act has defined outcome sets and (b) Act 2 and Act 3 outcomes are explicitly mapped to Act 1 prerequisites. Biggs's framework does not mandate that stages be named or that progression be enforced through gates — it requires only that outcomes be aligned across levels. The 3-Act structure adds a sequencing-and-naming layer Biggs's model does not require.

§ 4Backward design at program scale

Wiggins and McTighe's Understanding by Design (2005, ASCD) frames curriculum as beginning from desired results. The original UbD focuses on units and courses, but subsequent applications have extended the logic across program scale.

The 3-Act structure is compatible with backward-design logic: Act 3 specialty outcomes define what Act 2 integration must produce, which defines what Act 1 foundations must establish. The framework was not, however, designed using backward design — the acts were named first as an organizing model, and outcomes were specified within them. This is a retrospective compatibility, not a derivation. The honest framing: backward design and the 3-Act structure share a common logical orientation (work back from desired graduate capabilities); the framework would benefit from a deliberate backward-design pass at each act boundary to operationalize this compatibility more fully.

§ 5Cornerstone-capstone in engineering education

The closest empirically-tested analog to the 3-Act structure is the cornerstone-capstone model that dominates U.S. engineering education. ASEE has documented this structure extensively (Pierrakos et al., 2019; the West Point Systems Engineering paper, 2010; ABET's 2025–2026 accreditation criteria require demonstration of appropriate course sequencing). Engineering students encounter authentic disciplinary problems in their first year (cornerstone), build technical competencies through years 2–3, and complete an open-ended capstone in their senior year.

The 3-Act framework's most credible substantive contribution to this lineage is naming the middle act explicitly and proposing the integrative "everything comes together" moment at the Act 2 / Act 3 boundary rather than at the senior capstone. In the GC program specifically, GC 4400 and GC 4060 are mini-capstones that faculty and graduating students consistently identify as the courses where "it all came together" — where students first experience integrated practice and recognize how their developing competencies translate into professional value. The 3-Act proposal treats this empirical "aha" experience as the structural target of Act 2, and proposes completing Act 2 by mid-Junior year so that students would cross this integrative threshold while they still have 1.5+ years remaining to develop career-path-specific Act 3 specialization. This would invert the cornerstone-capstone bookend: rather than reserving the integrative experience for the senior capstone (which leaves no time for post-integration specialization), the 3-Act proposal deliberately accelerates students to the integration moment so the back half of the program can be career-targeted development.

This design rationale is the strongest pragmatic case for the 3-Act structure over a cornerstone-capstone alternative. Critiques of the cornerstone-capstone model consistently note that intermediate development is under-theorized — the bookends are named, the middle is implicit — and that the senior-capstone-as-only-integrative-moment leaves little time to apply the integration. The 3-Act structure addresses both: it names the integration phase as a diagnosable stage with its own scaffolding requirements, and it positions integration early enough that specialization can follow.

The framework also differs from cornerstone-capstone in an important way: engineering's gates are externally validated (ABET-accredited deliverables, capstone industry partnerships, often an FE exam). The 3-Act structure's gates are currently internal to the program. The framework's planned readiness criteria are the move toward closing this gap.

§ 6Scaffolding theory at program scale

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (Mind in Society, Harvard University Press, 1978) describes learning as occurring in the space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with assistance. Applied at program scale, scaffolding logic means earlier courses function as the "more knowledgeable other" — establishing competencies that students will later exercise independently.

The 3-Act structure operationalizes this in a specific way: Act 1 courses provide heavy scaffolding, Act 2 courses expect partial independence, Act 3 courses expect near-professional autonomy. The "brittle scaffold" diagnostic concept the framework introduces maps onto what the ZPD literature calls "premature fading" of support — removing scaffolding before the student has internalized the underlying competency.

It is worth being honest that the "brittle scaffold" framing is not novel as a diagnostic. Prerequisite network analysis as a formal methodology has been developed independently in computer-science education research since at least 2014, including the Stanford / arXiv "Curriculum Prerequisite Network" paper (2014) and subsequent work on bottleneck courses, backbone courses, and prerequisite-based student outcome modeling. The framework's contribution is applying this diagnostic logic to a named phase-boundary concept in a design curriculum, and pairing it with the depth-aware K/U/D scoring; the diagnostic logic itself has antecedents. This is a pedagogical contribution rather than an original research-methodology contribution.

§ 7Preparation for future learning

Bransford and Schwartz's "Rethinking Transfer: A Simple Proposal with Multiple Implications" (1999, Review of Research in Education) introduced Preparation for Future Learning (PFL) as a complement to classic transfer. PFL identifies a specific mechanism: students who engage in generative problem-solving before instruction subsequently learn more from that instruction than students who receive direct instruction first.

PFL is a unit-level and lesson-level finding about instructional sequencing within a course. The framework's earlier reading of PFL as a justification for a multi-year staged curriculum was a scale jump the original research does not validate. Where PFL is genuinely useful for the framework is in within-Act 1 course design: Act 1 works best when students encounter problems that the foundations will later solve, not when they simply acquire foundations in anticipation of future problems. This is a within-course commitment about how Act 1 instruction should be designed, not a justification for the existence of three acts.

Part II — Comparable models in peer fields

§ 8Multi-phase curricula across professional education

Multi-phase professional curricula are common across U.S. higher education. The 3-Act structure is consistent with this pattern, but the pattern itself is at a high level of abstraction. Most professional programs converge on "foundations before advanced work" because the alternative is structurally incoherent. Treating this as independent convergence on the specific 3-Act framework would overstate what the comparison shows.

A more honest framing: staged professional curricula are common, legitimate, and credentialed across fields, and the 3-Act structure is one principled instance of that pattern. The comparisons below illustrate the family resemblance and surface the important divergences.

Medical education

U.S. medical schools organize undergraduate medical education into preclinical years, clinical clerkships, and (for those who enter residency) specialty training. The history here is more complex than it is sometimes presented. Abraham Flexner's 1910 report argued for a two-phase structure — laboratory-based preclinical science plus hospital-based clinical training, modeled on the Johns Hopkins synthesis of German laboratory science and French bedside medicine. Specialty residencies and their associated certification boards emerged decades later, primarily in the 1930s–1940s, driven by the rise of medical specialization as a social and economic phenomenon. The USMLE Step 1 — the actual hard gate between preclinical and clinical training — was instituted in 1992, not by Flexner.

The 3-Act structure shares the foundations-before-advanced-work logic with the modern medical model but does not share the externally validated gate structure. Medical education's gates are statutory and high-stakes: USMLE Step 1 must be passed before clinical training; specialty boards must be passed before independent practice. The 3-Act framework's gates are currently internal to the program. This is a substantive difference, not a cosmetic one.

Engineering education

Discussed in §5. The closest empirical analog to the 3-Act framework with the most directly relevant research literature.

Graphic design programs (not the same field as graphic communication)

Comparisons to programs at RISD, SCAD, and other graphic design schools require a clarification: graphic design (RISD, SCAD's Graphic Design BFA) is a different disciplinary field from graphic communication (Cal Poly SLO's program) and from communication design (some programs at NC State, Texas Tech). The fields share visual-communication concerns but diverge on technical-production emphasis, industry orientation, and disciplinary genealogy. Treating them as interchangeable instances of "the same structure" risks suppressing the disciplinary differences that shape each program's actual organization.

That said, the broad pattern observed across these programs is real: a foundational year (often a common school-wide foundation, as at RISD and SCAD), a developmental middle period focused on principles and methods, and a senior year that mixes specialization with capstone work. The 3-Act structure matches this broad pattern. The framework should be honest that the comparisons here are illustrative rather than confirming.

Cal Poly SLO Graphic Communication

The closest peer in field and emphasis. The program does not name phases, but its prerequisite flowchart enforces a sequence: students must complete the GRC core before entering concentration-specific advanced courses. The five named concentrations are 29-unit specialty tracks structurally analogous to the 3-Act structure's specialty phase. The structural similarity is real and meaningful for benchmarking; the divergence is that Cal Poly's concentrations are formal curriculum artifacts with their own prerequisite chains, while the 3-Act structure's "specialty" is currently an organizing principle rather than a credentialed track.

Business education and teacher licensure

AACSB-accredited undergraduate business programs commonly organize as: business core → declared major coursework → integrative senior experience. AACSB accreditation does not mandate a three-phase structure; programs converge on it because accreditation requires capstone-type integrative experiences.

Teacher licensure programs structurally follow foundations coursework (learning theory, child development) → methods courses (subject-specific pedagogy) → student teaching (supervised authentic practice). Student teaching is gated by performance checkpoints in earlier phases — a formal implementation of the "brittle scaffold" diagnostic the framework approximates without (yet) operationalizing.

Part III — Critiques the framework needs to engage

§ 9The phantom prerequisite problem

Research on prerequisite-course transfer — a 2016 mixed-methods study by Spring, Heckler & Watkins published in CBE — Life Sciences Education, "What's in a Prerequisite? A Mixed-Methods Approach to Identifying the Impact of a Prerequisite Course" — found that for biology students, concepts categorized as "Familiar" (briefly covered in prerequisites) were statistically indistinguishable from "Not Familiar" concepts on downstream performance. Superficial prerequisite coverage did not transfer to later coursework. Students passed Act-1-equivalent courses without internalizing the foundational competencies those courses were supposed to establish, then arrived in Act-2-equivalent courses with the credential but not the capability.

The 3-Act framework's "brittle scaffold" diagnostic is directly aimed at this problem — but only if act-boundary assessment evaluates internalization, not merely course completion. The framework currently treats course completion plus the K/U/D depth values as the readiness signal. Strengthening this to include explicit readiness criteria at act boundaries (portfolio reviews, structured demonstrations, instructor sign-off) is one of the framework's load-bearing TODOs. Without it, the diagnostic detects scaffolding gaps after they have already produced poor downstream learning rather than preventing them.

§ 10The situated-learning critique (Lave & Wenger)

Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger's Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation (1991, Cambridge University Press) argues that learning is fundamentally a process of becoming a peripheral participant in a community of practice. The architectural implication is sharp: decontextualizing foundational instruction from authentic practice — learning rules and principles divorced from the situations in which expert practitioners apply them — produces inert knowledge that cannot transfer to professional settings. The critique is not that individual courses lack practical content. It is that a three-act sequence that separates "learn foundations" from "integrate" from "apply" is exactly the architecture that produces the inert-knowledge problem.

This is the strongest theoretical critique of the framework, and it deserves a substantive response rather than the deflection of "Act 1 includes courses with practical content."

The framework's actual response draws on the cognitive apprenticeship literature (Collins, Brown & Newman, 1989, "Cognitive Apprenticeship: Teaching the Crafts of Reading, Writing, and Mathematics"; subsequent work by Collins, Brown, and Holum, 1991). Cognitive apprenticeship is a deliberate attempt to bring features of authentic apprenticeship — modeling expert performance, coaching, scaffolding, articulation, reflection, exploration — into formal educational contexts. The 3-Act structure can operate as a sequenced cognitive apprenticeship: Act 1 emphasizes modeling and coaching with high scaffolding, Act 2 introduces articulation and reflection as students take on more authentic problems, Act 3 emphasizes exploration and authentic-practice participation.

This response succeeds only if Act 1 instruction is genuinely apprenticeship-shaped — with explicit modeling, structured coaching, and articulation tasks — rather than primarily declarative instruction with practical examples. The framework's productive-failure audit area is one tool for evaluating whether Act 1 courses operate this way in practice. Where they do, the cognitive-apprenticeship response holds. Where they don't, Lave & Wenger's critique stands and the diagnostic outputs need to be interpreted with that in mind.

The framework also makes an explicit fidelity trade-off: it accepts that staged school-based instruction is an approximation of community-of-practice learning, and that some loss of situated authenticity is the price of an analytical structure that can support program-level evaluation. This trade-off is named openly rather than rhetorically denied.

§ 11The artificial-boundary problem

Donald Schön's The Reflective Practitioner (1983, Basic Books) argues that professional competence develops through continuous reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action, not through staged phases. The "between acts" framing risks imposing discontinuities on what is actually a continuous developmental process.

The framework's response is administrative: the acts are diagnostic constructs for program-level analysis, not descriptive claims about how individual student learning unfolds. A student's developmental trajectory is continuous; the framework's analytical waypoints carve that continuum into named phases so program-level patterns can be surfaced. The acts are useful where they make patterns visible; they are misleading if treated as natural breakpoints in student development.

§ 12Flexibility and transfer students

Staged models assume linear progression and create problems for transfer students, students with prior professional experience, and students who retake foundational courses. The 3-Act structure currently has no defined protocol for credit for prior learning at act boundaries.

The honest acknowledgment: this is a structural hole, not an incremental enhancement. The framework's "brittle scaffold" diagnostic, applied to a transfer student who entered with significant self-taught competency, would consistently flag Act 1 gaps that may or may not reflect actual readiness. A prior-learning assessment mechanism at each act boundary is on the framework's TODO list.

Part IV — Foundations & Agency

§ 13The agency claim, done honestly

Act 1 is named "Foundations & Agency," pairing technical foundations with the disposition of agency. The framework treats agency as a developmental outcome — something Act 1 is meant to begin cultivating in students.

The theoretical grounding for treating agency as a developmental outcome (rather than as a feature of a motivational environment) is in the self-regulated learning literature, particularly Barry Zimmerman's work. Zimmerman's social-cognitive model of self-regulated learning (1989, 2002) identifies forethought, performance/volitional control, and self-reflection as cyclical phases of self-regulation that develop through guided practice. The Boekaerts / Zimmerman / Pintrich tradition treats self-regulation as learnable through specific interventions — explicit modeling of strategies, opportunities for goal-setting and self-monitoring, feedback on regulatory choices rather than only on task performance. Marcia Baxter Magolda's work on self-authorship (most notably Authoring Your Life, 2009) provides a developmental-stage account of how undergraduates move from external-formula-following toward internal authorship of their own commitments. Both bodies of work treat agency-like dispositions as learnable, sequentially developable, and amenable to instructional design.

Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985, 2017) is sometimes cited in connection with agency development. SDT is most precisely about the motivational consequences of autonomy-supportive environments — it predicts that when learners experience autonomy, competence, and relatedness, intrinsic motivation grows. SDT is not, strictly speaking, a theory of how agency develops as a learnable competency; it is a theory of what motivational environments enable. The framework's Act 1 design should draw on SDT for how to structure the learning environment (autonomy-supportive structures, meaningful choice, genuine competence development) and on Zimmerman / Baxter Magolda for what agency development looks like as a developmental trajectory. Conflating the two has been a source of looseness in earlier framings.

The Dreyfus model of skill acquisition (Dreyfus & Dreyfus, 1986, Mind over Machine) provides a complementary developmental framing. The Dreyfus stages (novice → advanced beginner → competent → proficient → expert) place explicit constraints on agency at the novice stage: novices need rules to follow precisely because they lack the situational pattern recognition that makes meaningful judgment possible. The Act 1 framing is most defensible when agency is treated as a seed rather than a demonstrated capability — Act 1 introduces students to the concept of professional self-direction, builds the structured-choice and reflection habits that support self-regulation, and creates the conditions for agency to grow alongside competence rather than precede it. Agency as a fully realized Act 1 outcome would contradict the Dreyfus developmental account; agency as a cultivated Act 1 disposition that fully expresses itself by Act 3 is consistent with it.

Part V — Accounting and open questions

§ 14What's research-supported vs. what's stipulated

A clean accounting of which 3-Act design choices are research-supported and which are framework commitments.

Research-supported (the framework can defend these from established literature)

  • The general logic of foundations-before-advanced-work in professional education is supported by the cornerstone-capstone literature, the medical-education and teacher-licensure structures, and Bruner's spiral.
  • The diagnostic logic of prerequisite-network analysis (the "brittle scaffold" concept under a different name) is established in CS education research.
  • The need for explicit readiness criteria at phase boundaries — rather than mere course completion — is supported by the prerequisite-transfer literature (Spring et al., 2016).
  • The cognitive-apprenticeship framing (modeling → coaching → scaffolding → articulation → reflection → exploration) is well-supported as a way to operate staged instruction in a community-of-practice-compatible way.
  • Agency development as a learnable, sequentially developable disposition is supported by the self-regulated learning literature (Zimmerman, Pintrich, Baxter Magolda).

Framework commitments (defensible working choices, not derivations)

  • Three acts specifically, rather than two (cornerstone-capstone), four (law school 1L / upper-division / clinic / bar), or five (Dreyfus stages, medical school years).
  • The specific names Foundations & Agency, Integration & Mastery, Specialty & Application — and the pairing of technical content with a dispositional construct in Act 1.
  • The specific course-to-act mapping in the Vision document.
  • The "mid-Junior year" timing for Act 2 completion. This is anchored on the empirical student experience of GC 4400 and GC 4060 as the integrative "aha" courses, with the structural intent of leaving 1.5+ years of Act 3 for career-path-specific development. The timing is pragmatically grounded in observed student outcomes; it is not derived from external literature on optimal integrative-milestone timing (which the literature doesn't actually specify). The framework treats this as a working hypothesis the snapshot corpus and graduate-outcome data will eventually validate or revise.

Structural holes the framework acknowledges

  • Explicit readiness criteria at act boundaries are not yet operationalized (Phase 1B Scaffolding Analysis is the planned location for this).
  • A prior-learning assessment protocol for transfer students and students with significant external preparation does not exist.
  • The 3-Act structure is proposed as an organizing principle; it would not be enforced by registrar gates the way medical education's USMLE Step 1 or engineering's FE exam enforces phase transitions, even if adopted.

§ 15Open empirical questions

Three questions the framework's data collection over time will eventually be able to address.

  1. Does the 3-Act structure produce better graduate outcomes than alternatives? This requires longitudinal tracking of graduate readiness against career-target competencies, and a comparison group from a program organized differently. The framework can generate the program-side data; the comparison-group data is harder. Advisory-board feedback and alumni outcomes can serve as proxies in the medium term.
  2. Is mid-Junior year the right timing for Act 2 completion? This is a program-specific empirical question. Snapshots over time will reveal which competencies are reached at which course-sequence positions; if the mid-Junior milestone is consistently met or consistently missed, the timing claim is testable.
  3. Are act boundaries the right diagnostic waypoints? The brittle-scaffold diagnostic uses the 3-Act boundaries. An alternative analysis might use Bloom-level transitions, Dreyfus-stage transitions, or course-prerequisite-graph cut-points. The framework's data shape is compatible with running these alternative diagnostics in parallel; doing so as the snapshot corpus accumulates would test whether the act-boundary framing is doing analytical work the alternatives don't.
