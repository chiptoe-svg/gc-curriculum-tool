Architecture Overview

How the GC Curriculum Tool Works

A system-level view: the framework, the two sides of capture (what our courses develop and what careers demand), the agentic pipeline that produces the records, and every infrastructure piece that runs it — plus an honest account of how the framework matches the research it draws on.

Audience Technical reviewers, faculty curious about the AI, future maintainers Date 2026-06-05

§ 1Overview

The GC Curriculum Tool is a curriculum-analysis platform for Clemson's Graphic Communications department. It maintains a structured, evidence-backed record of what each captured course is evidenced to develop — scored across three learning dimensions at graduated depths — and aggregates those records into program-wide views that expose gaps, scaffold patterns, and prerequisite sufficiency at a granularity that committee intuition and periodic review reliably miss.

The system has four layers that work together: a framework (KUD+ with evidence discipline) that defines what counts as coverage and at what depth; two sides of capture that point the same framework at two different things — what our courses develop (attainment) and what careers require (demand); a set of surfaces (per-course capture and analysis tools, plus program-level views) that faculty and administrators interact with; and an infrastructure layer — a single Mac running the web server, database, vector store, document extractor, LLM providers, and a self-maintaining wiki. This document describes all four and maps how they connect.

For the stakeholder-facing picture of what the tool does and why it matters, see the executive brief. For the end-state vision and governance context, see the vision document. For the day-to-day faculty workflow, see the faculty guide. This document is the "system as a system" read — architecture, not aspiration.

§ 2The Driving Questions

Every design decision in the tool traces back to two questions, which the department has historically answered through committee discussion, gut intuition, and occasional formal review:

Q1 How well does the GC curriculum build students toward the careers we claim to prepare them for?
Q2 For any individual course, do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what the course expects?

Q1 operates at program scale: it requires knowing what each course actually develops (not what its syllabus claims) and what the careers actually demand, then comparing the two. Q2 operates at course scale: it requires comparing a course's incoming-expectations profile against the confirmed output of its declared prerequisites. Both questions are unanswerable without structured, evidence-grounded records of what courses do — which is what the capture pipeline produces.

A distinction that runs through the whole architecture: Q1 has two halves. Breadth — "is this competency developed anywhere in the program?" — is computable today. Sufficiency — "is it developed deeply enough for what the career requires?" — depends on measuring career demand on the same depth scale and subtracting. The tool captures both sides of that subtraction now; the final differencing is the next major increment (§4).

The tool does not claim to answer these questions definitively. It produces AI-drafted, evidence-cited readings that faculty can review, dispute, and refine. The commitment is that those readings are disputable — backed by specific evidence, not syllabus aspiration — and that faculty disagreements are persistent rather than silently overwritten.

§ 3The KUD+ Framework

Every coverage judgment in the tool factors into three categories. The K/U/D planning vocabulary is drawn from Carol Ann Tomlinson's differentiated-instruction work (where "Know / Understand / Do" is a pre-unit planning aid) and is structurally compatible with Stage 1 of Wiggins and McTighe's Understanding by Design. The graduated depth extension and the cross-program mapping application are the framework's own contribution — not something either source prescribes (more on that honesty point below):

  • Know (K) — recall and identification of content: terms, facts, conventions, procedural sequences. Probe: "What is X?" / "Which of these is X?"
  • Understand (U) — reasoning about the why: principles, relationships, consequences. Probe: "Why does X work?" / "What follows from X?" / "When would you use X vs. Y?"
  • Do (D) — behavioral output: artifacts, performances, demonstrated skills. Probe: "Make X. Produce X. Demonstrate X."

Each dimension is scored on a 0–5 depth scale anchored to what the student does or grasps, not what the syllabus claims:

  • 0 — Not present (or listed only in aspirational syllabus language)
  • 1 — Exposure / restates the explanation as given / performs with per-step direction
  • 2 — Recognize / explains in own words / performs using a reference
  • 3 — Recall / predicts consequences / performs independently in familiar conditions
  • 4 — Uses correct terminology across the domain / reasons through novel cases / adapts to new conditions
  • 5 — Fluent across edge cases / critiques and extends / performs creatively with judgment, can guide others

These anchors matter because binary coverage ("covered/not covered") collapses in practice — most courses touch most relevant competencies at some level, and the matrix loses its ability to discriminate between a brief mention and an assessed performance. The depth scales restore that resolution. The authoritative rubric lives at lib/ai/prompts/shared/depth-scale.md and is injected into every scoring prompt; the schema (lib/ai/capture/schema.ts) enforces each depth as an integer 0–5.

Evidence discipline

Any score above the lowest exposure level requires evidence of student engagement, not just syllabus intent. Specifically: K=1 requires delivery evidence (a lecture, module item, or reading list entry); K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of assessed student engagement, reasoning, or performance. This is the evidence-above-zero rule, and it is the load-bearing constraint that distinguishes a coverage map from a marketing document. It is not merely prompt guidance: the synthesis schema enforces it with three .refine() guards — a profile that scores above the threshold without a real evidence excerpt is rejected and retried. The model cannot emit a passing profile that inflates a score past its evidence.

Foundational competencies

Five program-wide competencies — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, and Communication — are scored on D only. K and U are stored as null (not zero) for these rows, again schema-enforced. These are dispositions demonstrated through behavior; students don't recall facts about Resilience or reason their way through it — they exhibit it or they don't. Scoring K and U would imply the course tried and failed to develop the cognitive recall or reasoning aspect of a disposition, which is a category error. A foundational competency with D=0 is a valid and informative finding: the course doesn't create the conditions that develop the disposition.

Dissociation cases

The three-dimensional depth model surfaces patterns that binary coverage hides. K-high with U-low is jargon without rationale — students can name the thing but can't reason about why it works. U-high with D-low is theory without craft — students understand the principle but can't execute. D-high with U-low is craft without articulation — students can perform the procedure but can't explain why it works. The scoring prompt turns these into an active guard: a D≥3 with U≤1 must either downgrade or carry a rationale explicitly naming the dissociation, or downstream consumers treat it as undefended inflation. The depth model makes visible what binary coverage erases.

Why not Bloom's Taxonomy?

Bloom's revised six-level taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) is the right tool for analyzing the cognitive demand of a specific assessment task. It is not well-suited to cross-program coverage mapping, for two reasons. First, its six levels produce a compound judgment across hundreds of course-by-competency cells that makes faculty review harder rather than easier — the distinction between Apply and Analyze is a calibration burden at scale. Second, Bloom is assessment-design vocabulary; it doesn't natively represent the behavioral output dimension (Do) as separable from the cognitive dimension, which the GC curriculum needs because craft skills in a professional field (pressing, color management, layout, prepress) involve behavioral performance distinct from cognitive understanding of the same content. So Bloom is used inside the audit conversation (the agent asks which Bloom verb best matches a high-stakes assignment) but no Bloom level is stored on the profile — KUD with depth is the stored mapping vocabulary.

How the implementation matches the literature

A fair question for any framework is whether the running software actually embodies the research it cites, or just borrows the names. For the GC tool, the research-grounded elements are operationalized at the strongest level — schema constraints and deterministic scoring code, not prompt suggestions:

ElementWhat the research saysHow the software embodies it
Evidence-above-zero UbD Stage 2 ("determine acceptable evidence"); Messick / Wiliam on construct-irrelevant variance and underrepresentation. Three Zod .refine() guards reject any K>1 / U>0 / D>0 without an evidence excerpt. A schema-level rejection, not advice.
K/U/D dissociations Independent measurement of three dimensions surfaces jargon-without-rationale, theory-without-craft, craft-without-articulation that binary coverage collapses. A "downgrade-or-name" consistency check in the scoring prompt makes the diagnostic an active guard; dimensional_patterns surfaces them to faculty.
Foundational = D-only, K/U null Dispositions (NACE career-readiness, AAC&U, CASEL, executive-function research) are exhibited, not recalled. A true type distinction in the DB: foundationals with non-null K/U are schema-rejected. null ≠ zero.
Problem-solving as emergent Willingham / McPeck (domain-boundedness); Kapur's Productive Failure (PS-I); Schön / Kolb and Tannenbaum & Cerasoli (reflection ≈ the struggle→transfer converter). scaffolding.ts computes depth-weight × reflection-multiplier × condition score; "unproductive success" (high depth, no struggle) is flagged; not-assessed is excluded (null), never scored 0.
Prerequisite gaps Sato et al. (2017) "phantom prerequisite" — course completion ≠ concept transfer; depths are ordinal levels, not quantities. prereq-gaps.ts uses ordinal-MAX aggregation (no summing, no double-count) and a measured/intended/none basis stamp; uncaptured prereqs return no-data, never a false zero.
What is stipulated, not derived — read this before over-claiming

The framework is scrupulous in its own background documents about where it invents rather than inherits, and the architecture preserves that honesty:

The 0–5 depth grain is a framework invention — a "working-hypothesis instrument." The cited traditions (Tomlinson, Wiggins & McTighe, AAC&U, EU Tuning) are consistent with a compact-and-leveled approach; none prescribes this scale.

KUD+ scores are course-opportunity scores, not validated student-attainment claims. They record what a course is designed to and assesses, with evidence — not a measured outcome for any cohort. They become learning claims only through the still-planned Graduate Outcome Validation against 268 GC graduates.

The 3-Act program structure is explicitly a proposal, not institutional policy, and — importantly — the three named acts are not in the scoring engine. The scaffolding analysis implements generic introduction / practice / integration depth-phase sequencing by program order (the research-grounded part); the named acts live only as wiki/prose waypoints.

The five foundational competencies are a "defensible working set," not "the right set" — revisable as the corpus grows. And the artifact_verified evidence band is defined but currently unreachable in code (no student-artifact citation type exists yet), so credibility bands top out at "materials-supported" today.

The full academic case — theoretical grounding, relationship to Constructive Alignment, the evidence for the depth-scale extension — is in the Background document. The problem-solving position is detailed in the problem-solving deep-dive; the 3-Act accounting in the 3-Act deep-dive.

§ 4Two Sides of One Ruler — Attainment & Demand

The single most clarifying idea in the whole tool: it is one ruler measuring two different things. We score every competency on one K/U/D depth scale (0–5). CourseCapture points that ruler at our courses and asks "how deeply can we prove students can actually do this?" Position Capture points the very same ruler at a job and asks "how deeply does a new hire need to do this on day one?" Because both sides speak the same units — and, since the Position Capture pre-build amendments, both are keyed to the same catalog sub_competency_id — the program's headline question becomes simple subtraction: demand minus attainment.

The attainment side — CourseCapture (supply)

CourseCapture measures what students are evidenced to attain, course by course (the full pipeline is §6). An AI audit agent works over the syllabus, Canvas materials, and an instructor interview; a synthesis pass emits one structured Course Outcome Profile that scores each competency on independent K/U/D depth against the shared rubric. Its defining trait is evidence discipline — the schema-enforced rule that no depth rises above its evidence (§3). Each confirmed profile becomes an immutable, versioned snapshot keyed to catalog sub-competency IDs. Those keyed attainment depths land in snapshot_target_coverage, the heat map behind /program.

The demand side — Position Capture (formerly "CareerCapture")

Position Capture measures what a career requires of an entry-level hire, using the same depth scale. It runs a six-page partner flow (JD ingest → what makes the role unique → interview questions → trajectory → AI-rated "experiences worth having" → an agent interview), then a synthesis pass emits one immutable Position Profile in which each qualifying competency is scored required_for_success in K/U/D. Three disciplines keep the demand honest, mirroring the supply side:

  • A shared join key. Each qualifying competency and each rated experience carries a nullable structured sub_competency_id — the same FK the attainment side uses — populated best-effort by synthesis from the catalog list already in its context.
  • Day-one anchoring. required_for_success is anchored to what a new hire does on day one at entry level, not eventual mastery. Career trajectory (year 1, years 2–3) is captured separately and deliberately kept out of the scored demand, so a typical entry role lands near D3 rather than being inflated toward expert depth.
  • Demand-side evidence discipline. Just as a syllabus verb can't lift an attainment score, a vague endorsement can't lift a demand score: any above-floor depth must carry an evidenced_by note (what the partner actually said) plus a confidence level, or it's scored at the floor. "We want good communicators" is K1, not D3.

Many positions for one career roll up into a per-target aggregate (v1 is a deterministic side-by-side stub; AI aggregation is deferred).

The sufficiency seam — where the two sides meet

Because both sides emit K/U/D on the same rubric and both now carry sub_competency_id, a career's required depth for a competency and the program's evidenced depth for that same competency become unit-for-unit comparable. The planned unified demand/coverage layer (the "audit step 4" increment) is where they are actually differenced: it adds numeric per-dimension k/u/d_demand to the catalog, populated from the Position Capture aggregate, against which the existing keyed course-attainment depths are subtracted. The result, per competency, is a signed gap — where a career needs a 4 and graduates evidence a 2, that gap of 2 is precisely where the curriculum falls short.

Built vs. not-yet-built

Built and deployed: both capture engines. The full CourseCapture v2 pipeline and the /program coverage map on the attainment side; Position Capture v1 (the full six-page flow, immutable position records, the roll-up stub) on the demand side, with the join key, day-one anchoring, and demand-evidence discipline all enforced in the shipped schema.

Not yet built: the numeric k/u/d_demand columns and the actual demand-minus-attainment difference. Today demand is captured-and-keyed but never compared. The consequence: Q1 breadth ("is the competency touched anywhere?") is computable now; Q1 sufficiency ("is it touched deeply enough for the career?") is blocked precisely on wiring this seam — which now has a defined join key to build against. Position Capture v1's only obligation toward the seam was to populate that key, so the demand data never ships orphaned.

§ 5Surfaces

The tool presents two categories of surfaces: per-course editing surfaces, which are faculty-facing and gated by authentication, and read-only or public surfaces, accessible to anyone on the Clemson LAN. The infrastructure section (§9) explains why these run on different network origins.

CourseCapture
HTTPS · Faculty (Basic Auth) · /capture/<code>

Per-course audit conversation that produces an evidence-backed Course Outcome Profile. Reads catalog values, Canvas materials, Google Drive content, uploaded files. Tool-using AI agent retrieves from a per-course vector index during the chat. Faculty confirm the profile; the system writes an immutable, versioned snapshot.

Explore
HTTPS · Faculty (Basic Auth) · /explore/<code>

Alignment analysis against a confirmed snapshot. Two modes: custom target (instructor writes goals; AI structures them into a KUD+ target spec; analysis surfaces gaps and ranked recommendations) and downstream target (auto-detects prereq expectations from downstream courses' captured profiles). What-if scenarios simulate proposed changes. Ask tab: streaming chat over the curriculum wiki anchored to the focused course.

Program Coverage Matrix
HTTPS · Faculty (Basic Auth) · /program

All confirmed snapshots crossed with all career-target sub-competencies, rendered as a depth-aware heat map. On-demand AI scoring with prompt-cache batching. Click any cell to see the contributing snapshot competency, evidence excerpt, confidence, and full rationale. The program-wide answer to Q1 breadth.

Scaffolding Strip
HTTPS · Faculty (Basic Auth) · /program/scaffolding

Phase 1B view. Per-target heat map of depth, productive-failure conditions, and structured-reflection signals derived deterministically from confirmed snapshots. Surfaces brittle scaffolds — courses that expect mastery of competencies no earlier course actually introduces. Supplemental AI narrative layer is Phase 1B Stage 2 (specified, not yet built).

Course Catalog
HTTPS · Faculty (Basic Auth) · /courses

Editorial index of all catalog courses grouped by level, with computed capture-status ladders (not-started / in-audit / ai-drafted / reviewed / captured), last-captured date, data-state badges, a bulk "preload all" + add-a-class flow, and deep-link Ask buttons. Each row links to /courses/[code] — the prerequisite confirm/edit and gap view where faculty seed skill-tagged prerequisite edges and read computePrereqGaps output.

Wiki + Ask
HTTPS · Faculty (Basic Auth) · /wiki, /ask

Curriculum wiki index (markdown corpus from the gc-curriculum-wiki repo) plus standalone and course-anchored curriculum chat. The agent navigates the wiki via read/list/search tools and cites verbatim excerpts per turn. The same chat surface mounts in Explore (?tab=ask) and on the /wiki index page.

Public Landing + View
HTTP · Public (LAN) · /, /view/<code>

"Transparent curriculum" surfaces. Anyone on the Clemson LAN can browse the course list and read any captured profile without authentication. Edit links forward to the HTTPS faculty surfaces. Intentionally public — advisors, chairs, accreditors, and industry reviewers can read without faculty credentials.

Partner Survey — Position Capture
HTTPS · Magic-link · /partners/<token>

The live demand-side surface, served by the same Mac via Tailscale Funnel (was on Vercel until 2026-06-04). Partners receive a magic-link invitation, match themselves to a career target, and walk through the Position Capture v1 six-page flow (JD ingest via Docling + LLM extraction → uniqueness → interview questions → trajectory → AI-rated experiences → agent interview). Each completed position produces an immutable record; positions roll up per target. /admin/synthesis aggregates captured input into proposed KUD+ edits for faculty to adopt or reject. (An earlier non-functional prototype called "CareerCapture v1" was retired; its tables were renamed in schema 0029.)

Settings and Admin

Two additional faculty-only surfaces: /settings for per-function AI model tier configuration (light / default / heavy, with per-function model override) and a daily cost panel; /admin/partners and /admin/synthesis for partner management and AI synthesis review. These don't participate in the core data flow but are part of the operational toolkit.

§ 6The Agentic-Retrieval Pipeline

The pipeline that produces a Course Outcome Profile runs in three phases. This architecture — called CourseCapture v2 — replaced the earlier approach of dumping every course material into a single large context window. The core insight: when every material is visible to the AI at once, findings can emerge from pattern-matching across the entire context rather than from specific evidence, making provenance unverifiable. The v2 architecture ensures that every finding the audit agent surfaces is grounded in a specific, citable retrieval result.

Phase A — Material Ingestion

When a faculty member loads materials (Canvas import, file upload, Google Drive fetch, YouTube transcript), each material goes through an ingestion pipeline before the audit chat begins:

  1. Extract

    Raw content is converted to clean text. PDFs go through Docling, which produces markdown with figure descriptions via an optional local vision-language model. Legacy Office formats are converted via LibreOffice headless. Spreadsheets are compacted to strip empty rows, degenerate tables, and inline base64 image blobs that inflate token counts 20–30× over their information content.

  2. FERPA screening + materials policy

    An automated policy layer flags materials likely to be gradebook or roster data (by filename pattern) as high FERPA risk and auto-sets them aside with a dismissible warning. Other materials proceed. The policy is always overridable by the faculty reviewer.

  3. Chunk + contextualize + embed

    Each material is chunked into retrieval-sized segments. Before embedding, each chunk receives a short position blurb ("this chunk is from the GC 4800 Color Management assignment, page 2 of 4") that is prepended to the chunk text — so the vector encodes both position and content (the Anthropic contextual-retrieval pattern). Embedded vectors are upserted into a per-course Weaviate tenant via a 2560-dimension campus embedding model.

  4. Digest

    In parallel, each material receives a structured digest — a compressed but faithful summary optimized for the audit agent's at-rest view. Canvas Syllabus pages, assignments, and quiz content are kept verbatim (the agent needs exact assignment language and point values); long reference materials are digested. The digest substitutes for the full text in the audit context, with a per-row toggle for verbatim fallback.

After ingestion, an ingestion check-in agent makes one silent review pass over the assembled materials. It flags meaningful curation concerns — missing syllabus, stacked auto-set-asides, near-empty digests — but is silent the majority of the time.

Phase B — Audit Chat as Tool-Using Agent

The audit conversation is conducted by a tool-using AI agent, not a simple chat completion. On each turn, the agent:

  1. Loads the at-rest context

    System prompt + catalog values (description, prerequisites, learning objectives, major projects, required incoming skills) + per-material digests + a deterministic, verbatim session briefing (readiness, recent findings, the last faculty turn from prior sessions). This is the bounded context the agent operates from without retrieval.

  2. Decides whether to retrieve

    The agent determines whether the current finding or question requires specific evidence beyond what the digest layer provides. If so, it uses one or two tool calls (at most) before responding.

  3. Retrieves if needed

    Three retrieval tools: list_materials (see what's in the corpus), fetch_material_section (retrieve a specific section by chunk ID), and search_materials (hybrid vector + BM25 search across the per-course Weaviate tenant). Tool results carry chunk IDs that become citable sources.

  4. Responds in structured format

    Each turn returns a structured finding (the most consequential evidence-gap or conflict), a single focused question, citations (chunk IDs or instructor-turn message IDs), and a readiness signal (0–100, areas covered/remaining, a "good enough to generate" flag that flips at ~75).

  5. Persists the turn

    Every message — instructor and agent — is appended to a session-scoped, append-only message log in Postgres, uniquely constrained by session ID and turn index, so there is no overwriting. The transcript permanently links to any snapshot produced from that session.

The audit conversation systematically covers seven areas: prerequisite sufficiency, objectives vs. evidence, cross-source conflicts, cognitive-level probe, threshold concept, foundational dispositions, and inaccessible content references. The agent weaves these in order of what the materials surface. Turns are streamed (NDJSON), so the faculty reviewer sees the response appearing in real time rather than waiting 3–7 seconds.

Voice input

The microphone button in the audit chat sends audio to an on-device Whisper transcription pipeline (§9). Voice is particularly useful for narrative explanations — describing what a capstone actually requires, or why a rubric criterion means what it does. Transcription replaces the in-progress text; the reviewer edits before sending. Mic works because the faculty surfaces run as top-level HTTPS contexts on the Tailscale Funnel, where the browser grants microphone access natively.

Phase C — Synthesis

When the faculty member is satisfied, they click "Capture this profile." This triggers a separate synthesis call — distinct from the chat — that reads the full audit transcript plus the materials context and produces a structured Course Outcome Profile in a single pass: competencies (5–15 technical plus the 5 baseline foundational, each with K/U/D depth, evidence excerpts, rationale), incoming expectations, a verification summary, and audit notes. Every finding carries a source flag and citations (§7).

Optional: Stress-Test

After synthesis, faculty can run an adversarial stress-test reviewer — a separate on-demand agent whose only job is to find problems. It checks citation-to-claim ratios, evidence excerpts against the claims they support, internal consistency between rationale and scores, source-flag honesty, and objective-misalignment claims against the transcript. It returns per-competency confidence ratings and profile-level concerns. Results are ephemeral — never baked into the draft or snapshots, cleared whenever any score is edited. A thinking surface, not an authority.

Snapshot

When satisfied, the reviewer confirms and saves a snapshot: an immutable, versioned record freezing the profile JSON, the timestamp, the AI model that produced it, the instructor name, and a link to the transcript session. Snapshots are the artifact downstream views consume. Drafts remain mutable; only confirmed snapshots feed program-level analysis.

§ 7Provenance

Every finding in a Course Outcome Profile carries a source flag and citations. This is not optional decoration — it is a structural commitment the pipeline enforces at validation time.

Source flags

Three values:

  • materials — the finding is supported by citations that all trace to specific chunks in the vector store.
  • instructor — the citations all trace to instructor turns in the audit transcript (things the faculty member said that aren't in the materials).
  • inferred — no direct citations, or indirect ones. The weakest signal level.

The source flag is derived mechanically from citation types, not asserted by the synthesis agent. All chunk citations → materials; all transcript-turn citations → instructor; mixed or empty → inferred. This makes the flag verifiable rather than stylistic. A read-time evidence ladder layers on top, surfacing a credibility band (claimed / materials-supported / artifact-verified) so a high-but-instructor-claimed score is flagged, never gated.

Citations resolve to real sources

Every citation is either a chunk ID (resolving to a specific text segment in the course's Weaviate tenant) or a message ID (resolving to a specific instructor turn). The synthesis prompt and Zod schema both reject excerpt-only citations — a citation that exists only as a quoted string without a resolvable ID fails validation, causing the synthesis to be rejected and retried. This closes a class of hallucinated provenance where the model paraphrases "what it remembered" instead of pointing at a verifiable source. In the review UI, every source badge is a button: clicking it opens a citation drawer showing the actual chunk text or instructor turn, so faculty can verify directly that the evidence says what the finding claims.

Pre-v2 profiles

Snapshots confirmed before the v2 pipeline don't carry source flags or resolved citations — they were produced by the earlier "dump everything into context" approach. They render with a legacy banner and gracefully degrade the citation drawer to "excerpt only." They remain valid historical records and continue to feed program views; they just lack the provenance infrastructure v2 produces.

§ 8Instructor and Time

Per-instructor capture

The same course code can be taught very differently by different faculty. One instructor's GC 3800 may develop Color Management at D=4 through a semester-long production project; another's may develop it at D=2 through lab exercises and a final exam. Both are valid findings — depth scores are a function of what students under that specific instructor actually do, not a platonic average. Capturing one and displaying it as the course's profile erases variance that may matter for advising and review.

The system supports per-instructor captures on the same code. Each session begins with an instructor identification step (a dropdown from a faculty roster); the name is stored with every session message and resulting snapshot. The public course listing shows "by [instructor]" attribution; the read-only profile view notes how many other captures exist when more than one is on file.

Snapshots are immutable

Once confirmed, a snapshot cannot be edited. It is a timestamped record of what the course was evidenced to develop at that moment, by that instructor, under that session's materials. The immutability is intentional: curricula erode over decades, and the only way to make that erosion visible is to preserve the dated records that accumulate. Faculty can re-capture as many times as they like; each new snapshot adds to the historical record. The Program Coverage Matrix automatically uses the newest non-retired snapshot per course.

Soft-retirement is available: a snapshot can be hidden from downstream views (superseded, or reflecting a version of the course that no longer exists). Retired snapshots remain in the database as history and can be viewed directly but don't feed program-level analysis. Hard deletion is not exposed.

Instructor variance as signal

When multiple instructors have captured the same code, the system surfaces the variance. The matrix uses the newest snapshot per course (which may be by a different instructor than an older one); future phase work will expose cross-snapshot diffs and instructor-variance analysis explicitly. For now, per-snapshot attribution and the "N other captures on file" hint are the variance-visibility mechanism.

§ 9Infrastructure — The Pieces

The tool runs as a single Next.js application on one Mac Studio on the Clemson LAN. The earlier two-personality hybrid — local Mac for faculty surfaces plus Vercel for the partner survey, sharing a Neon Postgres, with Resend for email — retired on 2026-06-04. The Mac now serves everything; Postgres runs locally; partners reach it over a Tailscale Funnel. One codebase, one DB, one host. This section walks every piece.

The web server + the two-origin auth split

The application is Next.js 15 (App Router, Turbopack), bound to 0.0.0.0:3000 via pnpm dev:lan. The same process serves two effective origins:

  • The LAN HTTP origin (http://130.127.162.180:3000) — the public landing and read-only /view/* profiles. HTTP, no auth, browseable by anyone on the Clemson network.
  • The Tailscale Funnel HTTPS origin (https://admins-mac-studio-2.tailb723c1.ts.net) — the full app, reachable from the public internet, gated by HTTP Basic Auth in middleware for every path outside PUBLIC_PREFIXES (which permit /, /view/*, /partners/*, /api/partners/*).

Tailscale Funnel is an HTTPS reverse proxy that exposes the Mac's port 3000 to the public internet at a stable .ts.net hostname with a valid, CA-signed certificate — no local cert management. That valid certificate is load-bearing: browsers enforce that microphone access requires a secure context (HTTPS). An earlier architecture tried to deliver mic via an HTTPS iframe in an HTTP parent; per the W3C Secure Contexts spec that is not a secure context, and navigator.mediaDevices is undefined. Moving the faculty surfaces to top-level HTTPS via the Funnel makes the microphone a first-class browser feature. Faculty bookmark the HTTP landing, browse with no login, and click "Edit" to cross to the Funnel origin, where the browser prompts for Basic Auth once per session.

Middleware runs in the Node runtime (runtime: 'nodejs', not Edge) because it uses node-postgres for the per-request partner-session DB lookup. Partner surfaces are gated by URL-bearer magic tokens validated against partners.magic_token rather than Basic Auth, so partners need no credentials.

Database

Postgres 17 via Postgres.app, on 127.0.0.1:5433 (not the default 5432, to avoid colliding with any stock install), database gc_curriculum, launchd-managed (com.gc.postgres). It holds all application state — courses, capture snapshots and messages, career targets and sub-competencies, partner records and sessions, position captures, prerequisite edges, intended coverage, coverage scores, AI settings, and the rate-limit/cost tables. The app connects via a node-postgres Pool (lib/db/client.ts, swapped from the Neon serverless driver in the cutover); schema is managed with Drizzle migrations. The Neon database was dumped and restored row-for-row; its legacy URL is preserved in .env.local as NEON_LEGACY_URL pending a manual dashboard decommission (dead, not an active connection).

AI providers + the provider abstraction

All model calls go through a provider abstraction at lib/ai/provider.ts — getProvider() returns one of OpenAI / Anthropic / Local / Campus / Fake, selected by the AI_PROVIDER env var. Switching providers is a one-line change with no application-layer edits:

  • OpenAI (current production, AI_PROVIDER=openai). The function-tier system maps light → gpt-5.4-mini, default → gpt-5.4, heavy → gpt-5.5. Uses Vercel AI SDK v6 for tool-using + streaming completions and OpenAI strict JSON-schema mode for structured output (strict mode requires every property in required — a sweep of all schemas enforces this).
  • Anthropic (AI_PROVIDER=anthropic, claude-sonnet-4-6). Supports native PDF document blocks; a switchable alternative.
  • Campus (AI_PROVIDER=campus) — Clemson RCD's OpenAI-compatible cluster at llm.rcd.clemson.edu/v1. Default model qwen3.6-35b-a3b-fp8 (MoE, ~3B active params, ~180 tok/s); heavier glm-5.1-fp8 and deepseek-v4-pro reachable per-call. Free; cost reported as 0. Its provider implements a manual two-phase tool-call loop because the Qwen model doesn't reliably use the SDK's combined structured-output-plus-tools path.
  • Local (AI_PROVIDER=local) — omlx, a local OpenAI-compatible MLX inference server on localhost:8000/v1 running the Qwen3.6 family on the M4 Max GPU. Zero marginal cost, no data leaves the Mac. omlx lazy-loads models on first request and keeps them resident. (It is an external dependency, not managed by the project's launchd.)

Embeddings are decoupled from the generation provider. lib/ai/embeddings.ts always calls the campus endpoint's /embeddings with qwen3-embedding-4b (2560-dim), regardless of which AI_PROVIDER is active. (A known follow-up: the tier system stores OpenAI model names, so using campus models through the tiers needs a provider-aware tier refactor — deferred.)

Vector store

Weaviate v1.37.5 (Homebrew, launchd com.weaviate, anonymous access) on 127.0.0.1:8090 HTTP + :50051 gRPC — port 8090 because 8080 is held by Caddy on this machine. It stores per-course text-chunk vectors with per-course tenant isolation (each course is a tenant named coursecapture-<slug>), so the audit agent's retrieval is scoped to one course with no cross-course contamination, and supports hybrid BM25 + vector search. The VectorStore interface (lib/capture/vector-store.ts) selects the Weaviate backend when VECTOR_STORE=weaviate, else an in-memory backend for dev/test; the v2 ingestion path runs only when COURSECAPTURE_V2_INGESTION=1.

Document extraction

Docling (IBM's document parser), run as the docling-serve HTTP microservice on 127.0.0.1:5001 (launchd com.gc.docling-serve), selected by PDF_PARSER=docling. It converts PDF / DOCX / PPTX / XLSX / CSV / HTML / images to structured Markdown with real layout + table detection + OCR — vastly better than the in-process unpdf fallback, and it handles multi-format files unpdf cannot. It runs in CPU mode on Apple Silicon (an upstream MPS float64 bug blocks the GPU path; CPU adds a few seconds per page but is reliable). An optional vision-language pass describes figures in slides and papers (DOCLING_VLM_*, pointed at omlx) — a real evidence source text-only extraction misses. Docling serves both the CourseCapture material pipeline and the Position Capture JD-ingest path.

Transcription

Audio transcription (faculty mic in the audit chat, and YouTube-video fallback) uses a three-tier ladder, first success wins: (1) omlx persistent MLX server — opportunistic, gated by a 100ms idle pre-flight, fast because the model stays resident; (2) the mlx_whisper CLI with whisper-large-v3-turbo on Apple Metal — free, on-device, ~1/60 realtime; (3) the OpenAI Whisper-1 API — last resort, only when the local binary is missing. This replaced the older whisper.cpp + ggml-medium.en path on 2026-06-03 for a ~2× speedup. For YouTube videos without captions, yt-dlp + ffmpeg downsample to 16 kHz mono before the same Whisper backends run; such transcripts are tagged (Whisper) in the filename.

Material storage

Uploaded course-material bytes live on the local filesystem under ~/Library/Application Support/gc-curriculum-tool/materials/, served back through /api/storage/materials/<key>. This replaced Vercel Blob in the 2026-06-04 cutover; a simple local path is sufficient for a single-Mac deploy.

The curriculum wiki (a sibling repo)

A separate Git repository, gc-curriculum-wiki (cloned alongside the app), is an LLM-maintained markdown knowledge base in the Karpathy pattern. It has two layers: a raw/ layer (immutable snapshot JSON + transcripts) and a regeneratable narrative layer (courses/, competencies/, targets/, concepts/ pages). It gives the curriculum-chat agent a navigable, citable knowledge base without dumping the whole database into context, and being git-backed makes every edit auditable and reversible.

This is a shipped v1, not a plan: updateWikiForSnapshot (lib/ai/wiki/update.ts) generates prose with the heavy-tier wiki-update function, and writeAndPush (lib/wiki/git-ops.ts) handles the git pull/write/commit/push with one retry on conflict. It fires fire-and-forget on snapshot creation, writing course + concept pages and index.md. Competency and target pages derive from coverage scores, so they regenerate after the coverage refresh routes re-fire wiki generation (fixed 2026-06-05). The planned v2 compile-loop — a watermark/diff trigger, a ≥2-source concept-promotion gate, a deterministic lint pass, and a /refresh-wiki reconcile that is idempotent and resilient to interrupted background jobs — is the next increment, not a from-scratch build.

Backups

A two-tier scheme via com.gc.pg-backup (every 6h): (1) local pg_dump/gzip to ~/Library/Application Support/gc-curriculum-tool/backups/, auto-pruned past 365 days, using Postgres.app's matched-version client; (2) off-site — a weekly Sunday push of the latest dump to the private repo chiptoe-svg/gc-curriculum-backups, retained indefinitely. The earlier Neon-PITR and Neon-branch-checkpoint tiers retired with the source DB (the dead checkpoint code was removed entirely). Restore procedure lives in the backups repo's README.

A note on ports

Local service ports are tracked in ~/.dev-ports.yaml so nothing collides: app 3000, Postgres 5433, Docling 5001, Weaviate 8090/50051, omlx 8000. The registry is the source of truth before binding anything new.

§ 10Cost and Discipline

Function-tier model selection

Every AI operation is assigned to one of twenty-one named function IDs (the count as of the latest migration), each with a default tier — light / default / heavy — that maps to a model. The mapping is provider-aware and overridable per-function from /settings.

  • Light tier — per-material digest, per-chunk contextualization, ingestion check-in, JD field extraction (jd-extract). These run frequently, so cost efficiency matters more than reasoning depth.
  • Default tier — the audit chat agent, synthesis, Explore alignment/what-if, curriculum chat, prerequisite-edge seeding, intended-skills extraction, and the Position Capture functions (position-rated-items, position-interview-agent, position-synthesis). The high-value calls where reasoning quality affects the output faculty review.
  • Heavy tier — program coverage scoring (the most context-intensive call; batches by career target for prompt-cache reuse), the adversarial stress-test reviewer, and the wiki-update narrative generator. Heavy-tier calls are rare, batched, or high-stakes enough to justify the cost.

The newer functions beyond the original core (which the earlier version of this doc undercounted) include capture-chat-agent, capture-stress-test, wiki-update, curriculum-chat, jd-extract, the three position-* functions, prereq-edge-seed, and intended-skills-extract.

Daily cost cap

A daily cost guard prevents runaway spending. Every AI call records its cost against a daily_cost table; when the day's total reaches the configured cap (DAILY_COST_CAP_USD), subsequent calls return a 429 with an informative error. The cap is set in .env.local and shown on /settings with a 14-day sparkline. The feedback-automation cron also checks the cap before each dispatch and halts at limit.

"AI is your assistant, not your authority"

The cost model reflects the epistemic model: AI calls are tools for producing better-informed drafts that faculty review, not oracles that produce authoritative verdicts. Every depth score is editable. Every recommendation is a starting point. Every stress-test concern is advisory. The AI's job is to reduce the cognitive load of processing dozens of assignments and rubrics, surface patterns committee-level review tends to miss, and produce a disputable reading specific enough to argue against. Faculty deliberation is where the analysis becomes institutional record.

§ 11Connections

The Course Outcome Profile is not a terminal artifact — it feeds a network of connected views. Here is the explicit map:

Snapshots → Program Coverage Matrix

Every confirmed snapshot contributes to the matrix — the cross-product of all confirmed snapshots against all career-target sub-competencies. For each cell, an AI scoring call reads the snapshot's competency list and determines whether, and at what depth, its evidence supports the sub-competency. The score is the K/U/D depth at which the snapshot is evidenced to develop the sub-competency — not the snapshot's own internal competency score — because the snapshot organizes around what the course teaches and the matrix organizes around what career destinations require.

Snapshots → Scaffolding Analysis

The Scaffolding Strip reads the same snapshots through a different lens: not "does the course develop this" but "at what depth, and with what evidence of productive struggle and structured reflection." Its deterministic engine classifies each cell as well-scaffolded, top-heavy, bottom-heavy, coverage-only, brittle-scaffold, or not-addressed. A brittle scaffold — a downstream course expecting mastery of a competency no upstream course introduces — is the diagnostic finding that drives the most redesign work.

Snapshots → Explore Downstream Mode

Explore's downstream mode auto-constructs a prerequisite-sufficiency target by reading the incoming_expectations of every captured downstream course (those listing the focal course as a prerequisite). The union of their demands is what the focal course must collectively produce. As more courses are captured, the auto-detected target becomes richer and this becomes the primary prereq-sufficiency diagnostic.

Snapshots → Prerequisite Gap Analysis (shipped as skill-tagged prerequisite edges)

The Phase 1C Prerequisite Gap Analysis shipped (2026-06-05) as persisted, skill-tagged prerequisite edges. Rather than reconstructing gaps on-demand from free text, the system maintains a prerequisite_edges table: each row is a direct FK edge from a focal course to a prerequisite course, tagged to a specific sub-competency with expected K/U/D and a basis stamp (measured | intended | none). Edges are LLM-seeded from free-text prerequisites + incoming expectations and faculty-confirmed. computePrereqGaps differences the focal need against confirmed prerequisite attainment via ordinal-MAX aggregation (depths are levels, not quantities). Uncaptured prerequisites yield no_data, never an assumed gap. A companion intended-skills-extract function (migration 0031, the course_intended_coverage table) seeds syllabus-asserted K/U/D from catalog fields, banded claimed, to give a non-empty cold-start gap view before real captures exist — and these intended values never merge into measured attainment. Per-course gap view: /courses/[code]; program-wide graph viz is deferred.

Position Capture → Q1 Sufficiency (the demand seam)

The demand side (§4) connects here. Position Capture records employer-required KUD+ depth keyed to sub_competency_id; positions roll up into a per-target aggregate. The planned unified demand/coverage layer will difference that demand against the matrix's evidenced attainment — turning Q1 from "is the competency covered?" (breadth, computable now) into "is it covered deeply enough?" (sufficiency). This is the one major seam not yet wired; it is unblocked now that both sides share the join key.

Snapshots → Curriculum Wiki

The gc-curriculum-wiki sibling repo (§9) regenerates narrative markdown from confirmed snapshots. wiki-update v1 fires fire-and-forget on snapshot creation, writing course + concept pages and the index; competency and target pages regenerate after coverage scoring. The curriculum-chat agent navigates this wiki via read/list/search tools and cites verbatim excerpts. The v2 compile-loop (watermark/diff trigger, concept-promotion gate, lint pass, reconcile) is the next increment.

Industry Partner Input → Career-Target Descriptors

Beyond the per-position demand capture, the partner survey also feeds an AI synthesis pipeline (/admin/synthesis) that aggregates partner submissions into proposed updates to career-target sub-competency descriptors. These proposals are advisory — a faculty reviewer adopts, modifies, or rejects each before it takes effect. When a descriptor changes, existing coverage scores against that target are marked stale and queued for re-scoring; the program's self-understanding updates rather than silently inheriting a new descriptor with old scores.

The accumulation pattern

Most of the system's value is cumulative. A single snapshot is useful for the course it describes. Ten make the coverage matrix informative. Twenty make Explore's downstream mode load-bearing. A full cohort makes the scaffolding analysis diagnostic. And the demand side compounds the same way — more captured positions sharpen the sufficiency picture. The trial period is about confirming the per-snapshot analysis is credible before investing in the program-scale infrastructure that depends on accumulation.
