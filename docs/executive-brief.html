What the tool does

The GC Curriculum Tool answers two questions that the department has historically answered through committee discussion, faculty intuition, and periodic curriculum review:

Q1 How well does the curriculum build students toward the careers we claim to prepare them for?
Q2 For any individual course, do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what the course expects?

It answers them by maintaining a structured, evidence-backed record of each captured course — what learning opportunities the course is evidenced to create, at what depth, against which career destinations — and surfacing the program-wide picture that emerges as captures accumulate. Faculty deliberation, advising decisions, accreditation conversations, and curriculum redesigns all benefit from having that picture in the room.

Why this matters

Faculty know parts of the answer for the courses they teach. Nobody has the whole picture, and nobody has a way to keep it current as courses evolve. The tool exists because three kinds of conversations are hard to have without one:

  • Curriculum redesigns often re-create courses without the program-wide view of what each course was actually delivering — so the redesigned curriculum inherits the gaps the old one had.
  • Accreditation and advisory-board reviews require defensible claims about what graduates can do; "graduates of this program are ready for X" should be a verifiable statement backed by evidence of what each course was evidenced to develop.
  • Advising works better when "if you want career X, take this sequence and watch for these gaps" can be backed by evidence about what each course actually develops.

Three commitments make these conversations possible: every coverage judgment is evidence-backed (the scoring rules are detailed in the Framework section below), disputable (every AI reading can be flagged with a faculty note that persists across re-scores), and versioned (every snapshot is dated and immutable, so the curriculum's evolution is a record, not a memory).

The framework

Every coverage judgment factors into three categories — Know (recall and use of terminology), Understand (reasoning about why), Do (behavioral output, what a student can actually execute). Each is scored independently on a 0–5 depth scale anchored to what the student does or grasps, not what the syllabus claims. Binary coverage ("covered / not covered") collapses in practice — most courses touch most relevant competencies at some level, and the matrix loses its ability to discriminate between a brief mention and an assessed performance. The depth scales restore that resolution.

This is the KUD+ framework, drawn from Carol Ann Tomlinson's work on differentiated instruction and compatible with Wiggins & McTighe's Understanding by Design. Three rules anchor the rubric across the program:

  • Evidence-typed scoring. Every nonzero score requires evidence, but the evidence type varies by level: K=1 requires delivery evidence; K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of assessed student engagement, reasoning, or performance. Aspirational syllabus verbs do not by themselves justify depth above those thresholds.
  • Foundational competencies — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication — are scored on D only. K and U are stored as null. These are dispositions demonstrated through behavior, not recalled or reasoned-about.
  • Dissociation cases are signal. K-high with U-low is jargon without rationale; U-high with D-low is theory without craft; D-high with U-low is craft without articulation. The depth scales surface these patterns; binary coverage hides them.

The framework also takes a specific position on problem-solving — an attribute employers consistently rank among the most important when reviewing graduates' resumes. The framework treats problem-solving as domain-embedded rather than reliably transferable as a free-floating skill. In technical domains (the GC core: press, ink, plates, prepress, color management, finishing), it looks for domain depth plus the productive-failure and structured-reflection conditions evidenced by Kapur's PS-I research. In design-judgment work (typography, layout, brand identity), it uses the broader language of productive struggle, critique, revision, and reflective practice in the Schön tradition. Whether these patterns cumulate into graduate-level problem-solving gains across the four-year curriculum is part of the validation agenda, not a settled finding. The tool's per-course audit surfaces these conditions; the program-level scaffolding analysis (Stage 1 live) aggregates them.

The full academic case for KUD+ and its theoretical antecedents is in the Background document. The full research synthesis on problem-solving is in Problem-Solving as a Program-Level Emergent Property.

How it functions

Five connected surfaces form the per-course-to-program-wide pipeline; the Vision document covers each in full.

CourseCapture · /capture/<code>
An instructor-facing audit conversation: the instructor confirms the evidence corpus (syllabus, Canvas materials, uploads), then works through a focused AI-guided interview that produces a provenance-tagged, immutable Course Outcome Profile snapshot.
Explore · /explore/<code>
A prescriptive-analysis module that reads a confirmed snapshot and surfaces ranked recommendations against either a custom instructor-defined target or the inferred expectations of downstream courses.
Program Coverage Matrix · /program
Every confirmed snapshot crossed with every career-target sub-competency, rendered as a depth-aware heat map scoped to the courses that intentionally build toward the career.
Position Capture & Industry Input · /partners
The employer-demand half of Q1: industry partners turn real job postings into structured Position Profiles; a magic-link survey feeds an AI synthesis layer that aggregates input into proposed career-target edits faculty curate before adopting.
Curriculum knowledge base · /wiki · /ask
Confirmed snapshots compiled into cross-linked pages browsable at /wiki and answerable in plain language at /ask — the program's self-maintaining institutional memory.

The surfaces form one continuous chain: a course audit and a program-wide career-readiness claim draw on the same KUD+ evidence-scored records, zoomed in or out. Q1 is the join of the program coverage matrix and the captured employer demand — a real computation over real data, scoped to the courses that intentionally build toward each career. (The sufficiency computation is built and awaiting activation.) Faculty walkthrough: Using CourseCapture & Explore.

Trust and governance

The tool drafts; faculty decide. Three commitments make the partnership work:

  • Every AI reading is disputable. Every depth score is editable before a snapshot is confirmed, and a ⚑ Flag button on each program-matrix cell and each profile competency files a named dispute note. Flags persist across re-scores — if a flagged score changes, the change is shown alongside the flag, never applied silently — and stay open until someone resolves them with a recorded name, note, and date. An on-demand adversarial stress-test gives every draft profile a second, skeptical AI reading before faculty confirm it.
  • Snapshots are owned by the faculty member who confirmed them. Visible to the program coordinator and the curriculum committee; retired by request (soft-deleted from views, retained as historical record). Hard deletion is not exposed.
  • The curriculum committee retains all authority over curriculum changes. The tool informs deliberation. It does not replace it. A proposed change still goes through the committee; what the tool adds is the ability to bring evidence-grounded readings into the conversation and to model the proposed change's coverage impact before the committee votes.

AI cost stays bounded and provider-swappable — see the Vision document for operational detail.

Where it stands today

The faculty trial period is underway. CourseCapture (now the v2 agentic-retrieval pipeline — the v1 single-context version was retired), Explore, the Program Coverage Matrix (Phase 1A), and the Prerequisite Gap Analysis (Phase 1C — shipped as skill-tagged prerequisite edges with a per-course gap view at /courses/[code]) are live. Scaffolding analysis (Phase 1B Stage 1) is also live; Stage 2's AI narrative layer is next. The curriculum knowledge base (the self-maintaining wiki at /wiki, the plain-language assistant at /ask, and an MCP endpoint for internal agents) regenerates from confirmed snapshots; its trust-hardening pass shipped — a deterministic structural linter, loud-not-silent reconciliation, and evidence-credibility markers carried into the narrative. Industry Partner Input is live end-to-end: the magic-link survey, the AI synthesis layer, and Position Capture v1 (full 6-page flow). The advising view (Phase 1D) is specified and queued; the demand→coverage sufficiency seam is built and awaiting activation.

The honest list of what's not yet in place is also worth naming: per-user authentication for faculty is currently a stopgap; the snapshot dispute pipeline is informal; the program-wide cross-snapshot diff that operationalizes "honest defense against curricular drift" is Phase 2 work. The framework expects to refine these as the trial period reveals what matters.

The detailed, continually-updated status of every surface, every plan, and every blocker lives in the project's STATE document.
