What the tool does

Curriculum review usually runs on memory and paperwork: catalog language, aspirational syllabi, and what faculty can recall about courses taught years ago. The GC Curriculum Tool changes the burden of proof — a course earns credit for developing a competency not because the syllabus names it, but when assignments, rubrics, or student work show students actually engage it. Built on that evidentiary shift, it answers two questions the department has historically settled through committee discussion, faculty intuition, and periodic review:

Q1 How well does the curriculum build students toward the careers we claim to prepare them for?
Q2 For any individual course, do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what the course expects?

It answers them by maintaining a structured, evidence-backed record of each captured course — what learning opportunities the course is evidenced to create, at what depth, against which career destinations — and surfacing the program-wide picture that emerges as captures accumulate. Faculty deliberation, advising decisions, accreditation conversations, and curriculum redesigns all benefit from having that picture in the room.

Why this matters

Faculty know parts of the answer for the courses they teach. Nobody has the whole picture, and nobody has a way to keep it current as courses evolve. The tool exists because three kinds of conversations are hard to have without one:

  • Curriculum redesigns often re-create courses without the program-wide view of what each course was actually delivering — so the redesigned curriculum inherits the gaps the old one had.
  • Accreditation and advisory-board reviews require defensible claims about what graduates can do; "graduates of this program are ready for X" should be a verifiable statement backed by evidence of what each course was evidenced to develop.
  • Advising works better when "if you want career X, take this sequence and watch for these gaps" can be backed by evidence about what each course actually develops.

Three commitments make these conversations possible: every coverage judgment is evidence-backed (the scoring rules are detailed in the Framework section below), disputable (every AI reading can be flagged with a faculty note that persists across re-scores), and versioned (every snapshot is dated and immutable, so the curriculum's evolution is a record, not a memory).

The framework

Every coverage judgment factors into three categories, each scored on its own 0–5 depth scale: Know (recall and use of terminology), Understand (reasoning about why), and Do (behavioral output — what a student can actually execute).

This is the KUD+ framework, drawn from Carol Ann Tomlinson's work on differentiated instruction and compatible with Wiggins & McTighe's Understanding by Design. It is deliberately not Bloom's taxonomy, and the reason is fit to purpose. Bloom's six cognitive levels (Remember, Analyze, Evaluate, Create…) are built to classify a single assessment — not to map a whole program against the careers it feeds — and they simply don't line up with how jobs are described. Careers are defined by the knowledge a role requires and the work it performs — which is exactly Know and Do, with Understand the reasoning that carries a graduate into unfamiliar cases. That shared vocabulary is what makes KUD a universal translator between curriculum and career: program coverage and job requirements sit on the same three axes, so comparing them is one scale on both sides rather than a lossy translation — and unlike Bloom, KUD carries the performance (Do) dimension where career readiness and habitual problem-solving are actually evidenced. A few rules anchor the rubric across the program:

  • Depth, not just coverage. Most courses touch most relevant competencies at some level, so “covered / not covered” can't tell a brief mention from an assessed performance — the 0–5 scale can. “Covers color management” earns nothing on its own; a Do score requires the graded artifact in which a student corrects color under novel conditions.
  • Evidence-typed scoring. Every nonzero score requires evidence, but the evidence type varies by level: K=1 requires delivery evidence; K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of assessed student engagement, reasoning, or performance. Aspirational syllabus verbs do not by themselves justify depth above those thresholds.
  • Foundational competencies — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication — are scored on D only. K and U are stored as null. These are dispositions demonstrated through behavior, not recalled or reasoned-about.
  • Dissociation cases are signal. K-high with U-low is jargon without rationale; U-high with D-low is theory without craft; D-high with U-low is craft without articulation. The depth scales surface these patterns; binary coverage hides them.

The framework also takes a deliberate position on problem-solving — the attribute employers rank among the most important. It is not ignored or reduced to a checkbox: the framework treats it as a program-level emergent property that shows up in the pattern across courses — domain depth, productive failure, and structured reflection, accumulated over the sequence — rather than as a single course trait, which is why it is not added as a sixth foundational competency. Whether those conditions cumulate into graduate-level gains across the four years is part of the validation agenda, not a settled finding.

The full academic case for KUD+ and its theoretical antecedents is in the Background document. The full research synthesis on problem-solving is in Problem-Solving as a Program-Level Emergent Property.

How it functions

Five connected surfaces take a course from a single capture to the program-wide picture; the Vision document covers each in full.

CourseCapture
An instructor-facing audit conversation: the instructor confirms the evidence corpus (syllabus, Canvas materials, uploads), then works through a focused AI-guided interview that produces a provenance-tagged, immutable Course Outcome Profile snapshot.
Explore
A prescriptive-analysis module that reads a confirmed snapshot and surfaces ranked recommendations against either a custom instructor-defined target or the inferred expectations of downstream courses.
Program Coverage Matrix
Every confirmed snapshot crossed with every career-target sub-competency, rendered as a depth-aware heat map scoped to the courses that intentionally build toward the career.
Position Capture & Industry Input
The employer-demand half of Q1: industry partners turn real job postings into structured Position Profiles; a magic-link survey feeds an AI synthesis layer that aggregates input into proposed career-target edits faculty curate before adopting.
Curriculum knowledge base
Confirmed snapshots compiled into a cross-linked knowledge base — browsable as pages and answerable in plain language — the program's self-maintaining institutional memory.

These surfaces are one connected chain, not separate tools, because they all use the same KUD+ scale: the same Know / Understand / Do depths describe what a course develops, what the next course expects, and — once employer demand is captured — what a career requires. The same evidence-backed reading of a course can therefore be looked at up close as a course audit or stepped back to fill in the program-wide picture, without being re-translated along the way. The course captures, the coverage matrix, and the prerequisite and scaffolding views are live today; the final step — comparing program coverage against captured employer demand to answer Q1 — is built and awaiting activation and the first employer captures. Trust and governance

The tool drafts; faculty decide. Three commitments make the partnership work:

  • Every AI reading is disputable. Every depth score is editable before a snapshot is confirmed, and a ⚑ Flag button on each program-matrix cell and each profile competency files a named dispute note. Flags persist across re-scores — if a flagged score changes, the change is shown alongside the flag, never applied silently — and stay open until someone resolves them with a recorded name, note, and date. An on-demand adversarial stress-test gives every draft profile a second, skeptical AI reading before faculty confirm it.
  • Snapshots are owned by the faculty member who confirmed them. Visible to the program coordinator and the curriculum committee; retired by request (soft-deleted from views, retained as historical record). Hard deletion is not exposed.
  • The curriculum committee retains all authority over curriculum changes. The tool informs deliberation. It does not replace it. A proposed change still goes through the committee; what the tool adds is the ability to bring evidence-grounded readings into the conversation and to model the proposed change's coverage impact before the committee votes.

AI cost stays bounded and provider-swappable — see the Vision document for operational detail.

Where it stands today

The faculty trial is underway, and most of the tool is usable now. Instructors can run a CourseCapture audit and get Explore's recommendations; the Program Coverage Matrix and the per-course prerequisite-gap view work against confirmed captures; and the scaffolding analysis is live in its first form, with an AI-written narrative layer coming next. The curriculum knowledge base — a self-maintaining wiki and a plain-language assistant that answer from confirmed captures — regenerates automatically and has been through a reliability-hardening pass. Industry input works end-to-end: partners turn real job postings into structured demand through a guided flow and a short survey, which an AI layer aggregates into proposed career-target updates for faculty to curate. Two pieces are not yet switched on: the student advising view (specified and queued) and the final career-readiness comparison of program coverage against employer demand (built, awaiting activation and the first employer captures).

The honest list of what's not yet in place is also worth naming: individual faculty logins are still a stopgap; the process for disputing a snapshot is informal; and the program-wide comparison across snapshots over time — the real defense against curricular drift — is planned, not yet built. The framework expects to refine these as the trial reveals what matters.

The graduate-outcome study

One honest limit sits above the rest: the tool can make a claim like “this curriculum prepares students for these careers” visible and disputable, but it cannot confirm on its own that its readings track how graduates actually fare — its auditor is an AI, and the only real check is an outside signal. A Graduate Outcome Criterion-Relevance Study, now underway, supplies one: it compares the competencies the tool emphasizes against where GC graduates from the past ten years actually landed, with failure criteria set in advance so it can disconfirm the framework, not just flatter it. Until it reports, the framework's bigger claims are best read as working hypotheses the tool is built to test, not settled findings. Full design: Graduate Outcome Criterion-Relevance Study.
