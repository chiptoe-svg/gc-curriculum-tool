The GC Curriculum Tool

A versioned, evidence-backed working view of captured GC courses — designed to grow into a program-wide view as faculty-confirmed snapshots accumulate.

Three-minute read · Last updated 2026-05-26

What the tool does

The GC Curriculum Tool answers two questions that the department has historically answered through committee discussion, faculty intuition, and periodic curriculum review:

Q1 How well does the GC curriculum build students toward the careers we claim to prepare them for?
Q2 For any individual course, do the prerequisites students walk in with actually support what the course expects?

It answers them by maintaining a structured, evidence-backed record of each captured course — what learning opportunities the course is evidenced to create, at what depth, against which career destinations — and surfacing the program-wide picture that emerges as captures accumulate. Faculty deliberation, advising decisions, accreditation conversations, and curriculum redesigns all benefit from having that picture in the room.

Why this matters

Faculty know parts of the answer for the courses they teach. Nobody has the whole picture, and nobody has a way to keep it current as courses evolve. The tool exists because three kinds of conversations are hard to have without one:

  • Curriculum redesigns often re-create courses without the program-wide view of what each course was actually delivering — so the redesigned curriculum inherits the gaps the old one had.
  • Accreditation and advisory-board reviews require defensible claims about what graduates can do; "graduates of this program are ready for X" should be a verifiable statement, not a marketing assertion.
  • Advising works better when "if you want career X, take this sequence and watch for these gaps" can be backed by evidence about what each course actually develops.

The tool's commitments make these conversations possible: every coverage judgment is evidence-backed (every nonzero score requires evidence, but the evidence type varies by level — K=1 requires evidence of delivery; K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of assessed student engagement, reasoning, or performance), disputable (every AI reading can be flagged with a faculty note that persists across re-scores), and versioned (every snapshot is dated and immutable, so the curriculum's evolution is a record rather than an oral tradition).

The framework

Every coverage judgment factors into three categories — Know (recall and use of terminology), Understand (reasoning about why), Do (behavioral output, what a student can actually execute). Each is scored independently on a 0–5 depth scale anchored to what the student does or grasps, not what the syllabus claims. Binary coverage ("covered / not covered") collapses in practice — most courses touch most relevant competencies at some level, and the matrix loses its ability to discriminate between a brief mention and an assessed performance. The depth scales restore that resolution.

This is the KUD+ framework, drawn from Carol Ann Tomlinson's work on differentiated instruction and compatible with Wiggins & McTighe's Understanding by Design. Three rules anchor the rubric across the program:

  • Evidence-typed scoring. Every nonzero score requires evidence, but the evidence type varies by level: K=1 requires delivery evidence; K>1, U>0, and D>0 require evidence of assessed student engagement, reasoning, or performance. Aspirational syllabus verbs do not by themselves justify depth above those thresholds.
  • Foundational competencies — Agency, Attention to Detail, Resilience, Curiosity, Communication — are scored on D only. K and U are stored as null. These are dispositions demonstrated through behavior, not recalled or reasoned-about.
  • Dissociation cases are signal. K-high with U-low is jargon without rationale; U-high with D-low is theory without craft; D-high with U-low is craft without articulation. The depth scales surface these patterns; binary coverage hides them.

The framework also takes a specific position on problem-solving — an attribute employers consistently rank among the most important when reviewing graduates' resumes. The framework treats problem-solving as domain-embedded rather than reliably transferable as a free-floating skill. In technical domains (the GC core: press, ink, plates, prepress, color management, finishing), it looks for domain depth plus the productive-failure and structured-reflection conditions evidenced by Kapur's PS-I research. In design-judgment work (typography, layout, brand identity), it uses the broader language of productive struggle, critique, revision, and reflective practice in the Schön tradition. Whether these patterns cumulate into graduate-level problem-solving gains across the four-year curriculum is part of the validation agenda, not a settled finding. The tool's per-course audit surfaces these conditions; the planned program-level scaffolding analysis aggregates them.

The full academic case for KUD+ and its theoretical antecedents is in the Background document. The full research synthesis on problem-solving is in Problem-Solving as a Program-Level Emergent Property.

How it functions

Faculty work happens across three connected surfaces. Each is useful on its own; together they form the per-course-to-program-wide pipeline.

CourseCapture · /capture/<code>
An instructor-facing audit conversation that produces a Course Outcome Profile for one course. The system pulls in catalog data, Canvas materials (assignments, rubrics, pages, quizzes, file attachments), Google Docs/Slides/Sheets, Drive PDFs, YouTube transcripts, and any uploaded files. Voice or text input. One focused question per turn. When the instructor confirms, an immutable, versioned snapshot is written — the historical record. Drafts remain mutable for further iteration.
Explore · /explore/<code>
A read-only-on-the-snapshot module that asks what should change. Two modes: custom target (the instructor types in plain-language goals; AI proposes a structured KUD+ target; analysis surfaces alignment + ranked recommendations); downstream target (auto-detects courses that list this one as a prereq and unions their incoming expectations into a target). Includes what-if scenarios that simulate proposed changes.
Program Coverage Matrix · /program
The end-state view in snapshot form: every confirmed snapshot crossed with every career-target sub-competency, rendered as a depth-aware heat map. Click any cell to see which snapshot competency contributed, the evidence excerpt that justifies the depth, the AI's confidence, and the full rationale. On-demand AI scoring with prompt-cache batching keeps cost bounded.

The flow: faculty capture courses one at a time; explore prescriptive analyses against the resulting snapshots; the program matrix aggregates across all snapshots into the cross-course picture. Industry input feeds the career-target side through a separate magic-link survey for industry partners; an AI synthesis layer aggregates partner submissions into proposed updates to career-target descriptors that faculty curate before adopting.

Trust and governance

The tool drafts; faculty decide. Three commitments make the partnership work:

  • Every AI reading is disputable. A "Flag" button with a note field is attached to every judgment. Flags persist; they are not silently overwritten on the next re-score. Patterns of flagged disagreement update the prompts the system uses, so the tool sharpens over time.
  • Snapshots are owned by the faculty member who confirmed them. Visible to the program coordinator and the curriculum committee; retired by request (soft-deleted from views, retained as historical record). Hard deletion is not exposed.
  • The curriculum committee retains all authority over curriculum changes. The tool informs deliberation. It does not replace it. A proposed change still goes through the committee; what the tool adds is the ability to bring evidence-grounded readings into the conversation and to model the proposed change's coverage impact before the committee votes.

AI-model cost is a manageable variable as adoption grows. The current trial runs against the OpenAI API on credits; if usage scales beyond what credits absorb, the architecture supports a swap to Clemson RCD's campus-hosted models or a local runtime without changes to the application layer.

Where it stands today

The faculty trial period is underway. CourseCapture, Explore, and the Program Coverage Matrix (Phase 1A) are live. The other Phase 1 views — scaffolding analysis (Phase 1B), prerequisite-gap analysis (Phase 1C), and the advising view (Phase 1D) — are specified and queued; implementation order will follow what the trial period surfaces. Industry Partner Input is live in two of three planned increments (the magic-link survey and the AI synthesis layer; the position-ratings table is the gap).

The honest list of what's not yet in place is also worth naming: per-user authentication for faculty is currently a stopgap; the snapshot dispute pipeline is informal; the program-wide cross-snapshot diff that operationalizes "honest defense against curricular drift" is Phase 2 work. The framework expects to refine these as the trial period reveals what matters.

The detailed, continually-updated status of every surface, every plan, and every blocker lives in the project's STATE document.
