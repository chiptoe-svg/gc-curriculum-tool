Overview
What This Tool Is
A permanent, collaborative environment — not a one-time report.

A web-based curriculum design tool that serves as the living record of the GC department's curriculum — what it currently is, what it is becoming, and how well it builds toward five defined career targets. It is simultaneously a working design environment for faculty, an analytical engine that evaluates curriculum coverage, and a presentation-ready artifact for leadership.

Faculty own course pages. An industry and faculty panel co-owns career target pages. The coverage analysis runs continuously — it evaluates the curriculum against the targets whenever either changes. The map is always current. The resource document writes itself from proposal data. Leadership sees the tool, not a report about the tool.

The tool operates under a version-control-inspired model, using faculty-friendly language: every course has an Official Record, proposed changes live as Proposals alongside the original without replacing it, and curriculum-level changes (retiring or adding a course) happen through Curriculum Proposals on the map view.

A fourth dimension governs all design decisions: the program constraint model. The BS in GC has fixed required courses, constrained-choice slots (where one of several approved courses must be chosen to satisfy a requirement), and elective pools (a credit block from an approved list). The tool imports and models these constraints from the official catalog. Faculty working on coverage analysis or proposals are always working within the actual rule structure of the degree — not a hypothetical open curriculum. This constraint layer also powers the tool's student-facing capability: a career path advisor that tells a student, for any declared target, which constrained choices to make and which electives move the needle.

Access
User Roles
Five roles with distinct permissions and editing access.
Admin
Chip (Department Chair)
All pages, all proposals, all reviews, curriculum map, career targets, user management. The only role that can accept or reject Proposals and mark the curriculum map.
Faculty
Course owners
Edit their own course pages, start Proposals for their own courses, view all pages and the full curriculum map. Cannot edit career targets or accept/reject Proposals.
Panel Member
Industry + faculty panel
Edit career target pages, view all course pages and the curriculum map. Cannot create course Proposals or make curriculum-level changes.
Viewer
Leadership, guests
Read-only access to all pages and the curriculum map in Presentation View. No editing capability of any kind.
Student / Prospective Student
Current students, prospective students, public
Read-only access to the Career Path Advisor view and program overview. No account required for the public-facing version. No editing rights anywhere in the system. Initially internal-only; designed for public access in Phase 3.

Account Management
Bright Outlook
41-4012.00
▼
Definition

The consultative client-facing role that bridges a brand's marketing intent and the production or creative execution required to realize it. Account managers are credible in both directions — they can have a substantive conversation with a brand director and then translate that conversation accurately for a production team or creative partner.


Why It's Defensible

Trust, relationship continuity, and organizational navigation are not automatable. Understanding what a client actually needs — as opposed to what they asked for — requires human judgment and accumulated context that AI cannot replicate.


GC Differentiator

Domain knowledge of print, packaging, and brand production. Graduates serve as genuine partners rather than order-takers — credible with brand managers and production floors alike.

Core Competencies
  • Client needs diagnosis and relationship management
  • Proposal development and consultative communication
  • Project oversight across creative and production workflows
  • Results interpretation and client reporting
  • Domain literacy: print, packaging, brand production

Brand Strategy
Bright Outlook
13-1161.00
▼
Definition

The analytical and strategic layer of marketing — understanding consumers, competitors, and market conditions well enough to define where a brand should position itself and how. Brand strategists don't just describe what consumers do; they interpret why and recommend what the brand should do about it.


Why It's Defensible

AI can process consumer data but cannot make judgment calls about brand voice, cultural resonance, or when a data signal is meaningful versus misleading. Brand strategy requires weighing ambiguous information against business context.


GC Differentiator

Most brand strategy programs are digital-native. GC graduates understand how brands translate across print, packaging, and physical touchpoints — a cross-channel perspective most programs don't develop.

Core Competencies
  • Consumer research and insight synthesis
  • Competitive and market analysis
  • Brand positioning and messaging strategy
  • Campaign planning and effectiveness measurement
  • Quantitative literacy: data interpretation, statistical thinking
  • Cross-channel brand translation: print, packaging, digital

Definition

The role that makes creative and brand work actually happen — on time, on spec, and within budget. Production managers design and oversee the workflows, quality systems, vendor relationships, and team coordination that translate a creative brief into a finished physical or digital product.


Why It's Defensible

Production management requires real-time judgment in complex systems with human teams, physical constraints, and unexpected failures. AI can optimize known workflows, but it cannot manage a vendor relationship under pressure or navigate the human dynamics of a production floor.


GC Differentiator

Creative Generalist / AI-Native
Emerging Panel Required
No SOC
▼
Definition

A practitioner with broad creative capability across copy, design, photography, video, and print — who uses AI as a force multiplier that makes generalism viable at a professional level. Not a specialist in any one medium. The value is in aesthetic judgment, conceptual thinking, and the ability to direct AI across disciplines while providing the brand literacy and quality filter that AI cannot supply.


Why It's Defensible

AI executes but cannot direct itself. Generative tools require a human who knows what good looks like, what the brand requires, and when an output serves the brief versus when it doesn't. That judgment — developed through genuine creative practice across multiple disciplines — is what makes this role viable and irreplaceable.


GC Differentiator

This role maps closely to what GC's creative curriculum has always developed — broad exposure across print, design, and production. The new curriculum completes the original promise rather than redirecting it, with AI as the layer that makes the generalist identity professionally competitive.

Core Competencies
  • Conceptual development and creative ideation across disciplines
  • Aesthetic judgment and brand visual literacy
  • AI tool direction: prompt design, iteration, quality evaluation
  • Cross-medium production: copy, design, image, video, print
  • Brand standards interpretation and application
  • Client brief translation into creative direction

05
AI Workflow / Orchestrator
Emerging Panel Required · Priority
No SOC
▼
Definition

The person who designs, builds, and manages the AI-augmented workflows that allow creative and production organizations to scale output without proportionally scaling headcount. This role sits at the intersection of deep domain knowledge — creative, brand, print — and systems thinking about how AI tools can be integrated, sequenced, and quality-controlled across a production workflow.


Why It's Defensible

This role requires both domain expertise and systems fluency — the combination is rare. An AI workflow designer who doesn't understand creative and production work will build workflows that produce technically correct but creatively wrong outputs. Domain knowledge is the differentiator.


GC Differentiator

The orange-zone opportunity. Companies transitioning from traditional creative production to AI-augmented workflows need someone who can design the system — not just use the tools. GC graduates who understand print, brand, and packaging production from the inside are uniquely positioned for these specific contexts.

Core Competencies
  • AI tool evaluation: capabilities, limitations, appropriate use cases
  • Workflow architecture: sequencing human and AI work
  • Prompt design, documentation, and maintenance
  • Quality evaluation frameworks: defining and catching output failures
  • Change management: helping teams adopt new workflows
  • Domain knowledge: creative and production work, print, brand, packaging

SOC Status
⚑ Priority · Panel Required No SOC code exists. Closest proxies (Data Scientists 15-2051, Computer Systems Analysts 15-1211, Management Analysts 13-1111) all have Bright Outlook status but none capture the creative domain specificity. Make this the first panel-developed target — whoever defines it publicly holds a position of leadership.

KUD Framework
Know
How major AI tools work and where they fail; workflow design principles for creative production contexts; how to document workflows for reuse
Understand
Why AI outputs require domain-expert evaluation; why workflow design is continuous improvement, not a one-time build; why change management is the hardest part
Do
Design and document an AI-augmented workflow for a specific creative context; evaluate outputs against a quality standard; train a small team to operate a documented workflow

Assessment Framework
Three Acts, Three Gates
A program outcomes assessment system built to internal accountability standards that simultaneously satisfies external accreditation requirements.

The assessment framework is organized around the curriculum's three-act structure. Gates exist at the transitions between acts — where decisions are made and where assessment data can still change outcomes. Measurement at exit alone confirms failure after the fact; measurement at gates creates the opportunity to act.

Three-Act Structure
Act One
Foundational
1000-level courses · Common to all students

Three Gates
Gate 1
Foundational Gate
End of Act One · End of 1000-level
▼
Stakes
Diagnostic — not punitive
No student is dismissed for failing to clear this gate. The output is an advising record and a directed course of action. A student who doesn't clear it is not sorted out — they receive a clear, evidence-based recommendation before Act Two work compounds on a weak foundation.
Instrument
Common Foundation Knowledge Check — a structured test administered at the end of the final 1000-level course. Items are tied directly to the common foundation sub-competencies defined on the Career Target pages.
What It Measures
  • Know-level competency across the common foundation: print production basics, design fundamentals, digital workflow literacy, industry terminology, brand vocabulary
  • Early signal on which career target areas show natural strength or interest
  • Whether the student has the base required to succeed in Act Two work
What Goes in the Tool
Individual student results live in the student information system. The tool receives aggregate data: what percentage of the cohort cleared each sub-competency threshold. If 60% of a cohort fails to demonstrate Know-level on a specific sub-competency, that is a curriculum problem — the responsible course is flagged for Proposal.
Gate 2
Execution Gate
End of Act Two · Entry to Act Three
▼
Stakes
Consequential — governs track entry
Students who do not clear this gate do not enter their intended specialization track without a documented advising plan. This is the most important assessment event in the system — the point at which specialization is earned rather than assumed.
Instrument
Two components. First, the Common Foundation Test (full version) — same instrument as Gate 1, expanded, enabling a clean before/after growth comparison. Second, a structured Track Declaration: a short evidence-based document in which the student identifies their intended track, references specific work from Act Two, and articulates what Act Three needs to build. Assessed against a rubric — not a personal statement.
What It Measures
  • Know and Understand level across the full common foundation (growth from Gate 1)
  • Understand-level competency in the intended specialization track
  • Readiness for Do-level work in Act Three
  • Evidence-based intentionality of track selection
What Goes in the Tool
Growth from Gate 1 to Gate 2 on the common foundation items is the primary curriculum effectiveness signal for Acts One and Two. Courses responsible for sub-competencies showing weak growth between gates are candidates for Proposals. Track declaration distribution (how many students per track) is a program-level metric.
Gate 3
Exit Gate
End of Act Three · Program Exit
▼
Stakes
Summative — program outcome record
Results are documented, attributed to the student's declared track, and contribute to program-level reporting. This is what goes on the transcript as program outcome evidence and what feeds Tier 3 placement data.
Instrument
Two components. First, a Capstone Performance Assessment — a structured deliverable produced during the senior year, scored against career target performance standards by a rubric. At least one industry reviewer scores alongside the faculty reviewer; inter-rater reliability is documented. Second, a targeted knowledge test — track-specific items plus the common foundation component from Gates 1 and 2, completing the three-point growth curve.
Capstone by Track
  • Account Management — documented client project cycle from brief through delivery with revision evidence
  • Brand Strategy — research-grounded brand positioning recommendation with measurement framework
  • Production & Operations — production workflow design for a complex multi-vendor project
  • Creative Generalist — brand brief executed across three media using AI-assisted workflow with documented creative direction decisions
  • AI Workflow — documented and tested AI-augmented workflow for a specific creative or production context
What It Measures
  • Do-level competency in the declared career target — assessed by performance, not test
  • Know and Understand level on track-specific knowledge — assessed by test
  • Common foundation growth — same items from Gates 1 and 2, enabling a three-point growth curve across the full program
What Goes in the Tool
Aggregate Gate 3 data — percentage of cohort reaching Do level by track, growth curve across all three gates, inter-rater reliability scores — flows into the curriculum tool as Tier 2 metrics. Track-specific Do-level achievement rates feed back into coverage analysis: if students are not reaching Do level in a sub-competency at exit, the courses responsible are flagged for review.
Performance Standards
The specific rubric criteria for each track's capstone are defined as Performance Standards on the Career Target pages in the tool. These are panel-developed, not AI-generated. They must exist and be validated before the capstone can be administered with inter-rater reliability.

What Success Looks Like

Three levels of success, each with a concrete claim the data either supports or refutes.

For a Student
By graduation, they demonstrate Do-level competency in their declared career target — assessed by a rubric-scored capstone with at least one industry reviewer, not a test. Their three-point growth curve shows the program moved them, not just credentialed them.
For the Department
Each year's cohort shows measurable gate-to-gate growth. Curriculum gaps identified by gate data become Proposals within the same academic year. Placement data confirms that declared tracks map to actual careers — and when they don't, advising responds.
For the Field
Industry partners who describe what they look for in each target can see their input reflected in the curriculum. Graduates land in roles that match what the program claimed to prepare them for. The coverage analysis and placement data tell the same story.
Act-Level Checkpoints

At the end of each act, gate data answers one specific question: are students getting what the act promised to deliver? These are not student evaluations — they are curriculum audits.

End of Act 1 · Gate 1 Diagnostic — not punitive
What Act 1 promised: Foundational vocabulary, tool literacy, initial exposure across the discipline's breadth. Every student knows enough to make an informed choice about Act 2.
What Gate 1 confirms: ≥70% of the cohort reaches Know level in each common foundation sub-competency. No dismissal; gaps produce advising records not academic consequences.
Red flag: Any sub-competency below 60% cohort-wide is an immediate curriculum signal — the responsible 1000-level course is reviewed before year-end, not at the next program review.
End of Act 2 · Gate 2 Consequential — governs track entry
What Act 2 promised: Understand level in the common foundation. Beginning of Do-level in selected competencies. Evidence-based readiness for the declared specialization track.
What Gate 2 confirms: ≥15 percentile point growth from Gate 1 (Acts 1+2 together moved the cohort). ≥75% of students declaring each track reach Understand level in at least 3 of 5 track sub-competencies.
Red flag: Gate 1→2 growth below 10 percentile points on any sub-competency means the Act 1–2 sequence failed to develop it. The responsible course sequence is flagged for Proposal — not just for review.
End of Act 3 · Gate 3 Summative — program outcome record
What Act 3 promised: Do-level in the declared career target, assessed by performance and validated by an industry reviewer. The full three-point growth curve documented across all three gates.
What Gate 3 confirms: ≥65% of each track's cohort reaches Do level on the capstone assessment. ≥80% inter-rater agreement between faculty and industry reviewers. Full Gate 1→2→3 growth curve documented for every graduating cohort.
Red flag: Below 50% Do-level in any track means that track's curriculum is failing its primary mission. A Proposal is initiated immediately — this is not an annual-review item.

Assessment Loop Closure

The assessment framework only functions if data changes the curriculum. The loop is closed through the existing Proposals system — assessment-driven Proposals are tagged separately so the annual review can document that measurement produced action.

01Gate data is reviewed by the curriculum committee at the end of each academic year
02Sub-competencies below threshold trigger a formal review of the responsible courses
03Review produces one of three outcomes per course or target: continue as is (documented), flag for Proposal, or initiate a Proposal immediately
04Proposals originating from assessment data are tagged as "assessment-driven" in the rationale field — distinguishable from faculty-initiated redesign
05The following year's gate data evaluates whether the Proposal addressed the gap — closing the loop with documented evidence
This five-step annual cycle, documented in the tool, is the evidence that the assessment system is functioning — not just collecting data. It is exactly what SACSCOC Quality Enhancement Plans and ACEJMC outcomes assessment reports require. Build it right for internal purposes and it satisfies external review automatically.
Analysis Layer
Manning Skills Integration
The open-source pedagogical skill library that powers the tool's AI analysis layer.
Gareth Manning's Education Agent Skills Library is an open-source library of 131 evidence-based pedagogical skills for curriculum design, lesson planning, and assessment. Four domains from the library are integrated into this tool's AI layer: Domain 7 (Curriculum Design & Assessment), Domain 16 (Curriculum Alignment), Domain 13 (AI Literacy), and Domain 9 (Professional Learning). Each skill provides a structured, evidence-grounded prompt framework for a specific curriculum design task. The skills are not called at runtime — they are encoded into the system prompts at build time.

Repository: github.com/GarethManning/education-agent-skills
Implementation Instructions for Claude Code

Manning skills are prompt design patterns encoded at build time — not runtime API calls or MCP server connections. Before building any AI integration point listed below:

  1. Access the Manning library at the repository above
  2. Read the SKILL.md file for each skill referenced in the relevant integration point
  3. Encode the skill's prompt logic into the stored system prompt for that integration point
  4. The skill files define the reasoning framework — do not copy verbatim, encode the structure into the prompt architecture
  5. Where two skills are listed for the same integration point, chain them: the output of the first becomes context for the second

Philosophy
Design Principles
These govern decisions when the spec is ambiguous.
1
Faculty authorship, not faculty reaction
Faculty populate their own course pages. They are not responding to an administrator's analysis of their courses. The tool's political value depends on this distinction — when faculty question the coverage scores, they're questioning AI analysis of their own words, not Chip's judgment about their courses.
2
The map is always live
Coverage scores update when course pages are saved and when career targets are revised. The map never shows stale data. If something looks wrong on the map, the answer is to update the course page or the target — not to wait for a rerun.
3
Proposals sit alongside, never on top of
An Official Record is never modified by a Proposal until the Proposal is explicitly accepted. Faculty can see exactly what would change before anything changes. The original is always preserved — and the history of every change is always available.
4
AI reasons out loud
Every coverage score has visible reasoning accessible on click. Faculty can dispute any score and add a note. The tool does not present AI output as authoritative fact — it presents it as a starting position that faculty can confirm, refine, or challenge.
5
The resource document writes itself
The Resource Summary is assembled from proposal data — not authored separately. If the proposals are accurate, the resource request is accurate. Nobody writes a separate document to summarize what the tool already knows.
6
Leadership sees work, not reports
The Presentation View shows the actual curriculum tool in a clean skin. Leadership is seeing what faculty built, not a slide deck summarizing what someone else found. The tool is the evidence — not a source for evidence.
