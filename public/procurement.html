Clemson Procurement / how it works

A procurement assistant grounded in the actual rules.

Procurement questions turn on exact dollar thresholds, required methods, and approvals. This assistant looks those up in a compiled rulebook, shows its reasoning, and cites the policy it used, rather than generating an answer from similar-looking text.

Clemson + SC state + federal rules For buyers, business officers, procurement staff, and the help desk Web · voice · other software
01

Where a general chatbot falls short on procurement

The everyday tool

The common pattern: attach a PDF, ask a question, get a paragraph back.

For many tasks that works well. For procurement it is risky. These tools work by similarity — they retrieve passages that look related to the question, then a language model writes a fluent answer. In the process, the model can state a dollar threshold that is slightly off, blend a state rule with a federal one, or miss an exception.

Here, "almost right" is not a typo. It can mean a split purchase that should have been bid, a missing quote, or a grant expense that is later disallowed. Because the point of asking is to stay compliant, a plausible but wrong answer is worse than no answer.

What makes it hard
"Three written quotes are required — unless it's on a state contract; but a federal grant imposes its own limit; see also the IT carve-out." — the shape of nearly every real procurement rule

The rules live in long PDFs and sprawling websites, in tables of thresholds, across three overlapping rulebooks — Clemson policy, South Carolina state law, and federal Uniform Guidance for grants — that cross-reference and sometimes contradict each other. Reading is not the hard part. Applying the right rule, and proving you did, is.

02 The core idea

It resolves the governing rule with code and shows the sources, rather than describing the rules from memory.

04

What an answer looks like

"Can I buy a $300 table on my NSF grant?"

A regular chatbot returns a paragraph. ask_procurement returns a resolved card: what it understood, the rule it applied, the chain when more than one rule interacts, and a citation on every line.

The dollar figures and methods are looked up — never generated. And because a federal grant is involved, it knows to check the stricter of the state and federal limits, not just one.

Each underlined citation opens the exact policy passage it came from. If the category is wrong, correcting the chip re-resolves the answer.

05

The same answer, shaped for four roles

The same verified rules and the same proof underneath — but the answer is shaped for who's asking. Nothing is reworded into a different meaning; only the depth and the language change.

B

Buyer

"Just don't get me in trouble."

The bottom line, the next two steps, and the guardrails — like a warning not to split an order to dodge a threshold.

O

Business officer

"Did this follow the rules?"

The full obligation, the documents that must exist, and the reimbursement angle — framed for oversight.

P

Procurement pro

"Give me the rule and the cite."

Dense and exact, the full decision chain always shown, every threshold sourced to its section.

H

Help desk & vendors

"Walk me through it."

Step-by-step guidance — with a vendor-facing mode that uses outside language and hides internal-only procedures.

06

Why it's built this way

a. It shows the source and checks the figures

Each answer shows and links the policy text it's based on, so the original is one click away rather than a paraphrase. Dollar thresholds come from a list that procurement staff review — the model doesn't generate the numbers as it answers.

b. It includes its reasoning

Each answer lists the rules it applied and a citation for each, so the same answer can be used to act on and to check the work later.

c. It refuses rather than guesses

When no reviewed rule clearly applies, it says so and points to the relevant source instead of producing an answer it can't support.

d. It handles overlapping jurisdictions

Clemson, state, and federal rules are kept separate, because they interact and sometimes conflict. On grant funds, where two limits apply, it uses the stricter one.

e. It stays current

It re-checks the source sites and documents on a schedule. When a policy changes, the rules drawn from it are flagged for re-review rather than left unchanged.

f. It's reachable in more than one way

The same answers are available through a web page, by voice, and through a connector other software can call, so there is one source rather than separate copies.

07

How is it done?

Answering a procurement question well takes four steps: read the rules, understand the question, find the right passage, and know how the rules connect to each other. Here's how a normal AI chatbot handles each one — and what we do differently, and why it matters.

1 · Reading the documents

Getting the policy into the system.
A normal chatbotYou hand it a PDF or give it a URL, and it flattens whatever it finds into one long stream of text. Tables get mangled in the process — a row that means "$25,000 to $50,000 → sealed bid" dissolves into loose, disconnected numbers and words. And it only knows the one page or file you pointed it at, frozen at that moment — it won't follow the links to related rules living on other pages.
HereWe visit the real procurement websites and their linked PDFs, and pull tables in as tables, so "$25,000–$50,000 → sealed bid" stays one intact row. We also remember how pages link to each other, and we re-check the sources on a schedule — when a policy page changes, we notice and flag what it affects.

Why it matters. Almost every threshold lives in a table. If the table is scrambled on the way in, every answer built on it is shaky — and a policy that quietly changed last month shouldn't keep handing out last month's answer.

2 · What the AI is allowed to do

The role of the language model (the "LLM").
A normal chatbotThe language model writes the entire answer — including the dollar figure. And it produces that number the same way it produces every word: by predicting what's statistically likely to come next. That's exactly why it can sound completely confident and still be off by a digit or a decimal.
HereThe language model is never allowed to make up a number. It does two narrow jobs: at the start, turn your plain-English question into facts ("$300 · furniture · grant money"), and at the end, put an already-decided answer into readable language. The figure itself is looked up, not written.

Why it matters. On a threshold, "almost right" is a compliance failure, not a typo. Taking the number out of the model's hands removes the single most common way these tools go wrong.

3 · Finding the right passage

"Vector search" — matching by meaning.
A normal chatbotIt searches for text that looks similar to your question — this is "vector search," matching on meaning rather than exact words — and feeds the closest-looking paragraphs to the model. But closest-looking isn't the same as correct: it can confidently grab a paragraph about the wrong program, or the wrong dollar band.
HereWe use that same similarity search — but only for open-ended "how do I…" questions, where surfacing and explaining the right passage is the whole point. For anything that hinges on an exact rule or threshold, we don't trust "looks similar" at all; we look the rule up directly (next step).

Why it matters. Similarity is excellent for "explain this to me" and risky for "what's the limit." Using each tool only for what it's actually good at is the entire idea.

4 · Knowing how the rules connect

The "graph" — rules that reference each other.
A normal chatbotIt treats every paragraph as an island. It has no built-in idea that one rule overrides another, that an exception cancels the usual requirement, or that paying with a federal grant swaps in a stricter limit. Each "unless" and "but" is just more text it hopes to recall.
HereWe store the rules as a connected map — a "graph" — where each rule knows the others it depends on: this is an exception to that; this state rule is overridden by a stricter federal one; this purchase also needs that approval. When you ask, the system walks those connections and shows you the exact chain it followed.

Why it matters. Real procurement rules are built out of "unless," "but," and "see also." A pile of disconnected paragraphs can't follow that reliably; a connected map can — and can prove the path it took to the answer.
